# Depreciation, Section 179 Expensing, and Bonus Depreciation
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 167, 168, 168(k), 179, 280F
**Relevant Forms:** Form 4562 (Depreciation and Amortization), Form 4797 (Sales of Business Property)
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

The federal tax code provides three primary mechanisms for recovering the cost of business assets: regular MACRS depreciation (Sections 167/168), Section 179 immediate expensing, and bonus depreciation under Section 168(k). The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed into law on July 4, 2025 (Public Law 119-21), dramatically reshaped the depreciation landscape by permanently restoring 100% bonus depreciation, doubling Section 179 limits, and making both provisions permanent fixtures of the tax code. These changes reversed the TCJA phase-down schedule that had reduced bonus depreciation to 40% for property placed in service in early 2025.

## Rules

### MACRS Depreciation (Section 168)

The Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System (MACRS) is the default depreciation method for tangible business property placed in service after 1986. Under MACRS, each asset is assigned to a property class that determines its recovery period and depreciation method.

**Standard Property Classes (GDS):**

| Class | Recovery Period | Method | Examples |
|-------|----------------|--------|----------|
| 3-year | 3 years | 200% DB | Tractor units, racehorses over 2 years old |
| 5-year | 5 years | 200% DB | Automobiles, computers, office equipment, appliances |
| 7-year | 7 years | 200% DB | Office furniture, fixtures, most machinery |
| 10-year | 10 years | 200% DB | Water transportation equipment, single-purpose agricultural structures |
| 15-year | 15 years | 150% DB | QIP, land improvements, retail motor fuels outlets |
| 20-year | 20 years | 150% DB | Farm buildings, municipal sewers |
| 27.5-year | 27.5 years | Straight-line | Residential rental property |
| 39-year | 39 years | Straight-line | Nonresidential real property |

**Conventions:**

- **Half-year convention** (default for personal property): All property is treated as placed in service at the midpoint of the tax year, regardless of actual date. A half-year of depreciation is allowed in both the first and last recovery years.
- **Mid-quarter convention** (triggered when more than 40% of the total depreciable basis of MACRS personal property placed in service during the year is placed in service in Q4): All property placed in service that year is treated as placed in service at the midpoint of the quarter in which it was actually placed in service. This convention reduces first-year depreciation for Q4 purchases and exists to prevent year-end purchase bunching. The 40% test is applied after Section 179 expensing (179 reduces the depreciable basis in the quarter, which can help avoid triggering mid-quarter).
- **Mid-month convention** (real property): Applies to 27.5-year and 39-year property. Property is treated as placed in service at the midpoint of the month it is actually placed in service.

**Alternative Depreciation System (ADS):** Required for (a) tax-exempt use property, (b) tax-exempt bond-financed property, (c) property used predominantly outside the United States, (d) listed property used 50% or less for business, and (e) property of a real property trade or business or farming business that elects out of the Section 163(j) interest limitation. ADS uses straight-line depreciation over longer recovery periods (e.g., 20 years for QIP vs. 15 years under GDS; 30 years for residential rental property vs. 27.5 years; 40 years for nonresidential real property vs. 39 years).

### Section 179 Expensing

Section 179 permits an immediate deduction for the cost of qualifying tangible personal property and certain real property improvements placed in service during the tax year, in lieu of depreciating the cost over multiple years.

**Qualifying property:** Tangible personal property (machinery, equipment, furniture, vehicles), off-the-shelf computer software, qualified improvement property (QIP), and certain improvements to nonresidential real property (roofs, HVAC, fire protection, alarm/security systems).

**Key rules:**
- The asset must be purchased (not leased to others) and placed in service during the tax year.
- The deduction **cannot exceed the taxpayer's aggregate taxable income from all active trades or businesses.** Excess is carried forward indefinitely.
- Both new and used property qualify (must be new to the taxpayer).
- Not available for property used outside the United States, property acquired from related parties, or property used in certain regulated utilities.

### Bonus Depreciation -- Section 168(k)

Bonus depreciation allows an additional first-year depreciation deduction on qualified property. Under the OBBBA, 100% bonus depreciation is permanently available for qualifying property acquired and placed in service after January 19, 2025.

**Qualifying property:** Tangible property with a MACRS recovery period of 20 years or less (5-year, 7-year, 15-year, etc.), certain computer software, water utility property, and qualified film/television/live theatrical productions. Both new and used property qualify, provided the used property is "new to the taxpayer" and meets the acquisition requirements.

**Critical exclusions:** Bonus depreciation does **not** apply to 27.5-year residential rental property or 39-year nonresidential real property. These long-lived real property classes must be depreciated under standard MACRS (or ADS if elected/required).

**Key rules:**
- Bonus depreciation is mandatory unless the taxpayer elects out under Section 168(k)(7). The election out applies to an entire class of property for that tax year and is generally irrevocable.
- Unlike Section 179, bonus depreciation has no taxable income limitation and can create or increase a net operating loss (NOL).
- For the first tax year ending after January 19, 2025, taxpayers may elect to apply the pre-OBBBA (TCJA) 2025 rate of 40% instead of 100%.

**Transition rule:** Property acquired on or before January 19, 2025, qualifies for only 40% bonus depreciation (the pre-OBBBA TCJA rate), even if placed in service later in 2025. The acquisition date is no later than the date a written, binding contract is executed.

### Qualified Improvement Property (QIP)

QIP is any improvement to an interior portion of a nonresidential building placed in service after the building was originally placed in service. Excludes enlargements, elevators/escalators, and changes to the building's internal structural framework.

- **Recovery period:** 15 years (GDS) / 20 years (ADS)
- **Depreciation method:** 150% declining balance (GDS); straight-line (ADS)
- **Bonus eligible:** Yes. Under the OBBBA, QIP placed in service after January 19, 2025, qualifies for permanent 100% bonus depreciation. The 15-year recovery period keeps QIP within the 20-year-or-less threshold required for Section 168(k) eligibility.
- **Section 179 eligible:** Yes, subject to the annual 179 limits.

### Luxury Auto Limits (Section 280F)

Section 280F caps annual depreciation deductions (including Section 179 and bonus) for passenger automobiles (GVWR of 6,000 lbs or less), including cars, trucks, and vans.

### Heavy SUVs and Trucks (Over 6,000 lbs GVWR)

Vehicles with GVWR exceeding 6,000 lbs but not exceeding 14,000 lbs that are primarily designed to carry passengers on public roads are subject to a special Section 179 cap but are **not** subject to the Section 280F luxury auto depreciation limits. Any cost exceeding the Section 179 cap can be recovered through bonus depreciation (100% post-OBBBA) and regular MACRS over 5 years.

Vehicles exceeding 14,000 lbs GVWR and vehicles not primarily designed to carry passengers (cargo vans, box trucks, etc.) are not subject to either the Section 280F limits or the heavy SUV Section 179 cap.

### Listed Property (Section 280F(b))

Listed property includes passenger automobiles, entertainment/recreation equipment, and computers/peripherals (unless used exclusively at a regular business establishment). Listed property must be used **more than 50%** for qualified business use to claim MACRS depreciation and bonus depreciation. If business use is 50% or less, the taxpayer must use ADS straight-line depreciation.

**Recapture:** If business use drops to 50% or below in any subsequent year during the recovery period, the taxpayer must recapture the excess of accelerated depreciation (including any Section 179 and bonus depreciation) over what would have been allowed under ADS straight-line. The recaptured amount is reported as ordinary income on Form 4797.

### Standard Mileage Rate

Taxpayers may elect the IRS standard mileage rate in lieu of tracking actual vehicle expenses (depreciation, gas, insurance, repairs). However, once a taxpayer uses the actual expense method in the first year the vehicle is placed in service for business, the taxpayer **cannot** switch to the standard mileage rate for that vehicle in subsequent years. The reverse is permitted: a taxpayer who uses the standard mileage rate in the first year may switch to actual expenses later (but must use straight-line depreciation for the remaining useful life).

| 2025 | $2,500,000 | $4,000,000 | $6,500,000 |

## OBBBA Changes (if applicable)

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (Public Law 119-21, signed July 4, 2025) made the following changes to depreciation and expensing:

1. **100% Bonus Depreciation Permanently Restored** (Section 11011): Reversed the TCJA phase-down. 100% first-year bonus depreciation applies to qualifying property acquired and placed in service after January 19, 2025, with no expiration date. Both new and used property eligible.

2. **Section 179 Limits Doubled** (Section 11012): Maximum deduction increased from $1,250,000 to $2,500,000. Phase-out threshold increased from $3,130,000 to $4,000,000. Both limits are indexed for inflation going forward and are permanent.

3. **Qualified Production Property (Section 168(n))**: New elective 100% depreciation for "qualified production property" (QPP) -- production-related real property. This is a separate, temporary provision distinct from Section 168(k) bonus depreciation.

4. **Transition rules**: For the first tax year ending after January 19, 2025, taxpayers may elect to claim the TCJA 40% bonus rate instead of 100%. This is a per-class election. IRS Notice 2026-11 provides interim guidance directing taxpayers to follow Treasury Reg. Section 1.168(k)-2 with updated effective dates.

## Agent Rules

- **Always verify acquisition date vs. placed-in-service date.** Property acquired (binding contract executed) on or before January 19, 2025, gets only 40% bonus even if placed in service later in 2025.
- **Apply Section 179 before bonus depreciation.** Section 179 reduces the depreciable basis before calculating bonus depreciation on the remainder. This ordering also affects the mid-quarter convention test.
- **Test mid-quarter convention after Section 179.** Apply Section 179 first, then check whether more than 40% of remaining depreciable personal property basis was placed in service in Q4.
- **Section 179 is capped by active business income; bonus is not.** If the taxpayer has limited business income, bonus depreciation may be more advantageous since it can create an NOL.
- **Luxury auto limits are all-in caps.** The Section 280F limits cap the combined total of Section 179 + bonus + MACRS depreciation for the year. They are not additive on top of other deductions.
- **Check GVWR on the door sticker, not curb weight.** Vehicle classification for tax purposes uses Gross Vehicle Weight Rating. Trim level and configuration matter -- the same model may straddle the 6,000-lb threshold.
- **Standard mileage rate election is locked after Year 1.** If the taxpayer claims actual expenses (including depreciation) in the first year, they cannot switch to the standard mileage rate for that vehicle. Ever.
- **QIP must be interior improvements to nonresidential buildings.** Residential buildings (apartments, assisted living) do not generate QIP. Original construction does not qualify -- improvements must occur after the building is first placed in service.
- **Bonus depreciation election out is per-class and irrevocable.** A taxpayer who elects out of bonus for 7-year property in a given year must apply that election to all 7-year property placed in service that year.

## Common Traps

1. **Confusing acquisition date with placed-in-service date for the OBBBA transition.** Equipment ordered in December 2024 but installed in March 2025 only qualifies for 40% bonus, not 100%. The binding contract date controls.

2. **Exceeding Section 179 active business income limit.** Unlike bonus depreciation, Section 179 cannot create a loss. Taxpayers with limited business income should use bonus depreciation for the excess or carry forward the unused 179.

3. **Failing the mid-quarter convention test.** A large Q4 equipment purchase that exceeds 40% of the year's total depreciable personal property basis forces all assets placed in service that year onto the mid-quarter convention, reducing first-year depreciation for earlier purchases.

4. **Claiming bonus on 27.5-year or 39-year property.** Bonus depreciation under Section 168(k) does not apply to residential rental property (27.5-year) or nonresidential real property (39-year). Only property with a recovery period of 20 years or less qualifies.

5. **Listed property business use dropping to 50% or below.** Triggers recapture of all excess depreciation (179, bonus, and accelerated MACRS over ADS straight-line) as ordinary income. Maintain detailed usage logs.

6. **Applying the standard mileage rate after using actual expenses in Year 1.** The election is irrevocable for that vehicle. Taxpayers who claim actual expenses (including any depreciation) in the first year are permanently locked into the actual method.

7. **Treating the heavy SUV 179 cap as the total deduction limit.** The $31,300 (2025) / $32,000 (2026) cap applies only to Section 179. The remaining cost basis is still eligible for 100% bonus depreciation and MACRS, with no annual cap. A $75,000 heavy SUV can be fully deducted in Year 1 ($31,300 Section 179 + $43,700 bonus depreciation).

8. **Forgetting QIP exclusions.** Enlargements, elevators, escalators, and internal structural framework changes are not QIP, even if they are interior improvements to a nonresidential building.

9. **Not electing out of bonus when beneficial.** In low-income years or when expecting higher future rates, electing out of bonus to spread deductions over the recovery period may produce a better after-tax result. The election is irrevocable once made.
