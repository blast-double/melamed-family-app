# Entity Selection and Comparison
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 1, 11, 301, 701, 1361-1374; Treas. Reg. Sections 301.7701-1 through -3
**Relevant Forms:** Form 1040 (Schedule C), Form 1065, Form 1120, Form 1120-S, Form 8832, Form 2553
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

Entity selection determines (1) the level at which income is taxed, (2) the character and rate applied to that income, (3) employment tax exposure, and (4) the flexibility available for raising capital, compensating owners, and allocating economic items among stakeholders. The federal tax code does not create business entities -- state law does -- but Treas. Reg. Sections 301.7701-1 through -3 (the "check-the-box" regulations) classify those entities for federal tax purposes and allow eligible entities to elect a classification different from their default.

Six common structures are compared below: Sole Proprietor, Single-Member LLC (SMLLC), Multi-Member LLC (MMLLC), S Corporation, C Corporation, and Partnership.

**Sources:** IRS Form 8832 instructions; IRS Publication 542; IRC Sections 1, 11, 301, 531, 701-761, 1361-1374, 1202; Treas. Reg. Section 301.7701-3. *(irs.gov/pub/irs-pdf/f8832.pdf; eisneramper.com; thetaxadviser.com; sdocpa.com; kkca.io; bloomberg tax; fraimcpa.com)*

## Rules

### Check-the-Box Elections (Form 8832)

Under Treas. Reg. Section 301.7701-3, eligible entities choose their federal tax classification by filing Form 8832 (Entity Classification Election). Default rules apply when no election is filed:

| Entity | Default Classification |
|---|---|
| Domestic single-owner LLC | Disregarded entity (reported on owner's Form 1040, Schedule C) |
| Domestic multi-owner LLC or partnership | Partnership (files Form 1065) |
| Corporation (Inc., Corp.) | Corporation -- not eligible for Form 8832; classification is fixed |
| Foreign entity, all members with limited liability | Association taxable as corporation |
| Foreign entity, at least one member without limited liability | Partnership (two+ owners) or disregarded entity (one owner) |

**Election mechanics:**

- **Effective date window:** Up to 75 days retroactive from the filing date, or up to 12 months prospective. If no date is specified, the effective date is the date the IRS receives the form. *(IRS Practice Unit: irs.gov/pub/fatca/int_practice_units/ore_c_19_02_01.pdf)*
- **60-month lock-in:** Once an entity elects to change its classification, it generally cannot change again for 60 months after the effective date, unless more than 50% of ownership changes hands and the Commissioner grants permission. *(Treas. Reg. Section 301.7701-3(c)(1)(iv))*
- **S election uses Form 2553, not 8832.** An LLC electing S-Corp status files Form 2553 under Section 1362(a), not Form 8832. An entity already classified as a corporation that wants S status also files Form 2553.

### C Corporation (IRC Section 11)

- **Tax rate:** Flat 21% on all taxable income (made permanent by TCJA 2017; unchanged by OBBBA). *(bradfordtaxinstitute.com; romano-tax.com)*
- **Double taxation:** Corporate earnings are taxed first at the entity level (21%), then again when distributed as qualified dividends (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on shareholder income, plus 3.8% NIIT for high earners). Combined effective rate on distributed profits ranges from approximately 32% to 39.8%. *(sdocpa.com/c-corp-double-taxation-explained)*
- **Fiscal year flexibility:** C corporations may adopt any fiscal year-end. This is unique among the entity types -- all pass-through entities are generally required to use the calendar year or obtain IRS approval.
- **No QBI deduction:** C corporations are ineligible for the Section 199A qualified business income deduction.

### Accumulated Earnings Tax (IRC Section 531)

- **Rate:** 20% penalty tax on accumulated taxable income (assessed by IRS, not self-reported).
- **Threshold:** First $250,000 in accumulated earnings is generally exempt ($150,000 for personal service corporations in accounting, law, health, engineering, architecture, consulting, or performing arts).
- **Trigger:** The IRS examines whether the corporation has a reasonable business need for retained earnings or is retaining them primarily to help shareholders avoid dividend taxation.
- **Defense:** Document specific, definite business plans in board resolutions, meeting minutes, and budget documents. Vague justifications like "future growth" may not satisfy the IRS. *(jupid.com; teuscherwalpole.com; hwllp.cpa)*

### S Corporation (IRC Sections 1361-1374)

- **Pass-through taxation:** Income, losses, deductions, and credits pass through to shareholders on Schedule K-1, taxed at individual rates (up to 37%).
- **Eligibility constraints:** Maximum 100 shareholders; only U.S. citizens or residents (and certain trusts and estates); one class of stock only. No corporations, partnerships, or nonresident aliens as shareholders.
- **Reasonable compensation requirement:** Shareholder-employees must pay themselves a reasonable W-2 salary. The IRS can recharacterize distributions as wages and assess penalties if salary is unreasonably low. In 2025-2026, the IRS is using advanced data matching to flag S-Corps where distributions significantly exceed salaries. *(wolftax.com; sdocpa.com)*
- **SE tax split:** Only W-2 wages are subject to employment taxes (FICA). Distributions to shareholders are not subject to self-employment tax. This is the primary SE tax advantage over sole proprietorships and standard LLCs.

### Built-In Gains Tax (IRC Section 1374)

When a C corporation converts to S status, IRC Section 1374 imposes a corporate-level tax on net recognized built-in gain (NRBIG) realized during the **5-year recognition period** beginning on the first day of the first S corporation tax year.

- **Rate:** Highest corporate rate under Section 11(b) -- currently **21%**.
- **Calculation:** Each year, NRBIG equals the lesser of (a) gain recognized that year on assets held at conversion, or (b) remaining net unrealized built-in gain (NUBIG) after subtracting previously recognized amounts.
- **Offsets:** C-year net operating losses and loss carryforwards may offset NRBIG under Section 1374(b)(2).
- **Scope:** Applies to (1) S corporations that were formerly C corporations, and (2) S corporations that acquired assets with a transferred basis from a C corporation (Section 1374(d)(8)).
- **Permanent 5-year period:** Originally 10 years, temporarily reduced to 5 years, then made permanently 5 years by the Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2016. *(IRS 2025 Instructions for Schedule D (Form 1120-S); esoppartners.com; Bloomberg Tax)*

### Partnership (IRC Sections 701-761)

- **Special allocations (Section 704(b)):** Partners may allocate income, gain, loss, deduction, and credit items in ratios different from ownership percentages, provided the allocations have **substantial economic effect**. The regulations require proper capital account maintenance and liquidating distributions made according to positive capital account balances. Allocations lacking substantial economic effect are reallocated according to partners' interests in the partnership. *(Treas. Reg. Section 1.704-1(b)(2); sdocpa.com)*
- **Section 754 election:** Allows the partnership to adjust the inside basis of partnership assets upon (1) a transfer of a partnership interest (Section 743(b) adjustment) or (2) a distribution of partnership property (Section 734(b) adjustment). This election aligns the new partner's share of inside basis with their purchase price, preventing phantom gain. Once made, the election applies to all subsequent transfers and distributions unless revoked. *(IRS IRB 2025-06; kha.cpa)*
- **Hot asset rules (Section 751):** On the sale or exchange of a partnership interest, gain attributable to "hot assets" -- unrealized receivables and substantially appreciated inventory -- is recharacterized as **ordinary income** rather than capital gain. Section 751(b) applies to disproportionate distributions of hot assets. This prevents partners from converting ordinary income into capital gain through partnership transactions.

## OBBBA Changes

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (P.L. 119-21), signed July 4, 2025, made several changes affecting entity selection:

1. **QBI deduction made permanent.** Section 199A was set to expire after 2025 under TCJA; OBBBA made it permanent for all pass-through entities. SSTB phase-out ranges widened: the distance from threshold to full phase-out increased from $50,000/$100,000 to $75,000/$150,000 (single/MFJ). A $400 minimum QBI deduction applies beginning 2026 for taxpayers with at least $1,000 QBI. *(millancpa.com; otterz.co; nkcpa.com)*

2. **Section 1202 QSBS enhanced (C-Corps only).**
   - Per-issuer exclusion cap raised from $10M to **$15M** (or 10x basis), indexed for inflation after 2026.
   - Gross asset threshold raised from $50M to **$75M**, indexed after 2026.
   - New tiered holding periods: **3 years = 50%** exclusion, **4 years = 75%**, **5 years = 100%** (for stock acquired after July 4, 2025). Previously, only 100% exclusion was available and only after 5 years.
   - Partial exclusions (50% and 75%) are explicitly not treated as AMT preference items.
   - Pre-OBBBA stock retains pre-OBBBA rules; acquisition-date carryover prevents resets into the new regime. *(business.cch.com; savillecpa.com; millancpa.com; rehmann.com; vipwealthadvisors.com)*

3. **100% bonus depreciation restored.** IRC Section 168(k) bonus depreciation restored to 100% and made permanent, benefiting all entity types. *(kkca.io)*

4. **Excess business loss limitation made permanent and tightened.** Threshold reduced for 2026 to $256,000 single / $512,000 MFJ (down from $313,000 / $626,000 in 2025). Affects all pass-through entity owners. *(nkcpa.com)*

## Agent Rules

1. **Entity selection is multi-factor.** Never recommend an entity based solely on one dimension (e.g., SE tax savings). Always model income tax, SE tax, QBI impact, AET risk, exit strategy (QSBS), and compliance costs together.
2. **S-Corp salary vs. QBI tradeoff.** Higher W-2 salary reduces SE tax exposure but also reduces QBI-eligible income. At $150K profit with $75K salary at 24% bracket, SE savings (~$9,600) typically exceed lost QBI value (~$3,600), but model both.
3. **Check-the-box timing.** Form 8832 elections are retroactive up to 75 days and prospective up to 12 months. A missed window may require waiting until the next permissible effective date.
4. **BIG tax on C-to-S conversions.** Always calculate net unrealized built-in gain at conversion date. If appreciated assets will be sold within 5 years, the 21% BIG tax erodes the benefits of S election. Consider delaying asset dispositions past the recognition period.
5. **AET documentation.** If a C-Corp retains more than $250K in earnings, verify that board resolutions and business plans document specific, definite purposes. Flag for CPA review.
6. **Section 754 elections are irrevocable (practically).** Once made, the election applies to all future transfers and distributions. Advise that the election creates ongoing administrative burden but prevents phantom gain for incoming partners.
7. **Hot asset analysis on partnership interest sales.** Always check for Section 751 ordinary income recharacterization before estimating capital gain on sale of a partnership interest.

## Common Traps

1. **Ignoring SE tax until it is too late.** Sole proprietors and SMLLC owners pay 15.3% on all net profit. At $150K profit, that is approximately $21,000 in SE tax alone. An S-Corp election with $75K reasonable salary would cut this roughly in half.

2. **Setting S-Corp salary too low.** The IRS recharacterizes distributions as wages when salary is unreasonably low, assessing back payroll taxes plus penalties. Use industry salary surveys and document the basis for the chosen salary.

3. **Assuming the "21% vs. 37%" comparison is apples-to-apples.** The C-Corp 21% rate applies only at the entity level. Extracting profits as dividends adds another layer (up to 23.8%), making the combined effective rate 32-39.8%. Pass-through entities pay higher marginal rates but only once, and may claim the 20% QBI deduction, potentially bringing the effective rate to approximately 29.6% at the top bracket.

4. **Failing to file the Section 754 election timely.** The election must be filed with the partnership return for the year of the triggering event. A missed election means the new partner inherits basis mismatches that create phantom taxable gain on future asset sales.

5. **Overlooking the BIG tax window.** Converting C-to-S and selling appreciated assets in Year 3 triggers the 21% BIG tax on the built-in gain, in addition to the individual-level tax on the pass-through gain. Wait until Year 6 if possible.

6. **Special allocations without capital account compliance.** Partnerships that make non-pro-rata allocations without maintaining proper capital accounts under Treas. Reg. Section 1.704-1(b)(2) risk IRS reallocation of all items according to partners' interests in the partnership.

7. **QSBS disqualification by entity type.** Only C corporation stock qualifies for Section 1202 exclusion. S-Corps, LLCs, and partnerships are categorically ineligible. Founders planning a large exit should weigh the QSBS benefit (potentially $15M+ tax-free) against pass-through simplicity.

8. **60-month classification lock-in.** After filing Form 8832, the entity generally cannot change classification again for 60 months. Plan the election carefully; reversing course requires waiting five years or a qualifying ownership change.
