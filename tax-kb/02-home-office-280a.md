# Home Office Deduction
**IRC Section(s):** Section 280A, Section 162, Section 1250
**Relevant Forms:** Form 8829, Schedule C (Form 1040), Form 2106, Schedule A
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

IRC Section 280A generally disallows deductions for expenses related to the use of a dwelling unit that the taxpayer uses as a residence. However, Section 280A(c) carves out exceptions permitting self-employed taxpayers and certain business owners to deduct a portion of their home expenses when part of the home is used exclusively and regularly for business. Two computation methods are available: the actual expense method (regular method), which requires tracking all allocable home costs and reporting them on Form 8829, and the simplified method established by Rev. Proc. 2013-13, which provides a flat $5-per-square-foot safe harbor up to 300 square feet.

The deduction is available to sole proprietors, partners, and members of LLCs taxed as partnerships or sole proprietorships. S-corporation shareholders cannot claim the deduction directly and must instead use an accountable plan reimbursement structure. Employees lost the ability to deduct home office expenses under the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) beginning in 2018, and the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed July 4, 2025, made that elimination permanent. Narrow exceptions remain for Armed Forces reservists, qualified performing artists, and fee-basis state/local government officials, who claim the deduction on Form 2106 as an above-the-line adjustment.

**Sources:** [IRS Publication 587](https://www.irs.gov/publications/p587); [26 U.S.C. Section 280A](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/280A); [Journal of Accountancy, "Deducting Home Office Expenses"](https://www.journalofaccountancy.com/issues/2020/may/deduct-home-office-expenses-coronavirus-remote-work/); [The Tax Adviser, "Rev. ## Rules

### 1. Qualification: Regular and Exclusive Use (Section 280A(c)(1))

To qualify for the home office deduction, a taxpayer must use a specific area of the home **exclusively** and **regularly** for one of the following qualifying purposes:

**(a) Principal place of business.** The home office qualifies if it is the taxpayer's principal place of business. Under the Taxpayer Relief Act of 1997 amendment to Section 280A(c)(1), a home office qualifies as the principal place of business if the taxpayer uses it for **administrative or management activities** of the trade or business **and there is no other fixed location** where the taxpayer conducts substantial administrative or management activities. This means a consultant who meets clients at their locations but performs all bookkeeping, scheduling, and correspondence from a home office satisfies this test. The taxpayer need not spend the majority of working hours at home; the administrative/management activities test is determinative. (*Soliman v. Commissioner*, 506 U.S. 168 (1993), was legislatively overridden by this amendment.)

**(b) Place to meet clients or customers.** The home office qualifies if the taxpayer physically meets with patients, clients, or customers at the home in the normal course of business on a regular basis. Phone calls alone do not satisfy this test; physical presence is required. Courts have indicated that meetings on one to two days per week on a regular basis are sufficient. The space used for meetings must still satisfy the exclusive use requirement.

**(c) Separate structure.** A separate freestanding structure (detached garage, studio, barn, workshop, greenhouse) used exclusively and regularly in connection with the taxpayer's trade or business qualifies for the deduction. The separate structure does **not** need to be the taxpayer's principal place of business.

**Exclusive use defined.** The designated area must be used **only** for business. A guest bedroom that doubles as an office does not qualify. However, the space need not be an entire room; a clearly delineated portion of a room used solely for business can qualify. Two narrow exceptions to the exclusive use requirement exist: (1) storage of inventory or product samples if the home is the sole fixed business location (Section 280A(c)(2)), and (2) use as a licensed day-care facility (Section 280A(c)(4)).

**Regular use defined.** The business use must be on a continuing basis, not merely occasional or incidental. The Tax Court has held that as few as 12 hours per week can constitute regular use (*Green v. Commissioner*, 79 TC 428 (1982)), but the facts-and-circumstances analysis looks at consistency over time.

**Sources:** [Manay CPA, "Home Office Tax Deduction Guide (2026)"](https://www.manaycpa.com/home-office-tax-deduction-guide/); [Anderson Advisors, "Section 280A: Home Office Deduction Rules"](https://andersonadvisors.com/blog/section-280a-home-office-deduction-rules/); [Jupid, "Home Office Deduction 2026"](https://jupid.com/blog/home-office-deduction-guide-2026).

### 2. Regular Method: Actual Expenses (Form 8829)

Under the regular method, the taxpayer computes the deduction by multiplying actual home expenses by the **business-use percentage** (office square footage divided by total home square footage). Any reasonable allocation method is permitted, including a room-count method if rooms are approximately equal in size (Prop. Reg. Section 1.280A-2(i)(3)).

**Deductible expenses** fall into two categories:

- **Direct expenses** (100% deductible): Costs that benefit only the office space, such as painting the office room, installing built-in shelving for the office, or dedicated office repairs.
- **Indirect expenses** (deductible at the business-use percentage): Mortgage interest, rent, real estate taxes, homeowner's insurance, utilities (electric, gas, water, internet), general repairs and maintenance, HOA dues, security systems, and depreciation of the home.

**Depreciation.** Under the regular method, the business-use portion of the home (excluding land) is depreciated over 39 years using straight-line MACRS for nonresidential real property. Upon sale of the home, the depreciation claimed (or that should have been claimed) is subject to recapture as **unrecaptured Section 1250 gain**, taxed at a maximum rate of **25%** (see Section 7 below).

**SALT interaction.** The business-use portion of real estate taxes is deducted as a business expense on Form 8829 and is **not** subject to the $10,000 SALT cap (TCJA Section 164(b)(6), now made permanent by OBBBA). Only the personal-use portion of real estate taxes is combined with other state and local taxes for the SALT limitation on Schedule A.

**Sources:** [Journal of Accountancy, "Deducting Home Office Expenses"](https://www.journalofaccountancy.com/issues/2020/may/deduct-home-office-expenses-coronavirus-remote-work/); [IRS Form 8829 Instructions (2025)](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-pdf/i8829.pdf); [Blue & Co., "Actual vs. Simplified Method"](https://www.blueandco.com/actual-home-office-expenses-vs-the-simplified-method/).

### 3. Simplified Method (Rev. Proc. 2013-13)

The simplified method, available since tax year 2013, allows the taxpayer to deduct **$5 per square foot** of qualifying home office space, up to a **maximum of 300 square feet**, yielding a maximum deduction of **$1,500 per year**.

**Key features and limitations:**

- **No depreciation** is deducted under the simplified method, and therefore **no depreciation recapture** applies upon sale of the home. This is a significant advantage for taxpayers who anticipate selling at a gain.
- **No Form 8829** is required. The taxpayer elects the simplified method by entering two figures directly on Schedule C: total home square footage and office square footage.
- **Mortgage interest, property taxes, and casualty losses** remain deductible on Schedule A as personal itemized deductions (they are not consumed by the home office calculation).
- **No carryforward:** If the simplified method deduction is reduced by the gross income limitation (see Section 4 below), the disallowed amount is permanently lost; it cannot be carried to future years.
- **Year-by-year election:** The taxpayer may switch between the simplified method and the actual expense method each year. However, carryforward amounts generated under the actual expense method cannot be used in a year the taxpayer elects the simplified method; they remain suspended until the next actual-expense year.
- **One home per year:** If the taxpayer has a qualified business use of more than one home, the simplified method may be elected for only one of them (Rev. Proc. 2013-13, Section 4.08(7)).
- **MACRS impact of switching:** If a taxpayer uses the simplified method for one or more years and later switches back to the actual expense method, the simplified-method years still count toward the MACRS recovery period, but no depreciation is deemed to have been taken in those years. Upon reverting to actual expenses, the taxpayer must use the appropriate optional depreciation table from IRS Publication 946.

**Sources:** [Rev. Proc. 2013-13](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/rp-13-13.pdf); [The Tax Adviser, "Rev. Proc. 2013-13"](https://www.thetaxadviser.com/issues/2013/aug/morrow-aug-2013/); [IRS FAQs, Simplified Method](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/faqs-simplified-method-for-home-office-deduction); [Chris Whalen CPA, "Business Use of Your Home"](https://www.chriswhalencpa.com/2025-business-use-of-home/).

### 4. Gross Income Limitation (Section 280A(c)(5))

The home office deduction **cannot exceed the gross income** derived from the business use of the home, reduced by business deductions unrelated to the home (e.g., advertising, supplies, salaries). This prevents the home office deduction from creating or increasing a business loss.

Expenses subject to the limitation are deducted in a **mandatory ordering** under Prop. Reg. Section 1.280A-2(i)(5):

1. **Tier 1:** Mortgage interest and real estate taxes (the business-use portion). These are deductible regardless of the income limitation because they are otherwise deductible on Schedule A.
2. **Tier 2:** Operating expenses that are deductible only because of business use -- utilities, insurance, repairs, maintenance.
3. **Tier 3:** Depreciation. This is deducted last and is typically the first expense to be disallowed when gross income is insufficient.

**Carryforward (regular method only):** Amounts disallowed due to the gross income limitation under the regular method may be carried forward indefinitely and deducted in succeeding tax years, subject to the same income test in those years. However, the carryforward is available **only** in years the taxpayer uses the actual expense method; it cannot be claimed in a simplified-method year.

**Carryforward (simplified method):** No carryforward is available. Any amount disallowed under the gross income limitation when using the simplified method is permanently lost.

**Sources:** [Journal of Accountancy, "Gross Income Limitation, Ordering, and Carryover"](https://www.journalofaccountancy.com/issues/2020/may/deduct-home-office-expenses-coronavirus-remote-work/); [IRS FAQs, Q15-Q17](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/faqs-simplified-method-for-home-office-deduction); [TurboTax, "IRS Form 8829"](https://turbotax.intuit.com/tax-tips/small-business-taxes/what-is-irs-form-8829-expenses-for-business-use-of-your-home/L7df8dDta).

### 5. S-Corporation Shareholders

S-corporation shareholders **cannot** deduct home office expenses directly on their personal tax returns. Because an S-corp shareholder-employee is an employee of the corporation, the deduction would fall under unreimbursed employee business expenses, which are permanently disallowed under OBBBA (see Section 6).

**Accountable plan reimbursement.** The IRS-approved method for S-corp shareholders is to establish an **accountable plan** under IRC Section 62(c). The S-corporation reimburses the shareholder-employee for home office expenses, and the reimbursement is:

- **Tax-free** to the shareholder (not reported on Form W-2),
- **Deductible** by the S-corporation as a business expense, and
- **Not subject to payroll taxes** (FICA, FUTA).

**IRS compliance requirements for accountable plans:**

1. **Business connection:** The expense must have a bona fide business purpose.
2. **Substantiation:** The shareholder must submit documented expenses to the corporation within **60 days** of incurring them.
3. **Return of excess:** Any reimbursement exceeding substantiated expenses must be returned to the corporation within **120 days**.

**Calculation:** The shareholder computes actual home office expenses (mortgage interest, rent, utilities, insurance, repairs, depreciation) and multiplies by the business-use percentage. The S-corp then reimburses this amount. The **simplified method ($5/sq ft) cannot be used** with S-corp accountable plan reimbursements; actual expenses must be calculated.

**Depreciation tracking:** Even though the reimbursement flows through the corporation, the depreciation is on the shareholder's personal residence. WCG CPAs recommends tracking home office depreciation within the individual return (Form 1040) as an out-of-service asset, updating historical depreciation annually for eventual sale and recapture calculations.

**Alternative: arm's-length rental.** An S-corp shareholder can rent a portion of their home to the corporation at fair market value. However, this approach requires care: the rental income is reported on the shareholder's Schedule E, and Section 280A(c)(6) was enacted specifically to prevent circumvention of home office requirements through disguised rental arrangements. The rental must represent genuine fair market value and the space must serve a legitimate business need of the corporation.

**Sources:** [Elevated Tax, "Accountable Plans for S Corps"](https://elevatedtax.com/accountable-plans-for-s-corps-and-partnerships-maximize-tax-free-reimbursements-in-2025/); [Julie Merrill, "S Corporation Home Office Reimbursement Guide"](https://www.juliemerrill.me/blog/write-off-home-office-in-s-corporation); [WCG Inc., "S Corp Home Office Deduction"](https://wcginc.com/kb/home-office-deduction/); [Manay CPA, "S-Corp Owners and the Home Office Deduction"](https://www.manaycpa.com/home-office-tax-deduction-guide/).

### 6. Employees: Post-TCJA and Post-OBBBA Rules

**General rule (2018 forward, now permanent):** The TCJA suspended the deduction for unreimbursed employee business expenses (including home office expenses) for tax years 2018-2025. The OBBBA permanently eliminated miscellaneous itemized deductions subject to the 2% AGI floor, which included unreimbursed employee expenses. This means **W-2 employees cannot claim a home office deduction** even if the employer requires them to work from home.

**Three narrow exceptions survive** as above-the-line deductions on Schedule 1 (via Form 2106):

1. **Armed Forces reservists** (IRC Section 62(a)(2)(E)): Members of a reserve component of the Armed Forces who travel more than 100 miles from home in connection with reserve duties.
2. **Qualified performing artists** (IRC Section 62(a)(2)(B)): Must have performed services for at least two employers, received at least $200 from each, and have related business expenses exceeding 10% of gross income from performing. Adjusted gross income must not exceed $16,000 before the deduction.
3. **Fee-basis state or local government officials** (IRC Section 62(a)(2)(D)): Officials compensated in whole or in part on a fee basis (not salaried).

These qualified employees report expenses on **Form 2106** and deduct the result on Schedule 1, Line 12. This is an above-the-line deduction available regardless of whether the taxpayer itemizes or takes the standard deduction.

**Employer workaround:** Any employer (not just S-corps) can establish an accountable plan to reimburse employees for home office expenses. Reimbursements under a qualifying accountable plan are tax-free to the employee, not reported on Form W-2, and deductible by the employer.

**Sources:** [TurboTax, "Unreimbursed Employee Expenses"](https://turbotax.intuit.com/tax-tips/jobs-and-career/employees-can-deduct-workplace-expenses/L58LFjAPa); [Kirsch CPA, "Rethink Your Deduction Strategy in 2025"](https://kirschcpa.com/accounting-services-blog/rethink-your-deduction-strategy-in-2025/); [Landmark CPAs, "Employee Business Expenses"](https://www.landmarkcpas.com/deductible-business-expenses/); [Tax Policy Center, "TCJA and OBBBA Standard and Itemized Deductions"](https://taxpolicycenter.org/briefing-book/how-did-tcja-change-standard-deduction-and-itemized-deductions).

### 7. Depreciation Recapture on Sale of Home

When a taxpayer sells a home on which depreciation was claimed for a home office, the depreciation (or depreciation that should have been claimed) is subject to **unrecaptured Section 1250 gain**, taxed at a maximum federal rate of **25%**. This recapture applies even if the gain on the home would otherwise be excluded under the Section 121 exclusion ($250,000 single / $500,000 MFJ).

**How it works:**

- The Section 121 exclusion does **not** apply to depreciation claimed after May 6, 1997. The gain attributable to depreciation is carved out and taxed at up to 25%.
- If the taxpayer claimed $15,000 in home office depreciation over the years and sells the home at a gain, that $15,000 is taxed at up to 25%, regardless of whether the remaining gain is excluded under Section 121.
- The recapture amount is reported on the **Unrecaptured Section 1250 Gain Worksheet** in the Schedule D instructions and flows to the applicable line on Schedule D.

**Simplified method advantage:** Because no depreciation is taken under the simplified method, there is **no depreciation to recapture** upon sale. Taxpayers who anticipate selling their home should weigh this benefit against the potentially larger deduction available under the regular method.

**Sources:** [EisnerAmper, "Depreciation Recapture for Real Estate"](https://www.eisneramper.com/insights/real-estate/depreciation-recapture-real-estate-0124/); [Source Advisors, "Understanding Depreciation Recapture"](https://sourceadvisors.com/tax-guides/understanding-depreciation-recapture/); [Thomson Reuters, "Depreciation Recapture Tax"](https://tax.thomsonreuters.com/en/glossary/depreciation-recapture-tax); [WCG Inc., "S Corp Home Office Deduction"](https://wcginc.com/kb/home-office-deduction/).

## OBBBA Changes

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed into law on July 4, 2025, made the following changes relevant to the home office deduction:

1. **Permanent elimination of miscellaneous itemized deductions.** The TCJA's suspension of deductions for unreimbursed employee business expenses (including employee home office expenses) was set to expire after 2025. OBBBA makes this elimination permanent for 2026 and all future years. Employees who do not fall into one of the three statutory exceptions (reservists, performing artists, fee-basis officials) have no path to a home office deduction.

2. **No direct changes to Section 280A.** The OBBBA did not amend Section 280A itself. The qualification requirements (regular and exclusive use, principal place of business), the simplified method rate and cap, and the gross income limitation remain unchanged.

3. **SALT cap changes are relevant but indirect.** OBBBA increased the SALT cap to $40,000 for 2025-2029 (with a phase-down for high-income taxpayers). This does not affect the business-use portion of real estate taxes deducted on Form 8829, which bypasses the SALT cap entirely, but it does affect the personal-use portion claimed on Schedule A.

4. **Permanent repeal of personal exemptions.** OBBBA permanently eliminates personal and dependent exemptions, continuing the TCJA change. This does not directly alter the home office calculation but affects overall tax liability.

**Sources:** [Loeb & Loeb, "OBBBA Key Changes"](https://www.loeb.com/en/insights/publications/2025/07/the-one-big-beautiful-bill-act-breaking-down-key-changes-in-the-new-tax-legislation); [Kirsch CPA, "Rethink Your Deduction Strategy"](https://kirschcpa.com/accounting-services-blog/rethink-your-deduction-strategy-in-2025/); [H&R Block, "OBBBA Tax Impacts"](https://www.hrblock.com/tax-center/irs/tax-law-and-policy/one-big-beautiful-bill-taxes/).

## Agent Rules

1. **Determine entity type first.** Before analyzing the home office deduction, confirm the taxpayer's business entity type. Sole proprietors and single-member LLCs use Form 8829 / Schedule C. S-corp shareholders must use an accountable plan. Employees generally get no deduction.
2. **Check all three qualification tests.** Walk through (a) principal place of business, (b) meeting clients, and (c) separate structure. Only one must be met, but the regular-and-exclusive use requirement applies to all three.
3. **Compare both methods.** Always run both the simplified method ($5/sq ft, max $1,500) and the actual expense method to determine which produces the larger deduction. Factor in depreciation recapture risk if the taxpayer may sell the home.
4. **Apply the gross income limitation.** The deduction cannot exceed net business income. Check for carryforward amounts from prior actual-expense years.
5. **Track depreciation meticulously.** Even in simplified-method years, count the year toward the 39-year MACRS recovery period. Maintain a running depreciation schedule for eventual sale/recapture.
6. **S-corp reimbursements require documentation.** Verify that the accountable plan exists in writing, expenses are substantiated within 60 days, and excess reimbursements are returned within 120 days.
7. **Flag the OBBBA impact for employees.** If the taxpayer is a W-2 employee, confirm they do not fall into one of the three exceptions before concluding no deduction is available.

## Common Traps

1. **Mixed-use space.** Using the "office" as a guest bedroom, play area, or personal storage area destroys the exclusive use requirement. The IRS scrutinizes this aggressively. Even occasional personal use disqualifies the space.
2. **Employee vs. self-employed confusion.** S-corp shareholder-employees are employees of the corporation. They cannot take a direct home office deduction on their 1040. The deduction must flow through an accountable plan reimbursement from the S-corp.
3. **Forgetting depreciation recapture.** Taxpayers who use the regular method for years and then sell the home are often surprised by the 25% recapture tax on accumulated depreciation. This applies even when the overall gain is excluded under Section 121.
4. **Simplified method carryforward myth.** Taxpayers sometimes assume disallowed amounts under the simplified method can be carried forward. They cannot. The loss is permanent.
5. **Switching methods and losing carryforwards.** Carryforward amounts from an actual-expense year are suspended (not lost) during simplified-method years, but they can only be used in a future actual-expense year. Taxpayers who permanently switch to the simplified method effectively abandon their carryforwards.
6. **S-corp accountable plan timing failures.** Submitting expenses annually rather than monthly or quarterly risks violating the 60-day substantiation requirement. Best practice is monthly tracking and reimbursement.
7. **Overlooking the administrative/management test.** Taxpayers who work at a client site or other office but perform all administrative tasks at home often do not realize they qualify under the principal-place-of-business test. This is the most common path to qualification for contractors and consultants.
8. **Depreciation during simplified method years.** The simplified-method year counts toward the 39-year recovery period even though no depreciation is claimed. Failing to account for this when switching back to actual expenses produces incorrect depreciation calculations.
