# S Corporation Payroll Tax Compliance
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 3101, 3111, 3121, 3301, 6302, 6672
**Relevant Forms:** Form 941, Form 940, Form W-2, Form W-3, Form 7203
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

S corporations that employ shareholder-officers face a unique set of payroll tax obligations that sit at the intersection of employment law and pass-through entity taxation. Unlike distributions, which pass through free of FICA tax, compensation paid to shareholder-employees is subject to full employment taxes under IRC Sections 3101 (employee share) and 3111 (employer share). The IRS scrutinizes S corporation payroll to ensure shareholders are not minimizing wages to avoid employment taxes. Noncompliance can result in reclassification of distributions as wages, penalties for late deposits, and personal liability for unpaid trust fund taxes under IRC Section 6672.

This document covers the reasonable compensation requirement, quarterly and annual payroll reporting (Forms 941 and 940), deposit schedules, W-2/W-3 filing requirements, shareholder basis tracking on Form 7203, and the Trust Fund Recovery Penalty.

## Rules

### Reasonable Compensation Requirement

Any shareholder who owns more than 2% of an S corporation and performs services for the corporation must receive W-2 wages that constitute "reasonable compensation" for those services. The corporation cannot pay a shareholder-employee a nominal salary while distributing the remaining profits as employment-tax-free distributions.

**Landmark Case: Watson v. United States, 668 F.3d 1008 (8th Cir. 2012).** David Watson, a CPA and sole shareholder of his S corporation, paid himself $24,000 per year while taking distributions of approximately $175,000-$203,000. The IRS challenged the salary as unreasonably low. The Eighth Circuit affirmed the district court's reclassification of a significant portion of distributions as wages, setting Watson's reasonable compensation at $91,044 based on the government expert's analysis of AICPA Management of an Accounting Practice survey data. The court held that "special scrutiny must be given to salaries paid to employees who control a corporation" and that the reasonable compensation standard applies whenever the issue is whether payments constitute wages subject to FICA tax, not only when the issue is deductibility under Section 162(a). (Sources: The Tax Adviser, Feb. 2012; Fox Rothschild analysis via Lexology; McKelvey Inc. case study)

**Factors courts consider when evaluating reasonable compensation:**
- Training, education, and experience of the shareholder
- Duties and responsibilities performed
- Time and effort devoted to the business
- Comparable compensation for similar services in the industry
- Dividend history and distributions relative to salary
- Compensation agreements and corporate policies
- Size and complexity of the business
- General economic conditions and cost of living

### FICA Taxes (IRC Sections 3101, 3111, 3121)

Employment taxes under FICA are split equally between employer and employee:

| Component | Employee Rate (Sec. 3101) | Employer Rate (Sec. 3111) | 2025 Wage Base | 2026 Wage Base |
|-----------|--------------------------|--------------------------|----------------|----------------|
| Social Security (OASDI) | 6.2% | 6.2% | $176,100 | $184,500 |
| Medicare (HI) | 1.45% | 1.45% | No limit | No limit |
| Additional Medicare | 0.9% (on wages > $200K) | None | N/A | N/A |
| **Combined FICA** | **7.65%** (+ 0.9% surtax) | **7.65%** | | |

The employer must withhold the Additional Medicare Tax of 0.9% once wages exceed $200,000 in a calendar year, regardless of filing status. There is no employer match for this additional tax. (Sources: IRS Topic 751; EY Tax News Update, Nov. 2025; SSA 2026 Fact Sheet)

### Form 941: Employer's Quarterly Federal Tax Return

Form 941 reports aggregate wages paid, federal income tax withheld, and both employer and employee shares of Social Security and Medicare taxes. Filed quarterly with the following due dates:

| Quarter | Period Covered | Due Date |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| Q1 | January 1 - March 31 | April 30 |
| Q2 | April 1 - June 30 | July 31 |
| Q3 | July 1 - September 30 | October 31 |
| Q4 | October 1 - December 31 | January 31 (of following year) |

If a due date falls on a Saturday, Sunday, or legal holiday, the return is due the next business day. Form 941 is distinct from the deposit obligation: taxes must be deposited according to the deposit schedule rules (see below) even though reporting occurs quarterly. (Source: IRS Instructions for Form 941, Rev. 03/2026)

### Form 940: Annual FUTA Return (IRC Section 3301)

Form 940 reports the employer's annual Federal Unemployment Tax Act (FUTA) liability. Only the employer pays FUTA; it is not withheld from employee wages.

- **Gross FUTA rate:** 6.0% on the first $7,000 of each employee's wages
- **Credit for state unemployment taxes:** Up to 5.4% for employers who pay state unemployment taxes timely and in full
- **Net effective FUTA rate:** 0.6% (= 6.0% - 5.4%), yielding a maximum of $42 per employee per year
- **Form 940 due date:** January 31 of the following year (extended to February 2 for TY2025 because January 31, 2026 falls on a Saturday)

**FUTA deposits:** If cumulative FUTA liability exceeds $500 in any quarter, the employer must deposit by the last day of the month following that quarter. If $500 or less, carry the liability forward to the next quarter.

**Credit reduction states (TY2025):** California (1.2% reduction) and the U.S. Virgin Islands (4.5% reduction). Employers in these jurisdictions face an increased effective FUTA rate. New York repaid its federal unemployment loan before the November 10, 2025 deadline and avoided a 2025 credit reduction. (Sources: QuickBooks FUTA Guide 2026; Experian 2026 Unemployment Tax Outlook; Paylocity FUTA Credit Reduction Alert, Nov. 2025)

### Deposit Schedule Rules (IRC Section 6302)

The IRS uses a lookback period (July 1 of the second preceding year through June 30 of the preceding year) to classify employers as monthly or semiweekly depositors.

**Monthly depositors** (prior-year lookback liability of $50,000 or less):
- Deposit employment taxes accumulated during a calendar month by the 15th of the following month.

**Semiweekly depositors** (prior-year lookback liability exceeding $50,000):
- Wages paid Wednesday through Friday: deposit by the following Wednesday.
- Wages paid Saturday through Tuesday: deposit by the following Friday.

**$100,000 Next-Day Deposit Rule:**
If an employer accumulates $100,000 or more in tax liability on any single day during a deposit period, the tax must be deposited by the close of the next business day. A monthly depositor who triggers this rule is reclassified as a semiweekly depositor for the remainder of the calendar year and the entire following calendar year. The $100,000 threshold is determined before any reduction for nonrefundable credits. Accumulation does not carry across deposit period boundaries. (Sources: IRS Notice 931, Rev. Sept. 2025; IRS Instructions for Form 941, Rev. 03/2026; APA 2025 Important Payroll Filing Dates)

**De minimis exception:** If total quarterly tax liability (Form 941, line 12) is less than $2,500, or the prior quarter was under $2,500 and no $100,000 next-day obligation was triggered, the employer may remit the balance with the quarterly return rather than making separate deposits.

### W-2 and W-3 Filing Requirements

Employers must furnish Form W-2 to each employee and file Copy A with the Social Security Administration (SSA), accompanied by transmittal Form W-3.

- **Statutory deadline:** January 31 (for TY2025, extended to February 2, 2026 because January 31 falls on a Saturday)
- **E-file mandate:** Employers filing 10 or more aggregate information returns (W-2s combined with 1099s, 1095s, etc.) in a calendar year must file electronically. This threshold took effect beginning with calendar year 2024 returns.
- **Penalties for late filing:** Range from $60 per form (1-30 days late) to $330 per form (after August 1 or if intentional disregard), with small-business caps. (Sources: IRS General Instructions for Forms W-2 and W-3 (2026); TaxBandits W-2 Deadline Guide)

### Form 7203: S Corporation Shareholder Stock and Debt Basis Limitations

Introduced by the IRS for tax year 2021, Form 7203 replaced the former 3-part basis worksheet in the Schedule K-1 instructions. It is attached to the shareholder's Form 1040 (with Schedule E).

**When required:** A shareholder must file Form 7203 if they:
- Claim a loss, deduction, or credit from the S corporation
- Receive a non-dividend distribution from the S corporation
- Dispose of S corporation stock
- Receive a loan repayment from the S corporation

**Structure:**
- **Part I (Stock Basis):** Tracks beginning basis, additional capital contributions, stock purchases, Schedule K-1 income/loss items, distributions, and ending stock basis. Stock basis cannot go below zero.
- **Part II (Debt Basis):** Tracks shareholder loans to the corporation (not third-party guaranteed loans). Debt basis absorbs losses after stock basis is exhausted and is restored as loans are repaid.
- **Part III (Basis Limitation):** Applies the ordering rules and computes allowable loss deductions.

Even when Form 7203 is not technically required, maintaining an annual basis computation is a best practice to prevent cumulative tracking errors. (Sources: IRS Form 7203 and Instructions, Rev. Dec. 2022; Hiltzik CPA; RSM Tax Alert)

### Trust Fund Recovery Penalty (IRC Section 6672)

The Trust Fund Recovery Penalty (TFRP), also known as the "100-percent penalty," is a collection device that imposes personal liability on individuals who fail to remit withheld payroll taxes to the IRS.

**Trust fund taxes** include: federal income tax withheld from employee wages, the employee share of Social Security tax, and the employee share of Medicare tax. These amounts are held "in trust" by the employer for the U.S. government. The employer share of FICA is not a trust fund tax.

**Two-prong test for liability:**
1. **Responsible person:** Any person with the duty, status, or authority to collect, account for, and pay over trust fund taxes. This includes officers, directors, shareholders with significant control, and employees who sign checks or direct payment of bills. The IRS focuses on actual authority and control, not job title.
2. **Willfulness:** A voluntary, conscious, and intentional act to prefer other creditors over the United States. Reckless disregard of the duty to remit taxes can satisfy this prong, but mere negligence is insufficient. Paying net wages while knowing funds are insufficient to cover withholding taxes constitutes willfulness.

**Amount of penalty:** Equal to 100% of the unpaid trust fund taxes. The penalty is assessed against each responsible person individually; multiple responsible persons can be jointly and severally liable.

**No reasonable cause exception:** Unlike most tax penalties, Section 6672 does not provide a reasonable cause defense. The only defenses are that the person was not "responsible" or did not act "willfully."

**Challenge procedure:** A person assessed the TFRP may pay the trust fund portion attributable to one employee for one quarter and file a refund claim. If denied, the person may sue in U.S. District Court or the Court of Federal Claims. (Sources: IRS IRM 8.25.1; CPA Journal, Nov. 2017; Taxpayer Advocate Service 2016 Annual Report; Meadows Collier analysis)

## OBBBA Changes (if applicable)

No provisions of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) directly modify the FICA tax rates, FUTA rates, payroll deposit schedules, or the Trust Fund Recovery Penalty under Sections 3101, 3111, 3301, 6302, or 6672. The reasonable compensation doctrine and Form 7203 requirements remain unchanged. If OBBBA amendments affecting these provisions are enacted, this section will be updated.

## Agent Rules

1. **Reasonable compensation is non-negotiable.** When preparing S corporation returns, always verify that shareholder-officers receiving distributions also received W-2 wages. If a shareholder performs more than de minimis services, wages of $0 or a nominal amount will trigger IRS scrutiny. Use industry compensation data (BLS, Robert Half, AICPA surveys) to benchmark.
2. **Never skip Form 7203** when a shareholder claims a loss, receives a distribution, or disposes of stock. Basis tracking errors compound over time and are extremely difficult to reconstruct retroactively.
3. **Verify deposit classification** at the start of each calendar year. Check the lookback period liability to confirm whether the entity is a monthly or semiweekly depositor. Document the determination.
4. **FUTA credit reduction states change annually.** Before filing Form 940, check the Department of Labor's published list of credit reduction states for the applicable tax year.
5. **Trust fund taxes are personal liability.** When advising any S corporation officer or shareholder with check-signing authority, flag that payroll tax withholdings must be deposited before paying other creditors. The TFRP has no reasonable cause defense.
6. **Calendar-year W-2 deadline is firm.** January 31 (or the next business day) applies to both employee copies and SSA filing. Extensions are rarely granted and do not extend the employee copy deadline.

## Common Traps

1. **The "distributions instead of salary" trap.** An S corporation shareholder takes $200,000 in distributions and $0 in salary. The IRS reclassifies a substantial portion as wages, assessing back employment taxes plus penalties and interest. Watson v. US is the leading case, but the IRS does not need a court order to propose reclassification on examination.
2. **Confusing stock basis with debt basis.** A shareholder guarantees a third-party bank loan to the S corporation and assumes this creates debt basis. It does not. Only direct, bona fide loans from the shareholder to the corporation create debt basis under Section 1366(d)(1)(B). This error leads to improperly deducted losses.
3. **Crossing the $100,000 threshold unknowingly.** An employer issues a large bonus payroll mid-month that pushes single-day tax liability over $100,000. The next-day deposit rule kicks in, and the employer misses the deposit because they are on a monthly schedule. This triggers a mandatory reclassification to semiweekly depositor and potential failure-to-deposit penalties.
4. **Late W-2 filing penalties stacking.** Filing 50 W-2 forms 45 days late at $130 per form = $6,500 in penalties. The penalty is per form, and the IRS assesses it automatically.
5. **Assuming the TFRP requires bad intent.** Courts have consistently held that "willfulness" under Section 6672 does not require evil motive or intent to defraud. Simply choosing to pay vendors, rent, or employee net wages while knowing payroll taxes are unpaid satisfies the willfulness standard. A bookkeeper, office manager, or CFO with check-signing authority can be a "responsible person."
6. **Missing FUTA deposit deadlines.** Because FUTA liability is often small, employers forget the quarterly deposit requirement when cumulative liability exceeds $500. The penalty for late deposits is 2%-15% depending on lateness.
7. **Failing to reconcile Form 941 totals to W-2/W-3.** The SSA and IRS cross-match annual W-2/W-3 totals against the four quarterly 941 filings. Discrepancies generate automated notices and can delay employee refunds.
