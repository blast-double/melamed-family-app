# Hobby Loss & At-Risk Rules
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 183, 465
**Relevant Forms:** Schedule C (Form 1040), Schedule 1 (Form 1040), Form 5213, Form 6198
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

Sections 183 and 465 are two separate loss-limitation regimes that restrict a taxpayer's ability to deduct losses from certain activities. Section 183 addresses activities not engaged in for profit ("hobbies"), limiting or eliminating deductions when the IRS determines no genuine profit motive exists. Section 465 imposes "at-risk" limitations that cap deductible losses at the amount the taxpayer actually stands to lose economically. These rules serve different purposes but often interact with each other and with the Section 469 passive activity loss rules.

**Ordering of loss limitations.** When a taxpayer incurs a loss from a passthrough entity or sole proprietorship, the loss must clear four sequential hurdles before it reduces taxable income:

1. **Basis limitation** (Section 704(d) for partnerships, Section 1366(d) for S corps)
2. **At-risk limitation** (Section 465) -- covered here
3. **Passive activity loss limitation** (Section 469) -- see cross-references
4. **Excess business loss limitation** (Section 461(l))

If a loss is disallowed at any step, it does not proceed to the next. Section 183 operates independently of this ordering; if an activity is classified as a hobby, the loss-limitation question is moot because hobby expenses are not deductible at all under current law.

Sources: The Tax Adviser, "Partnership interests, Sec. 465 at-risk limit, and Form 6198" (Apr. 2021); IRS Instructions for Form 6198 (Rev. Nov. 2025).

## Rules

### Section 183: Hobby Loss

**Core principle.** If an activity is "not engaged in for profit," deductions attributable to that activity are disallowed. Under current law (post-TCJA as made permanent by OBBBA), hobby income is fully taxable as ordinary income, but hobby expenses are completely nondeductible.

**For-profit presumption (safe harbor).** An activity is presumed to be engaged in for profit if it produces gross income exceeding deductions in at least **3 of the most recent 5 consecutive tax years** ending with the current year. For activities involving breeding, training, showing, or racing horses, the threshold is **2 of the most recent 7 years** (Section 183(d)).

- Meeting the safe harbor shifts the burden of proof to the IRS. The IRS can still rebut the presumption (e.g., by showing profitable years had trivial profits while loss years had large losses), but in practice it rarely does absent extenuating circumstances.
- The safe harbor is a presumption, not a definitive test. Failing it does not automatically make the activity a hobby; the nine-factor test applies.

**Section 183(e) election (Form 5213).** A taxpayer may elect to postpone the IRS's determination of whether the safe harbor is met until the close of the fourth tax year (sixth for horse activities) after the year the activity began. During the election period, losses are tentatively allowed and reported on Schedule C. If the safe harbor is ultimately not met, deficiencies and interest (but not penalties) apply. Form 5213 must be filed separately (not with the return) by mail to the applicable IRS service center.

**Nine-factor test (Treas. Reg. Section 1.183-2(b)).** When the safe harbor is not met, the IRS and courts evaluate profit motive using nine nonexclusive factors. No single factor is determinative; the analysis weighs objective facts over subjective statements of intent:

1. **Manner in which the activity is carried on.** Maintaining complete books and records, having a written business plan, operating with separate bank accounts, and making data-driven changes to improve profitability all weigh in the taxpayer's favor.
2. **Expertise of the taxpayer or advisors.** Studying the activity, consulting with experts, and following their advice indicates profit motive.
3. **Time and effort expended.** Devoting substantial personal time -- especially when not employed full-time elsewhere -- suggests profit intent. Hiring competent staff to run the activity can also suffice.
4. **Expectation of asset appreciation.** An overall profit can include appreciation of assets used in the activity (e.g., land), even if current operations run at a loss (Treas. Reg. Section 1.183-2(b)(4)).
5. **Success in similar or dissimilar activities.** A track record of turning unprofitable ventures into profitable ones supports profit motive.
6. **History of income or losses.** Increasing revenues and declining losses over time are favorable. Losses during a startup phase or caused by circumstances beyond the taxpayer's control (drought, market downturn) carry less negative weight.
7. **Amount of occasional profits.** The ratio of profitable years' income to loss years' losses matters. Small, sporadic profits against large, persistent losses weigh against the taxpayer.
8. **Financial status of the taxpayer.** Substantial income from other sources -- especially when the activity's losses generate significant tax benefits -- can indicate the activity is not for profit. Conversely, if the activity is the taxpayer's primary income source, that supports profit motive.
9. **Elements of personal pleasure or recreation.** Personal enjoyment alone does not disqualify an activity, but combined with other negative factors, it weighs against profit motive.

Sources: Journal of Accountancy, "Business or hobby? The nine factors" (Oct. 2013); IRS Pub. 5558, "Audit Technique Guide - IRC Section 183"; IRS FS-2022-38; Young v. Commissioner, T.C. Memo. 2025-95; The Tax Adviser, "Avoiding the hobby loss trap after the TCJA" (Nov. 2018).

### Section 465: At-Risk Limitations

**Core principle.** A taxpayer may deduct losses from an activity only to the extent of the taxpayer's "amount at risk" in that activity at the close of the tax year (Section 465(a)). This rule is applied activity by activity, before the Section 469 passive activity rules.

**What counts as at risk:**
- Cash contributed to the activity
- Adjusted basis of other property contributed to the activity
- Amounts borrowed for use in the activity for which the taxpayer is **personally liable** (recourse debt)
- Amounts borrowed for use in the activity where the taxpayer has **pledged property** (other than property used in the activity) as security
- **Qualified nonrecourse financing** secured by real property used in the activity of holding real property (Section 465(b)(6))

**What does NOT count as at risk:**
- Nonrecourse debt (except qualified nonrecourse financing for real property)
- Amounts protected against loss through guarantees, stop-loss agreements, insurance, or similar arrangements (Section 465(b)(4))
- Amounts borrowed from persons who have an interest in the activity (other than as a creditor) or from related persons under Section 465(b)(3)(C)
- Any amount the taxpayer is not economically exposed to lose

**Qualified nonrecourse financing (real estate exception).** For the activity of holding real property (not mineral property), nonrecourse debt is treated as at-risk if it is:
1. Borrowed with respect to holding real property,
2. Secured by the real property used in the activity,
3. Borrowed from a "qualified person" (a person actively and regularly engaged in lending money, such as a bank) or from a federal, state, or local government, and
4. Not borrowed from a related party or person with an equity interest in the activity (10% relatedness threshold applies under Sections 267(b) and 707(b)(1), substituting "10 percent" for "50 percent").

**Suspended losses.** Losses disallowed under Section 465 are not lost permanently. They are suspended and carried forward indefinitely. They become deductible in any future year when the taxpayer's at-risk amount increases sufficiently (e.g., through additional contributions, recourse borrowing, or income from the activity).

**Recapture rule (Section 465(e)).** If the at-risk amount drops below zero at the end of any tax year (e.g., due to distributions or liability changes), the taxpayer must include the negative amount as income. That recapture amount is then treated as a deduction allocable to the activity in the following year, increasing the at-risk loss carryforward.

**Form 6198.** Taxpayers with losses from at-risk activities must file Form 6198 to compute the deductible loss. The form has four parts: Part I computes the current-year profit or loss; Part II (simplified) or Part III (detailed) computes the amount at risk; Part IV determines the deductible loss.

Sources: 26 U.S.C. Section 465; IRS Instructions for Form 6198 (Rev. Nov. 2025); The Tax Adviser, "Partnership interests, Sec. 465 at-risk limit, and Form 6198" (Apr. 2021); IRS Pub. 925, Passive Activity and At-Risk Rules.

## OBBBA Changes

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed into law on July 4, 2025, made the following changes relevant to Sections 183 and 465:

**Permanent repeal of miscellaneous itemized deductions.** The TCJA had suspended the 2% miscellaneous itemized deduction floor (which previously allowed hobby expenses as an itemized deduction subject to the 2% AGI floor) for tax years 2018-2025. Without legislative action, hobby expenses would have become partially deductible again in 2026. The OBBBA permanently eliminates this entire category of deductions, effective for all tax years beginning after December 31, 2024. This means:

- **Hobby income** remains fully taxable as ordinary income, reported on Schedule 1, line 8.
- **Hobby expenses** are permanently nondeductible. There is no floor, no partial deduction, no itemized deduction -- they simply cannot be claimed, period.
- This creates an asymmetric result: a taxpayer whose activity is classified as a hobby must pay tax on all gross income from the activity but cannot offset any of it with expenses.

**No direct changes to Section 465.** The OBBBA did not modify the at-risk rules themselves. However, the OBBBA's changes to the excess business loss limitation under Section 461(l) -- lowering the threshold to $256,000 ($512,000 MFJ) for 2026 and making it permanent -- affect the fourth step in the loss-limitation ordering. Losses that survive Section 465 and Section 469 may still be capped by Section 461(l).

Sources: CBIZ, "The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) FAQs" (2025); Cozen O'Connor, "Changes to Itemized Deductions in the OBBBA" (2025); Vancouver CPA, "Permanent: No More Miscellaneous Itemized Deductions" (2025); Brinker Simpson, "2026 Tax Changes You Should Already Be Planning For" (2025).

## Agent Rules

1. **Always determine hobby vs. business status before applying loss limitations.** If Section 183 applies, no losses are deductible at all under current law. The Section 465 at-risk analysis is irrelevant for a hobby.
2. **Apply loss limitations in the correct order.** Basis first, then at-risk (Section 465), then passive activity (Section 469), then excess business loss (Section 461(l)). Never skip a step.
3. **Track at-risk amounts annually.** The at-risk amount changes every year based on contributions, distributions, income, deducted losses, and liability changes. Maintain a running schedule.
4. **Separate activities for Section 465.** Each activity must be analyzed independently. Aggregation is generally not permitted (with limited exceptions for certain real estate activities).
5. **Document profit motive rigorously.** For any activity with personal-pleasure elements or persistent losses, maintain written business plans, separate financials, records of expert consultations, and evidence of operational changes made to improve profitability.
6. **Flag the 3-of-5 year safe harbor window.** When an activity approaches the end of a 5-year window, advise on whether accelerating income or deferring expenses into a specific year could meet the safe harbor.
7. **Distinguish recourse from nonrecourse debt.** The at-risk classification of debt is critical. Verify personal liability, guarantees, and pledged collateral for every loan used in an at-risk activity.
8. **Watch for related-party loans.** Borrowing from persons with an ownership interest in the activity (other than as a creditor) does not create at-risk basis, even if the loan is recourse. Apply the 10% relatedness threshold.
9. **Never assume qualified nonrecourse financing applies outside real property.** The exception is limited to the activity of holding real property (excluding mineral property). It does not apply to equipment, vehicles, or other non-real-property activities.

## Common Traps

1. **Hobby income without deductions.** Post-OBBBA, the most dangerous trap is having an activity reclassified as a hobby. All gross income is taxable, but zero expenses offset it. A side business with $50,000 gross income and $60,000 expenses goes from a $10,000 loss to $50,000 of taxable income if deemed a hobby.

2. **Confusing the safe harbor with a definitive test.** Meeting the 3-of-5 year profit test creates a rebuttable presumption, not absolute protection. Conversely, failing it does not automatically make the activity a hobby -- the nine-factor test still applies.

3. **Ignoring the Section 183(e) election.** New activities often lose money in early years. Filing Form 5213 buys time by deferring the hobby determination, allowing losses to be claimed tentatively. But the election must be filed on paper, separately from the return, and before the deadline.

4. **Nonrecourse debt inflating perceived at-risk basis.** Taxpayers (especially in partnerships) sometimes count their share of partnership nonrecourse liabilities as at-risk amounts. Unless the debt qualifies as qualified nonrecourse financing for real property, it does not count.

5. **Distributions reducing at-risk amount below zero.** Cash distributions from a partnership or S corporation reduce the at-risk amount. If distributions exceed the at-risk balance, the taxpayer must recognize recapture income under Section 465(e).

6. **Stop-loss agreements and guarantees.** Any arrangement that protects the taxpayer from economic loss eliminates the at-risk amount to that extent. This includes insurance against loss, put options on the investment, and third-party guarantees.

7. **Missing the 10% related-party threshold.** For qualified nonrecourse financing, relatedness is tested at 10% (not the usual 50%). A loan from someone owning just 10% of the activity fails to qualify.

8. **Applying Section 465 after Section 469.** The ordering matters. At-risk limits are applied before passive activity limits. Getting this backward can produce incorrect loss calculations and carryforward amounts.

9. **Assuming suspended at-risk losses expire.** They do not. Suspended at-risk losses carry forward indefinitely until the at-risk amount increases or the activity is disposed of.
