# Capital Gains and Losses
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 1001, 1(h), 1091, 1202, 1211, 1212
**Relevant Forms:** Form 8949, Schedule D (Form 1040)
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

A capital gain or loss is realized when a taxpayer sells or exchanges a capital asset for an amount different from the taxpayer's adjusted basis (IRC Section 1001). Gains and losses are classified as short-term (held one year or less) or long-term (held more than one year), with long-term gains receiving preferential tax rates of 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on taxable income and filing status. Net capital losses exceeding capital gains are deductible against ordinary income up to $3,000 per year ($1,500 if married filing separately), with excess losses carrying forward indefinitely.

## Rules

### Realization and Recognition (Section 1001)

1. Gain or loss is realized on the sale or exchange of property when the amount realized differs from the taxpayer's adjusted basis.
2. Amount realized = cash received + fair market value of property received + liabilities assumed by the buyer - selling expenses.
3. Gain or loss realized is generally recognized (reported) in the year of the sale unless a specific nonrecognition provision applies (e.g., Section 1031 like-kind exchange, Section 1033 involuntary conversion).

### Holding Period and Rate Classification

1. **Short-term**: Asset held for one year or less. Gain taxed at ordinary income rates (10% through 37%).
2. **Long-term**: Asset held for more than one year. Gain taxed at preferential rates (0%, 15%, or 20%) based on taxable income.
3. The holding period begins the day after acquisition and includes the day of disposition.
4. One calendar day determines the classification: an asset acquired on January 15, 2025 and sold on January 15, 2026 is short-term (held exactly one year); sold on January 16, 2026 is long-term (held more than one year).

### Special Capital Gains Rates

- **Collectibles (Section 1(h)(5))**: Long-term gains from collectibles (art, antiques, gems, stamps, coins, precious metals, wine, NFTs) are taxed at a maximum rate of 28%. If the taxpayer's ordinary income rate is below 28%, the lower ordinary rate applies instead.
- **Unrecaptured Section 1250 Gain**: The portion of gain on the sale of depreciable real property attributable to previously claimed depreciation (straight-line) is taxed at a maximum rate of 25%. Depreciation in excess of straight-line is recaptured as ordinary income under Section 1245.
- **Qualified Small Business Stock (Section 1202)**: See OBBBA Changes section below.
- **Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT)**: An additional 3.8% surtax applies to net investment income (including capital gains) for taxpayers with MAGI exceeding $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married filing jointly). These thresholds are not indexed for inflation.

### Wash Sale Rule (Section 1091)

1. A loss from the sale of stock or securities is **disallowed** if the taxpayer acquires substantially identical stock or securities within the 61-day window beginning 30 days before the sale and ending 30 days after the sale.
2. The disallowed loss is added to the cost basis of the replacement shares, effectively deferring (not eliminating) the loss.
3. The holding period of the original shares tacks onto the replacement shares.
4. The wash sale rule applies only to **losses**, never to gains.
5. The rule applies across **all accounts** the taxpayer controls, including taxable brokerage accounts, IRAs (traditional and Roth), and spousal accounts.
6. **IRA exception (Rev. Rul. 2008-5)**: If a taxpayer sells at a loss in a taxable account and repurchases substantially identical securities in an IRA within the 61-day window, the loss is permanently disallowed. The disallowed loss does not increase the IRA's basis because basis adjustments inside IRAs produce no tax benefit.
7. **Cryptocurrency**: As of 2026, the wash sale rule under Section 1091 does not explicitly apply to cryptocurrency or digital assets. Crypto is classified as property, not stock or securities, for federal income tax purposes. No enacted legislation has extended Section 1091 to digital assets, though proposals have been introduced. This may change in future legislation.

### Capital Loss Limitation (Sections 1211 and 1212)

1. Capital losses first offset capital gains dollar-for-dollar without limitation. Short-term losses offset short-term gains first; long-term losses offset long-term gains first. Any net loss in one category offsets net gains in the other.
2. If net capital losses exceed net capital gains, individuals may deduct the lesser of:
   - The net capital loss, or
   - $3,000 ($1,500 if married filing separately)
   against ordinary income (Section 1211(b)).
3. Excess capital losses carry forward indefinitely to subsequent tax years, retaining their short-term or long-term character (Section 1212(b)).
4. There is **no carryback** of capital losses for individual taxpayers. (Corporations may carry back net capital losses 3 years under Section 1212(a), but this does not apply to individuals.)
5. Capital loss carryforwards are reported on the Capital Loss Carryover Worksheet in the Schedule D instructions or IRS Publication 550.

### Form 8949 Reporting

Form 8949 is organized into two parts:
- **Part I**: Short-term transactions (held one year or less)
- **Part II**: Long-term transactions (held more than one year)

Each part uses checkbox categories to classify transactions:

| Box | Description | When Used |
|-----|-------------|-----------|
| **A** (short-term) / **D** (long-term) | Basis reported to the IRS on Form 1099-B; no adjustments needed | Covered securities with correct basis |
| **B** (short-term) / **E** (long-term) | Basis reported to the IRS on Form 1099-B; adjustments required | Covered securities needing wash sale, basis, or other adjustments |
| **C** (short-term) / **F** (long-term) | Basis NOT reported to the IRS | Non-covered securities, inherited assets, or transactions without 1099-B |

For 2025 and later, new Boxes G/H/I (short-term) and J/K/L (long-term) apply to digital asset transactions reported on Form 1099-DA. Digital assets should not be reported under Boxes C or F.

Totals from Form 8949 flow to Schedule D:
- Box A/G totals to Schedule D, line 1b
- Box B/H totals to Schedule D, line 2
- Box C/I totals to Schedule D, line 3
- Box D/J totals to Schedule D, line 8b
- Box E/K totals to Schedule D, line 9
- Box F/L totals to Schedule D, line 10

### Schedule D

Schedule D nets all short-term and long-term gains and losses, applies the preferential rate schedule to net long-term capital gains, and calculates the tax using the Qualified Dividends and Capital Gain Tax Worksheet or the Schedule D Tax Worksheet (for taxpayers with 28% rate gains, unrecaptured Section 1250 gain, or Section 1202 exclusions).

## OBBBA Changes (Section 1202 QSBS)

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed into law July 4, 2025, made significant changes to Section 1202. These changes apply only to stock **issued after July 4, 2025**. Stock issued before that date follows pre-OBBBA rules.

**OLD RULE (pre-OBBBA, stock issued before July 5, 2025):**
- 100% exclusion of gain requires holding QSBS for more than 5 years (for stock issued after September 27, 2010).
- Exclusion limited to the greater of $10 million or 10 times the taxpayer's adjusted basis, per issuing company.
- Issuing corporation's aggregate gross assets cannot exceed $50 million at any time before and immediately after issuance.
- No partial exclusion for holding periods under 5 years.

**NEW RULE (OBBBA, stock issued after July 4, 2025):**
- **Tiered exclusion** based on holding period:
  - Held more than 3 years: 50% exclusion
  - Held more than 4 years: 75% exclusion
  - Held more than 5 years: 100% exclusion
- Exclusion cap increased to the greater of **$15 million** or 10 times adjusted basis, per issuing company.
- The $15 million cap is indexed for inflation beginning in tax years after 2026.
- Aggregate gross asset threshold increased from $50 million to **$75 million**, also indexed for inflation after 2026.
- The taxable portion of QSBS gain (when exclusion is less than 100%) is taxed at a maximum 28% rate.

*Sources: OBBBA (P.L. 119-21); Grant Thornton; McLane; Rehmann; SDO CPA.*

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE:** Always determine holding period first. One day difference (held exactly 1 year vs. 1 year + 1 day) can change the tax rate from 37% to 20% on large gains. When a client asks about selling an asset, calculate the exact acquisition date and determine whether waiting even one additional day converts the gain from short-term to long-term.

> **AGENT RULE:** When processing capital gains, apply this netting order: (1) net short-term gains/losses within the short-term category, (2) net long-term gains/losses within the long-term category, (3) if one category shows a net gain and the other a net loss, offset them, (4) apply the $3,000 limitation only to any remaining net loss after all netting.

> **AGENT RULE:** For QSBS analysis, always check the stock issuance date. If issued before July 5, 2025, apply the pre-OBBBA $10M cap and 5-year-only exclusion. If issued after July 4, 2025, apply the OBBBA tiered exclusion with $15M cap.

## Common Traps

> **TRAP:** Wash sales apply across all accounts the taxpayer controls, including IRAs. Selling a stock at a loss in a taxable account and repurchasing in an IRA within the 61-day window triggers wash sale rules. Per Rev. Rul. 2008-5, the loss is **permanently lost** -- it is not added to the IRA basis because basis inside an IRA has no tax effect. This is worse than a standard wash sale, which merely defers the loss.

> **TRAP:** Brokers report wash sales only within a single account based on identical (not substantially identical) securities. The taxpayer is responsible for identifying wash sales across accounts and across substantially identical securities (e.g., selling an S&P 500 ETF in one account and buying a substantially identical S&P 500 ETF in another). Failure to report cross-account wash sales is a compliance risk.

> **TRAP:** The $3,000 capital loss deduction limit has not been indexed for inflation since 1978. Taxpayers with large capital losses and no offsetting gains may take decades to fully utilize their carryforwards at $3,000 per year.

> **TRAP:** QSBS stock issued before July 5, 2025 that is later exchanged in a tax-free Section 351 contribution or Section 368(a) reorganization remains subject to the original $10 million exclusion cap, even though the new stock may technically be "issued" after that date. The pre-OBBBA limit follows the original issuance.

> **TRAP:** Crypto wash sales are currently not prohibited under Section 1091 because crypto is classified as property, not stock or securities. However, this is a gray area. Aggressive same-day repurchase patterns may increase audit risk, and future legislation could close this gap retroactively or prospectively.
