# Retirement Plan Contributions and Limits
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 401, 402, 408, 408A, 415, 219
**Relevant Forms:** Form W-2 (Box 12), Form 5498, Form 8606, Form 1040 (Lines 4a-4b, 20)
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

Retirement plan contributions are governed by an interlocking set of IRC provisions that impose separate limits on employee deferrals (Section 402(g)), total annual additions (Section 415(c)), IRA contributions (Sections 219, 408, 408A), and catch-up contributions (Section 414(v)). The IRS publishes cost-of-living adjustments annually; 2025 figures were set by IRS Notice 2024-80 and 2026 figures by IRS Notice 2025-67 (IR-2025-111, Nov. 13, 2025). The SECURE 2.0 Act of 2022 introduced a higher "super catch-up" for ages 60-63, indexed IRA catch-up contributions for inflation starting in 2026, and mandated Roth treatment of catch-up contributions for high earners beginning in 2026.

---

## Rules

### Employer-Sponsored Plans: 401(k), 403(b), Governmental 457(b)

**Employee elective deferrals** are subject to the Section 402(g) limit, which applies across all plans in which a participant is enrolled in a given year. If a participant has two 401(k) plans at different employers, the combined deferral cannot exceed the annual limit.

**Employer contributions** (match, profit-sharing, nonelective) are separate from the employee deferral limit but count toward the total Section 415(c) annual additions limit. The Section 415(c) limit is the lesser of 100% of compensation or the dollar cap.

**Catch-up contributions** under Section 414(v) are available to participants who are age 50 or older by December 31 of the tax year. Catch-up amounts sit on top of both the 402(g) deferral limit and the 415(c) annual additions limit.

**SECURE 2.0 super catch-up (ages 60-63):** For tax years beginning in 2025, participants who turn age 60, 61, 62, or 63 during the year may contribute a higher catch-up amount in lieu of the standard catch-up. This is not additive -- it replaces the standard catch-up. Eligibility reverts to the standard catch-up at age 64. The $11,250 super catch-up figure is the greater of $10,000 or 150% of the regular catch-up limit, indexed for inflation.

**Mandatory Roth catch-up for high earners (effective January 1, 2026):** Participants whose prior-year FICA wages from the plan sponsor exceeded $150,000 (indexed; originally $145,000 per SECURE 2.0, adjusted to $150,000 by IRS Notice 2025-67) must make all catch-up contributions on a Roth (after-tax) basis. This applies to both the standard and super catch-up.

### Traditional IRA (Section 219, Section 408)

Any individual with earned income (or a spouse with earned income, for spousal IRAs) may contribute to a traditional IRA up to the annual limit. Deductibility depends on whether the taxpayer (or spouse) is an "active participant" in an employer-sponsored retirement plan:

- **Not an active participant:** Full deduction regardless of income.
- **Active participant:** Deduction is phased out over a MAGI range that depends on filing status (see thresholds below).
- **Nondeductible contributions:** Permitted regardless of income or active-participant status. Must be tracked on Form 8606 to establish basis and avoid double taxation on future distributions or conversions.

### Roth IRA (Section 408A)

Direct Roth IRA contributions are subject to income-based phase-outs. There is no deduction for Roth contributions, but qualified distributions (after age 59 1/2 and meeting the 5-year rule) are entirely tax-free. Roth IRAs are not subject to required minimum distributions (RMDs) during the owner's lifetime (confirmed by SECURE 2.0).

### SEP-IRA (Section 408(k))

Employer-only contributions. The contribution limit is the lesser of 25% of the employee's compensation or the dollar cap. For self-employed individuals, "compensation" is net self-employment income after the deduction for one-half of self-employment tax, effectively yielding approximately 20% of net Schedule C income. Contributions are deductible by the employer. Deadline: employer's tax filing deadline, including extensions.

### SIMPLE IRA (Section 408(p))

Available to employers with 100 or fewer employees. Employer must provide either a dollar-for-dollar matching contribution up to 3% of compensation (may be reduced to 1% in no more than 2 out of 5 years) or a 2% nonelective contribution on all eligible employees' compensation (up to $360,000 for 2026). SECURE 2.0 introduced enhanced limits for employers with 25 or fewer employees and a super catch-up for ages 60-63.

### Backdoor Roth IRA

There is no income limit on traditional IRA contributions (only on the deductibility) and no income limit on Roth conversions. High earners can therefore: (1) make a nondeductible traditional IRA contribution, then (2) convert that amount to a Roth IRA. The conversion is reported on Form 8606, Part II. The IRS has not formally endorsed or condemned this strategy, but it has survived multiple legislative attempts to close it.

**Pro-rata rule (Section 408(d)(2)):** The IRS aggregates all traditional IRA, SEP-IRA, and SIMPLE IRA balances when calculating the taxable portion of a conversion. If the taxpayer holds any pre-tax IRA balances, a pro-rata share of every conversion will be taxable. This is the single biggest trap in backdoor Roth execution.

*Mitigation:* Roll all pre-tax IRA balances into an employer 401(k) plan (if it accepts incoming rollovers) before converting, leaving the traditional IRA at zero pre-tax balance.

### Mega Backdoor Roth

If an employer's 401(k) plan permits after-tax (non-Roth) employee contributions and either in-plan Roth conversions or in-service distributions, a participant can contribute after-tax dollars up to the Section 415(c) limit (minus employee deferrals and employer contributions) and then convert those dollars to Roth. The mega backdoor potential equals:

`Section 415(c) limit - employee deferrals - employer contributions = available after-tax room`

**Example (age 45, 2026):** $72,000 - $24,500 deferral - $10,000 employer match = $37,500 available for after-tax contributions convertible to Roth.

Plans must specifically allow after-tax contributions and either in-plan Roth conversions or in-service withdrawals. ADP/ACP nondiscrimination testing applies to after-tax contributions and can limit highly compensated employees.

## OBBBA Changes

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (signed July 4, 2025) did not directly alter retirement contribution limits or Roth conversion mechanics. However, it has indirect implications for retirement planning:

1. **TCJA tax brackets made permanent.** The seven-bracket structure (10% through 37%) continues indefinitely. This removes the "sunset risk" that was driving accelerated Roth conversions prior to 2026 and changes the calculus on conversion sizing.
2. **SALT deduction cap raised to $40,000** (effective 2025, reverting to $10,000 in 2030). Higher SALT deductions lower taxable income, potentially creating more room for Roth conversions within a target bracket.
3. **Trump Accounts created.** New tax-preferred accounts for children under 18 (up to $5,000/year, employer may contribute up to $2,500). These are treated as traditional IRAs for tax purposes.
4. **Dependent Care FSA limit increased** from $5,000 to $7,500 per household.
5. **Backdoor Roth and mega backdoor Roth strategies remain intact.** OBBBA did not enact any of the previously proposed restrictions on Roth conversions or backdoor strategies.

## Agent Rules

1. **Contribution limits are per-person, not per-plan.** The Section 402(g) elective deferral limit is an aggregate limit across all 401(k)/403(b)/457(b) plans in which a person participates. Exceeding it triggers corrective distributions and potential double taxation.
2. **IRA limits are also aggregated** across all traditional and Roth IRAs. The combined contribution to all IRAs cannot exceed the annual limit.
3. **SEP and SIMPLE IRAs have separate deferral frameworks** but IRA contribution limits for traditional/Roth IRAs apply separately from SEP employer contributions.
4. **Catch-up eligibility is based on age on December 31** of the tax year. A participant who turns 50 on December 31 is catch-up eligible for the entire year.
5. **Super catch-up replaces the standard catch-up** for ages 60-63. It is not additive. At age 64, the participant reverts to the standard catch-up.
6. **For backdoor Roth analysis, always check for pre-tax IRA balances** (traditional, SEP, SIMPLE). The pro-rata rule is evaluated as of December 31 of the conversion year.
7. **Form 8606 is required** any year a taxpayer makes nondeductible traditional IRA contributions or converts any part of a traditional IRA to Roth. Failure to file carries a $50 penalty per occurrence (Section 6693).
8. **Roth IRA 5-year rules:** (a) earnings are tax-free only after 5 tax years from the first Roth contribution, and (b) each conversion has its own 5-year clock for penalty-free withdrawal of converted principal before age 59 1/2.
9. **SIMPLE IRA 2-year rule:** Assets in a SIMPLE IRA cannot be rolled over or converted to a Roth IRA within the first 2 years of participation without a 25% early withdrawal penalty (reduced from the normal 10%).
10. **For mega backdoor Roth, the plan document controls.** Always verify the plan permits after-tax contributions and in-plan Roth conversions or in-service distributions before advising on this strategy.

## Common Traps

1. **Exceeding the 402(g) limit across multiple employers.** If a participant defers $24,500 at Employer A and also defers at Employer B, the excess must be withdrawn by April 15 of the following year or it is taxed twice -- once when deferred and again when distributed.

2. **Pro-rata rule on backdoor Roth conversions.** Taxpayers who forget about a rollover IRA, SEP-IRA, or SIMPLE IRA when executing a backdoor Roth will face unexpected tax on the conversion. The IRS looks at the aggregate of all traditional IRA balances on December 31 of the year of conversion, regardless of which account the conversion came from.

3. **Filing Form 8606 incorrectly or not at all.** Nondeductible contributions that are not tracked on Form 8606 will be treated as pre-tax when distributed, resulting in double taxation. Taxpayers should retain copies of every Form 8606 filed, as basis carries forward indefinitely.

4. **Contributing to a Roth IRA over the income limit.** Excess contributions incur a 6% excise tax per year until corrected (Section 4973). The fix is either a recharacterization to a traditional IRA (by the filing deadline including extensions) or a withdrawal of the excess plus earnings.

5. **Ignoring the SIMPLE IRA 2-year rule.** Rolling SIMPLE IRA funds to a Roth IRA within the first 2 years of participation triggers a 25% penalty (not the usual 10%).

6. **Super catch-up age miscalculation.** Eligibility is based on the age the participant will attain during the calendar year, not their age at the time of the contribution. A participant who turns 60 on December 31, 2026 is eligible for the super catch-up for all of 2026. A participant who turns 64 on December 31, 2026 is NOT eligible.

7. **Roth catch-up mandate for 2026.** Participants whose prior-year FICA wages exceeded $150,000 must make catch-up contributions as Roth. If the plan does not offer a Roth option, the participant cannot make catch-up contributions at all.

8. **SEP-IRA contribution timing.** Unlike 401(k) deferrals, SEP contributions can be made until the employer's tax filing deadline (including extensions). However, the contribution must be based on compensation actually paid during the tax year.

9. **Spousal IRA phase-out confusion.** The phase-out for a non-active-participant spouse married to an active participant uses a different (higher) MAGI range than the active participant's own phase-out. Mixing these up can cause incorrect deduction claims.
