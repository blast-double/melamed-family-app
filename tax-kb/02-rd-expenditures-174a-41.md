# R&D Expenditures: Section 174A Expensing and Section 41 Research Credit
**IRC Section(s):** Section 174A (OBBBA), Section 174 (prior law / foreign R&E), Section 41, Section 280C
**Relevant Forms:** Form 6765 (Credit for Increasing Research Activities), Form 3115 (Change in Accounting Method), Form 4562 (Depreciation and Amortization), Form 8974 (Qualified Small Business Payroll Tax Credit)
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

Research and experimental (R&E) expenditures have undergone three major legislative regimes in under a decade:

1. **Pre-TCJA (through 2021):** Full immediate expensing of all domestic and foreign R&E expenditures under former Section 174.
2. **TCJA (2022-2024):** Mandatory capitalization and amortization -- 5 years for domestic R&E, 15 years for foreign R&E. Amortization began at the midpoint of the tax year in which costs were paid or incurred. No abandonment deduction was allowed.
3. **OBBBA (2025 forward):** New Section 174A, enacted July 4, 2025 as part of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, restores immediate expensing for **domestic** R&E. Foreign R&E remains subject to 15-year amortization. Effective for tax years beginning after December 31, 2024.

Separately, Section 41 provides a credit for increasing research activities (the "R&D credit"), computed on Form 6765. The credit and the deduction interact through Section 280C, which prevents a double tax benefit.

**Key IRS guidance:** Revenue Procedure 2025-28 (issued August 28, 2025) provides implementation procedures for Section 174A elections, method changes, transition rules, and Section 280C coordination.

*Sources: Anchin, "OBBBA, The Research Credit, and Section 174" (2025); Grant Thornton, "Permanent full expensing for U.S. research in OBBBA" (2025); RSM, "The OBBBA restores favorable tax treatment of domestic R&D expenses" (July 30, 2025).*

## Rules

### Section 174A -- Domestic R&E Expensing (OBBBA)

**Default treatment (174A deduction method):** Domestic R&E expenditures paid or incurred in tax years beginning after December 31, 2024 are fully deductible in the year incurred. This is treated as a change in accounting method applied on a **cut-off basis** -- no Section 481(a) adjustment for amounts paid or incurred after the effective date.

**Elective capitalization (174A amortization method):** Taxpayers may elect to capitalize domestic R&E and amortize ratably over a period of not less than 60 months, beginning with the month the taxpayer first realizes benefits from the expenditures. A separate 10-year write-off election (similar to Section 59(e)) is also available.

**Foreign R&E:** Continues to be capitalized and amortized over 15 years under Section 174. No abandonment, retirement, or disposition deduction is permitted for foreign capitalized R&E after May 12, 2025.

**Software development costs:** Continue to be treated as R&E expenditures under Section 174A. The same domestic/foreign distinction applies.

**Controlled groups:** Each entity in a controlled group (or separate trade or business) may independently choose its Section 174A method (immediate expensing vs. elective capitalization).

*Sources: BDO, "2025 Tax Year: New Choices for Domestic R&E Costs" (2025); Brady Ware, "OBBBA and Section 174(A): The New R&D Tax Landscape" (2025).*

### Section 41 -- Research Credit

The Section 41 credit rewards taxpayers for **increasing** qualified research activities. Two calculation methods are available:

**Regular Research Credit (RRC):**
- Credit = 20% x (Current-year QREs - Base Amount)
- Base Amount = Fixed-base percentage x Average annual gross receipts for 4 preceding years
- Fixed-base percentage is capped at 16%

**Alternative Simplified Credit (ASC):**
- Credit = 14% x (Current-year QREs - 50% of average QREs for 3 preceding years)
- If taxpayer had **no QREs in any one** of the 3 preceding years: credit = 6% of current-year QREs
- Election made on timely filed return; entire controlled group must use same method

Most businesses use the ASC because it does not require historical records going back to 1984 and is more favorable for companies with fluctuating R&D spending.

**Four-part test for qualified research (Section 41(d)):**

| Test | Requirement |
|------|------------|
| 1. Permitted purpose | Research must aim to develop or improve a business component's function, performance, reliability, or quality |
| 2. Technological in nature | Must rely on principles of engineering, computer science, physical science, or biological science |
| 3. Elimination of uncertainty | Uncertainty must exist regarding capability, method, or design at the outset |
| 4. Process of experimentation | Substantially all activities must constitute systematic evaluation of alternatives (modeling, simulation, testing, trial and error) |

All four prongs must be satisfied. Activities excluded: post-commercial-production research, adaptation to specific customer needs, duplication of existing components, surveys/studies, foreign research, social science/arts/humanities.

**Qualified Research Expenses (QREs):**
- **Wages** for employees performing or directly supervising/supporting qualified research
- **Supplies** consumed or used in research
- **Contract research** -- 65% of amounts paid to third parties (must retain substantial rights to results; research must be performed in the U.S.)
- Computer rental costs used in research (largely subsumed by cloud computing)

*Sources: NSKT Global, "How to Claim the R&D Tax Credit for 2025" (2025); 26 U.S.C. Section 41; IRS Form 6765 Instructions (December 2025); JD Supra, "New Federal Research Tax Credit Rules" (2025).*

## OBBBA Changes (if applicable)

### Restoration of Immediate Expensing

The OBBBA's core change is the creation of Section 174A, which permanently restores immediate deductibility for domestic R&E expenditures. This reverses the TCJA's 5-year amortization mandate that applied to tax years 2022-2024.

### Transition Rules for 2022-2024 Capitalized Amounts

Taxpayers who capitalized domestic R&E during 2022-2024 under the TCJA have three options for the remaining unamortized balance:

| Option | Treatment | When |
|--------|-----------|------|
| **Default -- continue amortization** | Unamortized amounts continue on original 5-year schedule | No election needed |
| **Accelerate into one year** | Deduct entire unamortized balance in first tax year beginning after 12/31/2024 (typically 2025) | Requires election |
| **Accelerate over two years** | Deduct unamortized balance ratably over 2025 and 2026 | Requires election |

### Small Business Retroactive Election

Eligible small businesses (average annual gross receipts of $31 million or less under Section 448(c), not a tax shelter) may retroactively apply Section 174A expensing to tax years 2022-2024. Key details:

- Must elect for **all three years** (2022, 2023, 2024) -- cannot cherry-pick individual years
- Implemented via amended return or Administrative Adjustment Request (AAR) for BBA partnerships
- May also be accomplished through an automatic accounting method change filed with the 2024 return (per Rev. Proc. 2025-28, Section 7.02(3)(c)), avoiding the need to amend 2022 and 2023 returns separately
- **Deadline:** Earlier of July 6, 2026 or expiration of the statute of limitations for the relevant year
- Section 280C elections must be consistent across retroactive years

### Section 280C Interaction (Modified)

The OBBBA revised Section 280C(c)(1): domestic R&E expenditures deducted (or capitalized) under Section 174A are reduced by the Section 41 research credit, unless the taxpayer makes a **Section 280C(c)(2) election** to take a reduced credit instead. The reduced credit equals the gross credit minus 21% (the maximum corporate tax rate). This mirrors the pre-TCJA treatment.

Small businesses making the retroactive 174A election may also make or revoke a **late Section 280C(c)(2) election** for affected prior years by filing an amended return with an amended Form 6765. Same deadline: earlier of July 6, 2026 or statute expiration.

### Form 3115 Waiver

For the first tax year beginning after December 31, 2024, the Form 3115 requirement for the 174A method change is waived. Taxpayers may file a **statement in lieu of Form 3115** with their return.

(2025); Wiss, "New IRS R&D Tax Guidance: Rev. Proc. 2025-28 Explained" (2025).*

## Agent Rules

1. **Deduction vs. credit -- always distinguish.** Section 174A governs the **deduction** (or capitalization) of R&E expenditures. Section 41 governs the **credit** for increasing research activities. They are separate provisions with separate forms, separate elections, and separate calculations.

2. **280C coordination is mandatory.** If a taxpayer claims both a 174A deduction and a Section 41 credit, the 280C interaction must be addressed. Default: reduce the 174A deduction by the credit amount. Alternative: elect reduced credit (gross credit x (1 - 21%)) and keep the full deduction. Model both scenarios.

3. **Domestic vs. foreign must be tracked separately.** Only domestic R&E qualifies for Section 174A immediate expensing. Foreign R&E remains on 15-year amortization. A Section 174 analysis is required to segregate costs.

4. **QRE determination feeds the credit.** The same activities and costs that are R&E expenditures under Section 174/174A are generally the starting population for Section 41 QREs, but the Section 41 four-part test imposes additional constraints (e.g., no post-production research, no adaptation, no foreign research).

5. **Startup payroll offset.** For clients with < $5M gross receipts and 5 or fewer years of existence, flag the Form 6765 Section D election to offset up to $500,000 against FICA. This is reported on Form 8974 with the payroll tax return.

6. **Controlled group consistency.** Each entity picks its own 174A method, but the entire controlled group must use the same Section 41 credit method (RRC or ASC).

7. **Deadline sensitivity for retroactive elections.** The earlier of July 6, 2026 or statute expiration. For 2022 returns, statutes may begin expiring in early-to-mid 2026 (3 years from extended due date). Flag this urgently for any client with 2022 capitalized R&E.

8. **NOL interaction.** Immediate expensing of domestic R&E in 2025 may generate or increase an NOL. NOLs are limited to 80% of taxable income in carryforward years. Model whether elective capitalization under 174A(c) produces a better outcome for taxpayers expecting significant future income.

## Common Traps

1. **Missing the 2022 amended return deadline.** For calendar-year taxpayers who filed 2022 on extension (October 2023), the 3-year statute of limitations expires October 2026. But for those who filed early, it may expire sooner. Evaluate immediately.

2. **Forgetting the 280C haircut on retroactive claims.** If a small business retroactively elects 174A for 2022-2024 but claimed a full Section 41 credit in those years, the deduction must be reduced by the credit amount -- or the taxpayer must go back and elect (or revoke) the 280C(c)(2) reduced credit. Failing to coordinate creates an inconsistency the IRS will catch.

3. **Claiming the credit without satisfying all four prongs.** The IRS has increased scrutiny of R&D credit claims. Form 6765 Section G (business component detail) becomes **mandatory** for tax years beginning after 2025. Document each business component with specificity: what the uncertainty was, what experimentation was performed, and how it relied on hard science principles.

4. **Including foreign research in QREs.** Section 41(d)(4)(F) explicitly excludes research conducted outside the U.S. This is a frequent audit adjustment for companies with offshore development teams.

5. **Misclassifying contract research.** Only 65% of payments to third-party contractors count as QREs, and the taxpayer must retain **substantial rights** to the research results. Payments to related parties (5% or more common ownership) have stricter rules.

6. **Generating an excessive NOL.** Immediately expensing a large R&E spend in 2025 (plus catching up 2022-2024 unamortized amounts) could create a massive NOL. Because NOL carryforwards are limited to 80% of taxable income, the taxpayer may not recover the full benefit for years. Consider the 2-year spread option or elective capitalization.

7. **Ignoring the business interest limitation.** Section 163(j) limits business interest deductions to 30% of adjusted taxable income (ATI). Large R&E deductions reduce ATI, which reduces allowable interest deductions. Model the interaction.

8. **State conformity gaps.** Not all states conform to Section 174A or the OBBBA transition rules. California, for example, adopted a modified ASC at lower rates (3% / 1.3%) beginning 2025 but may not conform to 174A expensing. Evaluate state-by-state.
