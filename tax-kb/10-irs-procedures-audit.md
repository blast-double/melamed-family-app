# IRS Procedures, Audits, and Collections
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 6501, 6502, 6503, 6015, 7122, 7421-7430
**Relevant Forms:** Form 4564 (IDR), Form 8857 (Innocent Spouse), Form 656/656-L (OIC), Form 12203 (Appeals Request), Form 872 (Consent to Extend SOL), Form 433-A/433-F (Collection Information), Form 1040-X (Amended Return)
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

IRS enforcement follows a structured lifecycle: assessment, examination, appeals, and collection. Understanding the statute of limitations, audit procedures, dispute resolution options, and collection alternatives is critical for any taxpayer -- particularly those with complex returns involving business income, rental properties, foreign accounts, or pass-through entities. This document covers the key procedural rules governing how the IRS audits returns, issues deficiencies, collects tax debts, and how taxpayers can contest or resolve disputes.

## Rules

### Statute of Limitations on Assessment (Section 6501)

**General Rule -- 3 Years (Section 6501(a)).** The IRS must assess any additional tax within 3 years after the return was filed. If the return was filed early (before the due date), the SOL runs from the due date. If filed late, it runs from the actual filing date. [26 U.S.C. Section 6501(a); IRS IRM 25.6.1]

**6-Year Rule -- Substantial Omission (Section 6501(e)(1)(A)).** If a taxpayer omits gross income exceeding 25% of the gross income reported on the return, the assessment period extends to 6 years. For foreign financial asset income, the 6-year period applies when more than $5,000 of gross income attributable to specified foreign financial assets (Section 6038D) is omitted. [26 U.S.C. Section 6501(e)(1)(A)(ii); IRS Practice Unit, "Overview of Statute of Limitations on the Assessment of Tax"]

**Unlimited SOL -- No Return Filed (Section 6501(c)(3)).** If no return is filed, there is no statute of limitations. The IRS may assess tax at any time.

**Unlimited SOL -- Fraud (Section 6501(c)(1)).** For a false or fraudulent return filed with intent to evade tax, the statute never runs. Per the Third Circuit's 2025 decision in *Murrin v. Commissioner*, even a tax preparer's intent to evade tax can trigger the unlimited period -- taxpayer intent is not required. [Murrin, No. 24-2037 (3d Cir. 8/18/25); The Tax Adviser, Nov. 2025]

**Unlimited SOL -- Undisclosed Foreign Transfers (Section 6501(c)(8)).** When required international information returns (Forms 5471, 3520, 8865, 926, 8938) are not filed, the SOL on the entire return does not begin to run until the information return is filed. Once filed, the 3-year period then begins. [IRS Practice Unit, "Overview of Statute of Limitations"]

**Extension by Agreement (Section 6501(c)(4)).** The IRS and taxpayer can mutually agree to extend the SOL using Form 872 (fixed date) or Form 872-A (open-ended). The taxpayer has the right to limit or refuse the extension. [TIGTA, "Fiscal Year 2025 Review of Assessment Statute Extension Dates"]

### Statute of Limitations on Collection (Section 6502)

The IRS has **10 years from the date of assessment** to collect a tax debt by levy or court proceeding. This is the Collection Statute Expiration Date (CSED). After the CSED expires, the IRS must write off the balance and the taxpayer has no further legal obligation to pay. The 10-year clock can be suspended by an installment agreement request, an offer in compromise submission, bankruptcy, or the taxpayer being outside the United States (Section 6503(c)). [26 U.S.C. Section 6502(a)(1); Taxpayer Advocate Service, Sept. 2025]

### FBAR Statute of Limitations

The IRS has **6 years from the FBAR due date** to assess FBAR civil penalties. Note: FBAR penalties are assessed under the Bank Secrecy Act (31 U.S.C. Section 5321), not the IRC. FBAR litigation must be brought in Federal District Court or the Court of Federal Claims, not Tax Court. [Jenner v. Commissioner, 136 AFTR2d 2025 (11th Cir.); taxesforexpats.com, 2026]

### Audit Types

**Correspondence Audit.** The most common type. Conducted entirely by mail. The IRS sends a letter requesting documentation for specific items (charitable contributions, mortgage interest, business expenses, dependent claims). Taxpayers typically have 30 days to respond. [IRS.gov; nsktglobal.com, 2025]

**Office Audit.** Requires an in-person meeting at a local IRS office with an IRS auditor. More comprehensive than correspondence audits; commonly covers Schedule C business income, rental property income, or significant itemized deductions. Taxpayers must bring organized documentation to the appointment. [IRS.gov; morgansebastianlaw.com, 2025]

**Field Audit.** The most intensive type. An IRS revenue agent visits the taxpayer's home, business, or representative's office. Used for larger amounts, complex issues, or business operations. May involve extensive review of books, records, and operations. [IRS.gov; morgansebastianlaw.com, 2025]

**Information Document Request (IDR) -- Form 4564.** During office and field audits, examiners issue IDRs to formally request specific documents. Each IDR includes a purpose statement identifying the issue under examination. The LB&I division phased out the Acknowledgement of Facts (AOF) IDR process effective early 2026. [IRS IRM 4.46.4.7.1; Snell & Wilmer LLP, July 2025]

### CP2000 Automated Underreporter

A CP2000 notice is **not an audit**. It is generated by the IRS Automated Underreporter (AUR) program, which cross-checks third-party information returns (W-2s, 1099s, 1098s) against the taxpayer's filed return. When discrepancies are identified, the IRS issues a CP2000 proposing adjustments. The CP2000 is a proposed change, not a bill. Taxpayers should respond within 30 days (60 days if outside the US). If no response is received, the IRS will issue a Statutory Notice of Deficiency. [IRS Topic No. 652; IRS IRM 4.19.3]

### Notice of Deficiency (90-Day Letter)

The Notice of Deficiency (also called the "90-day letter" or Statutory Notice of Deficiency, SNOD) is the IRS's formal legal determination of a tax deficiency under Section 6212. Once mailed, the taxpayer has **90 days** (150 days if addressed to a person outside the United States) to file a petition with the U.S. Tax Court. During this period and while any petition is pending, the IRS is prohibited from assessing the deficiency or initiating collection. The 90-day deadline has historically been treated as jurisdictional, though the Second Circuit held in 2025 that it is a nonjurisdictional claim-processing rule subject to equitable tolling. [26 U.S.C. Section 6213(a); IRS IRM 4.8.9; Journal of Accountancy, Dec. 2025]

### Appeals

The IRS Independent Office of Appeals is an independent function within the IRS, separate from the examination division. Appeals resolves disputes by considering arguments from both sides and applying tax law to the facts.

- **30-Day Letter.** After an audit, the IRS issues a 30-day letter (Letter 525) proposing changes. The taxpayer has 30 days to agree, pay, or request Appeals review.
- **Small Case Request.** If the total proposed change in tax and penalties is **$25,000 or less** per tax period, the taxpayer may submit a small case request using Form 12203.
- **Formal Written Protest.** Required when the proposed change exceeds **$25,000** for any tax period, and always required for partnership, S corporation, and exempt organization cases regardless of dollar amount. Must include the taxpayer's name, tax periods, specific disagreements, supporting facts, legal reasoning, and signature.
- **Appeals Conferences** may be held by telephone, video, or in person. [IRS Publication 5; IRS Publication 3498 (Rev. 5-2025)]

### Forum Selection for Litigation

**U.S. Tax Court (prepayment forum).** The taxpayer does not pay the disputed tax before litigating. Jurisdiction arises from the Notice of Deficiency. Petition must be filed within 90/150 days. Handles over 95% of federal tax cases. Judges specialize in tax law. Small case procedures available for disputes under $50,000. Also has exclusive jurisdiction over CDP hearings, innocent spouse determinations, and interest abatement review. [Chambers, "Tax Controversy 2025 -- USA"; fedbar.org]

**U.S. District Court (refund forum).** Taxpayer must pay the tax in full and file a refund claim first, then sue for a refund. Unique advantage: right to a jury trial. Appeals go to the applicable Circuit Court of Appeals. [Chambers, "Tax Controversy 2025 -- USA"]

**U.S. Court of Federal Claims (refund forum).** Taxpayer must also pay first and file a refund claim. No jury trial. Often chosen for large, complex refund disputes or cases involving technical statutory interpretation. Appeals go to the Federal Circuit. [wkm-law.com, Dec. 2025]

### Collections

**Installment Agreements (Section 6159).** Taxpayers who cannot pay in full can request a payment plan. While an installment agreement request is pending, the IRS is generally prohibited from levying. The collection statute (CSED) is suspended during the pendency, plus 30 days after rejection or termination, plus any appeal period. Partial Payment Installment Agreements (PPIAs) are available when full payment cannot be achieved before the CSED expires; the CSED may be extended up to 5 years in connection with a PPIA under Section 6502(a)(2)(A). [IRS.gov; IRS IRM 5.14.2]

**Offer in Compromise (Section 7122).** An OIC settles tax liabilities for less than the full amount owed. Three grounds:

1. **Doubt as to Liability (Form 656-L).** A genuine dispute exists about whether or how much tax is owed. No application fee or financial disclosure required.
2. **Doubt as to Collectibility (Form 656).** The taxpayer agrees the tax is owed but cannot pay in full. Must demonstrate that the offer reflects the taxpayer's reasonable collection potential (RCP). Requires Form 433-A (OIC) financial disclosure and a $205 application fee (low-income exception available).
3. **Effective Tax Administration (Form 656).** The taxpayer can pay but collection would create economic hardship or would be inequitable. Congress added this ground via Section 7122(c) as part of the IRS Restructuring and Reform Act of 1998, allowing consideration of hardship, public policy, and equity.

Taxpayers must be current on all filing obligations and estimated tax payments to qualify. If accepted, the taxpayer must remain in full compliance for 5 years. [IRS Topic No. 204; IRS OIC FAQs; 26 CFR 301.7122-1]

**Currently Not Collectible (CNC).** If the taxpayer cannot afford to pay any amount without hardship, the IRS may report the account as CNC, temporarily halting collection. The debt remains; penalties and interest continue to accrue. The IRS periodically reviews the taxpayer's financial situation. CNC status can be a strategic tool to run out the 10-year CSED. [IRS.gov, "Temporarily Delay the Collection Process"]

### Innocent Spouse Relief (Section 6015)

Spouses who file joint returns are jointly and severally liable for the entire tax (Section 6013(d)(3)). Section 6015 provides three forms of relief via Form 8857:

1. **Traditional Innocent Spouse Relief (Section 6015(b)).** Relieves a requesting spouse from an understatement attributable to erroneous items of the other spouse. Requires: (a) joint return was filed; (b) understatement due to erroneous items of the nonrequesting spouse; (c) requesting spouse did not know and had no reason to know of the understatement; (d) it would be inequitable to hold the requesting spouse liable. Must be requested within 2 years of the first collection activity.

2. **Separation of Liability (Section 6015(c)).** Allocates the deficiency between spouses. Available only to requesting spouses who are divorced, legally separated, widowed, or have not lived in the same household for 12 months before the election. Uses an "actual knowledge" standard -- a higher bar than the "reason to know" standard in 6015(b). The IRS bears the burden of proving actual knowledge. Must also be elected within 2 years of first collection activity.

3. **Equitable Relief (Section 6015(f)).** A catch-all provision for taxpayers who do not qualify under (b) or (c). Critically, this is the **only form of relief available for underpayments** (where the return was correct but taxes were not paid). The IRS considers multiple factors including marital status, economic hardship, knowledge or reason to know, legal obligation, significant benefit, compliance history, and mental or physical health. No 2-year deadline applies. [IRS IRM 25.15.3; The Tax Adviser, Jan. 2022; Sample v. Commissioner, T.C. Memo. 2025-118]

## OBBBA Changes (if applicable)

However, the legislation's impact on IRS funding and enforcement resources may indirectly affect audit rates, collection staffing, and the speed of OIC and appeals processing. ## Agent Rules

- **SOL awareness.** Before engaging with any IRS notice or audit, determine the applicable statute of limitations. If the SOL is close to expiring, the IRS may rush to issue a Notice of Deficiency or request a Form 872 consent to extend.
- **Never sign Form 872-A (open-ended extension) without careful evaluation.** Prefer Form 872 with a fixed expiration date. A taxpayer can always refuse to extend the SOL; the consequence is the IRS will issue a Notice of Deficiency based on available information.
- **CP2000 is not an audit.** Do not treat it as one. Respond substantively within the deadline. If ignored, it escalates to a Notice of Deficiency.
- **Track the CSED.** For any collection matter, calculate the 10-year CSED from the assessment date. Account for suspensions (installment agreement requests, OIC submissions, bankruptcy, time outside the US).
- **Forum selection matters.** Default recommendation is Tax Court (no prepayment required). Only consider District Court if a jury trial is strategically valuable or the taxpayer has already paid.
- **Appeals before litigation.** Exhaust administrative appeals before going to court. Appeals officers settle the majority of cases and are independent from the exam division.
- **OIC strategy.** Doubt as to collectibility is the most common ground. Always verify filing compliance before submitting. An OIC rejected for non-compliance wastes time and fees.
- **Innocent spouse.** For equitable relief under 6015(f), there is no 2-year deadline -- but traditional relief and separation of liability must be elected within 2 years of first collection activity. Do not miss this window.

## Common Traps

1. **Assuming the SOL is always 3 years.** The 6-year and unlimited periods are commonly overlooked, especially for taxpayers with foreign accounts, unreported income, or complex entity structures.
2. **Ignoring a CP2000 notice.** Silence results in a Notice of Deficiency and an assessed balance with penalties and interest. The CP2000 stage is the cheapest and easiest point to resolve discrepancies.
3. **Missing the 90-day Tax Court deadline.** If the taxpayer misses the 90-day (or 150-day) window, the deficiency is assessed, and the only path is to pay in full and sue for a refund in District Court or the Court of Federal Claims.
4. **Signing an open-ended SOL extension.** Form 872-A gives the IRS unlimited time to complete the audit. Prefer a fixed-date Form 872.
5. **Filing an OIC without compliance.** The IRS will return (not reject) an OIC if the taxpayer has unfiled returns or is not current on estimated tax payments. This resets the process and wastes the application fee.
6. **Confusing OIC grounds.** Doubt as to liability (Form 656-L) and doubt as to collectibility (Form 656) cannot be submitted simultaneously. Choose the correct ground.
7. **Overlooking CSED suspensions.** Requesting an installment agreement or submitting an OIC suspends the 10-year collection clock. In some cases, this can extend the IRS's collection window significantly.
8. **Missing the innocent spouse 2-year deadline.** Traditional relief (Section 6015(b)) and separation of liability (Section 6015(c)) must be requested within 2 years of the first collection activity. Equitable relief (Section 6015(f)) has no such deadline but should still be filed promptly.
9. **Treating FBAR penalties as Tax Court matters.** FBAR penalties are not taxes under the IRC. Tax Court has no jurisdiction. Litigation must be in District Court or the Court of Federal Claims.
10. **Failing to request Appeals before litigation.** The Appeals process is free, independent, and settles most disputes. Skipping it forces the taxpayer into litigation, which is expensive and time-consuming.
