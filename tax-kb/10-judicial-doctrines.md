# Judicial Doctrines and Anti-Abuse Rules
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 7701(o), 111, 267, 318, 1341; Treas. Reg. Section 1.451-2
**Relevant Forms:** Form 1040, Form 8886 (Reportable Transaction Disclosure), Schedule A, Schedule 3
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

Federal tax law includes judicial doctrines that allow the IRS and courts to look beyond the literal text of the Internal Revenue Code to prevent tax avoidance. The economic substance doctrine was codified at Section 7701(o) in 2010 with strict-liability penalties. The remaining doctrines exist as court-created rules applied on a case-by-case basis. Collectively, these rules enforce the principle that substance, not form, governs tax consequences.

## Rules

### 1. Economic Substance Doctrine (Section 7701(o))

Codified in 2010 via P.L. 111-152. Section 7701(o) imposes a **conjunctive two-prong test** -- a transaction has economic substance only if:

- **(A) Objective prong:** The transaction meaningfully changes the taxpayer's economic position apart from federal income tax effects; **AND**
- **(B) Subjective prong:** The taxpayer has a substantial non-tax purpose for the transaction.

Both prongs must be satisfied. In *Patel v. Commissioner* (T.C. 2025), the Tax Court held unanimously that Section 7701(o) requires a threshold relevancy determination before applying the two-prong test.

**Penalty exposure:** Section 6662(b)(6) imposes a strict-liability **20% penalty** on underpayments from lack of economic substance. Section 6662(i) increases this to **40%** for nondisclosed transactions. The reasonable cause exception under Section 6664(c) is **not available**. No substantial authority defense applies. *See* IRS Notice 2014-58.

**Sources:** *Gregory v. Helvering*, 293 U.S. 465 (1935); *Knetsch v. United States*, 364 U.S. 361 (1960); *Patel v. Commissioner* (T.C. 2025); IRS Notices 2010-62, 2014-58.

### 2. Substance Over Form

The IRS may recharacterize a transaction based on objective economic realities rather than the legal form employed. Courts look to "the objective economic realities of a transaction rather than to the particular form the parties employed." *Frank Lyon Co. v. United States*, 435 U.S. 561, 573 (1978). The "similar rule of law" penalty under Section 6662(b)(6) was **not** intended to extend to this doctrine per legislative history.

**Sources:** *Frank Lyon Co. v. United States*, 435 U.S. 561 (1978); *Gregory v. Helvering*, 293 U.S. 465 (1935); *Commissioner v. Court Holding Co.*, 324 U.S. 331 (1945).

### 3. Step Transaction Doctrine

Collapses a series of formally separate transactions into a single transaction. Three tests:

- **Binding commitment test:** A binding obligation to complete all steps existed at the outset. Most restrictive; seldom applied. *King Enterprises, Inc. v. United States*, 418 F.2d 511 (Ct. Cl. 1969).
- **End result test:** Steps were prearranged to achieve a particular end result. Most frequently applied. *King Enterprises*, 418 F.2d at 516.
- **Interdependence test:** Steps are so interdependent that "the legal relations created by one transaction would have been fruitless without a completion of the series." *Redding v. Commissioner*, 630 F.2d 1169, 1177 (7th Cir. 1980).

Independent business purposes for individual steps do not necessarily prevent application.

### 4. Business Purpose Doctrine

Transactions without a genuine non-tax business purpose may be disregarded. Originates from *Gregory v. Helvering*, 293 U.S. 465 (1935), where the Court denied tax benefits for a corporate reorganization that was "a mere device which put on the form of a corporate reorganization as a disguise." Never codified as a standalone provision, but embedded in the subjective prong of Section 7701(o)(1)(B). IRS regulations require reorganizations under Section 368 be undertaken for a valid business purpose. *See* Treas. Reg. Section 1.368-1(b).

### 5. Sham Transaction Doctrine

A transaction lacking both economic substance and non-tax purpose may be disregarded entirely. Two forms:

- **Sham in fact:** Transactions never actually occurred.
- **Sham in substance:** Transactions occurred but lack economic substance. *Lerman v. Commissioner*, 939 F.2d 44 (3d Cir. 1991).

In *Knetsch v. United States*, 364 U.S. 361 (1960), the Court denied interest deductions on a circular borrowing arrangement, holding there was "nothing of substance to be realized by [the taxpayer] from the transaction beyond a tax deduction." Post-codification, these transactions are subject to strict-liability penalties under Sections 6662(b)(6) and 6662(i).

### 6. Assignment of Income

Income is taxed to the person who earns it. A taxpayer cannot shift tax by assigning income through anticipatory arrangements. Justice Holmes: "no distinction can be taken according to the motives leading to the arrangement by which the fruits are attributed to a different tree from that on which they grew." *Lucas v. Earl*, 281 U.S. 111, 115 (1930).

The **fruit-and-tree analogy:** the income-producing source is the "tree" and income is the "fruit." Only a genuine transfer of the tree shifts the tax obligation. Expanded in *Helvering v. Horst*, 311 U.S. 112 (1940) to cover income from property.

### 7. Constructive Receipt (Treas. Reg. Section 1.451-2)

Income is constructively received when credited to the taxpayer's account, set apart, or otherwise made available so the taxpayer may draw upon it at any time. Treas. Reg. Section 1.451-2(a).

**Key limitation:** Income is **not** constructively received if control of its receipt is subject to **substantial limitations or restrictions**. Bonus stock credited but not yet available does not constitute receipt. Early-withdrawal penalties on bank deposits are NOT substantial limitations -- interest is constructively received when credited. *Corliss v. Bowers*, 281 U.S. 376 (1930).

### 8. Tax Benefit Rule (Section 111)

A recovery of a previously deducted amount is includable in income **only to the extent the prior deduction provided a tax benefit** -- i.e., actually reduced the taxpayer's tax liability. The **recovery exclusion** equals the portion of the prior deduction that did not reduce tax. Applies to bad debts, prior taxes, delinquency amounts, and all other prior deductions **except** depreciation, depletion, and amortization. *Hillsboro National Bank v. Commissioner*, 460 U.S. 370 (1983).

### 9. Claim of Right Doctrine (Section 1341)

Income received under an apparent unrestricted right must be included in gross income in the year of receipt, even if the right is later reversed. Section 1341 provides relief when the taxpayer repays. **All three requirements must be met:**

1. Item was included in prior-year gross income under an apparent unrestricted right;
2. A deduction is allowable because the taxpayer's right was later established to be restricted; and
3. The deduction exceeds **$3,000**.

**Two-method calculation** (use whichever produces lower tax):
- **Method 1 (Deduction):** Deduct repayment in current year on the applicable schedule.
- **Method 2 (Credit):** Compute credit equal to the tax difference (prior year tax minus recomputed tax without the repaid income). Claim on Schedule 3, Line 13b.

If repayment is $3,000 or less, Section 1341 does not apply; deduct on the same form where originally reported. *United States v. Lewis*, 340 U.S. 590 (1951); IRM 21.6.6.2.10.2.

### 10. Related Party Rules (Sections 267 and 318)

Section 267 disallows loss deductions on sales between related parties, even if bona fide and at fair market value. Also requires deduction/income matching (accrual-basis payor cannot deduct until cash-basis related payee includes in income).

**Section 267(b) -- 13 categories of related parties:**

1. Family members (siblings by whole or half blood, spouse, ancestors, lineal descendants)
2. Individual and corporation (individual owns >50% directly/indirectly)
3. Two corporations in the same controlled group (Section 267(f))
4. Grantor and fiduciary of any trust
5. Fiduciary and beneficiary of the same trust
6. Fiduciaries of two trusts with the same grantor
7. Trust fiduciary and beneficiary of another trust with the same grantor
8. Trust fiduciary and corporation (>50% owned by trust or grantor)
9. Person and tax-exempt organization controlled by that person/family
10. Corporation and partnership (same persons own >50% of each)
11. Two S corporations (same persons own >50% of each)
12. S corporation and C corporation (same persons own >50% of each)
13. Executor and beneficiary of same estate (except pecuniary bequest satisfaction)

**Note:** In-laws and step-relationships are **not** related parties. *Rev. Rul. 71-50*. Family attribution applies even with hostility. *Miller v. Commissioner*, 75 T.C. 182 (1980).

**Section 267(c) constructive ownership:** Entity-to-owner proportionate attribution; family attribution (spouse, siblings, ancestors, lineal descendants); anti-double-attribution rule (stock attributed through a family member cannot be reattributed to another family member).

**Section 318 attribution:** Applies to corporate redemptions, CFC status, and PFIC determinations. Uses 5% threshold for partnership/trust/estate attribution.

**Disallowed loss recovery:** Previously disallowed loss may offset gain when the related-party-acquired property is later sold to an unrelated party. Cannot create or increase a loss.

## OBBBA Changes (if applicable)

No OBBBA provisions modified the judicial doctrines in this document. The economic substance penalty structure (Sections 6662(b)(6), 6662(i)) and the codified two-prong test (Section 7701(o)) remain as enacted in 2010. The Section 1341 threshold of $3,000 is not indexed for inflation.

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE:** Before allowing any loss deduction on a sale, check whether the transaction involves related parties under Section 267(b). Apply constructive ownership rules under Sections 267(c) and 318. If ownership exceeds 50% (directly or constructively), disallow the loss.

> **AGENT RULE:** For repayment of previously reported income exceeding $3,000, compute tax under both Method 1 (deduction) and Method 2 (credit) per Section 1341. Apply whichever produces lower current-year tax. For repayments of $3,000 or less, deduct on the same form where originally reported.

> **AGENT RULE:** When reviewing transactions for economic substance risk, apply the conjunctive test: BOTH objective prong (meaningful change in economic position apart from tax effects) AND subjective prong (substantial non-tax purpose) must be satisfied. Failure of either prong triggers strict-liability penalties with no reasonable cause defense.

> **AGENT RULE:** For state tax refund recoveries, apply the tax benefit rule (Section 111) before including in income. Include only to the extent the prior deduction actually reduced federal tax. If the taxpayer used the standard deduction in the prior year, the refund is fully excludable.

## Common Traps

> **TRAP:** The economic substance penalty is strict liability. No reasonable cause defense, no substantial authority defense, no reasonable basis exception. The only mitigation is adequate disclosure (reducing penalty from 40% to 20%).

> **TRAP:** Step-relationships and in-laws are NOT related parties under Section 267. A spouse IS a related party. Family attribution applies even when there is hostility between family members.

> **TRAP:** Section 267(c) anti-double-attribution rule: stock attributed through a family member cannot be reattributed to yet another family member. Misapplying this produces incorrect related-party determinations.

> **TRAP:** The Section 1341 threshold is the deduction amount, not the repayment amount. Section 1341 only applies if the repayment qualifies as a deductible expense or loss; if not otherwise deductible, Section 1341 relief is unavailable.

> **TRAP:** Constructive receipt does not apply when the taxpayer's control is subject to substantial limitations. However, early-withdrawal penalties on bank deposits are NOT substantial limitations -- interest is constructively received when credited.

> **TRAP:** The tax benefit rule (Section 111) does not apply to depreciation, depletion, or amortization recoveries. Those are governed by recapture rules under Sections 1245 and 1250.
