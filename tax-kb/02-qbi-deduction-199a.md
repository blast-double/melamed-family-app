# Qualified Business Income (QBI) Deduction
**IRC Section(s):** Section 199A; Reg. Sections 1.199A-1 through 1.199A-6
**Relevant Forms:** Form 8995 (simplified), Form 8995-A (detailed)
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

IRC Section 199A provides noncorporate taxpayers -- including sole proprietors, partners, S corporation shareholders, and certain trusts and estates -- a deduction of up to 20% of qualified business income (QBI) from domestic pass-through trades or businesses, plus 20% of qualified REIT dividends and qualified publicly traded partnership (PTP) income. Originally enacted as a temporary provision by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) of 2017, the QBI deduction was made permanent by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed into law in July 2025. The OBBBA also widened the phase-in ranges for income-based limitations, introduced a $400 minimum deduction for active business owners, and ensured the deduction's long-term availability for entity-structure planning.

## Rules

### QBI Definition (Section 199A(c); Reg. Section 1.199A-3)

1. **Qualified business income** is the net amount of qualified items of income, gain, deduction, and loss with respect to a qualified trade or business carried on within the United States.
2. Qualified items must be effectively connected with a U.S. trade or business and included or allowed in determining taxable income for the year.
3. The following items are **excluded** from QBI:
   - Capital gains and losses (short-term and long-term)
   - Dividends and interest income not allocable to a trade or business
   - Commodity transaction gains/losses, foreign currency gains/losses
   - Annuity income not received in connection with a trade or business
   - Reasonable compensation paid to the taxpayer by an S corporation (W-2 wages received as officer/employee)
   - Guaranteed payments to a partner for services rendered under Section 707(c)
   - Payments under Section 707(a) to a partner acting in a capacity other than as a partner
4. QBI is determined separately for each qualified trade or business. Losses from one business reduce QBI from other businesses before computing the deduction.
5. QBI is reduced by the deductible portion of self-employment tax, self-employed health insurance premiums, and deductible contributions to qualified retirement plans (SEP, SIMPLE) attributable to income from the qualified trade or business.

### Basic Deduction Calculation (Section 199A(a))

1. The QBI deduction equals the lesser of:
   - **(A)** The combined QBI amount (20% of QBI from each qualified trade or business, plus 20% of qualified REIT dividends and PTP income), or
   - **(B)** 20% of taxable income minus net capital gain (net long-term capital gain over net short-term capital loss, plus qualified dividends).
2. The 20%-of-taxable-income cap (item B) is the **overall limitation**. It prevents the QBI deduction from exceeding 20% of taxable income computed without regard to the QBI deduction itself and without regard to net capital gain.
3. If taxable income (before QBI deduction) is at or below the applicable threshold amount, the deduction for each business is simply 20% of QBI with no further limitation.

### W-2 Wage and UBIA Limitation (Section 199A(b)(2); Reg. Section 1.199A-2)

1. When taxable income exceeds the applicable threshold amount plus the phase-in range, the QBI deduction for each qualified trade or business cannot exceed the **greater of**:
   - **(a)** 50% of the W-2 wages paid by that qualified trade or business, or
   - **(b)** 25% of the W-2 wages paid by that qualified trade or business **plus** 2.5% of the unadjusted basis immediately after acquisition (UBIA) of all qualified property held by the business.
2. **W-2 wages** for this purpose include total wages subject to income tax withholding as reported on Forms W-2, elective deferrals to retirement plans (401(k), SIMPLE, etc.), designated Roth contributions, and deferred compensation amounts. Wages must be properly allocable to QBI of the trade or business.
3. **UBIA of qualified property** means the unadjusted cost basis of tangible, depreciable property held by the business as of the close of the taxable year and used in the production of QBI. The property must be within its depreciable period (the later of 10 years from the date placed in service or the last day of the last full year of the regular depreciation period). Land is not included because it is not depreciable.
4. When taxable income is within the phase-in range (above the threshold but below the upper bound), the W-2/UBIA limitation is phased in proportionally. The excess of the tentative QBI deduction (20% of QBI) over the W-2/UBIA cap is reduced by the "reduction amount," which equals that excess multiplied by the ratio of (taxable income minus threshold) to the phase-in range width.

### Specified Service Trade or Business (SSTB) Rules (Section 199A(d); Reg. Section 1.199A-5)

1. A **specified service trade or business (SSTB)** is a trade or business involving the performance of services in any of the following fields:
   - Health care
   - Law
   - Accounting
   - Actuarial science
   - Performing arts
   - Consulting
   - Athletics
   - Financial services
   - Brokerage services
   - Any trade or business where the principal asset is the reputation or skill of one or more employees or owners (narrowly interpreted under the regulations to mean receiving income for endorsements, licensing of name/likeness, or appearance fees)
2. **Excluded from SSTB classification**: Engineering and architecture are specifically excluded and are not treated as SSTBs (Section 199A(d)(2)(B)).
3. **Below the threshold**: SSTB status is irrelevant. All qualifying taxpayers receive the full 20% deduction regardless of business type.
4. **Within the phase-in range**: Only the "applicable percentage" of QBI, W-2 wages, and UBIA from the SSTB is taken into account. The applicable percentage equals 100% minus the percentage calculated as (taxable income minus threshold amount) / phase-in range width.
5. **Above the phase-in range**: Taxpayers receive **zero** QBI deduction from an SSTB. Income from SSTBs is completely excluded from the deduction once taxable income exceeds the upper bound.
6. **De minimis rule**: A trade or business with gross receipts of $25 million or less is not treated as an SSTB if less than 10% of gross receipts are attributable to SSTB activities. For businesses with gross receipts above $25 million, the threshold is 5%.

### Aggregation Election (Reg. Section 1.199A-4)

1. Taxpayers may elect to aggregate two or more qualified trades or businesses for purposes of applying the W-2 wage and UBIA limitations, provided all of the following requirements are met:
   - The same person or group of persons directly or indirectly owns 50% or more of each trade or business to be aggregated for a majority of the taxable year.
   - At least two of the following three factors are shared:
     - (i) The businesses provide goods or services that are the same or are customarily offered together.
     - (ii) The businesses share significant centralized business elements (e.g., centralized purchasing, accounting, legal, human resources, IT, or administrative services).
     - (iii) The businesses are operated in coordination with or reliance upon one or more of the other businesses in the aggregated group.
2. When businesses are aggregated, their QBI, W-2 wages, and UBIA of qualified property are **combined** for purposes of applying the W-2/UBIA limitation. This can be beneficial when one entity has W-2 wages but little QBI and another has significant QBI but low W-2 wages.
3. An SSTB may **not** be aggregated with a non-SSTB.
4. The aggregation election must be disclosed **annually** on the return (unlike the Section 469 grouping election which is made once). A statement identifying each aggregated trade or business must be attached to the return.
5. Once made, the aggregation election is generally consistent from year to year; new businesses may be added, but businesses may not be subtracted unless there is a change in facts.

### Real Estate Safe Harbor (Rev. Proc. 2019-38)

1. A rental real estate enterprise is treated as a qualified trade or business for Section 199A purposes if it satisfies the requirements of Rev. Proc. 2019-38. This safe harbor applies solely for purposes of Section 199A.
2. Requirements:
   - Separate books and records are maintained for each rental real estate enterprise.
   - For enterprises in existence less than 4 years: at least 250 hours of rental services are performed per year.
   - For enterprises in existence 4 or more years: at least 250 hours of rental services are performed in at least 3 of the prior 5 consecutive taxable years.
   - The taxpayer maintains contemporaneous records (time logs) documenting hours, tasks, dates, and who performed the services.
3. **Qualifying rental services** include: advertising, negotiating and executing leases, tenant screening, rent collection, maintenance and repairs, and supervision of employees/contractors.
4. **Non-qualifying activities**: Financial or investment management activities, arranging financing, procuring property, reviewing financial statements, capital improvements under Reg. Section 1.263(a)-3(d), and travel to/from the real estate do not count toward the 250-hour requirement.
5. **Excluded property types**:
   - Real estate used by the taxpayer as a residence under Section 280A(d).
   - Real estate rented under a **triple-net lease** (the tenant pays taxes, insurance, and maintenance in addition to rent).
6. If the safe harbor is not met, the rental activity may still qualify as a trade or business under the general Section 162 standard or the self-rental rule.

### Negative QBI and Loss Carryforward (Section 199A(c)(2); Reg. Section 1.199A-1(d)(2)(iii))

1. If a taxpayer's net QBI across all qualified trades or businesses is negative for the year, the QBI deduction for the year is **zero**.
2. The net negative QBI amount carries forward to the succeeding taxable year and is treated as arising from a separate qualified trade or business.
3. In the subsequent year, the carryforward loss reduces the QBI from positive businesses **before** the 20% deduction is calculated. The positive QBI is reduced (but not below zero) by 20% of the carried-over qualified business loss.
4. QBI losses carry forward **indefinitely** until fully offset by positive QBI.
5. W-2 wages and UBIA of qualified property amounts attributable to a year with net negative QBI are **disregarded** and do **not** carry forward.
6. Losses suspended under other provisions (Section 465 at-risk rules, Section 469 passive activity rules, Section 461(l) excess business loss rules) are not included in QBI until the year they are allowed in computing taxable income. When allowed, they retain their QBI character proportionally.

### Overall Limitation (Section 199A(a)(2))

The QBI deduction may not exceed 20% of the taxpayer's taxable income for the year (computed without regard to the QBI deduction and without regard to net capital gain, which includes net long-term capital gain plus qualified dividends). This is the final cap applied after all business-level calculations.

| Item | Amount | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|

### SSTB Phase-Out Effect

| Taxable Income Position | SSTB Treatment |
|------------------------|----------------|
| Below threshold | Full 20% deduction; SSTB status irrelevant |
| Within phase-in range | Applicable percentage of QBI, W-2 wages, and UBIA taken into account; deduction reduced proportionally |
| Above phase-in range | Zero deduction from SSTB income |

| Form | When to Use |
|------|------------|

## OBBBA Changes

**OLD RULE (pre-2026, TCJA):** The Section 199A deduction was set to expire after December 31, 2025. Phase-in ranges were $50,000 for non-joint filers and $100,000 for married filing jointly. No minimum deduction existed.

**NEW RULE (OBBBA, effective for tax years beginning after December 31, 2025):**
1. **Permanence**: The 20% QBI deduction is made permanent. The sunset provision is removed.
2. **Expanded phase-in ranges**: Increased to $75,000 for non-joint filers and $150,000 for married filing jointly. Indexed for inflation after 2026. This widens the income band over which the W-2/UBIA limitation and the SSTB exclusion are phased in, allowing more taxpayers to claim partial deductions at higher income levels.
3. **$400 minimum deduction**: If a taxpayer's aggregate QBI from all qualified trades or businesses in which the taxpayer materially participates is at least $1,000, the taxpayer receives a minimum QBI deduction of $400 (or the regular calculated deduction, if greater). Both the $400 and $1,000 amounts are indexed for inflation after 2026.
4. **Pease limitation exclusion**: The OBBBA expressly states that taxable income for purposes of Section 199A is determined without regard to the new itemized deduction phaseout for high-income taxpayers.
5. **Deduction rate unchanged**: The 20% rate remains. Earlier House versions proposed increasing the rate to 23% and replacing the sliding-scale limitation with a fixed-rate reduction, but these changes were not enacted in the final version.

*Sources: Barnes Dennig (2025); RSM, "Permanent QBI deduction provides some tax planning certainty" (July 2025); TXCPA, "Key Updates to Section 199A" (July 2025); Foster LLP, "OBBBA Part 4" (2025); IRS Rev. Proc. 2025-32.*

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE -- S-Corp / Partnership QBI Calculation:** For S corporation and partnership owners claiming the Section 199A deduction, apply the following steps in order:
>
> 1. **Confirm not SSTB above threshold.** Determine whether each trade or business is a specified service trade or business. If yes and the taxpayer's taxable income exceeds the phase-in range upper bound, the deduction for that business is zero. If taxable income is within the phase-in range, apply the applicable percentage to QBI, W-2 wages, and UBIA.
> 2. **Calculate QBI net of all adjustments.** Start with net income/gain/deduction/loss from the qualified trade or business. Subtract reasonable compensation (S-corps) or guaranteed payments (partnerships). Subtract the deductible portion of SE tax, SE health insurance, and retirement plan contributions. Exclude investment income, capital gains/losses, and other non-qualifying items. Net any negative QBI carryforwards from prior years.
> 3. **Apply W-2/UBIA limitation if above phase-out.** If taxable income exceeds the applicable threshold, calculate the greater of (a) 50% of W-2 wages or (b) 25% of W-2 wages plus 2.5% of UBIA of qualified property. Phase in the limitation proportionally if within the phase-in range; apply fully if above.
> 4. **Apply overall 20% of taxable income cap.** The final deduction cannot exceed 20% of taxable income minus net capital gain (LTCG + qualified dividends). Compare the combined QBI amount to this cap and take the lesser.
> 5. **Check minimum deduction (2026+).** If aggregate QBI from materially participated businesses is at least $1,000 and the calculated deduction is less than $400, apply the $400 minimum.

## Common Traps

> **TRAP -- Reasonable Compensation Reduces QBI:** S corporation shareholders must pay themselves reasonable compensation. Those W-2 wages reduce the owner's QBI (since compensation is excluded from QBI), creating a direct tension between maximizing QBI and meeting the reasonable compensation requirement. Setting compensation too low invites IRS reclassification; setting it too high unnecessarily reduces the QBI deduction.

> **TRAP -- Guaranteed Payments Excluded from QBI:** Partnership guaranteed payments under Section 707(c) are excluded from QBI of the recipient partner. Partners who receive guaranteed payments for services should model the effect on their QBI deduction. Converting guaranteed payments to preferential profit allocations may increase QBI, but must have economic substance.

> **TRAP -- SSTB "Cracking" Prohibited:** Taxpayers cannot split an SSTB into separate entities (one providing services, one holding assets) to avoid the SSTB limitation. Reg. Section 1.199A-5(c)(2) treats a trade or business that provides property or services to a commonly controlled SSTB as itself an SSTB to the extent of common ownership. Separating a law firm's billing entity from its real estate holding entity will not avoid SSTB classification.

> **TRAP -- Triple-Net Leases Excluded from Safe Harbor:** Rental income from triple-net leases does not qualify under the Rev. Proc. 2019-38 safe harbor. These activities may still qualify under the general Section 162 trade-or-business standard, but the 250-hour safe harbor shortcut is unavailable.

> **TRAP -- Self-Rental Rule Cuts Both Ways:** When an owner rents property to their own S corporation or partnership, the rental income is recharacterized from passive to nonpassive under the self-rental rule (Reg. Section 1.469-2(f)(6)). This can affect QBI calculations. While the rental may qualify as a trade or business (particularly as a self-rental), the interplay between Section 469 recharacterization and Section 199A must be analyzed carefully.

> **TRAP -- Negative QBI Consumes Future Deductions:** A net negative QBI in the current year produces no deduction but generates a carryforward that reduces future QBI at a 20% rate. Taxpayers with businesses producing both gains and losses should consider timing strategies and whether aggregation could produce a net positive QBI for the current year.

> **TRAP -- W-2 Wages and UBIA Do Not Carry Forward:** When net QBI is negative and no deduction is allowed, the associated W-2 wages and UBIA amounts are lost. They do not carry forward to future years. This means the limitation components that would have supported a deduction in the loss year are permanently foregone.
