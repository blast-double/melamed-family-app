# Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) and Additional Medicare Tax
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 1411, 3101(b)(2), 1401(b)(2)
**Relevant Forms:** Form 8960, Form 8959
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

Two parallel surtaxes were enacted under the Affordable Care Act (Health Care and Education Reconciliation Act of 2010, P.L. 111-152) effective January 1, 2013, to fund Medicare expansion. They target different income types at the same MAGI thresholds but operate independently:

1. **Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT)** -- IRC Section 1411: a 3.8% surtax on the lesser of (a) net investment income or (b) the amount by which MAGI exceeds the filing-status threshold. Reported on Form 8960.

2. **Additional Medicare Tax** -- IRC Sections 3101(b)(2) (wages) and 1401(b)(2) (self-employment income): a 0.9% surtax on wages, compensation, and self-employment income exceeding the filing-status threshold. Reported on Form 8959.

These taxes are additive and non-overlapping: NIIT applies to investment income, while the Additional Medicare Tax applies to earned income. A taxpayer with both high investment income and high earned income can owe both taxes simultaneously.

Sources: IRS Instructions for Form 8960 (2025), IRS Instructions for Form 8959 (2025), 26 U.S.C. Section 1411, IRS Questions and Answers on the Net Investment Income Tax (irs.gov/newsroom), Tax Foundation OBBBA analysis (taxfoundation.org/research/all/federal/one-big-beautiful-bill-act-tax-changes).

## Rules

### NIIT (Section 1411)

**Who owes it.** U.S. citizens and residents with (a) MAGI exceeding the applicable threshold AND (b) net investment income greater than zero. Estates and trusts also owe NIIT on undistributed net investment income above the highest trust bracket threshold ($16,000 AGI for 2026). Nonresident aliens are generally exempt.

**Calculation.** The tax is 3.8% of the lesser of:
- Net investment income (NII), or
- The excess of MAGI over the applicable threshold.

Example: Single filer, $270,000 MAGI, $90,000 NII. MAGI exceeds threshold by $70,000 ($270,000 - $200,000). NIIT = 3.8% x $70,000 (the lesser amount) = $2,660.

**Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI).** For NIIT purposes, MAGI equals AGI increased by the amount excluded under Section 911(a)(1) (foreign earned income exclusion). For most domestic taxpayers, MAGI equals AGI.

**What counts as Net Investment Income (NII).** Under Section 1411(c), NII includes:
- **Interest** -- savings accounts, CDs, bonds, margin interest received
- **Dividends** -- ordinary and qualified dividends from stocks, mutual funds, ETFs
- **Capital gains** -- net gains from dispositions of property (stocks, bonds, real estate other than excluded primary residence gain), including short-term and long-term
- **Annuities** -- nonqualified annuity income (taxable portion)
- **Rents and royalties** -- gross rental income and royalty income, reduced by allocable deductions (depreciation, repairs, etc.)
- **Passive activity income** -- income from businesses in which the taxpayer does not materially participate (per Section 469)
- **Income from trading businesses** -- businesses involved in trading financial instruments or commodities

**What is excluded from NII:**
- Wages, salaries, and other compensation
- Self-employment income subject to SE tax under Section 1401(b)
- Distributions from qualified retirement plans: 401(a), 403(a), 403(b), 408 (traditional IRA), 408A (Roth IRA), 457(b)
- Social Security benefits
- Tax-exempt interest (e.g., municipal bond interest)
- Gain excluded under Section 121 (up to $250,000/$500,000 on primary residence sale)
- Income from an active trade or business in which the taxpayer materially participates (unless trading in financial instruments/commodities)
- Alaska Permanent Fund dividends
- Veterans' benefits

**Deductions against NII.** NII is reduced by properly allocable deductions including: investment interest expense (Section 163(d)), investment advisory and brokerage fees (suspended for 2018-2025 under TCJA for individuals as miscellaneous itemized deductions, but note the OBBBA makes this suspension permanent), state and local income taxes allocable to NII, rental property expenses, and depreciation.

### Additional Medicare Tax (Sections 3101(b)(2) and 1401(b)(2))

**Who owes it.** Individuals with Medicare wages, Railroad Retirement Tax Act (RRTA) compensation, or self-employment income exceeding the filing-status threshold.

**Calculation.** The tax is 0.9% on earned income (wages + SE income) exceeding the threshold. There is no employer match -- the employee/self-employed individual bears the full 0.9%.

**Income subject to the tax:**
- Medicare wages (W-2, Box 5)
- Self-employment income (Schedule SE)
- RRTA compensation (compared separately to the threshold)

**Stacking rule for wages + SE income.** Medicare wages and self-employment income are combined to determine if the threshold is exceeded. The threshold is applied first to wages, then the remainder is applied against SE income. A self-employment loss is not netted against wages for this purpose.

Example: MFJ couple. Spouse A has $130,000 W-2 wages. Spouse B has $140,000 SE income. Threshold: $250,000. Remaining threshold after wages: $250,000 - $130,000 = $120,000. Taxable SE income: $140,000 - $120,000 = $20,000. Additional Medicare Tax = $20,000 x 0.9% = $180.

**Employer withholding.** Employers must begin withholding the 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax in the pay period when an employee's wages exceed $200,000 for the calendar year, regardless of filing status or wages from other employers. The employee cannot request the employer to stop withholding once triggered. If withholding exceeds the actual liability (e.g., MFJ filer whose combined income is under $250,000), the excess is credited against total tax on the return.

**Reconciliation.** Form 8959 reconciles the Additional Medicare Tax actually owed against amounts withheld by employers:
- Part I: Calculates tax on Medicare wages
- Part II: Calculates tax on self-employment income (applying the reduced threshold after wages)
- Part III: Calculates tax on RRTA compensation
- Part IV: Totals the Additional Medicare Tax
- Part V: Compares total tax to employer withholding; the difference flows to Schedule 2 (additional tax owed) or Form 1040 line 25d (excess withholding credit)

## Key Thresholds and Figures

### MAGI Thresholds (Same for Both NIIT and Additional Medicare Tax)

| Filing Status | Threshold |
|---------------|-----------|
| Married Filing Jointly (MFJ) | $250,000 |
| Qualifying Surviving Spouse | $250,000 |
| Single | $200,000 |
| Head of Household | $200,000 |
| Married Filing Separately (MFS) | $125,000 |

**These thresholds are NOT indexed for inflation.** They have remained unchanged since the taxes took effect in 2013. As nominal incomes rise, more taxpayers are captured each year. Between 2013 and 2021, the number of NIIT-liable taxpayers grew from 3.1 million to 7.3 million, and total NIIT collections increased from $16.5 billion to $59.8 billion.

### Employer Withholding Threshold (Additional Medicare Tax Only)

Employers withhold the 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax on wages exceeding **$200,000** per calendar year per employer, regardless of the employee's filing status. This is a flat withholding trigger, not tied to filing status.

### Tax Rates

| Tax | Rate | Applied To |
|-----|------|------------|
| NIIT | 3.8% | Lesser of NII or MAGI excess over threshold |
| Additional Medicare Tax | 0.9% | Earned income (wages + SE income) exceeding threshold |

### Estates and Trusts (NIIT Only)

For 2026, estates and trusts owe the 3.8% NIIT on undistributed net investment income when AGI exceeds **$16,000** (this threshold IS inflation-adjusted annually, unlike the individual thresholds).

## OBBBA Changes (if applicable)

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (P.L. 119-21, signed July 4, 2025) **did not modify the NIIT or Additional Medicare Tax**. Specifically:

- The 3.8% NIIT rate and the $200,000/$250,000/$125,000 thresholds remain unchanged and continue to not be indexed for inflation.
- The 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax rate and thresholds remain unchanged.
- Capital gains tax rates (0%/15%/20%) remain unchanged; no new surcharges were added.
- The OBBBA **permanently suspends miscellaneous itemized deductions** (Section 67(g)), which means investment management fees and tax preparation fees remain non-deductible against NII for individuals. This was a TCJA provision scheduled to sunset after 2025; it is now permanent.

Sources: TLD Law OBBBA analysis (tldlaw.com), Tax Foundation FAQ (taxfoundation.org), Katten OBBBA analysis (katten.com).

## Agent Rules

1. **Both taxes require separate analysis.** When reviewing a taxpayer's liability, check NIIT (Form 8960) and Additional Medicare Tax (Form 8959) independently. They apply to different income types.

2. **NIIT applies only to investment income.** Never include wages, salaries, SE income, or retirement distributions in the NII calculation. Conversely, never include passive rental income or capital gains in the Additional Medicare Tax calculation.

3. **Use the correct threshold for filing status.** The $200,000 employer withholding trigger is NOT the threshold for MFJ filers ($250,000) or MFS filers ($125,000). Always reconcile using the actual filing-status threshold.

4. **Rental income from Aaron's property (1771 11th Ave SF) is included in NII.** Net rental income (gross rents minus allocable expenses like depreciation, repairs, insurance, property management) is net investment income subject to NIIT, assuming the rental activity is passive. See 03-passive-activity-rules-469.md for material participation rules that could change the classification.

5. **Business distributions flow differently.** K-1 income from Itero, Keynote, and Palisades Labs: if Aaron materially participates in these businesses, the active trade/business income portion is excluded from NII. However, any passive income component, interest, dividends, or capital gains flowing through the K-1 may be NII. Analyze each K-1 line item separately.

6. **Section 121 exclusion reduces NII.** If gain on a primary residence sale qualifies for the Section 121 exclusion ($250K single / $500K MFJ), the excluded portion is not NII. Only gain exceeding the exclusion amount is included.

7. **Estimated payments must account for both taxes.** If withholding will be insufficient to cover NIIT and/or Additional Medicare Tax liability, estimated tax payments (Form 1040-ES) must be made. These estimated payments cannot be designated specifically for NIIT or Additional Medicare Tax -- they apply to total tax liability. Failure to make adequate estimated payments triggers Section 6654 underpayment penalties.

8. **Self-employment loss rule.** A self-employment loss does NOT reduce the Additional Medicare Tax threshold. Do not net SE losses against wages when computing the Additional Medicare Tax.

9. **QEF and PFIC elections.** If the taxpayer holds interests in qualified electing funds (QEFs) or passive foreign investment companies (PFICs), the Section 1.1411-10(g) election may be necessary to align NIIT treatment with regular income tax treatment. This election must be made in the year Section 1411 first applies to the taxpayer.

## Common Traps

### Trap 1: Assuming Thresholds Are Inflation-Adjusted
The NIIT and Additional Medicare Tax thresholds have been frozen at their 2013 levels. They are NOT indexed for inflation. Bracket creep means more taxpayers are captured each year. Do not look up "2026 NIIT thresholds" expecting updated numbers -- they remain $200,000 / $250,000 / $125,000.

### Trap 2: Confusing the Employer Withholding Trigger with the MFJ Threshold
Employers must withhold the 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax on wages exceeding $200,000 regardless of filing status. For MFJ filers, the actual threshold is $250,000. This mismatch creates two common problems:
- **Over-withholding**: An MFJ spouse earning $210,000 has 0.9% withheld on $10,000 of wages, but the couple may not owe Additional Medicare Tax if combined wages are under $250,000. The excess withholding is credited on Form 1040.
- **Under-withholding**: Two MFJ spouses each earning $180,000 (combined $360,000) will have zero Additional Medicare Tax withheld by their employers (neither exceeds $200,000 individually), but they owe 0.9% on $110,000 ($360,000 - $250,000 = $110,000). They must pay via estimated payments or face underpayment penalties.

### Trap 3: Forgetting NIIT on Passive K-1 Income
K-1 income from partnerships or S corporations where the taxpayer does not materially participate is passive income and thus NII. Taxpayers with multiple business interests may have both active (excluded from NII) and passive (included in NII) K-1 income streams.

### Trap 4: Double-Counting Income Across the Two Taxes
NIIT and Additional Medicare Tax never apply to the same dollar of income. Section 1411(c)(6) explicitly excludes from NII any income on which self-employment tax is imposed under Section 1401(b). Wages are excluded from NII by definition. The two taxes are parallel, not overlapping.

### Trap 5: Missing the MFS Penalty
Married Filing Separately triggers the lowest threshold ($125,000) for both taxes. This is exactly half the MFJ threshold, but combined with the loss of other MFJ benefits, MFS status can create significantly higher total surtax liability. Always model MFJ vs. MFS when both taxes are in play.

### Trap 6: Ignoring NIIT on Real Estate Sales
Gain from the sale of investment real estate (not a primary residence, or gain exceeding the Section 121 exclusion) is NII. This includes Section 1250 unrecaptured gain taxed at 25%. A large real estate transaction can spike NII and MAGI in a single year, triggering NIIT even for taxpayers who normally fall below the thresholds. Installment sales (Section 453) can spread the gain and potentially keep MAGI below threshold in each year. See 03-installment-sales-453.md.

### Trap 7: Estimated Payments Not Covering Surtaxes
Both taxes are subject to estimated tax provisions. If a taxpayer's W-2 withholding does not cover the combined regular tax + NIIT + Additional Medicare Tax, estimated payments must be made quarterly via Form 1040-ES. The safe harbor (110% of prior year tax for AGI > $150,000) includes these surtaxes. Estimated payments cannot be earmarked for a specific tax -- they apply to total liability.
