# Filing Status
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 1, 2, 7703
**Relevant Forms:** Form 1040, Schedule A
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

Filing status determines a taxpayer's standard deduction, tax bracket thresholds, eligibility for credits and deductions, and phase-out ranges for various tax benefits. There are five filing statuses: Single, Married Filing Jointly (MFJ), Married Filing Separately (MFS), Head of Household (HoH), and Qualifying Surviving Spouse (QSS). Filing status is determined based on the taxpayer's marital and family situation on the last day of the tax year (December 31 for calendar-year filers). The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed into law July 4, 2025, made the TCJA's expanded standard deduction permanent and further increased the amounts beginning in tax year 2025.

## Rules

### 1. Single (IRC Section 1(c))

A taxpayer files as Single if, on December 31 of the tax year, they are: (a) unmarried, (b) legally separated under a decree of divorce or separate maintenance, or (c) widowed and do not qualify as a Qualifying Surviving Spouse. A taxpayer who does not qualify for Head of Household or any other status defaults to Single.

### 2. Married Filing Jointly (MFJ) (IRC Section 1(a))

A married couple may elect to file a joint return. "Married" is determined under state law as of December 31. Following the Supreme Court's decision in *Obergefell v. Hodges* (2015), all legally married same-sex couples are recognized as married for federal tax purposes. Both spouses report all income and deductions on a single return and are jointly and severally liable for the tax. If one spouse dies during the tax year, the surviving spouse may file a joint return for that year.

### 3. Married Filing Separately (MFS) (IRC Section 1(d))

A married taxpayer may elect to file a separate return. The MFS standard deduction is exactly half the MFJ standard deduction. MFS carries significant consequences:

- **Credits lost entirely:** Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC), Premium Tax Credit (PTC) (exception: victims of domestic abuse or spousal abandonment), education credits (American Opportunity, Lifetime Learning), adoption credit.
- **Deductions lost:** Student loan interest deduction (IRC Section 221).
- **Reduced limits:** Dependent care exclusion capped at $2,500 (50% of the normal $5,000). Child and Dependent Care Tax Credit is generally unavailable.
- **Itemization lockstep:** If one spouse itemizes deductions, the other must also itemize; neither can take the standard deduction (IRC Section 63(c)(6)(A)).
- **Compressed brackets:** The 35% bracket for MFS begins at $250,526 (2025) / $256,226 (2026) and the 37% bracket at $375,800 (2025) / $384,350 (2026), compared to $501,051/$751,600 (2025 MFJ) and $512,451/$768,700 (2026 MFJ).
- **Roth IRA:** Direct Roth IRA contributions are phased out at $0-$10,000 MAGI if the taxpayer lived with their spouse at any time during the year.

### 4. Head of Household (HoH) (IRC Section 2(b))

To file as Head of Household, a taxpayer must meet all three requirements:

1. **Unmarried or "considered unmarried"** on December 31 of the tax year.
2. **Paid more than half the cost** of maintaining the home for the tax year.
3. **A qualifying person** lived with the taxpayer in the home for more than half the year (exception: a dependent parent need not live with the taxpayer; the taxpayer must pay more than half the cost of the parent's home).

**"Considered unmarried" rules (IRC Section 7703(b)):** A married taxpayer is treated as unmarried if all of the following are met: (a) they file a separate return; (b) they paid more than half the cost of maintaining their home for the tax year; (c) their spouse did not live in the home during the last six months of the tax year; and (d) the home was the principal place of abode for a qualifying child or dependent for more than half the year.

### 5. Qualifying Surviving Spouse (QSS) (IRC Section 2(a))

A taxpayer may file as Qualifying Surviving Spouse for the two tax years following the year of the spouse's death (not the year of death itself, in which a joint return may be filed). Requirements: (a) the taxpayer was entitled to file a joint return in the year the spouse died; (b) the taxpayer has not remarried; (c) the taxpayer maintains a home that is the principal place of abode for a dependent child (son, daughter, or stepchild) for the entire tax year. QSS uses the same standard deduction and tax brackets as MFJ.

### December 31 Determination Date

Marital status is determined on the last day of the tax year (IRC Section 7703(a)(1)). A taxpayer who marries on December 31 is considered married for the entire year. A taxpayer who divorces on December 31 is considered unmarried for the entire year.

### Community Property States and Section 66

In nine community property states (Arizona, California, Idaho, Louisiana, Nevada, New Mexico, Texas, Washington, Wisconsin), married couples who file separately must generally report income according to community property rules: each spouse reports 50% of combined community income. IRC Section 66 provides exceptions for spouses who live apart for the entire year and do not file jointly, allowing each spouse to report only their own earned income as their own (rather than splitting it) if: (a) the spouses live apart for the entire tax year, (b) they do not file a joint return, and (c) no community income is transferred between them beyond de minimis amounts. Section 66(c) provides additional relief where a spouse did not know of and had no reason to know of community income items.

## OBBBA Changes (if applicable)

**OLD RULE (pre-2025 / TCJA as enacted):** The TCJA (2017) temporarily increased the standard deduction through 2025 ($15,000 Single / $30,000 MFJ / $22,500 HoH for 2025). Without legislation, these amounts would have reverted to pre-TCJA levels (approximately $8,300 Single / $16,600 MFJ) in 2026, and personal exemptions ($4,150 per person) would have returned.

**NEW RULE (OBBBA, signed July 4, 2025):** The OBBBA made the TCJA's expanded standard deduction permanent and further increased the 2025 amounts by $750 for single filers and $1,500 for joint filers above prior-law projections (to $15,750 Single / $31,500 MFJ / $23,625 HoH). The seven-bracket rate structure (10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, 37%) is made permanent. Personal exemptions remain at $0 permanently. The OBBBA also provides a new temporary $6,000 above-the-line deduction for taxpayers age 65+ (2025-2028), phasing out at $75,000 (Single) / $150,000 (MFJ) MAGI. The 10% and 12% bracket thresholds received an initial inflation adjustment in 2026.

Sources: Tax Foundation; H&R Block; Kaufman Rossin; Thomson Reuters.

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE:** Filing status is determined on December 31. A taxpayer who divorces on December 31 is considered unmarried for the entire year. When determining a taxpayer's filing status, always check the December 31 date first. A taxpayer married on December 31 cannot file as Single regardless of how many months they were married during the year. For Qualifying Surviving Spouse, count forward two tax years from the year of death (not from December 31 of that year). Example: spouse dies in 2024; QSS is available for 2025 and 2026 only.

> **AGENT RULE:** When a taxpayer resides in a community property state (AZ, CA, ID, LA, NV, NM, TX, WA, WI) and files MFS, apply community property income-splitting rules per Publication 555 unless IRC Section 66 relief applies. Always ask whether the spouses lived apart for the entire tax year.

## Common Traps

> **TRAP:** MFS is almost always more expensive than MFJ. It eliminates access to EITC, PTC, education credits, student loan interest deduction, and adoption credit. It halves the dependent care exclusion ($2,500 vs. $5,000). It compresses the 35% and 37% brackets. Run an MFJ vs. MFS comparison before recommending MFS. The primary exception where MFS may be advantageous: income-driven student loan repayment plans (PAYE, IBR, SAVE), where MFS lowers the calculated payment by excluding the other spouse's income from the repayment calculation.

> **TRAP:** Head of Household requires the taxpayer to be unmarried OR meet the "considered unmarried" test under IRC Section 7703(b). A taxpayer who is legally married but living apart must meet ALL four prongs of the considered-unmarried test. Simply living apart is not sufficient; the spouse must not have lived in the home during the last six months of the year, and the taxpayer must have a qualifying person in the home.

> **TRAP:** NIIT and Additional Medicare Tax thresholds are NOT indexed for inflation. MFS taxpayers face a $125,000 threshold (vs. $250,000 for MFJ), which doubles the surtax exposure per dollar of income when filing separately.
