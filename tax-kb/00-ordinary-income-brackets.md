# Ordinary Income Tax Brackets
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 1(a)-(d), 1(h), 1(j)(5)
**Relevant Forms:** Form 1040, Schedule D, Schedule SE, Form 8960, Form 8959, Form 6251
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

The federal individual income tax uses seven marginal brackets -- 10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, and 37% -- applied progressively so that only income within each bracket is taxed at that rate. Bracket thresholds are inflation-adjusted annually by the IRS via Revenue Procedure (Rev. Proc. 2024-40 for 2025; Rev. Proc. 2025-32 for 2026). The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed July 4, 2025, made the TCJA rate structure permanent, eliminating the scheduled 2026 sunset that would have reverted rates to the pre-2018 schedule. In addition to ordinary income tax, several parallel tax systems (SE tax, NIIT, Additional Medicare Tax, AMT) can apply to the same income.

## Rules

### Marginal vs. Effective Rate

Only income that falls within a given bracket is taxed at that bracket's rate. A taxpayer in the "37% bracket" does not pay 37% on all income -- only on the portion exceeding the 35% bracket threshold. The effective tax rate (total tax divided by total taxable income) is always lower than the marginal rate.

### How Brackets Work

Taxable income (gross income minus deductions) flows into the bracket table from the bottom up. Each layer of income fills the next bracket in sequence. The tax owed is the sum of tax computed within each bracket.

### Deduction Value

The tax benefit of any deduction equals the deduction amount multiplied by the taxpayer's marginal rate. A deduction reduces taxable income at the top of the income stack, so it saves tax at whatever rate that top slice would have been taxed.

## LTCG and Qualified Dividend Rate Schedule

Long-term capital gains (assets held > 1 year) and qualified dividends are taxed under IRC Section 1(h) at preferential rates of 0%, 15%, or 20%. These rates are determined by the taxpayer's total taxable income, not just the capital gain itself.

### Stacking Order

Ordinary income fills the brackets first. LTCG and qualified dividends then "stack" on top. The applicable LTCG/QD rate is determined by where the total taxable income (ordinary + capital gains) falls within the LTCG threshold table. This means a taxpayer with high ordinary income may have all of their LTCG taxed at 20%, even if the gain itself is modest.

## Parallel Tax Systems on the Same Income

Multiple tax regimes can apply to the same dollar of income beyond the ordinary income tax:

- **Self-Employment Tax (Schedule SE):** 15.3% (12.4% SS + 2.9% Medicare) on net SE income. The SS portion stops at the wage base ($176,100 in 2025 / $184,500 in 2026). The 2.9% Medicare portion has no cap.
- **Additional Medicare Tax (Form 8959):** 0.9% surtax on earned income exceeding $200,000 (Single) / $250,000 (MFJ) / $125,000 (MFS). Not indexed for inflation.
- **Net Investment Income Tax (Form 8960):** 3.8% surtax on the lesser of net investment income or MAGI exceeding $200,000 (Single) / $250,000 (MFJ) / $125,000 (MFS). Not indexed for inflation. Applies to interest, dividends, capital gains, rents, royalties, and passive business income. IRC Section 1411.
- **Alternative Minimum Tax (Form 6251):** Parallel tax calculation that adds back certain deductions (SALT, certain interest). For 2026, AMT exemption is $90,100 (Single) / $140,200 (MFJ), phasing out at $500,000 (Single) / $1,000,000 (MFJ). IRC Sections 55-59.

## OBBBA Changes

**OLD RULE (pre-2025):** The TCJA's seven-bracket rate structure (10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, 37%) was set to expire after December 31, 2025. Without legislative action, 2026 rates would have reverted to the pre-TCJA schedule of 10%, 15%, 25%, 28%, 33%, 35%, and 39.6%, with narrower brackets.

**NEW RULE (OBBBA):** The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (Pub. L. No. 119-21), signed July 4, 2025, made the TCJA rate structure permanent. The seven brackets (10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, 37%) continue indefinitely. The OBBBA also provided an additional inflation adjustment for the 10% and 12% bracket thresholds beginning in 2026, slightly widening those brackets beyond what the standard inflation adjustment alone would have produced. The capital gains rate structure (0%/15%/20%) was also permanently retained.

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE:** To calculate the tax benefit of any deduction, multiply the deduction amount by the taxpayer's marginal rate. A $10,000 deduction is worth $2,200 to a 22% bracket taxpayer and $3,700 to a 37% bracket taxpayer.

> **AGENT RULE:** When computing tax on LTCG/QD, first determine where the taxpayer's ordinary taxable income falls, then stack the capital gain on top. The LTCG rate is determined by where the combined total lands in the LTCG threshold table, not by the capital gain amount in isolation. A taxpayer with $500,000 of ordinary income filing Single will have all LTCG taxed at 15% or 20%, never 0%.

> **AGENT RULE:** Always check for parallel tax exposure. A self-employed taxpayer with $300,000 of net SE income and $50,000 of investment income owes ordinary income tax, SE tax, Additional Medicare Tax (0.9% on earned income over $200,000), and NIIT (3.8% on investment income to the extent MAGI exceeds $200,000).

## Common Traps

> **TRAP:** Confusing marginal rate with effective rate. A Single filer with $250,000 of taxable income is "in the 35% bracket" but their effective federal rate is approximately 23.5%. Quoting the marginal rate as the overall tax burden overstates the actual tax by roughly 50%.

> **TRAP:** Ignoring the LTCG stacking rule. A taxpayer may assume their capital gains qualify for the 0% rate because the gain is small, but if their ordinary income already pushes them past the 0% threshold, the entire gain is taxed at 15% or 20%.

> **TRAP:** Forgetting the NIIT and Additional Medicare Tax thresholds are not indexed for inflation. The $200,000/$250,000 thresholds have been fixed since 2013. Bracket creep means more taxpayers hit these surtaxes each year.

> **TRAP:** Assuming SS tax stops entirely above the wage base. Only the 6.2% OASDI portion stops at $176,100 (2025) / $184,500 (2026). The 1.45% Medicare tax (and the 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax above the threshold) continues on all earned income with no cap.
