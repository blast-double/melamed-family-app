# Gross Income -- Definition and Scope
**IRC Section(s):** Section 61, Section 451, Reg. Section 1.451-2
**Relevant Forms:** Form 1040, Form 1099 (series), Form W-2, Schedule C, Schedule D, Schedule E
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

IRC Section 61(a) defines gross income as "all income from whatever source derived," establishing the broadest possible statutory reach for federal income taxation. The definition is rooted in the Sixteenth Amendment and interpreted by the Supreme Court in *Commissioner v. Glenshaw Glass Co.*, 348 U.S. 426 (1955), to encompass all "undeniable accessions to wealth, clearly realized, and over which the taxpayers have complete dominion." Section 61 is not an exhaustive list but an illustrative one; any economic gain not specifically excluded by Sections 101-140 is gross income. Timing of inclusion is governed by Section 451 and Reg. Section 1.451-2 (constructive receipt).

## Rules

### A. Statutory Enumeration (Section 61(a)(1)-(14))

Section 61(a) lists the following categories of gross income. The list is illustrative, not exhaustive:

1. Compensation for services -- wages, salaries, tips, fees, commissions, fringe benefits, and similar items
2. Gross income derived from business
3. Gains derived from dealings in property
4. Interest
5. Rents
6. Royalties
7. Dividends
8. Annuities
9. Income from life insurance and endowment contracts
10. Pensions
11. Income from discharge of indebtedness
12. Distributive share of partnership gross income (including S-corporation passthrough)
13. Income in respect of a decedent
14. Income from an interest in an estate or trust

**Additional items recognized as gross income by statute, regulation, or case law:**
- Alimony received under divorce or separation agreements executed **before** January 1, 2019 (Section 71, repealed for post-2018 agreements by TCJA Section 11051)
- Gambling winnings (Section 61; reported on Form W-2G when thresholds are met)
- Prizes and awards (Section 74, unless donated under Section 74(b))
- Barter income -- fair market value of property or services received in exchange (Reg. Section 1.61-2(d))
- Imputed income -- below-market loans (Section 7872), employer-provided benefits exceeding exclusion limits
- Found property -- treasure trove includable in gross income in the year reduced to undisputed possession (Reg. Section 1.61-14)

### B. The Glenshaw Glass Test

The Supreme Court in *Commissioner v. Glenshaw Glass Co.*, 348 U.S. 426 (1955), established a three-part test for gross income. An item is gross income if it constitutes:

1. An **undeniable accession to wealth**
2. That is **clearly realized**
3. Over which the taxpayer has **complete dominion**

This test superseded the narrower *Eisner v. Macomber* formulation (income as gain from capital or labor). Since *Glenshaw Glass*, courts read Section 61 expansively to include all realized gains regardless of source -- including punitive damages, insider-profit recoveries, embezzled funds, and windfall gains -- unless a specific Code provision excludes them.[^1]

### C. Cash Equivalency Doctrine

Under Reg. Section 1.61-1(a), gross income includes income realized in any form -- money, property, or services. When property or services are received in lieu of cash, the amount included in gross income is the **fair market value** (FMV) at the time of receipt. If services are paid for at a stipulated price, that price is presumed to be FMV absent evidence to the contrary (Reg. Section 1.61-2(d)(1)).[^2]

### D. Constructive Receipt (Reg. Section 1.451-2(a))

Income is constructively received -- and therefore taxable -- in the taxable year during which it is:

- **Credited** to the taxpayer's account, **or**
- **Set apart** for the taxpayer, **or**
- **Otherwise made available** so that the taxpayer may draw upon it at any time

Income is **not** constructively received if the taxpayer's control of its receipt is subject to **substantial limitations or restrictions**.

**Examples of constructive receipt:**
- Uncashed checks -- a check received or available for pickup is income when available, not when deposited
- Bank interest credited to an account -- taxable when credited, regardless of withdrawal
- Matured bond coupons -- taxable when the coupon matures and is available for redemption
- Bonus credited on corporate books -- constructively received only if no restriction on access

**Not constructive receipt:**
- Employer credits bonus stock to employee but stock is not available until a future vesting date
- Withdrawal penalties that are **substantial** relative to total earnings (e.g., forfeiture of more than three months' interest on early CD withdrawal)[^3]

### E. Claim of Right Doctrine (Section 1341)

Income received under a **claim of right** -- without restriction on its use -- is taxable in the year received, even if an obligation to repay may arise later. If the taxpayer subsequently repays the amount:

- **Repayment of $3,000 or less:** Deducted in the year of repayment on the same form/schedule where originally reported.
- **Repayment exceeding $3,000:** Section 1341 relief applies. The taxpayer computes tax two ways and uses whichever produces the lower tax: (a) deduct the repayment in the current year, or (b) recompute the prior year's tax as if the income had never been included and claim a credit for the difference.[^4]

### F. Tax Benefit Rule (Section 111)

Section 111(a) provides that a recovery of an amount deducted in a prior year is includable in gross income **only to the extent** the prior deduction produced a tax benefit (i.e., reduced the taxpayer's tax liability). If the taxpayer took the standard deduction in the prior year and the recovered item was part of unused itemized deductions, the recovery is excluded from gross income.[^5]

### G. Assignment of Income Doctrine (*Lucas v. Earl*)

Under *Lucas v. Earl*, 281 U.S. 111 (1930), income is taxed to the **person who earns it**. A taxpayer cannot avoid tax by contractually assigning income to another person before it is earned. Justice Holmes wrote: "the fruits [cannot] be attributed to a different tree from that on which they grew." This doctrine applies to:

- Service income assigned to a spouse or family member
- Income from property where the taxpayer retains control (distinguished from *Blair v. Commissioner*, 300 U.S. 5 (1937), where a complete transfer of a property interest shifts the income)
- Compensation directed to a third party at the taxpayer's instruction[^6]

## OBBBA Changes (if applicable)

**OLD RULE (pre-2025):** Tip income and overtime pay were fully includable in gross income under Section 61(a)(1) with no above-the-line or below-the-line deduction available.

**NEW RULE (OBBBA):** The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (signed July 4, 2025) created new below-the-line deductions effective for tax years 2025-2028:

1. **Qualified tip income deduction** (Section 61 as amended): Up to $25,000 of voluntary tips in occupations that "traditionally and customarily" received tips before 2025 may be deducted. Available to both itemizers and non-itemizers. Tips must be voluntary (not mandatory service charges). Phases out at 10% rate above $150,000 MAGI ($300,000 MFJ).
2. **Qualified overtime compensation deduction**: Up to $12,500 ($25,000 MFJ) of overtime premium pay for nonexempt employees under the FLSA may be deducted. Only the premium portion (the extra half of "time-and-a-half") qualifies. Same phase-out thresholds as tip deduction.

These are **income tax deductions only**. FICA (Social Security and Medicare) continues to apply to all tip and overtime income. The deductions do not change the definition of gross income under Section 61; they reduce taxable income after gross income is calculated.[^7]

**Effective date:** Tax years beginning after December 31, 2024. Sunset: December 31, 2028.

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE:** Section 61 is the starting gate. Before analyzing any deduction, credit, or exclusion, confirm the item is gross income under the Glenshaw Glass three-part test. If the item falls under Sections 101-140 (exclusions), it is removed before any further calculation begins.

> **AGENT RULE:** When evaluating alimony, check the execution date of the divorce or separation agreement. If executed on or after January 1, 2019, alimony is **not** gross income to the recipient and **not** deductible by the payor (TCJA Section 11051). Only pre-2019 agreements (not modified to adopt the new rule) produce includable alimony income.

> **AGENT RULE:** When a taxpayer receives income in property or services rather than cash, determine FMV at the time of receipt and include that amount as gross income. Do not use the taxpayer's subjective valuation.

## Common Traps

> **TRAP:** Constructive receipt -- a taxpayer cannot defer income by refusing to cash a check or declining to access a credited account. Availability, not actual receipt, triggers taxation. An uncashed year-end check is income in the year it was available, not the year it was deposited.

> **TRAP:** Discharge of indebtedness -- cancellation of a debt of $600 or more triggers Form 1099-C and is gross income under Section 61(a)(11) unless an exception applies (insolvency under Section 108(a)(1)(B), bankruptcy under Section 108(a)(1)(A), qualified principal residence indebtedness, or qualified farm/real property indebtedness). Taxpayers routinely fail to report COD income when no 1099-C is issued.

> **TRAP:** Barter transactions -- exchanging services for services or property is a taxable event at FMV for both parties. Failure to report barter income is a common audit trigger, particularly for small business owners and independent contractors.

> **TRAP:** Assignment of income -- directing an employer to pay compensation to a family member, charity, or trust does not shift the tax obligation. The earner is taxed on the full amount regardless of who receives payment.
