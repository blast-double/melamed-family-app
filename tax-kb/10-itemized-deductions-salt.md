# Itemized Deductions and SALT
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 68, 163, 164, 165, 170, 213
**Relevant Forms:** Schedule A (Form 1040), Form 8283 (Noncash Charitable Contributions), Form 4684 (Casualties and Thefts), Publication 936 (Home Mortgage Interest)
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

Itemized deductions under Schedule A represent one of the most complex areas of individual taxation, and the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed July 4, 2025, made sweeping changes. The OBBBA permanently extended most TCJA modifications to itemized deductions while introducing new rules -- notably a quadrupled SALT cap, a charitable deduction floor, expanded casualty loss eligibility, and a new Pease-like overall limitation (the "2/37 rule") for top-bracket taxpayers.

This document covers the six major categories of itemized deductions: SALT (Section 164), mortgage interest (Section 163), charitable contributions (Section 170), casualty and theft losses (Section 165), medical expenses (Section 213), and miscellaneous itemized deductions. It also addresses the overall limitation under Section 68 and the pass-through entity tax (PTET) workaround.

**Standard Deduction Context (2025-2026):** Taxpayers only benefit from itemizing when total itemized deductions exceed the standard deduction. For 2025: $15,750 (single), $23,625 (HOH), $31,500 (MFJ). For 2026: $16,100 (single), $24,150 (HOH), $32,200 (MFJ). The higher SALT cap under OBBBA may push some taxpayers back into itemizing who had been taking the standard deduction since 2018.

## Rules

### 1. State and Local Taxes (Section 164)

**Election.** Taxpayers choose to deduct either state/local income taxes OR state/local general sales taxes (not both), plus state/local real and personal property taxes. The election is annual and claimed on Schedule A.

**SALT Cap (2025-2029).** The OBBBA raised the SALT deduction cap from $10,000 to $40,000 for MFJ and single filers ($20,000 MFS), effective for tax year 2025. The cap increases by 1% annually through 2029, then reverts permanently to $10,000 in 2030.

**MAGI Phase-Down.** For taxpayers with MAGI exceeding $500,000 ($250,000 MFS), the SALT cap is reduced by $0.30 for each dollar of MAGI above the threshold. The cap cannot be reduced below $10,000. The MAGI threshold also increases 1% annually through 2029.

**Scope.** The SALT deduction covers state and local income taxes (or sales taxes), real property taxes, and personal property taxes. Foreign income taxes are NOT deductible under SALT; they are claimed via the foreign tax credit (Section 901) or as a separate itemized deduction where applicable.

**Prepayment Rules.** Prepaid state income taxes are deductible in the year paid if assessed and owed. Estimated payments for a future year are generally deductible when paid. However, prepaid property taxes for a year not yet assessed are not deductible until the year to which they relate.

### 2. Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET) Elections

**IRS Notice 2020-75.** In November 2020, the IRS authorized the PTET workaround. Under this mechanism, pass-through entities (partnerships, S corporations, certain LLCs) in states that have enacted PTET legislation can elect to pay state income tax at the entity level rather than the owner level.

**Effect.** The entity-level tax payment is deductible as a business expense on the entity's return, bypassing the individual SALT cap entirely. The owner receives a corresponding state tax credit that offsets state liability but reduces their individual SALT deduction dollar-for-dollar.

**OBBBA Preservation.** Despite earlier legislative proposals that would have restricted PTET elections, the final OBBBA text did not change PTET rules. IRS Notice 2020-75 remains fully in effect. The PTET workaround continues to be available for PTE owners who exceed the elevated SALT cap or the MAGI threshold.

**State Availability.** As of 2026, 36 jurisdictions have enacted PTET regimes. Twenty-six states and localities (including New York, New Jersey, Connecticut) have permanent regimes. Five states (Colorado, Iowa, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota) have regimes linked to the federal SALT cap. Four states (Illinois, Oregon, Utah, Virginia) had regimes set to sunset December 31, 2025, and required legislative extension. California extended its PTET program for an additional five years.

### 3. Mortgage Interest (Section 163(h))

**Acquisition Indebtedness.** The OBBBA permanently extends the TCJA rule limiting the mortgage interest deduction to acquisition debt of up to $750,000 ($375,000 MFS). Acquisition debt is debt incurred to buy, build, or substantially improve a qualified residence (main home plus one second home).

**Grandfathered Debt.** Mortgages originated on or before December 15, 2017 (or under binding contracts entered before that date with closing by April 1, 2018) retain the prior $1,000,000 limit ($500,000 MFS). If grandfathered debt is refinanced, the new loan retains grandfathered status up to the balance of the original loan.

**Home Equity Interest.** The OBBBA permanently disallows deductions for home equity loan interest unless the proceeds are used to buy, build, or substantially improve the qualifying residence. Even then, the debt counts against the $750,000 acquisition debt limit.

**Points.** Points paid on a purchase mortgage for a principal residence are generally deductible in the year paid if they meet standard requirements (paid at closing, computed as a percentage of the loan, within the range of points charged in the area). Points on refinanced mortgages must be amortized over the life of the loan.

**Mortgage Insurance Premiums.** Starting in 2026, the OBBBA permanently reinstates the treatment of qualified mortgage insurance premiums (PMI) as deductible mortgage interest (IRC Section 163(h)(3)(F)(i)(III)). Premiums are NOT subject to the $750,000 debt limit, but the deduction phases out ratably by 10% for each $1,000 ($500 MFS) of AGI exceeding $100,000 ($50,000 MFS), fully phased out at $109,000 ($54,500 MFS). These thresholds are not indexed for inflation.

### 4. Charitable Contributions (Section 170)

**AGI Floor for Itemizers (Effective 2026).** The OBBBA introduces a 0.5% AGI floor for charitable deductions. An individual's otherwise allowable charitable deduction is reduced by 0.5% of their contribution base (generally AGI). This is a permanent reduction -- amounts below the floor are lost and cannot be carried forward.

**Percentage Limitations (Unchanged).**
- Cash contributions to public charities: 60% of AGI (contribution base).
- Capital gain property to public charities: 30% of AGI.
- Cash to private non-operating foundations: 30% of AGI.
- Capital gain property to private foundations: 20% of AGI.
- Five-year carryforward applies to contributions exceeding the applicable percentage limit.

**Substantiation Requirements.**
- $250 or more: contemporaneous written acknowledgment from the donee organization required.
- Noncash contributions exceeding $500: Form 8283, Section A required.
- Noncash contributions exceeding $5,000: qualified appraisal and Form 8283, Section B required.
- Clothing and household items must be in good used condition or better.

**Non-Itemizer Deduction (Effective 2026).** Individuals who claim the standard deduction may deduct up to $1,000 ($2,000 MFJ) of cash contributions to qualified public charities as an above-the-line deduction. This echoes (and increases) the COVID-era provision that was available for 2020-2021 ($300/$600 limits).

**Rate Cap (Effective 2026).** The overall limitation under the 2/37 rule (see Section 7 below) effectively caps the tax benefit of charitable deductions at 35% for taxpayers in the 37% bracket.

### 5. Casualty and Theft Losses (Section 165)

**Disaster Requirement.** The TCJA restricted personal casualty loss deductions to losses in federally declared disaster areas (2018-2025). The OBBBA makes this restriction permanent but expands it beginning in 2026 to also include losses in state-declared disaster areas (IRC Section 165(h)(5)).

**Per-Casualty Floor.** Each casualty or theft event is reduced by $100 (technically $500 per the statute, but $100 is the operative amount under pre-2010 language carried forward; practitioners should verify current IRS instructions as this amount has been subject to legislative revision).

**10% AGI Floor.** After applying the per-casualty reduction and subtracting insurance/reimbursement, aggregate net personal casualty losses are deductible only to the extent they exceed 10% of AGI.

**Qualified Disaster Loss Exception.** Qualified disaster losses may be deducted without itemizing and are not subject to the 10% AGI floor, but are subject to a $500 per-casualty reduction. Taxpayers may elect to claim the loss on the prior year's return under Section 165(i).

**Gambling Losses (2026+).** The OBBBA limits gambling loss deductions to 90% of gambling losses incurred, then further limited to the extent of gambling winnings.

### 6. Medical and Dental Expenses (Section 213)

**7.5% AGI Floor -- Permanent.** The OBBBA permanently extends the 7.5% AGI threshold for deducting unreimbursed medical expenses. (The threshold had been scheduled to revert to 10% when the TCJA expired.) Only the amount exceeding 7.5% of AGI is deductible.

**Qualifying Expenses.** Include: physician/surgeon/dentist fees, hospital stays, prescription drugs and insulin, diagnostic tests, medical equipment, mental health treatment, substance abuse treatment, long-term care services, health insurance premiums (including Medicare Part B and D), transportation for medical care. Do NOT include: cosmetic surgery (unless for disfigurement from disease, accident, or congenital abnormality), general health items (vitamins, gym memberships), nonprescription drugs (except insulin).

**Long-Term Care Premiums.** Deductible up to age-based limits (adjusted annually for inflation). For 2025, the limits range from $480 (age 40 or under) to $5,960 (age 70+).

**HSA/FSA Coordination.** Expenses paid through HSAs or FSAs are not deductible because they were already paid with pre-tax dollars. Only unreimbursed amounts qualify.

### 7. Miscellaneous Itemized Deductions

**Permanently Eliminated.** The OBBBA permanently suspends all miscellaneous itemized deductions that were formerly subject to the 2% AGI floor. This includes: unreimbursed employee business expenses, tax preparation fees, investment advisory and management fees, safe deposit box rental, hobby expenses (to the extent of hobby income), and other income-production expenses.

**Exceptions Preserved.** Certain above-the-line deductions remain available for specific categories of taxpayers:
- **Armed Forces reservists:** unreimbursed travel expenses for reserve duty more than 100 miles from home.
- **Qualified performing artists:** deduction for performing-arts-related employee business expenses if adjusted gross income is $16,000 or less (before the deduction), among other requirements.
- **Fee-basis state or local government officials:** unreimbursed employee business expenses.
- **Educator expense deduction:** up to $300 ($600 MFJ if both spouses are educators) for classroom supplies. The OBBBA also enacted a limited miscellaneous deduction for teachers and other school personnel.

## OBBBA Changes (if applicable)

The OBBBA made the following changes to itemized deductions, organized by effective date:

**Effective 2025:**
- SALT cap increased from $10,000 to $40,000 ($20,000 MFS) with 1% annual escalator through 2029.
- MAGI phase-down introduced at $500,000 ($250,000 MFS); reduces cap by $0.30 per dollar of excess; floor of $10,000.
- PTET elections preserved (no restrictions added despite earlier proposals).

**Effective 2026 (Permanent Unless Noted):**
- Charitable deduction 0.5% AGI floor for itemizers.
- Non-itemizer charitable deduction reinstated at $1,000/$2,000.
- 2/37 overall limitation on itemized deductions replaces the repealed Pease limitation.
- Casualty loss deduction expanded to include state-declared disasters.
- Mortgage interest $750,000 limit made permanent (was temporary under TCJA through 2025).
- Home equity interest disallowance made permanent (unless used to buy/build/improve).
- PMI premium deduction reinstated permanently (was expired since 12/31/2021).
- Medical expense 7.5% AGI floor made permanent (was set to revert to 10%).
- Miscellaneous itemized deductions permanently eliminated.
- Gambling losses limited to 90% of losses, then capped at winnings.

**Sunset 2030:**
- SALT cap reverts to $10,000 ($5,000 MFS) unless extended by Congress.

## Agent Rules

1. **SALT vs. Standard Deduction Check.** Before analyzing SALT strategies, calculate whether the taxpayer's total itemized deductions (including the elevated SALT cap) exceed their standard deduction. Many taxpayers shifted to standard deduction post-TCJA; the $40,000 SALT cap may flip them back.

2. **PTET Election Analysis.** For any taxpayer who is a partner, S corp shareholder, or LLC member in a PTET-eligible state, evaluate whether entity-level tax election produces a greater benefit than the individual SALT deduction, particularly when MAGI exceeds $500,000.

3. **Charitable Bunching.** Beginning 2026, the 0.5% AGI floor makes small annual donations less tax-efficient. Model bunching strategies (concentrating two or more years of giving into one year) and donor-advised fund contributions. In non-bunching years, the taxpayer can use the $1,000/$2,000 non-itemizer deduction.

4. **QCD for 70.5+ Taxpayers.** Qualified Charitable Distributions from IRAs (up to $108,000 in 2025, $111,000 in 2026) count toward RMDs without increasing AGI, avoiding both the charitable floor and the SALT MAGI phase-down.

5. **Mortgage Interest Documentation.** Verify whether existing mortgage debt is grandfathered ($1M limit) or post-12/15/2017 ($750K limit). For refinanced grandfathered debt, confirm the new loan balance does not exceed the original to preserve grandfathered status.

6. **Medical Expense Timing.** If a taxpayer is near the 7.5% AGI floor in a given year, consider accelerating elective medical procedures or prepaying medical costs into that year.

7. **2/37 Rule Impact.** For taxpayers in the 37% bracket, calculate the effective deduction reduction. The rule caps tax benefit at 35% -- a 2-percentage-point haircut. This applies after all other floors and phase-outs.

8. **Casualty Loss Prior-Year Election.** Under Section 165(i), losses from a declared disaster may be deducted on the prior year's return. Evaluate whether the prior year's AGI produces a better result after the 10% floor.

## Common Traps

1. **Foreign Taxes on Schedule A.** Foreign income taxes are NOT part of the SALT deduction and are NOT subject to the SALT cap. Taxpayers who conflate foreign taxes with SALT may either miss the foreign tax credit or incorrectly apply the SALT cap to foreign taxes.

2. **PTET Double-Counting.** If the entity makes a PTET election, the owner must reduce their individual SALT deduction by the corresponding state tax credit. Failing to do so results in a double deduction.

3. **SALT Prepayment Disallowance.** Prepaying property taxes for a year not yet assessed or due does not accelerate the deduction. Only taxes that have been assessed and are legally owed are deductible when paid.

4. **Charitable Floor Is Not a Carryforward.** Amounts lost to the 0.5% AGI floor are permanently nondeductible. This differs from the percentage limitation carryforward, which carries excess contributions forward five years. The floor applies before the percentage limitation.

5. **Home Equity Trap.** Home equity loan interest is deductible only if proceeds were used to buy, build, or substantially improve the qualifying home. HELOCs used for debt consolidation, education, or other personal purposes produce nondeductible interest permanently under OBBBA.

6. **Medical Expense Reimbursement Offset.** Insurance reimbursements received in a later year for expenses previously deducted must be included in gross income in the year received (tax benefit rule). Taxpayers sometimes fail to report these reimbursements.

7. **Casualty Loss Substantiation.** The IRS requires contemporaneous documentation: photographs, appraisals, insurance claims, and FEMA/state disaster declarations. Losses without adequate proof are routinely disallowed on audit.

8. **Miscellaneous Deduction Ghost Claims.** The permanent elimination of miscellaneous itemized deductions means investment management fees, tax prep fees, and unreimbursed employee expenses are never deductible on Schedule A. Taxpayers (and some software) may still attempt to claim them.

9. **2/37 Rule Interaction with AMT.** The overall limitation applies for regular tax purposes. Taxpayers near AMT thresholds should model the interaction, as AMT disallows SALT entirely and uses different rules for other deductions.

10. **SALT Cap Sunset Planning.** The $40,000 cap is temporary (2025-2029). Taxpayers who restructure finances around the higher cap (e.g., declining PTET elections) should be aware the cap reverts to $10,000 in 2030.
