# Property Sales and Depreciation Recapture
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 1231, 1245, 1250, 64, 65
**Relevant Forms:** Form 4797, Schedule D, Form 1040 Schedule 1
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

When a taxpayer sells depreciable property used in a trade or business, the gain is not simply a capital gain. The IRC requires a layered analysis under Sections 1231, 1245, and 1250 to determine how much gain is ordinary income (recapturing prior depreciation) and how much qualifies for LTCG rates. Sections 64 and 65 provide foundational definitions: Section 64 defines "ordinary income" as any income other than gain from selling a capital asset; Section 65 defines "ordinary loss" as any loss other than a loss from selling a capital asset. Sections 1245 and 1250 recharacterize what would otherwise be Section 1231 capital gain back into ordinary income to the extent of prior depreciation claimed.

## Rules

### Section 1231: The Best-of-Both-Worlds Rule

Section 1231 property is real property or depreciable personal property used in a trade or business and held for more than one year (IRC Section 1231(b)(1)). Property held primarily for sale to customers does not qualify.

All Section 1231 gains and losses for the tax year are netted:

- **Net gain:** Treated as **long-term capital gain** (0%, 15%, or 20%).
- **Net loss:** Treated as **ordinary loss**, fully deductible against ordinary income with no $3,000 annual cap.

Recapture rules under Sections 1245 and 1250 apply *first* and reduce the amount receiving Section 1231 treatment.

**Sources:** 26 USC Section 1231 (law.cornell.edu); IRS Pub. 544 (2025); stevenjcpa.com.

### The 5-Year Lookback Rule (Nonrecaptured Section 1231 Losses)

Section 1231(c) prevents cherry-picking by imposing a lookback: if a taxpayer had a net Section 1231 loss in any of the **five preceding tax years**, current-year net Section 1231 gain is recharacterized as **ordinary income** to the extent of those prior unrecaptured losses.

- Nonrecaptured Section 1231 loss = total net Section 1231 losses deducted in the prior five years, reduced by amounts already recaptured.
- Once fully recaptured, a prior loss does not recapture again.
- For passthrough entities, the lookback applies at the **owner level**, not the entity level.

**Example:** $30,000 net Section 1231 loss in 2022. In 2026, $40,000 net Section 1231 gain. First $30,000 recharacterized as ordinary income; remaining $10,000 is LTCG.

**Sources:** cpabr.com; cpaexamsmastery.com; timbertax.org.

### Section 1245 Recapture: Personal Property

Section 1245 applies to tangible personal property (equipment, vehicles, machinery, furniture), intangible assets (patents, copyrights), and property expensed under Section 179. It also covers certain real property depreciated under pre-1987 ACRS accelerated methods.

**Rule:** Upon sale, **all depreciation previously claimed (or allowable)** is recaptured as **ordinary income** up to the recognized gain. Full-recapture rule -- taxed at ordinary rates up to 37%.

- Includes depreciation, Section 179 expensing, and bonus depreciation.
- If gain < total depreciation claimed, recapture = gain.
- If gain > total depreciation claimed, excess is Section 1231 gain at LTCG rates.
- No recapture if the asset is sold at a loss.

**Example:** Vehicle purchased for $50,000, $35,000 depreciation claimed (basis = $15,000), sold for $22,000. Gain = $7,000. All $7,000 recaptured as ordinary income ($7,000 < $35,000 depreciation).

**Sources:** IRS Pub. 544 (2025); tax.thomsonreuters.com; mcguiresponsel.com; turbotax.intuit.com.

### Section 1250 Recapture: Real Property

Section 1250 applies to depreciable real property (buildings, structural components, rental properties).

**Rule:** Section 1250 recaptures only **additional depreciation** -- the excess of actual depreciation over what straight-line would have allowed. This excess is ordinary income, limited to the gain.

**Practical reality:** Since 1987, MACRS requires straight-line for all real property (residential 27.5 years, nonresidential 39 years). Straight-line is mandatory, so there is typically **zero Section 1250 ordinary income recapture** on post-1986 real property.

**Sources:** IRS Pub. 544 (2025); taxcpe.com; eisneramper.com; thetaxadviser.com.

### Unrecaptured Section 1250 Gain: The 25% Rate

Under IRC Section 1(h)(1)(E), gain attributable to straight-line depreciation on Section 1250 property is **unrecaptured Section 1250 gain**, taxed at a **maximum 25%** rate.

- The 25% is a *ceiling*; if the taxpayer's marginal rate is lower, the lower rate applies.
- Limited to the lesser of (a) total depreciation claimed, or (b) the recognized gain.
- Gain exceeding depreciation is Section 1231 gain at standard LTCG rates.
- Reported via the Unrecaptured Section 1250 Gain Worksheet in the Schedule D instructions.

**Sources:** IRS Pub. 544 (2025); taxcpe.com; turbotax.intuit.com; jmco.com; eisneramper.com.

### Priority of Application

When a depreciable business asset is sold at a gain, the Code applies in this order:

1. **Section 1245 recapture** -- all depreciation on personal property recaptured as ordinary income (up to the gain).
2. **Section 1250 recapture** -- excess depreciation (above straight-line) on real property recaptured as ordinary income (up to the gain). Rarely applies post-1986.
3. **Unrecaptured Section 1250 gain** -- straight-line depreciation on real property taxed at max 25%.
4. **Section 1231 gain** -- remaining gain taxed at LTCG rates (0%/15%/20%), subject to the 5-year lookback rule.

### Sections 64 and 65: Definitional Foundation

- **Section 64** defines "ordinary income" as any income other than gain from the sale or exchange of a capital asset.
- **Section 65** defines "ordinary loss" as any loss other than a loss from the sale or exchange of a capital asset.

These definitions are why recaptured depreciation is "ordinary" -- Sections 1245 and 1250 recharacterize capital gain into ordinary income per Section 64.

## OBBBA Changes (One Big Beautiful Bill Act -- signed July 4, 2025)

The OBBBA did not directly amend Sections 1231, 1245, or 1250. However, it significantly affects recapture exposure through the following changes:

1. **Permanent 100% bonus depreciation.** Permanently reinstated for qualified property (MACRS class life 20 years or less) acquired after January 19, 2025, reversing the TCJA phasedown. More upfront deduction = larger Section 1245 recapture exposure on sale.

2. **Section 179 expanded.** Limit doubled to $2.5M; phase-out raised to $4M (inflation-indexed from 2026). More Section 179 expense = more Section 1245 recapture on sale.

3. **Qualified Production Property (QPP).** New category: 100% first-year depreciation for certain nonresidential real property in qualified production activities. Special 10-year recapture: if disposed of within 10 years, all depreciation recaptured as ordinary income.

4. **QOF interaction.** For QOF investments held 10+ years, gains (including recapture amounts) may be excluded. Narrow planning exception.

**Sources:** bdo.com; rsmus.com; grantthornton.com; dhjj.com; mayerbrown.com.

## Walkthrough Example: Sale of Rental Property

**Facts:** Residential rental purchased in 2015 for $500,000 ($400,000 building, $100,000 land). Straight-line MACRS at 27.5 years. No cost segregation. After 10 years: depreciation claimed = $145,455 ($400K / 27.5 x 10). Adjusted basis = $354,545. Sold for $650,000.

**Step 1 -- Total gain:** $650,000 - $354,545 = **$295,455**.

**Step 2 -- Section 1245 recapture:** No personal property was separately depreciated (no cost seg). **$0.**

**Step 3 -- Section 1250 recapture:** All depreciation was straight-line. No excess over straight-line. **$0.**

**Step 4 -- Unrecaptured Section 1250 gain:** Depreciation = $145,455, gain = $295,455. **$145,455 at max 25%.**

**Step 5 -- Section 1231 gain:** $295,455 - $145,455 = **$150,000 at LTCG rates**, subject to the 5-year lookback.

**Step 6 -- Lookback:** If no net Section 1231 losses in 2021-2025, the full $150,000 is LTCG. A $20,000 prior loss would recharacterize $20,000 as ordinary income.

**Tax summary (15% LTCG rate, no lookback):**

| Component | Amount | Rate | Tax |
|-----------|--------|------|-----|
| Unrecaptured Sec. 1250 gain | $145,455 | 25% | $36,364 |
| Section 1231 gain (LTCG) | $150,000 | 15% | $22,500 |
| **Subtotal** | | | **$58,864** |
| NIIT if applicable | $295,455 | 3.8% | $11,227 |

**Reporting:** Form 4797 Part III for recapture. Unrecaptured Sec. 1250 gain to Schedule D worksheet. Section 1231 gain through Form 4797 Part I to Schedule D.

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE:** Every sale of a depreciable business asset must be analyzed in order: (1) How much depreciation was previously claimed (or was allowable, even if not claimed)? (2) Is the property Section 1245 or Section 1250? If Section 1245, recapture ALL depreciation as ordinary income up to the gain. If Section 1250, recapture only excess depreciation above straight-line as ordinary income (usually zero under MACRS), then apply the 25% maximum rate to unrecaptured Section 1250 gain equal to straight-line depreciation claimed. (3) Remaining gain is Section 1231 gain at LTCG rates, subject to the 5-year lookback. Never report a business property sale as a simple capital gain without checking for recapture.

> **AGENT RULE:** When computing unrecaptured Section 1250 gain, use depreciation "allowed or allowable" -- even if the taxpayer failed to claim depreciation on their returns, the IRS treats the depreciation as having been taken. Always use the allowable amount.

> **AGENT RULE:** For passthrough entities (partnerships, LLCs, S corps), apply the Section 1231 lookback rule at the individual owner level, not the entity level. Check each owner's personal history of Section 1231 losses.

## Common Traps

> **TRAP:** Cost segregation accelerates depreciation into personal property categories (5-year, 7-year, 15-year property), reclassifying portions of a building from Section 1250 to Section 1245 property. This increases Section 1245 recapture exposure upon sale because Section 1245 recapture is at ordinary rates (up to 37%) rather than the 25% maximum for unrecaptured Section 1250 gain. Always model recapture before advising a sale of property that underwent cost segregation.

> **TRAP:** Under OBBBA's permanent 100% bonus depreciation, a taxpayer who immediately expenses the full cost of Section 1245 property will owe ordinary income recapture on the entire amount if the asset is later sold at a gain. The upfront tax benefit is real, but the recapture bill on exit can be substantial.

> **TRAP:** The IRS taxes recapture on depreciation "allowed or allowable." If a taxpayer neglected to claim depreciation on a rental property, they still owe recapture on the amount they *could* have claimed. There is no benefit to skipping depreciation deductions -- the recapture applies regardless.

> **TRAP:** A Section 1031 like-kind exchange defers Section 1250/unrecaptured Section 1250 gain on real property, but does NOT defer Section 1245 recapture on personal property components. If cost segregation was performed, the Section 1245 personal property must be separately matched with replacement Section 1245 property of equal or greater value, or the shortfall triggers immediate ordinary income recapture.

> **TRAP:** The 5-year lookback rule can surprise taxpayers who took large Section 1231 ordinary losses in prior years (e.g., from a casualty or abandonment). A subsequent Section 1231 gain will be recharacterized as ordinary income to the extent of those prior losses, eliminating the expected capital gain rate benefit.
