# Cryptocurrency and Digital Assets
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 61, 1001, 1012, 1091; Notice 2014-21; Notice 2023-34; Rev. Rul. 2023-14; Rev. Rul. 2019-24
**Relevant Forms:** Form 1099-DA, Form 8949, Schedule D (Form 1040), Form 1040 (Digital Asset Question)
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

Virtual currency (cryptocurrency) is treated as **property** for federal tax purposes, not as currency. This foundational classification was established in IRS Notice 2014-21 (2014-16 I.R.B. 938) and means that general tax principles applicable to property transactions -- capital gains, ordinary income, basis, and holding periods -- govern all crypto transactions.

Notice 2023-34 later modified Notice 2014-21 by removing the statement that virtual currency "does not have legal tender status in any jurisdiction," reflecting El Salvador's adoption of Bitcoin as legal tender, but confirmed the modification does not change the property treatment or any of the FAQ answers from the original notice.

The practical consequence: every sale, exchange, or disposition of cryptocurrency triggers a taxable event that must be reported. There is no special regime -- crypto follows the same rules as stocks, real estate, or any other property asset under IRC Sections 61 (gross income) and 1001 (gain or loss on disposition).

**Sources:** IRS Notice 2014-21; IRS Notice 2023-34; IRS FAQ on Virtual Currency Transactions (irs.gov, updated Feb. 2026); altoira.com/insights/crypto-tax-rules-ira-2025.

## Rules

### Taxable Events

The following cryptocurrency transactions are taxable:

1. **Sale for fiat currency.** Selling crypto for USD or any government-issued currency is a disposition under IRC Section 1001. Capital gain or loss equals proceeds minus adjusted basis.

2. **Exchange of one crypto for another.** Trading BTC for ETH, or any crypto-to-crypto swap, is treated as selling the first asset for its fair market value and purchasing the second. This includes stablecoin conversions (e.g., BTC to USDC). (IRS FAQ A-6; gordonlaw.com)

3. **Use to purchase goods or services.** Spending crypto triggers a disposition at the fair market value of the goods or services received. (Notice 2014-21, Q&A-3, Q&A-4)

4. **Mining income.** Crypto received through proof-of-work mining is ordinary income equal to the FMV on the date of receipt. (Notice 2014-21, Q&A-8)

5. **Staking rewards.** Per Rev. Rul. 2023-14, staking rewards on proof-of-stake blockchains are includible in gross income at FMV when the taxpayer gains "dominion and control" -- i.e., the ability to sell, exchange, or dispose of the reward units. This applies whether the taxpayer stakes directly on-chain or through a centralized exchange. The ruling applies to cash-method taxpayers. (Cooley alert, Aug. 2023; BDO insights; wiss.com)

6. **Airdrops.** Crypto received via airdrop is ordinary income at FMV on date of receipt. (Rev. Rul. 2019-24)

7. **Hard forks.** If a hard fork results in receipt of new cryptocurrency, the FMV of the new units is ordinary income when the taxpayer obtains dominion and control. A hard fork alone, without an airdrop of new tokens, does not create income. (Rev. Rul. 2019-24)

8. **DeFi swaps and liquidity pool transactions.** Token swaps through decentralized exchanges are taxable dispositions, the same as centralized exchange trades. Providing liquidity may trigger taxable events depending on the protocol mechanics.

### Non-Taxable Events

- **Buying crypto with fiat** and holding it -- no disposition occurs.
- **Transferring between own wallets** -- moving crypto between wallets you control is not a taxable event (no change of ownership).
- **Holding (HODLing)** -- unrealized appreciation is not taxed.
- **Gifting crypto** -- generally not taxable to the donor (subject to gift tax rules; $18,000 annual exclusion for 2025). Recipient takes donor's basis (carryover basis).
- **Donating crypto** to a qualified charity -- may generate a charitable deduction at FMV if held more than one year.

### Mining and Staking: Detailed Treatment

For mining income (proof-of-work) and staking rewards (proof-of-stake):

- **Character:** Ordinary income, not capital gain, at the time of receipt.
- **Amount:** FMV in USD on the date and time the taxpayer gains dominion and control.
- **Basis:** The taxpayer's basis in the received units equals the amount of income recognized. If you receive 0.5 ETH as a staking reward and include $1,000 as ordinary income, your basis in that 0.5 ETH is $1,000.
- **Self-employment tax:** Mining income may be subject to self-employment tax if the mining activity constitutes a trade or business. Staking income treatment for SE tax is less settled but may apply depending on facts and circumstances.
- **Lock-up periods:** If staking rewards are subject to a lock-up and are non-transferable, income recognition is deferred until the lock-up ends and the taxpayer gains the ability to sell or transfer. (Rev. Rul. 2023-14, Fact Pattern)

**Sources:** Rev. Rul. 2023-14; Notice 2014-21 Q&A-8; cooley.com/news/insight/2023; bdo.com/insights/tax; taxbit.com/blogs/staking-rewards.

### Basis Tracking Methods

- **FIFO (First-In, First-Out):** Default method. If no specific identification is made, units are deemed sold in chronological order beginning with the earliest acquired. Per IRS FAQ and Notice 2025-07, FIFO is the mandatory default starting 2026 on a **wallet-by-wallet** basis.
- **Specific Identification:** Permitted if the taxpayer maintains adequate contemporaneous records identifying the specific units sold, including acquisition date, amount, and cost. For 2025 and later, identification must be made **before the trade is executed**, not retroactively.
- **Average Cost:** Not permitted for general cryptocurrency. Average basis is available only for tokenized securities meeting the requirements of IRC Section 1012(c) or (d). Using average cost for standard crypto is non-compliant.
- **Wallet-by-wallet tracking (2025+):** Starting with 2025 dispositions, basis must be tracked per wallet/exchange account, not universally across all holdings. Different wallets may use different methods, but each wallet must use its method consistently for the entire tax year.

## Form 1099-DA and Reporting Requirements

### Form 1099-DA (New for 2025)

- **Effective date:** Custodial brokers must file Form 1099-DA for digital asset transactions occurring on or after January 1, 2025.
- **Tax year 2025:** Brokers report **gross proceeds only**. Cost basis is NOT required for 2025.
- **Tax year 2026:** Brokers must also report **cost basis** for covered securities (digital assets acquired and held with the same broker after 2025), using wallet-by-wallet FIFO unless the taxpayer elects specific identification.
- **Deadline:** Forms 1099-DA for 2025 must be furnished to taxpayers by February 17, 2026, and e-filed with the IRS by March 31, 2026.
- **DeFi / non-custodial platforms:** NOT treated as brokers. Congress and President Trump in 2025 ordered Treasury to repeal the Biden-era DeFi broker regulations. Form 1099-DA reporting is limited to custodial brokers only.
- **Penalty relief:** Notice 2024-56 provides transition relief for 2025 -- no penalties for good-faith compliance efforts.
- **Stablecoin reporting:** Brokers may elect an aggregate reporting method for "qualifying stablecoins" on Form 1099-DA if aggregate designated sales exceed $10,000 annually.

### Form 8949 and Schedule D

All capital gains and losses from crypto must be reported on Form 8949, then summarized on Schedule D (Form 1040). This requirement exists regardless of whether a Form 1099-DA was received. Taxpayers using non-custodial wallets, foreign exchanges, or DeFi platforms must self-report all transactions.

### Form 1040 Digital Asset Question

All taxpayers filing Forms 1040, 1040-SR, 1065, 1120S, 1120, and 709 must answer a yes/no question about digital asset transactions. The question asks whether the taxpayer received, sold, exchanged, or otherwise disposed of any digital asset during the tax year. **This question must be answered even if no Form 1099-DA was received.** Checking "No" when the answer is "Yes" exposes the taxpayer to accuracy-related penalties.

Answer "Yes" if you: received crypto as payment, sold crypto, exchanged one crypto for another, received mining/staking rewards, received an airdrop, or otherwise disposed of a digital asset.

Answer "No" only if you: merely held digital assets without any transactions, or only purchased crypto with fiat and did not dispose of any.

## OBBBA Changes

### One Big Beautiful Bill Act (Pub. L. No. 119-27, July 18, 2025)

The OBBBA, signed into law on July 4, 2025, did **not** include a cryptocurrency-specific de minimis exclusion. Senator Cynthia Lummis (R-WY) proposed a **$300 per-transaction de minimis exclusion** for personal-use crypto transactions (with a $5,000 annual cap) during reconciliation, but the provision failed to secure sufficient support and was not included in the final bill.

**Current status (as of March 2026):**

- **No enacted de minimis exclusion** for cryptocurrency personal-use transactions exists in federal law.
- Hours after the OBBBA was signed, Senator Lummis filed a standalone bill proposing the $300 per-transaction / $5,000 annual cap threshold. The Joint Committee on Taxation scored it as revenue-positive (~$600 million over 10 years). The bill remains pending.
- A separate bipartisan discussion draft (Representatives Miller and Horsford) proposed a de minimis provision limited to **payment stablecoins only**, excluding Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies.
- The House Ways and Means Committee held a hearing on digital asset tax policy on July 16, 2025. The Senate Finance Committee held a hearing in October 2025. Senator Daines has targeted August 2026 for legislation.

**What the OBBBA did change (non-crypto):** The OBBBA raised the general Form 1099 reporting threshold under Section 6041 from $600 to $2,000 for payments made after December 31, 2025, and modified the Section 6050W TPSO (third-party settlement organization) de minimis threshold retroactively.

**Bottom line:** Every crypto transaction, regardless of dollar amount, remains a reportable taxable event. There is no de minimis safe harbor for personal-use crypto purchases under current law.

**Sources:** btcpolicy.org/articles/the-bitcoin-de-minimis-tax-exemption; bdo.com/insights/tax/congress-working-to-reform-tax-treatment-of-digital-assets; IRS Bulletin 2025-48 (irs.gov/pub/irs-irbs/irb25-48.pdf); klgates.com.

## Agent Rules

1. **Every crypto trade is a taxable event.** If the taxpayer cannot produce cost basis records, use FIFO as default and document the limitation. Note the inability to substantiate basis as a risk factor on the return.

2. **Always check the Form 1040 digital asset question.** If the taxpayer held, received, or disposed of any digital asset, the answer must be "Yes." Failure to answer correctly is an audit flag.

3. **Mining and staking income is ordinary income.** Do not treat it as capital gain. Basis in received units equals the income recognized.

4. **Do not use average cost for crypto.** Only FIFO and specific identification are IRS-approved methods. If a taxpayer's records show average cost, recompute using FIFO.

5. **Track basis per wallet/account starting 2025.** Universal basis pooling is no longer permitted. Each wallet or exchange account is a separate basis pool.

6. **Self-report all non-custodial and DeFi transactions.** No Form 1099-DA will be issued for these. The taxpayer is still required to report on Form 8949.

7. **No wash sale rule applies to crypto (as of March 2026).** The taxpayer may sell at a loss and immediately repurchase the same token without disallowance under IRC Section 1091. However, document this position and warn the taxpayer that legislation may change this treatment retroactively.

8. **No de minimis exclusion exists.** Do not exclude any crypto transaction from reporting based on dollar amount, regardless of pending legislation.

## Common Traps

1. **Crypto-to-crypto swaps treated as non-taxable.** Taxpayers frequently assume trading one cryptocurrency for another (including stablecoin conversions) is not taxable. It is. Every swap is a disposition of the first asset.

2. **Ignoring staking and airdrop income.** Rewards received through staking, mining, airdrops, and hard forks are ordinary income at FMV on date of receipt, even if the taxpayer did not sell the received tokens.

3. **Using average cost or LIFO.** These methods are not approved for general cryptocurrency. Crypto tax software may default to HIFO or average cost -- verify the method before filing.

4. **Failing to answer the Form 1040 digital asset question.** This is a required question on the return. Leaving it blank or answering "No" incorrectly invites IRS scrutiny.

5. **Not reporting because no 1099 was received.** The obligation to report exists regardless of whether a Form 1099-DA was issued. Non-custodial wallet and DeFi transactions are self-reported.

6. **Assuming wash sale rules apply.** Some taxpayers or preparers mistakenly apply wash sale disallowance to crypto. Under current law (IRC Section 1091), the rule applies only to stock and securities, not property. Crypto losses are currently deductible even with immediate repurchase. This may change with future legislation.

7. **Inconsistent basis methods across the year.** For 2025 and later, a single wallet or account must use the same basis method for the entire tax year. Switching methods mid-year within the same wallet is non-compliant.

8. **Ignoring self-employment tax on mining.** If mining constitutes a trade or business, the income is subject to self-employment tax (Schedule SE), not just income tax.

9. **Overlooking foreign exchange reporting.** Crypto held on foreign exchanges may trigger FBAR (FinCEN 114) and Form 8938 (FATCA) reporting obligations if thresholds are met. This is an evolving area -- FinCEN has indicated virtual currency held in foreign accounts may be reportable.
