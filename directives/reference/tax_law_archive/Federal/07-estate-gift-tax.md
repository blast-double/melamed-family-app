# Estate and Gift Tax (Unified Transfer Tax System)
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 2001-2210 (estate tax), 2501-2524 (gift tax), 2601-2664 (GST tax)
**Relevant Forms:** Form 706 (estate tax), Form 709 (gift tax)
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

The federal transfer tax system imposes taxes on the gratuitous movement of wealth, whether during life (gift tax), at death (estate tax), or to beneficiaries two or more generations below the transferor (generation-skipping transfer tax). These three taxes operate as a unified regime with a single combined exemption amount (called the "basic exclusion amount" or "unified credit") and a flat top rate of 40%. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed into law on July 4, 2025, permanently increased the basic exclusion amount to $15,000,000 per individual effective January 1, 2026, eliminating the sunset that had been scheduled under the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act.

**Sources:** Wiss, "Estate Tax Exemption 2026: How OBBBA Changes Require 2025 Planning Today" (Oct. 2025), https://wiss.com/estate-tax-exemption-2026/; Congressional Research Service, "The Estate and Gift Tax: An Overview," https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R48183; Pollock Firm, "Permanent Federal Estate Tax Exemption Under New Law" (Jul. 2025), https://pollockfirm.com/permanent-federal-estate-tax-exemption-obbba.

## Rules

### Unified Credit / Basic Exclusion Amount (Section 2010)

Every individual receives a basic exclusion amount that shelters cumulative lifetime gifts and bequests at death from transfer tax. The same exemption pool applies to both the gift tax and the estate tax -- taxable gifts made during life reduce the exemption available at death.

- **2025:** $13,990,000 per individual ($27,980,000 for married couples).
- **2026 (OBBBA):** $15,000,000 per individual ($30,000,000 for married couples), indexed for inflation in future years.

The OBBBA increase is permanent with no scheduled sunset. Under prior law (TCJA), the exemption would have reverted to approximately $7,000,000 (inflation-adjusted from the pre-TCJA $5,000,000 base) on January 1, 2026.

### Estate Tax (Section 2001)

The estate tax is imposed on the taxable estate of a decedent who is a U.S. citizen or resident. The taxable estate equals the gross estate (all property in which the decedent had an interest at death) minus allowable deductions (debts, expenses, marital deduction, charitable deduction). The tax is computed on the sum of the taxable estate plus adjusted taxable gifts, with a credit for gift tax previously paid and the unified credit.

### Gift Tax (Sections 2501-2524)

The gift tax applies to transfers of property by gift during the donor's lifetime. Two exclusions reduce the tax burden:

1. **Annual exclusion (Section 2503(b)):** $19,000 per recipient for both 2025 and 2026. No gift tax return required for gifts at or below this amount. Married couples may elect gift splitting on Form 709 to effectively give $38,000 per recipient.
2. **Lifetime exemption:** Shared with the estate tax exemption ($13,990,000 in 2025; $15,000,000 in 2026). Gifts exceeding the annual exclusion reduce the lifetime exemption; actual tax is owed only after the lifetime exemption is exhausted.

Gifts to a non-U.S.-citizen spouse are limited to $190,000 (2025) / $194,000 (2026) annually, rather than qualifying for the unlimited marital deduction.

**Source:** Morgan Lewis, "IRS Announces Increased Gift and Estate Tax Exemption Amounts for 2026" (Oct. 2025), https://www.morganlewis.com/pubs/2025/10/irs-announces-increased-gift-and-estate-tax-exemption-amounts-for-2026; Kiplinger, "Gift Tax Exclusion 2026," https://www.kiplinger.com/taxes/gift-tax-exclusion.

### Form 709 Filing

Form 709 (United States Gift and Generation-Skipping Transfer Tax Return) must be filed whenever a donor makes gifts to any single recipient exceeding the annual exclusion ($19,000), elects gift splitting with a spouse, or makes gifts of future interests regardless of amount. The return is due April 15 of the year following the gift (extensions to October 15 available but do not extend time to pay). Both spouses must file Form 709 when electing gift splitting, even if only one spouse made the gift.

### Marital Deduction (Section 2056)

An unlimited deduction is allowed for property passing from a decedent to a surviving spouse who is a U.S. citizen. This applies to outright bequests and to qualifying trust arrangements. A Qualified Terminable Interest Property (QTIP) trust under Section 2056(b)(7) qualifies for the marital deduction if: (a) the surviving spouse receives all trust income at least annually, and (b) the executor elects QTIP treatment on Form 706. At the surviving spouse's later death, the QTIP trust assets are included in the survivor's gross estate under Section 2044.

For a surviving spouse who is not a U.S. citizen, the marital deduction is available only through a Qualified Domestic Trust (QDOT) under Section 2056A. Without a QDOT, no marital deduction is allowed regardless of the amount.

### Charitable Deduction (Section 2055)

An unlimited estate tax deduction is allowed for property passing to qualifying charitable organizations. No cap applies. The deduction covers outright bequests and interests in charitable remainder trusts, charitable lead trusts, and pooled income funds.

### Portability of DSUE (Section 2010(c)(4))

A surviving spouse may use the Deceased Spousal Unused Exclusion (DSUE) amount -- the unused portion of the deceased spouse's basic exclusion amount. The portability election must be made on a timely filed Form 706 for the deceased spouse's estate, even if the estate is not otherwise required to file.

**Rev. Proc. 2022-32** provides a simplified method for late portability elections: the executor may file a complete Form 706 on or before the fifth anniversary of the decedent's date of death, noting at the top: "FILED PURSUANT TO REV. PROC. 2022-32 TO ELECT PORTABILITY UNDER Section 2010(c)(5)(A)." This relief is available only when the estate was not otherwise required to file Form 706. No user fee is required.

**Source:** Dentons, "Revenue Procedure 2022-32 Extends Time Period to Elect Portability" (Jul. 2022), https://www.dentons.com/en/insights/articles/2022/july/18/revenue-procedure; IRS, Rev. Proc. 2022-32, https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/rp-22-32.pdf.

### Generation-Skipping Transfer Tax (Sections 2601-2664)

A separate 40% tax applies to transfers that skip a generation (e.g., grandparent to grandchild). Each individual receives a GST exemption equal to the basic exclusion amount:

- **2025:** $13,990,000
- **2026:** $15,000,000

Unlike the estate tax exemption, the GST exemption is **not portable** between spouses. Each spouse must independently allocate their own GST exemption. Failure to properly allocate GST exemption to a trust can result in the trust being fully subject to the 40% GST tax on future distributions and terminations.

The GST tax applies in three contexts: (1) direct skips (outright transfers to skip persons), (2) taxable distributions (distributions from a trust to skip persons), and (3) taxable terminations (when a trust interest terminates and only skip persons remain as beneficiaries).

**Source:** Spencer Fane, "Generation-Skipping What? An Overview of the GST Tax" (Dec. 2025), https://www.spencerfane.com/insight/generation-skipping-what-an-overview-of-the-gst-tax-and-strategies-to-mitigate-exposure/; Harris Beach Murtha, "2026 Adjustments to Estate & Gift Tax," https://www.harrisbeachmurtha.com/insights/2026-adjustments-to-estate-gift-generation-skipping-transfer-tax-and-income-tax-brackets/.

### Special Use Valuation (Section 2032A)

Qualifying farm or closely held business real property may be valued based on its actual use rather than highest-and-best-use fair market value. The maximum reduction from FMV is:

- **2024:** $1,390,000
- **2025:** $1,420,000
- **2026:** $1,460,000

Requirements include: (a) the decedent was a U.S. citizen or resident; (b) the qualifying real and personal property constitutes at least 50% of the adjusted gross estate; (c) the real property alone constitutes at least 25% of the adjusted gross estate; (d) the property was owned and used for farming/business purposes for 5 of the 8 years preceding death; and (e) the decedent or a family member materially participated in the operation. A 10-year recapture period applies: if the property is sold to a non-family member or ceases qualifying use within 10 years of death, the tax savings are recaptured with interest.

**Source:** Iowa State CALT, "Estate and Gift Tax Rules," https://www.calt.iastate.edu/files/inline-files/chapter_8.pdf; Washington DOR, "Estate tax special-use valuation per IRC Section 2032A," https://dor.wa.gov/taxes-rates/other-taxes/estate-tax/estate-tax-special-use-valuation-irc-ss2032a.

### Installment Payment of Estate Tax on Closely Held Business (Section 6166)

Estates in which a closely held business interest exceeds 35% of the adjusted gross estate may elect to pay the estate tax attributable to that business over up to 14 years. The structure is:

- **Years 1-4:** Interest-only payments (beginning one year after the estate tax return due date).
- **Year 5:** First principal installment plus interest.
- **Years 6-14:** Remaining nine equal annual installments of principal plus interest.

A special 2% interest rate applies to the estate tax deferred on the first $1,000,000 in taxable value (indexed; approximately $1,820,000 in 2025). The IRS may require a special lien on Section 6166 property or a bond to secure payment.

**Source:** Wells Fargo, "Estate Taxes on a Closely Held Business Under IRC 6166," https://conversations.wf.com/deferring_estate_taxes_on_a_closely_held_business_with_irc_6166/; Private Wealth Insights, "Preserving Your Legacy: Estate Tax Deferral" (Aug. 2024), https://www.privatewealthinsights.com/2024/08/preserving-your-legacy-estate-tax-deferral-for-closely-held-businesses/.

## OBBBA Changes

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (P.L. 119-21), signed July 4, 2025, made the following changes to the transfer tax system:

1. **Permanent $15,000,000 exemption.** The basic exclusion amount is set at $15,000,000 per individual effective January 1, 2026, with inflation indexing in future years. This replaces the TCJA's temporary doubling, which was scheduled to sunset after December 31, 2025, reverting the exemption to approximately $7,000,000.
2. **GST exemption aligned.** The GST exemption also increases to $15,000,000, matching the estate/gift exemption.
3. **No rate change.** The 40% top rate on estate, gift, and GST taxes is unchanged.
4. **No change to annual exclusion.** The annual gift exclusion remains $19,000 for 2026 (same as 2025), set by existing inflation adjustment mechanisms.
5. **Portability rules unchanged.** DSUE portability continues to operate under existing rules; no modifications were made by OBBBA.
6. **Planning shift.** With the sunset eliminated, the urgency to make large lifetime gifts before 2026 is removed. The focus shifts toward basis step-up planning (retaining appreciated assets until death) rather than accelerated gifting.

**Source:** Saiber, "OBBBA Changes Create New Tax Planning Opportunities" (Dec. 2025), https://www.saiber.com/insights/publications/2025-12-17-obbba-changes-create-new-tax-planning-opportunities; Pierro Law, "The OBBBA and its Effect on Estate Planning," https://www.pierrolaw.com/estate-planning/one-big-beautiful-bill-act-and-its-effect-on-estate-planning/; Citizens Bank, "Estate Tax and Gift Tax Exemption," https://www.citizensbank.com/private-banking/insights/estate-tax-exemption.aspx.

## Agent Rules

1. **Use exact figures for the relevant tax year.** Always confirm whether a transfer or death occurs in 2025 (exemption $13,990,000) or 2026+ (exemption $15,000,000) before calculating tax exposure.
2. **Annual exclusion gifts do not require Form 709** unless gift splitting is elected. Gifts to multiple recipients of $19,000 or less each do not trigger filing.
3. **Form 709 is always required for gift splitting**, even if the total gifts are below the annual exclusion threshold after splitting.
4. **GST exemption is not portable.** Never assume a surviving spouse inherits unused GST exemption. Each spouse must allocate independently.
5. **DSUE requires affirmative election.** Portability is not automatic. A timely Form 706 must be filed for the deceased spouse's estate. Check Rev. Proc. 2022-32 for late elections (5-year window from death).
6. **Non-citizen spouse rules.** The unlimited marital deduction does not apply unless a QDOT is established. Use the special annual gift exclusion ($190,000 in 2025; $194,000 in 2026) for lifetime transfers. **This is directly relevant to Aaron's situation with Eugenia (Mexican national).**
7. **Section 2032A and Section 6166** are relevant only for estates containing qualifying farm or closely held business real property. Verify the 50%/25%/35% thresholds respectively before considering these elections.
8. **State estate taxes operate independently.** Many states have lower exemptions (e.g., New York at $7,160,000) and do not recognize portability. Always check domicile-state rules.

## Common Traps

1. **Failing to file Form 706 for portability.** If the first spouse's estate is below the filing threshold, no Form 706 is required by law -- but the DSUE election is lost forever unless a return is filed. Executors frequently overlook this, forfeiting millions in transferable exemption. Rev. Proc. 2022-32 provides relief up to 5 years after death, but only if no return was previously required.
2. **"Last deceased spouse" rule.** The DSUE amount comes only from the last deceased spouse. If a surviving spouse remarries and the new spouse dies with zero unused exemption, the DSUE from the first spouse is lost entirely.
3. **Forgetting GST allocation.** Unlike the estate/gift exemption, GST exemption must be affirmatively allocated to trusts (either on Form 709 for lifetime transfers or Form 706 at death). Automatic allocation rules exist but have exceptions and can produce unintended results.
4. **Gift splitting without filing.** Both spouses must file Form 709 to elect gift splitting, even if only one spouse made gifts. Failure to file means no splitting, potentially triggering unexpected use of the gifting spouse's lifetime exemption.
5. **Non-citizen spouse trap.** Outright bequests to a non-citizen spouse do not qualify for the marital deduction. Without a QDOT, the full value is included in the taxable estate with no offsetting deduction. This can produce enormous, unexpected estate tax liability.
6. **Section 2032A recapture.** Heirs who dispose of specially valued property or cease qualified use within 10 years of death trigger recapture of the estate tax benefit, plus interest. This can create a surprise tax bill years after the estate was settled.
7. **Basis trade-off with gifting.** Gifted assets carry over the donor's basis (carryover basis), while inherited assets receive a stepped-up basis to FMV at death. With the OBBBA's permanent high exemption, retaining appreciated assets until death may save more in capital gains tax than gifting saves in estate tax.
8. **Section 6166 acceleration events.** The IRS can accelerate the entire deferred tax balance if more than 50% of the business interest is disposed of, or if installment payments are missed.
