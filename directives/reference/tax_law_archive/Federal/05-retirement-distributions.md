# Retirement Distributions: Early Withdrawals, RMDs, Inherited IRAs, and Roth Conversions
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 72, 401(a)(9), 4974; SECURE Act of 2019; SECURE 2.0 Act of 2022
**Relevant Forms:** Form 1099-R, Form 5329, Form 8606
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

Retirement account distributions are governed by an interconnected web of Code provisions that control *when* funds must or may be withdrawn and *how* those withdrawals are taxed. The major pillars are:

1. **Section 72(t)** -- imposes a 10% additional tax on distributions taken before age 59 1/2, subject to enumerated exceptions.
2. **Section 401(a)(9)** -- mandates required minimum distributions (RMDs) beginning at a specified age, ensuring tax-deferred funds are eventually taxed.
3. **Section 4974** -- imposes an excise tax on RMD shortfalls.
4. **SECURE Act (2019) and SECURE 2.0 Act (2022)** -- significantly restructured RMD starting ages, inherited IRA distribution rules, and penalty amounts.
5. **Roth conversion rules** -- govern the tax treatment when pre-tax retirement funds are converted to Roth accounts, including separate 5-year holding clocks.

Sources: IRC Sections 72, 401(a)(9), 4974; SECURE 2.0 Act of 2022 (Pub. L. No. 117-328, Div. T); IRS Notice 2022-6; IRS Final Regulations T.D. 10001 (July 2024).

## Rules

### 1. Early Withdrawal Penalty -- Section 72(t)

Distributions from qualified retirement plans and IRAs before age 59 1/2 are subject to a **10% additional tax** (on top of ordinary income tax) unless an exception applies. The following exceptions are recognized under Section 72(t)(2) and subsequent SECURE 2.0 amendments:

| Exception | Code Reference | Limit / Notes |
|-----------|---------------|---------------|
| Death | 72(t)(2)(A)(ii) | Distribution to beneficiary after account owner's death |
| Disability | 72(t)(2)(A)(iii) | Total and permanent disability per Section 72(m)(7) |
| Substantially Equal Periodic Payments (SEPP) | 72(t)(2)(A)(iv) | Must continue for 5 years or until age 59 1/2, whichever is later |
| Separation from service after age 55 | 72(t)(2)(A)(v) | Employer plans only (age 50 for public safety employees); does not apply to IRAs |
| Medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of AGI | 72(t)(2)(B) | Only the amount above the 7.5% floor |
| Health insurance premiums during unemployment | 72(t)(2)(D) | IRA only; must have received unemployment compensation for 12+ consecutive weeks |
| Qualified higher education expenses | 72(t)(2)(E) | IRA only; tuition, fees, books, supplies, room and board |
| First-time homebuyer | 72(t)(2)(F) | IRA only; lifetime cap of $10,000 |
| IRS levy | 72(t)(2)(A)(vii) | Distribution to satisfy an IRS levy on the account |
| Qualified reservist distribution | 72(t)(2)(G) | Called to active duty for 180+ days |
| Qualified Domestic Relations Order (QDRO) | 72(t)(2)(C) | Employer plans only; distribution to alternate payee under a QDRO |
| Qualified birth or adoption | 72(t)(2)(H) | Up to $5,000 per event; may be repaid within 3 years (SECURE Act 2019) |
| Terminal illness | 72(t)(2)(L) | Physician-certified illness expected to result in death within 84 months; effective for distributions after 12/29/2022 (SECURE 2.0 Section 326) |
| Emergency personal expenses | 72(t)(2)(M) | Up to the lesser of $1,000 or vested balance minus $1,000; one per year; effective 2024 (SECURE 2.0 Section 115) |
| Domestic abuse victim | 72(t)(2)(N) | Lesser of $10,000 (indexed) or 50% of vested balance; repayable within 3 years; effective 2024 (SECURE 2.0 Section 314) |
| Federally declared disaster | 72(t)(2)(O) | Up to $22,000 per disaster; income spreadable over 3 years; repayable within 3 years (SECURE 2.0 Section 331) |

Sources: IRC Section 72(t); SECURE 2.0 Act Sections 115, 314, 326, 331; IRS Notice 2024-55; IRS Notice 2024-2.

### 2. Substantially Equal Periodic Payments (SEPP) -- Section 72(t)(2)(A)(iv)

SEPP is the exception that allows ongoing penalty-free distributions before age 59 1/2 if the payments are part of a series of substantially equal periodic payments over the life expectancy of the account owner (or joint life expectancy with a beneficiary).

**Duration requirement:** Payments must continue for the longer of:
- **5 years**, or
- Until the account owner reaches **age 59 1/2**

**Three IRS-approved calculation methods** (per IRS Notice 2022-6 and Rev. Rul. 2002-62):

1. **Required Minimum Distribution (RMD) Method** -- Divide the account balance by the life expectancy factor from the applicable IRS table. Recalculated each year, producing variable payments. Yields the smallest annual amount.
2. **Fixed Amortization Method** -- Amortize the account balance over life expectancy using an IRS-approved interest rate (not exceeding 120% of the federal mid-term rate for either of the two months preceding the first distribution). Produces a fixed annual payment.
3. **Fixed Annuitization Method** -- Divide the account balance by an annuity factor derived from the IRS mortality table and an approved interest rate. Also produces a fixed annual payment.

**One-time method switch:** An individual using either fixed method may make a one-time, permanent switch to the RMD method in any subsequent year without triggering the modification penalty. No other changes are permitted.

**Modification penalty (Section 72(t)(4)):** If the SEPP series is modified before the end of the required period (the later of 5 years or age 59 1/2), the 10% additional tax is retroactively imposed on *all* prior distributions that were penalty-free under the SEPP exception, **plus interest** from the original due dates. Modifications include: changing the payment amount, adding to or withdrawing from the account outside the SEPP schedule, rolling over funds, or stopping payments early.

Sources: IRS Notice 2022-6; Rev. Rul. 2002-62; IRC Section 72(t)(4); IRS.gov, "Substantially Equal Periodic Payments."

### 3. Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) -- Section 401(a)(9)

**Starting age (SECURE 2.0 Section 107):**

| Birth Year | RMD Starting Age | First RMD Year |
|------------|-----------------|----------------|
| 1950 or earlier | 72 | Already in RMD status |
| 1951-1959 | 73 | 2024-2032 |
| 1960 or later | 75 | 2035 or later |

**Required Beginning Date (RBD):** April 1 of the year *following* the year the owner reaches RMD age. Delaying the first RMD to April 1 means taking two RMDs in that second calendar year (the delayed first-year RMD plus the current-year RMD), which can significantly increase taxable income.

**Calculation:** RMD = Prior year-end account balance / Uniform Lifetime Table distribution period factor for the owner's current age. If the sole beneficiary is a spouse more than 10 years younger, the Joint Life and Last Survivor Table is used instead (producing a smaller RMD).

**Key Uniform Lifetime Table factors (selected):**

| Age | Distribution Period |
|-----|-------------------|
| 73 | 26.5 |
| 75 | 24.6 |
| 80 | 20.2 |
| 85 | 16.0 |
| 90 | 12.2 |

**Accounts subject to RMDs:** Traditional IRAs, SEP IRAs, SIMPLE IRAs, 401(k), 403(b), 457(b), and other tax-deferred plans. Roth IRAs are NOT subject to lifetime RMDs for the original owner. SECURE 2.0 Section 325 also eliminated RMDs for designated Roth accounts in employer plans (effective 2024).

**Still-working exception:** Participants in employer plans (not IRAs) who are still employed by the plan sponsor and do not own more than 5% of the business may delay RMDs until retirement.

Sources: IRC Section 401(a)(9); SECURE 2.0 Act Sections 107, 325; IRS Uniform Lifetime Table (updated 2022); SmartAsset RMD Table (2026); Schwab.com, "Required Minimum Distributions" (2026).

### 4. RMD Penalty -- Section 4974

**Current penalty structure (SECURE 2.0 Section 302, effective 2023):**

| Scenario | Excise Tax Rate |
|----------|----------------|
| Pre-SECURE 2.0 (before 2023) | 50% of shortfall |
| Standard penalty (2023 forward) | **25%** of shortfall |
| Corrected within 2-year window | **10%** of shortfall |

The **correction window** runs from when the excise tax is imposed (the date the RMD was due) to the earliest of: (a) the IRS mailing a notice of deficiency, (b) the IRS assessing the tax, or (c) the last day of the second taxable year after the tax was imposed.

To qualify for the 10% reduced rate, the taxpayer must: (1) take the missed RMD amount during the correction window, and (2) file a return reflecting the 10% excise tax during that window.

The IRS may waive the penalty entirely if the shortfall was due to reasonable error and reasonable steps are taken to remedy it. Report on **Form 5329**.

**Statute of limitations (SECURE 2.0 Section 313):** For excise taxes on excess accumulations (Section 4974) related to IRAs, the SOL is 3 years from the filing due date of the individual income tax return (Form 1040) for the applicable year, or the actual filing date if later. Increased to 6 years if the excise tax omission exceeds 25% of the eventual penalty.

Sources: IRC Section 4974; SECURE 2.0 Act Sections 302, 313; Greenbush Financial Group, "Missed RMD? How to Fix It" (2025); Milliman, "Required Minimum Distributions After SECURE 2.0."

### 5. Inherited IRAs Post-SECURE Act -- The 10-Year Rule

The SECURE Act of 2019 eliminated the "stretch IRA" for most non-spouse beneficiaries inheriting from owners who died after December 31, 2019. The IRS issued **final regulations (T.D. 10001) on July 19, 2024**, clarifying application.

**Beneficiary categories and distribution rules:**

| Beneficiary Type | Distribution Rule |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Spouse** | Roll over to own IRA, treat as own, or use life expectancy stretch |
| **Minor child of the decedent** (eligible designated beneficiary) | Life expectancy stretch until majority, then 10-year rule applies |
| **Disabled individual** (eligible designated beneficiary) | Life expectancy stretch |
| **Chronically ill individual** (eligible designated beneficiary) | Life expectancy stretch |
| **Individual not more than 10 years younger than decedent** (eligible designated beneficiary) | Life expectancy stretch |
| **All other designated beneficiaries** (adult children, siblings, friends, etc.) | **10-year rule** -- entire balance must be distributed by December 31 of the year containing the 10th anniversary of the owner's death |
| **Non-person entities** (estates, charities, non-see-through trusts) | 5-year rule (if owner died before RBD) or life expectancy of decedent (if owner died on or after RBD) |

**Critical 2024 final regulation clarification -- annual RMDs within the 10-year window:**
- If the original owner **had already reached RMD age** (was on or past the RBD) at time of death: the non-spouse beneficiary must take **annual RMDs** during years 1 through 9, with the remaining balance distributed by the end of year 10.
- If the original owner **had not yet reached RMD age**: no annual RMDs are required during the 10-year window; the beneficiary simply must empty the account by the end of year 10.

**Transition relief:** The IRS waived penalties (via Notices 2022-53, 2023-54, and 2024-35) for beneficiaries who failed to take annual RMDs from inherited IRAs during 2021-2024 under the 10-year rule. Beginning in **2025**, annual RMDs are enforced for the first time under the final regulations.

**Year-of-death RMD (2025 final regulations):** If the original owner died before taking their full RMD for the year of death, the beneficiary must take the remaining RMD by the later of (a) the tax filing deadline for the beneficiary's taxable year beginning in the calendar year of death, or (b) the end of the following calendar year. Failure is automatically waived under the new regulations.

Sources: SECURE Act of 2019 Section 401; IRS Final Regulations T.D. 10001 (July 19, 2024); IRS Notices 2022-53, 2023-54, 2024-35; Grant Thornton, "Final RMD Rules" (2024); Fidelity, "Inherited IRA Withdrawals" (2025); Ascensus, "The 10-Year Rule Is Here to Stay" (2025).

### 6. Roth Conversions

A Roth conversion moves funds from a traditional IRA or qualified employer plan into a Roth IRA. The converted amount is included in **gross income as ordinary income** in the year of conversion. There is no 10% early withdrawal penalty on a direct conversion (the penalty applies only to distributions, and a conversion is not treated as a distribution for penalty purposes if done as a direct rollover or trustee-to-trustee transfer).

**Two 5-year rules apply to Roth IRAs:**

1. **Contributions 5-year rule (earnings):** To withdraw *earnings* tax-free, the Roth IRA must have been open and funded for at least 5 tax years (starting January 1 of the year of the first contribution to *any* Roth IRA), AND the owner must be 59 1/2 or older, disabled, deceased, or a first-time homebuyer ($10,000 limit).

2. **Conversion 5-year rule (penalty on converted amounts):** Each Roth conversion has its own separate 5-year clock. If the converted amount is withdrawn within 5 years of the conversion AND the owner is under age 59 1/2, the 10% early withdrawal penalty applies to the taxable portion of the conversion. The 5-year period starts January 1 of the tax year in which the conversion occurred.

**Roth IRA ordering rules for distributions:**
1. Regular contributions (always tax- and penalty-free)
2. Converted and rollover amounts, on a first-in-first-out (FIFO) basis -- taxable portion first, then non-taxable portion
3. Earnings (taxable and potentially penalized if not a qualified distribution)

**No income limit on conversions.** Unlike Roth IRA contributions, which phase out at higher income levels, there is no income restriction on converting to a Roth IRA.

**RMD interaction:** You cannot convert an RMD to a Roth. If you are required to take an RMD for the year, you must satisfy the RMD *first*, then convert additional amounts.

Sources: IRC Section 408A; Investopedia, "Roth IRA Conversion Rules" (2025); Fidelity, "Roth IRA 5-Year Rule" (2025); Wealth Enhancement Group, "Understanding the 5-Year Rule" (2025); Schwab.com (2026).

## OBBBA Changes

The **One Big Beautiful Bill Act of 2025** (OBBBA), signed July 4, 2025, did not directly alter RMD ages, the 10% early withdrawal penalty structure, or the inherited IRA 10-year rule. However, it affects retirement distribution planning in the following ways:

- **TCJA individual tax brackets made permanent:** The seven-bracket structure (10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, 37%) continues indefinitely, adjusted for inflation. This provides certainty for multi-year Roth conversion strategies -- lower brackets are locked in, making planned partial conversions more predictable.
- **SALT deduction cap increased to $40,000 for 2025** (phasing out at $500,000 MAGI). This temporary provision may reduce taxable income for some filers, creating additional bracket space for Roth conversions.
- **Estate and gift tax exemption raised to $15 million** (indexed for inflation starting 2026). This changes the calculus for wealthy taxpayers evaluating whether Roth conversions or stretch distributions benefit heirs.
- **"Trump Accounts" introduced:** New birth-based custodial IRAs with special rules. Subject to traditional IRA rules after age 18, including eventual RMD requirements.
- **No new early withdrawal exceptions** were added by the OBBBA.
- **Backdoor Roth conversions remain available.** No income limits on conversions were imposed.

Sources: OBBBA (Pub. L. No. 119-XXX, 2025); Highland Planning, "Roth IRA Conversions Under OBBBA" (2025); Liberty Group LLC, "Roth Conversions in 2025" (2025); FuturePlan, "2025 Tax Bill Key Changes for Savers" (2025).

## Agent Rules

1. **Never assume RMD age.** Always confirm birth year: 1951-1959 = age 73; 1960+ = age 75. People born 1950 or earlier are already well into RMD status.
2. **First-year RMD double-distribution trap.** If the owner delays the first RMD to April 1 of the following year, flag that two RMDs will be taxable in a single year. Model the income spike.
3. **Inherited IRA: always determine (a) year of death, (b) whether owner had reached RBD, and (c) beneficiary category** before advising on distribution schedule. The rules differ dramatically based on these three inputs.
4. **SEPP modifications are catastrophic.** Any deviation from the schedule triggers retroactive penalty plus interest on all prior SEPP distributions. Never recommend contributing to, rolling over, or withdrawing extra from an account with an active SEPP.
5. **Roth conversion ordering.** When modeling withdrawals, apply the ordering rules (contributions first, then conversions FIFO, then earnings). Each conversion has its own 5-year clock.
6. **RMD before conversion.** If the taxpayer is in RMD status, the year's RMD must be satisfied *before* any Roth conversion. The RMD itself cannot be converted.
7. **Use Form 5329 for penalty reporting.** Both the 10% early withdrawal penalty and the Section 4974 RMD excise tax are reported on Form 5329. If requesting a waiver for reasonable cause, attach a statement.
8. **Inherited IRA 2021-2024 transition relief.** Do not calculate penalties for missed annual RMDs from inherited IRAs during 2021-2024. The IRS waived those penalties. Enforcement begins in 2025.

## Common Traps

1. **Age 55 exception does not apply to IRAs.** Separation from service at age 55+ exempts distributions from employer plans only. IRA distributions before 59 1/2 still incur the 10% penalty unless another exception applies.
2. **SEPP account contamination.** Adding funds to or taking extra distributions from an IRA subject to a SEPP plan constitutes a modification. If an account holds both SEPP and non-SEPP assets, they must be clearly segregated into separate IRA accounts.
3. **10-year rule clock is not extended.** The IRS penalty waivers for 2021-2024 did not extend the 10-year distribution deadline. A beneficiary inheriting from a 2020 decedent must still fully distribute by December 31, 2030.
4. **Roth conversion in a high-income year.** Conversion income is ordinary income. A large conversion can trigger or increase: Medicare IRMAA surcharges (2-year lookback), phase-out of deductions, and net investment income tax exposure. Model total effective rate, not just marginal bracket.
5. **Year-of-death RMD belongs to the beneficiary.** If the decedent had not yet taken their full RMD for the year of death, the beneficiary must take it. This is separate from the beneficiary's own distribution obligations.
6. **Roth 5-year rule per conversion, not per account.** Each conversion starts a separate 5-year clock. A taxpayer who converts in multiple years has multiple clocks running simultaneously.
7. **QCD cannot be combined with Roth conversion.** A qualified charitable distribution satisfies the RMD but cannot be converted. Ensure the RMD strategy (QCD vs. standard distribution vs. conversion) is determined before year-end.
8. **Correcting an RMD shortfall requires action within 2 years.** To get the reduced 10% penalty rate, the missed amount must actually be distributed and the corrected return filed within the correction window. Simply filing Form 5329 alone is insufficient.
