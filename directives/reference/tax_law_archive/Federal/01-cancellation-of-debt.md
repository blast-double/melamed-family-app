# Cancellation of Debt (COD) Income
**IRC Section(s):** Section 61(a)(12), Section 108, Section 1001, Section 1017
**Relevant Forms:** Form 982, Form 1099-C, Schedule 1 (Form 1040)
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

When a creditor cancels, forgives, or discharges a debt for less than the amount owed, the debtor generally realizes income equal to the amount discharged. This is cancellation-of-debt (COD) income, includable in gross income under IRC Section 61(a)(12). The principle is straightforward: the debtor received value (the borrowed funds) and is no longer required to repay it, creating an accession to wealth.

COD income is **ordinary income** -- it is not capital gain, regardless of the character of the underlying asset that secured the debt.

A creditor that cancels $600 or more of debt must report the discharge to the IRS and the debtor on **Form 1099-C, Cancellation of Debt**. The debtor must include the cancelled amount in gross income on Schedule 1 (Form 1040), line 8c, unless an exclusion under Section 108 applies.

Sources: IRS Publication 4681 (2025); IRC Section 61(a)(12); The Tax Adviser, "A Primer on Cancellation-of-Debt Income and Exclusions" (April 2023).

---

## Rules

### General Rule: COD Income Is Taxable

Any discharge of indebtedness triggers gross income to the debtor equal to the excess of the debt over any amount paid in satisfaction of it. No distinction is made between business and personal debt for inclusion purposes -- all COD income is includable unless a specific exclusion or exception applies.

### Exclusions Under Section 108(a)(1)

Section 108 provides five statutory exclusions from COD income. When an exclusion applies, the debtor excludes the discharged amount from gross income but must generally reduce tax attributes as the price of exclusion.

**1. Bankruptcy Discharge (Section 108(a)(1)(A))**
The discharge must occur in a **Title 11 case** (Chapter 7 liquidation or Chapter 11 reorganization). The debtor must be under the jurisdiction of the bankruptcy court, and the discharge must be granted by the court or pursuant to a plan approved by the court. The bankruptcy exclusion is **unlimited** -- the full amount of COD income is excluded regardless of the debtor's solvency. Attribute reduction under Section 108(b) is required.

**2. Insolvency Exclusion (Section 108(a)(1)(B))**
The debtor must be insolvent **immediately before the discharge** -- meaning total liabilities exceed the fair market value of total assets. The exclusion is **limited to the extent of insolvency**. For example, if a debtor is insolvent by $50,000 and has $80,000 of COD income, only $50,000 is excluded; the remaining $30,000 is taxable. Insolvency is determined at the partner level for partnerships (Section 108(d)(6); Rev. Rul. 2012-14). Attribute reduction is required on the excluded portion.

**3. Qualified Farm Indebtedness (Section 108(a)(1)(C))**
Available to taxpayers engaged in the trade or business of farming. The debt must be owed to a qualified person (generally, someone actively and regularly engaged in lending). The exclusion is limited to the sum of the taxpayer's adjusted tax attributes plus the aggregate adjusted basis of qualified property. Attribute reduction is required.

**4. Qualified Real Property Business Indebtedness -- QRPBI (Section 108(a)(1)(D))**
Available only to taxpayers **other than C corporations**. The debt must have been incurred or assumed in connection with real property used in a trade or business and must be secured by such real property. The exclusion is limited to the excess (if any) of the outstanding principal of the debt over the FMV of the real property, further limited by the aggregate adjusted basis of all depreciable real property held by the taxpayer. This is an **elective** exclusion -- the taxpayer must affirmatively elect it on Form 982 (line 1d) filed with a timely return. The required reduction is to the **basis of depreciable real property only** (not the general attribute ordering).

**5. Qualified Principal Residence Indebtedness (Section 108(a)(1)(E))**
Applies to acquisition indebtedness on the taxpayer's principal residence discharged before January 1, 2026, or subject to an arrangement entered into and evidenced in writing before that date. This exclusion is capped at $2 million ($1 million if married filing separately). The basis of the principal residence is reduced (but not below zero) by the excluded amount.

### Ordering Rule for Multiple Exclusions

If more than one exclusion applies, Section 108(a)(2) establishes a priority: the bankruptcy exclusion is applied first, then the insolvency exclusion, then qualified farm indebtedness, then QRPBI.

Sources: IRC Section 108(a)(1)-(2); IRS Publication 4681 (2025), Chapter 1; Bradford Tax Institute, IRC Section 108 full text; Riveron, "Tax Impacts of Cancellation of Debt Income When Restructuring"; Forvis Mazars, "Troubled Debt Restructurings" (December 2025).

## Attribute Reduction (Section 108(b))

When COD income is excluded under the bankruptcy, insolvency, or qualified farm indebtedness exclusions, the taxpayer must reduce tax attributes in the following mandatory order (Section 108(b)(2)):

1. **Net operating losses (NOLs)** -- current-year loss first, then carryovers (oldest first). Dollar-for-dollar.
2. **General business credits** -- 33-1/3 cents per dollar excluded.
3. **Minimum tax credits** -- 33-1/3 cents per dollar excluded.
4. **Capital loss carryovers** -- current-year loss first, then carryovers (oldest first). Dollar-for-dollar.
5. **Basis of property** -- under Section 1017 rules. Dollar-for-dollar. Cannot reduce basis of exempt property for individual debtors (Section 1017(c)(1)).
6. **Passive activity loss and credit carryovers** -- 33-1/3 cents per dollar for credits; dollar-for-dollar for losses.
7. **Foreign tax credit carryovers** -- 33-1/3 cents per dollar excluded.

**Critical timing rule:** Attribute reduction occurs *after* determining the tax for the year of discharge (Section 108(b)(4)(A)). The taxpayer may still use NOLs or other attributes for the current tax year before reducing them.

**Section 108(b)(5) election:** The taxpayer may elect to reduce the basis of depreciable property *first*, before applying the standard ordering rules. This election is made on Form 982, Part II, line 4. It can be beneficial when the taxpayer has NOLs or credits that are more valuable to preserve.

If excluded COD income exceeds total available tax attributes, the excess is **permanently excluded** from income -- there is no deferred recognition of the remainder.

**Form 982, Part II** is used to report all attribute reductions. The form is attached to the federal income tax return for the year of discharge.

Sources: IRC Section 108(b)(1)-(5); Treas. Reg. Section 1.108-7(a); RSM US, "Attribute Reduction Rules for Stand-Alone C Corporations"; The Tax Adviser (April 2023); Bradford Tax Institute, IRC Section 108(b)(2) full text.

## Recourse vs. Nonrecourse Debt: Foreclosure Treatment

The tax consequences of a foreclosure or deed in lieu depend critically on whether the debt is recourse or nonrecourse.

### Nonrecourse Debt Foreclosure

A foreclosure on nonrecourse debt is treated as a **single transaction** -- a deemed sale under Section 1001. The amount realized equals the **full outstanding principal balance of the nonrecourse debt**, regardless of the property's FMV (Treas. Reg. Section 1.1001-2(a)(1), (c), Example 7). Gain or loss is the difference between the nonrecourse debt amount and the taxpayer's adjusted basis. There is **no COD income** in a nonrecourse foreclosure. The character of gain (ordinary vs. capital) depends on the nature of the property and depreciation recapture rules.

**Example:** Property with adjusted basis of $300,000, FMV of $450,000, nonrecourse debt of $500,000. Amount realized = $500,000 (full debt). Gain = $200,000 ($500,000 - $300,000). No COD income. The debtor's insolvency status is irrelevant.

### Recourse Debt Foreclosure

A foreclosure on recourse debt is a **bifurcated transaction** with two separate tax events:

1. **Section 1001 gain or loss on the deemed sale.** Amount realized equals the **lesser of FMV or the debt amount** (in practice, the FMV of the property). Gain or loss = FMV minus adjusted basis.

2. **COD income.** If the creditor forgives the excess debt (the amount by which the recourse debt exceeds FMV), the debtor has COD income on the forgiven excess. This COD income may be excluded under Section 108 if an exclusion applies.

**Example:** Property with adjusted basis of $300,000, FMV of $450,000, recourse debt of $500,000. Gain on deemed sale = $150,000 ($450,000 FMV - $300,000 basis). COD income = $50,000 ($500,000 debt - $450,000 FMV), assuming the excess is forgiven. The gain may be capital; the COD income is ordinary.

The distinction matters enormously for insolvent or bankrupt taxpayers: with recourse debt, the COD portion can potentially be excluded under Section 108, while with nonrecourse debt the entire amount is gain with no exclusion available.

Sources: Treas. Reg. Section 1.1001-2(a)(1)-(2), (c), Examples 7-8; Rev. Rul. 90-16, 1990-1 C.B. 12; CLA (CliftonLarsonAllen), "Hidden Tax Consequences of Real Estate Foreclosures"; Cadwalader, "Recourse vs. Non-Recourse Debt" (Section 12 materials); Smith Law, "A Primer on Cancellation of Debt and Related Partnership Matters."

## OBBBA Changes

The **One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA)**, enacted in 2025, made the following changes relevant to COD income and student loan forgiveness:

**Student Loan Forgiveness -- ARPA Expiration.** The American Rescue Plan Act (ARPA, 2021) excluded all student loan forgiveness from federal income tax through December 31, 2025. The OBBBA **did not extend** this broad exclusion. As a result, beginning January 1, 2026, student loan forgiveness under **income-driven repayment (IDR) plans** (SAVE, PAYE, IBR, ICR) is once again treated as taxable COD income. Borrowers receiving IDR forgiveness in 2026 or later will receive a Form 1099-C and must include the forgiven amount in gross income unless a Section 108 exclusion (e.g., insolvency) applies.

**Permanent Exclusion for Death/Disability Discharges.** The OBBBA **permanently extended** the exclusion for student loans discharged due to the borrower's death or total and permanent disability. This exclusion was originally enacted in the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (2017) with a sunset of December 31, 2025; it is now permanent.

**Still Tax-Free (Unaffected by OBBBA).** The following forms of student loan forgiveness remain excluded from gross income:
- Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF) and Temporary Expanded PSLF (TEPSLF) -- excluded under IRC Section 108(f)(1)
- Teacher Loan Forgiveness
- Closed School Discharges
- Borrower Defense to Repayment Discharges
- Total and Permanent Disability Discharges (now permanent under OBBBA)

**Employer Student Loan Repayment Assistance.** The OBBBA made permanent the provision (originally from the CARES Act) allowing employers to contribute up to $5,250 per year toward employee student loan payments on a tax-free basis, with the threshold indexed for inflation.

Sources: NASFAA, "Welcome to 2026: Some Student Loan Forgiveness Is Now Taxable" (January 2026); Thomson Reuters, "Changes Ahead for Taxpayers with Discharged Student Loan Debt" (November 2025); protectborrowers.org, "Memo: How OBBBA Will Raise Taxes" (2026); CNBC, "Student Loan Forgiveness Is Taxable Again" (January 2026); IRS Taxpayer Advocate, "What to Know about Student Loan Forgiveness and Your Taxes" (March 2026); H&R Block, "One Big Beautiful Bill Act Tax Impacts" (2026); Student Loan Planner, "4 Tax Updates for Student Loans in 2026."

## Agent Rules

When a debt cancellation event occurs, apply this decision tree:

**Step 1: Is the cancelled amount includable in gross income?**
- Check whether any **exception** applies first (e.g., purchase-price reduction under Section 108(e)(5), deductible-if-paid rule, gift, bequest).
- If no exception, the amount is presumptively taxable COD income under Section 61(a)(12).

**Step 2: Is a Section 108 exclusion available?**
- (A) Bankruptcy discharge? (Title 11 case -- unlimited exclusion)
- (B) Insolvent? (Compute: total liabilities vs. FMV of total assets immediately before discharge; exclude only to extent of insolvency)
- (C) Qualified farm indebtedness?
- (D) QRPBI? (Not available to C corps; elective; must file Form 982 with timely return)
- (E) Qualified principal residence indebtedness? (Discharge before 1/1/2026 or written arrangement before that date)

**Step 3: If excluded, what attributes must be reduced and in what order?**
- Apply the Section 108(b)(2) ordering rules (NOLs, general business credits at 33-1/3 cents, minimum tax credits at 33-1/3 cents, capital loss carryovers, basis, passive activity carryovers, foreign tax credits at 33-1/3 cents).
- Consider whether the Section 108(b)(5) election (basis of depreciable property first) is beneficial.
- For QRPBI: reduce basis of depreciable real property only (not the general ordering).

**Step 4: Is Form 982 required?**
- **Yes**, whenever any amount of COD income is excluded under Section 108. File Form 982 with the federal return for the year of discharge.
- Part I: check the applicable exclusion box and enter the excluded amount on line 2.
- Part II: report the attribute reductions.

**Step 5: For foreclosures, determine debt character.**
- Nonrecourse debt: single transaction, Section 1001 gain/loss only, no COD income, no Form 982.
- Recourse debt: bifurcated -- compute Section 1001 gain/loss (FMV vs. basis) and COD income (debt excess over FMV) separately. Apply Steps 1-4 to the COD portion.

## Common Traps

1. **Confusing nonrecourse and recourse treatment.** With nonrecourse debt, the full debt is the amount realized -- there is no COD income, and insolvency/bankruptcy exclusions are irrelevant. Getting the debt character wrong produces the wrong answer entirely.

2. **Insolvency miscalculation.** Insolvency must be measured **immediately before** the discharge. Include all liabilities (including contingent liabilities to the extent of their FMV) and all assets at FMV (including exempt assets like retirement accounts per IRS guidance in Publication 4681). Missing a liability or an asset can flip the result.

3. **Forgetting attribute reduction.** Excluding COD income is not free -- attributes must be reduced. Failing to file Form 982 or failing to reduce attributes can trigger IRS adjustments in future years when those attributes are used.

4. **Missing the QRPBI election.** The QRPBI exclusion is **elective** -- it requires checking line 1d on Form 982 filed with a timely return (including extensions). A late or amended return may not qualify.

5. **Student loan forgiveness after 2025.** With the ARPA exclusion expired, IDR plan forgiveness in 2026+ is taxable. Borrowers who assumed it would be tax-free face an unexpected "tax bomb." The insolvency exclusion under Section 108(a)(1)(B) may still apply if the borrower's liabilities exceed assets at the time of discharge. PSLF remains tax-free.

6. **Partnership-level vs. partner-level application.** For partnerships, the bankruptcy and insolvency exclusions are applied at the **partner level**, not the entity level (Section 108(d)(6)). Each partner must independently establish insolvency or bankruptcy status.

7. **Timing of attribute reduction.** Attributes are reduced *after* computing tax for the discharge year. A taxpayer can still use a current-year NOL to offset other income in the discharge year before the NOL is reduced. Misapplying this timing can result in overpayment.

8. **Recourse debt foreclosure -- forgetting the COD piece.** When a property is foreclosed on recourse debt, many taxpayers (and preparers) compute only the gain/loss on the deemed sale and forget the separate COD income arising from the forgiven excess debt.
