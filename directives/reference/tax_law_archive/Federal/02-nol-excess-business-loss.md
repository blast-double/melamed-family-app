# Net Operating Losses (NOLs) and Excess Business Loss Limitation
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 172, 461(l)
**Relevant Forms:** Form 172, Form 461, Form 1045, Form 1040 Schedule 1
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

NOLs and the excess business loss limitation form a layered system governing how noncorporate taxpayers use business losses. IRC Section 172 provides the core NOL framework -- indefinite carryforward, 80% taxable income cap per year. IRC Section 461(l) gates how much aggregate business loss can be claimed in a single year, converting the excess into an NOL carryforward. Together with Sections 465 (at-risk) and 469 (passive activity), these create a four-layer loss limitation stack applied in sequence.

The TCJA (2017) rewrote NOL rules and introduced Section 461(l). The CARES Act (2020) temporarily relaxed both for 2018-2020. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), enacted July 4, 2025, made Section 461(l) permanent and reset its inflation base, lowering thresholds beginning in 2026.

---

## Rules

### A. NOL Rules Under IRC Section 172

**Carryforward only (no carryback).** NOLs arising in tax years ending after 2020 may only be carried forward. There is no general carryback. The pre-TCJA 2-year carryback / 20-year carryforward regime is gone. The OBBBA retained these TCJA changes. [Source: IRS Instructions for Form 172 (Rev. Dec 2024); 26 USC 172(b)(1)]

**Indefinite carryforward period.** Post-2017 NOLs carry forward indefinitely until fully absorbed. There is no expiration. [Source: IRC 172(b)(1)(A)(ii); Porte Brown, "Demystifying the Tax Rules for Net Operating Losses"]

**80% of taxable income limitation.** Post-2017 NOL carryforwards may offset only 80% of taxable income in the carryforward year. Taxable income for this purpose is computed without regard to the NOL deduction itself, the Section 199A QBI deduction, or the Section 250 FDII deduction. [Source: IRC 172(a)(2); Bloomberg Tax, "How to Calculate Net Operating Loss for Corporations"]

**Farming loss exception (2-year carryback).** NOLs from a farming business (as defined under IRC 172(b)(1)(B)) may be carried back 2 years. Only the farming loss portion qualifies; nonfarm losses in the same year are carryforward-only. The 80% limitation does not apply to carryback years before 2021. Farmers may elect to waive the carryback under IRC 172(b)(3). [Source: IRS Instructions for Form 172; IRC 172(b)(1)(B); Pub. 225]

**CARES Act 5-year carryback (expired).** The CARES Act (March 2020) provided a special 5-year carryback for NOLs arising in tax years beginning in 2018, 2019, and 2020, and temporarily suspended the 80% limitation for those years. This provision is fully expired and does not apply to any NOL arising after December 31, 2020. [Source: CARES Act Section 2303; Thomson Reuters, "How the CARES Act Affected NOLs and Carrybacks"]

### B. NOL Computation

An NOL exists when allowable deductions exceed gross income for the year. However, several items are excluded from the NOL computation for individuals under IRC 172(d):

1. **Capital losses in excess of capital gains.** Only capital losses to the extent of capital gains are included. Net capital losses beyond capital gains are backed out.
   - Nonbusiness capital losses are deductible only up to nonbusiness capital gains.
   - Business capital losses are deductible only up to the sum of business capital gains plus any excess nonbusiness capital gains (after absorbing nonbusiness losses and excess nonbusiness deductions).
2. **NOL deduction from other years.** Any NOL carryforward or carryback from another year is excluded.
3. **Section 199A QBI deduction.** Removed from the computation.
4. **Section 250 FDII/GILTI deduction.** Removed from the computation.
5. **Nonbusiness deductions in excess of nonbusiness income.** Excess personal deductions (standard or itemized) over nonbusiness income cannot create or increase an NOL.

[Source: IRC 172(d); IRS Form 172 Instructions (Rev. Dec 2024); IRS Pub. 536]

### C. Excess Business Loss Limitation Under IRC Section 461(l)

**Scope.** Applies to all noncorporate taxpayers -- individuals, trusts, and estates. Does not apply to C corporations.

**Mechanism.** Aggregate business deductions are compared against aggregate business gross income/gains plus a threshold amount. If deductions exceed that sum, the excess is disallowed and converted to an NOL carryforward. [Source: IRC 461(l)(1)-(2)]

**Formula:**

```
Excess Business Loss = Total Business Deductions
                       - Total Business Gross Income & Gains
                       - Threshold Amount
```

If the result is positive, that amount is disallowed and treated as an NOL carryforward.

**Ordering rules.** Section 461(l) applies LAST in the loss limitation chain:

1. IRC 163(j) -- Business interest limitation
2. IRC 704(d) / 1366(d) -- Passthrough entity basis limitations
3. IRC 465 -- At-risk limitation
4. IRC 469 -- Passive activity loss limitation
5. **IRC 461(l) -- Excess business loss limitation**

[Source: IRC 461(l)(6); IRS 2025 Instructions for Form 461; KPMG, "Excess Business Losses -- One More Hurdle to Clear"]

**Wages excluded from business income.** W-2 wages are not considered "business income" for purposes of Section 461(l). The CARES Act clarified this: income from performing services as an employee is excluded from the computation. [Source: CARES Act Section 2303(b); Spidell, "Net Operating Losses"]

**Capital gains treatment.** Net capital gains from a trade or business are included in business income, but only the lesser of (a) capital gain net income solely attributable to trades or businesses, or (b) total capital gain net income. Capital losses from a business are included in business deductions. [Source: IRC 461(l)(3)(A)(ii); CARES Act Section 2303(b)]

| Tax Year | Single / HOH / MFS | Married Filing Jointly | Source |
|----------|---------------------|----------------------|--------|

The 2026 reduction from $313,000 to $256,000 (single) reflects the OBBBA's reset of the inflation-adjustment base year from 2017 to 2024, not a reduction in the statutory amount. ## OBBBA Changes

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (Pub. L. 119-21), enacted July 4, 2025, made the following changes relevant to NOLs and excess business losses:

1. **Section 461(l) made permanent.** The TCJA's excess business loss limitation was originally set to expire after December 31, 2028. The OBBBA removes the sunset, making it permanent for all tax years beginning after December 31, 2026. [Source: OBBBA Section 70602; Tax Executives Institute, "The One Big Beautiful Bill Act"; Barnes Dennig, "What the OBBBA Means for Excess Business Losses"]

2. **Inflation adjustment base reset.** The OBBBA reset the base year from 2017 to 2024, resulting in lower thresholds beginning in 2026 ($256,000 / $512,000 vs. $313,000 / $626,000 in 2025). [Source: Baker Tilly, "2025 Year-end Planning"; KPMG, "Passthroughs Tax Provisions in OBBBA"]

3. **Carryforward treatment preserved.** The enacted Senate version maintained the rule that excess business losses convert to NOL carryforwards and are NOT retested under Section 461(l) in subsequent years. The House version would have required retesting -- it was rejected. The excess becomes a standard NOL subject only to the 80% limitation. [Source: KPMG, "Passthroughs Tax Provisions in OBBBA"; Baker Tilly, "2025 Year-end Planning"]

4. **Section 172 largely unchanged.** The OBBBA retained the TCJA's NOL framework (no carryback, indefinite carryforward, 80% cap). [Source: 26 USC 172 as amended by Pub. L. 119-21, Section 70323]

## Agent Rules

1. **Apply loss limitations in order.** Always process losses through the full stack: basis (704(d)/1366(d)) -> at-risk (465) -> passive activity (469) -> excess business loss (461(l)) -> NOL carryforward rules (172). Skipping a layer produces incorrect results.

2. **Do not commingle business and nonbusiness items in the 461(l) computation.** W-2 wages are nonbusiness income. Only trade or business income (Schedule C, Schedule F, passthrough K-1 business income) counts as business income for the threshold test.

3. **When computing an NOL, back out capital losses exceeding capital gains.** An individual's NOL cannot include net capital losses. Nonbusiness capital losses offset only nonbusiness capital gains; business capital losses offset only business capital gains plus excess nonbusiness capital gains.

4. **Track NOL vintages.** Pre-2018 NOLs (if any remain) are not subject to the 80% limitation. Post-2017 NOLs are subject to the 80% cap. When both vintages exist, pre-2018 NOLs are applied first (they offset income dollar-for-dollar), and the 80% limitation applies only to the excess taxable income after absorbing pre-2018 NOLs.

5. **Farming loss carryback is the only remaining carryback.** Do not apply carryback rules to nonfarm losses for any year after 2020. The CARES Act 5-year carryback is expired and applies to no current-year losses.

6. **Use the correct 461(l) threshold for the tax year.** The thresholds change annually with inflation and dropped significantly for 2026 due to the OBBBA base-year reset. Always look up the applicable year's threshold from the relevant Revenue Procedure.

7. **NOL from excess business loss is not retested.** Once Section 461(l) converts an excess to an NOL carryforward, that carryforward is subject only to Section 172's 80% limitation in future years. It does not run through Section 461(l) again.

## Common Traps

### TRAP 1: The Three-Layer Limitation Stack

Section 461(l) is applied AFTER Section 469 passive activity rules. A loss that survives the passive activity limitation (e.g., because the taxpayer qualifies as a real estate professional and materially participates) is still subject to the excess business loss cap under Section 461(l). If the loss exceeds the threshold, the excess converts to an NOL carryforward -- which is then subject to the 80% of taxable income limitation under Section 172. This creates a three-layer limitation stack:

**Layer 1: Section 469** -- Loss must be nonpassive (or allowed under the $25,000 rental exception or RE professional rules)
**Layer 2: Section 461(l)** -- Surviving loss is capped at the threshold ($313,000 / $626,000 for 2025; $256,000 / $512,000 for 2026)
**Layer 3: Section 172** -- The excess becomes an NOL, deductible in future years at only 80% of taxable income

A taxpayer with a $700,000 business loss (MFJ, 2025) that fully survives Section 469 can deduct only $626,000 currently. The remaining $74,000 converts to an NOL. In the following year, that $74,000 NOL can offset only 80% of taxable income -- meaning with $74,000 of income, only $59,200 is offset, and the remaining $14,800 carries forward again.

### TRAP 2: 2026 Threshold Drop

The OBBBA reset drops the single-filer threshold from $313,000 (2025) to $256,000 (2026) -- a $57,000 reduction. For MFJ, it drops from $626,000 to $512,000 -- a $114,000 reduction. Taxpayers with large business losses straddling 2025-2026 should consider accelerating deductions into 2025 to use the higher threshold.

### TRAP 3: Capital Losses Do Not Create NOLs

Capital losses in excess of capital gains are excluded from the NOL computation. Net capital losses carry forward under Sections 1211/1212 as capital loss carryforwards -- NOT as NOLs. These are separate regimes with different rules and different forms.

### TRAP 4: NOL Deduction Cannot Reduce Taxable Income Below 20%

The 80% cap means a taxpayer always pays tax on at least 20% of current-year taxable income. With $500,000 of income and $2,000,000 of NOL carryforwards, only $400,000 is deductible. Tax is owed on $100,000.

### TRAP 5: W-2 Wages Are Not Business Income for 461(l)

W-2 wages cannot increase "business income" for the 461(l) computation. A taxpayer with $500,000 in wages and $800,000 in business losses measures the loss against only other trade or business income plus the threshold -- not the wages.
