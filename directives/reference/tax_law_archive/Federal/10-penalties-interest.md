# Penalties and Interest
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 6601, 6621, 6651, 6654, 6662, 6663, 6672, 6694
**Relevant Forms:** Form 1040, Form 2210, Form 843, Form 4868, Form 9465, Form 2751, Form 8275/8275-R
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

The IRS penalty and interest regime operates as a multi-layered enforcement system designed to incentivize timely filing, timely payment, accurate reporting, and proper withholding of trust fund taxes. Penalties range from administrative (failure to file/pay) to quasi-criminal (civil fraud at 75%). Interest on underpayments compounds daily and is not deductible for individuals. Understanding this framework is critical for tax planning, particularly for taxpayers with multiple income sources, estimated tax obligations, and business payroll responsibilities.

---

## Rules

### Failure to File (Section 6651(a)(1))

The penalty for failing to file a return by its due date (including extensions) is **5% of the unpaid tax per month** (or fraction thereof), up to a **maximum of 25%**. If both failure-to-file (FTF) and failure-to-pay (FTP) penalties apply in the same month, the FTF penalty is reduced by the FTP amount (0.5%), resulting in a net FTF rate of 4.5% per month for the first five months.

- **Minimum penalty**: If a return is filed more than 60 days late, the minimum penalty is the lesser of **$525** (for returns due after 12/31/2025) or 100% of the unpaid tax. For returns due in calendar year 2025, the minimum is $510. ([IRS, Failure to File Penalty](https://www.irs.gov/payments/failure-to-file-penalty))
- **Fraudulent failure to file**: If the failure is fraudulent, the penalty increases to **15% per month**, up to **75%** (Section 6651(f)).
- **Reasonable cause defense**: The penalty does not apply if the taxpayer shows the failure was due to reasonable cause and not willful neglect.
- **Extension protection**: Filing Form 4868 extends the filing deadline but does not extend the time to pay.

### Failure to Pay (Section 6651(a)(2))

The penalty for failing to pay tax shown on a return by the due date is **0.5% of the unpaid tax per month**, up to **25%**.

- **Installment agreement reduction**: The rate drops to **0.25% per month** while an approved installment agreement (Form 9465) is in effect. ([The Tax Adviser, IRS Penalties](https://www.thetaxadviser.com/issues/2024/jul/irs-penalties-abatements-and-other-relief/))
- **Notice and demand penalty** (Section 6651(a)(3)): If tax not shown on a return is assessed and not paid within 21 calendar days of notice and demand (10 business days if the amount equals or exceeds $100,000), the same 0.5%/month penalty applies.
- **Reasonable cause defense**: Available; taxpayer must demonstrate ordinary business care and prudence and inability to pay or that paying would cause undue hardship.

### Combined FTF + FTP Maximum

The theoretical combined maximum is **47.5%** of the unpaid tax: FTF at 22.5% (4.5% net x 5 months) plus FTP at 25% (0.5% x 50 months). After the FTF penalty maxes out at month 5, only FTP continues to accrue.

### Failure to Pay Estimated Tax (Section 6654)

This addition to tax applies when individuals fail to make sufficient quarterly estimated tax payments. It is computed as interest on the underpayment at the IRS underpayment rate (Section 6621) and reported on **Form 2210**.

**Four safe harbors** -- meeting any one avoids the penalty:

1. **Balance due under $1,000**: No penalty if total tax owed after withholding and credits is less than $1,000.
2. **90% of current year**: Payments equal at least 90% of the tax shown on the current year's return.
3. **100% of prior year**: Payments equal at least 100% of the tax shown on the prior year's return (the prior year must have been a 12-month year with a filed return).
4. **110% of prior year for high earners**: If prior-year AGI exceeds **$150,000** ($75,000 if married filing separately), the threshold is **110%** of prior-year tax instead of 100%.

([Fraim CPA, Safe Harbor Rule](https://fraimcpa.com/safe-harbor-rule-how-to-avoid-irs-penalties/); [IRS Form 2210 Instructions](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-pdf/i2210.pdf))

- **Farmers and fishers exception**: If at least two-thirds of gross income is from farming or fishing, substitute 66 2/3% for 90% in safe harbor #2.
- **Annualized income installment method** (Section 6654(d)(2)): Taxpayers with uneven income throughout the year may compute required installments based on income earned through each quarter, potentially reducing earlier installments.
- **Quarterly due dates**: April 15, June 15, September 15 of the tax year, and January 15 of the following year.
- **Withholding advantage**: Federal income tax withheld from wages is treated as paid evenly throughout the year, even if withheld late in the year, making increased W-2 withholding a strategic tool for penalty avoidance.

### Accuracy-Related Penalty (Section 6662)

A **20% penalty** on the portion of any underpayment attributable to specified behaviors:

1. **Negligence or disregard** (Section 6662(b)(1)): Failure to make a reasonable attempt to comply with the IRC, or careless, reckless, or intentional disregard of rules or regulations. A position with at least a "reasonable basis" is not treated as negligent.
2. **Substantial understatement** (Section 6662(b)(2)): An understatement exceeds the greater of **10% of the tax required to be shown** on the return or **$5,000**. For C corporations, the threshold is $10,000 or 10% of tax (or $10 million for large corporations).
3. **Substantial valuation misstatement** (Section 6662(b)(3)): Value or adjusted basis claimed is 150% or more of the correct amount. The penalty increases to **40%** for a "gross valuation misstatement" (200% or more).

([IRS, Accuracy-Related Penalty](https://www.irs.gov/payments/accuracy-related-penalty); [26 U.S.C. Section 6662](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?edition=1994&num=0&req=granuleid%3AUSC-1994-title26-section6662))

**Defenses to the accuracy-related penalty**:
- **Reasonable cause and good faith** (Section 6664(c)): The taxpayer acted in good faith and with reasonable cause for the position taken.
- **Adequate disclosure**: For negligence and substantial understatement components, adequate disclosure on Form 8275 or 8275-R with at least a "reasonable basis" can eliminate the penalty.
- **Substantial authority**: For substantial understatements, the penalty does not apply to the extent the position had substantial authority (weight-of-authorities test), even without disclosure.

**Note**: The Section 6662 accuracy-related penalty and the Section 6663 fraud penalty are mutually exclusive -- the IRS must choose one or the other for the same portion of underpayment.

### Economic Substance Penalty (Section 6662(b)(6))

A strict liability penalty for underpayments arising from transactions lacking economic substance under Section 7701(o):

- **20%** if the relevant facts are adequately disclosed (Form 8275/8275-R).
- **40%** if the transaction is a "nondisclosed noneconomic substance transaction."

**Critical distinction**: There is **no reasonable cause defense** for economic substance penalties. Sections 6664(c)(2) and (d)(2) explicitly preclude the reasonable cause and good faith exception. Outside legal opinions and internal analyses do not protect against the penalty. The substantial authority defense likewise does not apply. This is the only accuracy-related penalty with true strict liability. ([Baker McKenzie, Economic Substance Penalties](https://insightplus.bakermckenzie.com/bm/dispute-resolution/united-states-irs-signals-increased-assertion-of-the-economic-substance-doctrine-and-related-penalties-in-transfer-pricing-cases); [IRS Notice 2014-58](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-14-58.pdf))

Under Section 7701(o)(1), a transaction has economic substance only if it: (1) changes the taxpayer's economic position in a meaningful way apart from federal tax effects, and (2) the taxpayer has a substantial non-tax business purpose. Both prongs must be met (conjunctive test).

### Civil Fraud Penalty (Section 6663)

The most severe civil penalty: **75% of the portion of underpayment attributable to fraud**.

- **Burden of proof**: IRS must prove fraud by **clear and convincing evidence** -- a higher standard than preponderance of the evidence.
- **Presumption of fraud**: Once the IRS establishes that any portion of an underpayment is fraudulent, the **entire underpayment** is presumed fraudulent. The taxpayer must then prove by a preponderance of the evidence that specific portions are not attributable to fraud.
- **Joint returns**: The penalty applies only to the spouse whose fraud caused the underpayment (Section 6663(c)).
- **Statute of limitations**: Fraud **eliminates** the statute of limitations for tax assessment. Under Section 6501(c)(1), a false or fraudulent return filed with intent to evade tax may be assessed "at any time" -- there is no expiration. ([26 U.S.C. Section 6663](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/6663); [Meadows Collier, IRS SOL and Civil Fraud](https://www.meadowscollier.com/irs-statute-of-limitations-and-civil-fraud))
- **Badges of fraud**: Courts look to circumstantial indicators including understated income, inadequate records, failure to file, implausible explanations, concealment of assets, and dealing in cash.

### Trust Fund Recovery Penalty (Section 6672)

A **100% penalty** assessed personally against any "responsible person" who willfully fails to collect, account for, or pay over withheld employment taxes (trust fund taxes: federal income tax withholding, employee share of Social Security and Medicare).

- **Responsible person**: Anyone with significant control over the company's finances -- owners, officers, directors, CFOs, bookkeepers, or payroll administrators with signature authority.
- **Willfulness**: Does not require malicious intent. Voluntarily, consciously, and intentionally paying other creditors while knowing payroll taxes were due satisfies the standard. Reckless disregard also qualifies.
- **Scope**: Covers only the employee's share of withheld taxes, not the employer's matching FICA contributions.
- **Not dischargeable in bankruptcy**.
- **Multiple liable parties**: The IRS may assess the penalty against multiple responsible persons for the same liability.
- **Procedural protection**: The IRS must issue Letter 1153 proposing the penalty; the taxpayer has 60 days (75 days if outside the US) to appeal before assessment. ([IRS, TFRP](https://www.irs.gov/businesses/small-businesses-self-employed/employment-taxes-and-the-trust-fund-recovery-penalty-tfrp))

### Tax Preparer Penalties (Section 6694)

**Section 6694(a) -- Unreasonable positions**: Penalty of the greater of **$1,000 or 50% of the income** derived by the preparer for the return. Applies when a preparer takes a position that lacks substantial authority (or, for disclosed positions, lacks a reasonable basis). A "reasonable cause and good faith" defense is available.

**Section 6694(b) -- Willful or reckless conduct**: Penalty of the greater of **$5,000 or 75% of the income** derived by the preparer for the return. Applies to willful attempts to understate liability or reckless/intentional disregard of rules or regulations. No reasonable cause defense for this tier. ([IRS IRM 20.1.6, Preparer Penalties](https://www.irs.gov/irm/part20/irm_20-001-006); [Hive Tax AI, Preparer Penalties 2025](https://hivetax.ai/what-are-the-preparer-penalties-for-2025/))

### IRS Interest on Underpayments (Sections 6601, 6621)

- **Rate formula**: Federal short-term rate (rounded to nearest whole percent) **+ 3 percentage points**.
- **Compounding**: Daily compounding.
- **Current rates**:
  - Q1 2025 through Q1 2026: **7%** (based on 4% federal short-term rate).
  - Q2 2026: **6%** (based on 3% federal short-term rate).
  - Large corporate underpayments: federal short-term rate + 5% (9% in Q1 2026, 8% in Q2 2026).
- **Interest on penalties**: The IRS charges interest on unpaid penalties from the due date of the return.
- **Not deductible**: Interest on personal federal tax underpayments is **not deductible** for individuals (it is considered personal interest under Section 163(h)).
- **Rate set quarterly**: Published in Revenue Rulings. ([Rev. Rul. 2025-22](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/rr-25-22.pdf); [Rev. Rul. 2026-5, IRB 2026-8](https://www.irs.gov/irb/2026-08_IRB); [DOL Table of Underpayment Rates](https://www.dol.gov/agencies/ebsa/employers-and-advisers/plan-administration-and-compliance/correction-programs/vfcp/table-of-underpayment-rates))

## OBBBA Changes

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed July 4, 2025, introduced several penalty-related changes:

1. **Erroneous refund penalty expanded** (Section 6676): The 20% penalty for erroneous claims for refund or credit was expanded from "income tax" to "income or employment tax," effective for claims filed after July 4, 2025. This significantly broadens IRS enforcement authority for employment tax refund claims. ([IRS, Erroneous Claim for Refund](https://www.irs.gov/payments/erroneous-claim-for-refund-or-credit); [RSM, OBBBA Penalties](https://rsmus.com/insights/tax-alerts/2025/new-penalties-for-taxpayers-in-the-one-big-beautiful-bill-act.html))

2. **Increased ERC promoter penalties**: The OBBBA targets Employee Retention Credit (ERC) fraud with enhanced penalties for promoters of abusive ERC claims.

3. **Opportunity zone reporting penalties**: New penalties (up to $250,000) for qualified opportunity funds and businesses that fail to comply with expanded QOZ reporting requirements, effective for taxable years beginning after enactment.

4. **Trump Account penalties**: Penalties of $50 per missed filing for Section 6693 reporting and up to $1,000 for fraudulent elections related to newly created Trump Accounts, effective for tax years beginning after December 31, 2025.

5. **Information reporting transition relief**: For tax year 2025, the IRS is providing penalty relief for the new information reporting requirements for cash tips and qualified overtime compensation under the OBBBA (Notice 2025-62), recognizing that employers may not yet have systems in place. ([IRS, OBBBA Penalty Relief](https://www.irs.gov/newsroom/treasury-irs-provide-penalty-relief-for-tax-year-2025-for-information-reporting-on-tips-and-overtime-under-the-one-big-beautiful-bill))

6. **Estimated tax relief for qualified farmland sales**: Notice 2026-3 provides limited relief from estimated tax penalties under Section 6654 for additions to tax attributable to qualified sales of farmland property under Section 1062(a). ([IRS Notice 2026-3](https://www.irs.gov/pub/irs-drop/n-26-03.pdf))

## Agent Rules

1. **Always check all four estimated tax safe harbors** before concluding an underpayment penalty applies. The 110% prior-year threshold applies when prior-year AGI exceeds $150,000 ($75,000 MFS) -- this is based on prior-year AGI, not current-year.
2. **Distinguish between penalties and interest**: Penalties can potentially be abated through reasonable cause arguments; interest generally cannot (it accrues by operation of law).
3. **FTF vs. FTP interaction**: When both apply, always reduce the FTF by the FTP amount (0.5%) for each concurrent month. Never double-count.
4. **Economic substance penalties have no defense**: Do not suggest reasonable cause arguments or reliance on professional advice as a path to abate Section 6662(b)(6) penalties. This is strict liability by statute.
5. **Fraud penalty is mutually exclusive with accuracy-related**: The IRS cannot impose both Section 6662 and Section 6663 on the same portion of underpayment.
6. **Fraud tolls the statute indefinitely**: If fraud is at issue, the normal 3-year assessment period under Section 6501(a) does not apply. Alert the user to this exposure.
7. **TFRP is personal**: Section 6672 pierces the corporate veil. Any individual with control and knowledge is at risk, regardless of title. Not dischargeable in bankruptcy.
8. **Form 4868 does not extend payment deadline**: An extension to file is not an extension to pay. FTP penalties and interest begin accruing from the original due date.
9. **Installment agreement benefit**: Advise eligible taxpayers that the FTP rate drops from 0.5% to 0.25% per month during an approved installment agreement.
10. **Preparer penalties apply to both signing and nonsigning preparers**: Anyone who prepares a "substantial portion" of a return for compensation may be subject to Section 6694.

## Common Traps

1. **Assuming an extension eliminates penalties**: Form 4868 extends the filing deadline only. If tax is owed, failure-to-pay penalties and interest begin on the original due date. Taxpayers must estimate and pay tax due with the extension.
2. **Using the wrong AGI threshold for the 110% rule**: The $150,000 threshold is based on **prior-year** AGI, not current-year. Using current-year AGI to determine which safe harbor applies is a common error.
3. **Ignoring the quarterly timing of estimated taxes**: Meeting the annual safe harbor in total is not sufficient; each quarterly installment must satisfy the required amount as of its due date unless the annualized method is elected.
4. **Relying on professional advice for economic substance**: Unlike other accuracy-related penalties, obtaining a legal opinion or CPA analysis provides zero protection against the Section 6662(b)(6) penalty. This surprises taxpayers who believe reliance on professionals is always a defense.
5. **Treating the TFRP as a business problem**: The entire point of Section 6672 is personal liability. Business owners and officers who pay vendors before payroll taxes expose their personal assets, including after bankruptcy.
6. **Confusing negligence penalty threshold with substantial understatement**: Negligence has no dollar threshold -- any careless error can trigger it. Substantial understatement requires exceeding the greater of $5,000 or 10% of tax.
7. **Filing an amended return to "cure" fraud**: A completely truthful amended return does not purge fraud committed on an original return. The unlimited statute of limitations and fraud penalty remain in effect.
8. **Ignoring the minimum failure-to-file penalty**: Even if the tax owed is small, filing more than 60 days late triggers a minimum $525 penalty (2026) that can exceed the actual tax liability.
9. **Failing to request penalty abatement**: First-time penalty abatement (FTA) is available for taxpayers with a clean compliance history for the prior three years. This administrative waiver is often overlooked.
10. **Misunderstanding deductibility of IRS interest**: Individual taxpayers cannot deduct interest on federal tax underpayments. It is classified as nondeductible personal interest under Section 163(h).
