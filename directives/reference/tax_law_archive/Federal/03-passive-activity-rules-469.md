# Passive Activity Loss Rules
**IRC Section(s):** Section 469; Reg. Sections 1.469-1 through 1.469-11
**Relevant Forms:** Form 8582 (Passive Activity Loss Limitations), Form 8582-CR (Passive Activity Credit Limitations)
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

Section 469 limits the ability of individuals, estates, trusts, closely held C corporations, and personal service corporations to deduct losses from passive activities against non-passive income (wages, portfolio income, active business income). Enacted as part of the Tax Reform Act of 1986, the provision targets tax-shelter-driven loss deductions by requiring that passive losses offset only passive income. Excess losses are suspended and carried forward indefinitely until the taxpayer generates sufficient passive income or completely disposes of the activity in a fully taxable transaction.

The passive activity rules apply after the basis limitation (Section 704(d) for partnerships, Section 1366(d) for S corporations), the at-risk rules (Section 465), and before the excess business loss limitation (Section 461(l)). See IRS Publication 925 for ordering guidance.

**Two categories of passive activity:**

1. **Trade or business activity without material participation.** Any trade or business in which the taxpayer does not materially participate is passive. Income and losses flow through as passive regardless of the taxpayer's other involvement. (IRC Section 469(c)(1).)

2. **Rental activity.** Any activity where payments are principally for the use of tangible property is treated as passive regardless of the taxpayer's level of participation, subject to specific exceptions for real estate professionals and the $25,000 special allowance. (IRC Section 469(c)(2), (4).) Six exceptions to rental activity treatment exist under Reg. Section 1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii)(A)-(F), including activities where the average customer use period is seven days or less, or where extraordinary personal services are provided.

*Sources: 26 U.S.C. Section 469; IRS Publication 925 (2025); IRS Instructions for Form 8582 (2025).*

## Rules

### Material Participation Tests

A taxpayer materially participates in a trade or business activity if the taxpayer meets **any one** of seven tests under Temporary Regulation Section 1.469-5T(a). The tests are applied on an activity-by-activity basis for each taxable year:

| Test | Reg. Section | Requirement |
|------|-------------|-------------|
| **1. 500-Hour Test** | 1.469-5T(a)(1) | The individual participates in the activity for more than 500 hours during the taxable year. |
| **2. Substantially All Test** | 1.469-5T(a)(2) | The individual's participation constitutes substantially all of the participation in the activity by all individuals (including non-owners) for the year. |
| **3. 100-Hour / Not-Less-Than Test** | 1.469-5T(a)(3) | The individual participates for more than 100 hours during the year, and that participation is not less than any other individual's participation in the activity. |
| **4. Significant Participation Activity (SPA) Aggregation** | 1.469-5T(a)(4) | The activity is a significant participation activity (more than 100 hours but failing tests 1-3), and the individual's aggregate participation in all SPAs exceeds 500 hours. |
| **5. Five-of-Ten-Years Test** | 1.469-5T(a)(5) | The individual materially participated in the activity in any 5 of the 10 taxable years immediately preceding the current year (years need not be consecutive). |
| **6. Three-Year Personal Service Test** | 1.469-5T(a)(6) | The activity is a personal service activity, and the individual materially participated in it for any 3 prior taxable years (need not be consecutive or recent). |
| **7. Facts and Circumstances** | 1.469-5T(a)(7) | Based on all facts and circumstances, the individual participates on a regular, continuous, and substantial basis. However, participation of 100 hours or less during the year automatically fails this test. Additionally, no compensated manager may perform more management hours than the taxpayer. |

**Participation counting rules:** Only the taxpayer's own work counts. Work done as an investor (studying financials, monitoring activity in a non-managerial capacity) generally does not count unless the taxpayer is directly involved in day-to-day operations. A spouse's participation counts toward the taxpayer's hours under Section 469(h)(5). Contemporaneous records are not strictly required by statute, but the IRS and Tax Court heavily favor taxpayers who maintain contemporaneous logs. (See Reg. Section 1.469-5T(f)(4).)

*Sources: Treas. Reg. Section 1.469-5T(a)(1)-(7); GovInfo CFR Title 26 Vol. 6; CPA Journal, "Material Participation as Significant Participation Activity" (Dec. 2021).*

### Passive Loss Limitation and Carryforward

Passive activity losses may offset only passive activity income. The excess -- the passive activity loss (PAL) -- is suspended under Section 469(b) and carried forward indefinitely. Suspended losses retain their character (ordinary, capital, Section 1231) and are allocated ratably among the taxpayer's loss-generating passive activities. IRS Form 8582, Part VII is used to compute the ratable allocation of disallowed losses among activities.

Suspended losses can be utilized when the taxpayer:
- Generates net passive income in a future year from any passive activity
- Qualifies for the $25,000 special rental allowance (see below)
- Completely disposes of the activity in a fully taxable transaction

*Sources: IRC Section 469(b), (d); IRS Publication 925 (2025).*

### Suspended Loss Release on Disposition

Under Section 469(g)(1)(A), when a taxpayer disposes of an **entire interest** in a passive activity in a **fully taxable transaction** to an **unrelated party**, all current-year and suspended passive losses from that activity are released. Released losses offset income in the following order:

1. Net income or gain from the passive activity itself (including gain on the disposition)
2. Net income or gain from all other passive activities
3. Any other income (active, portfolio)

A gift, bequest, or installment sale does not qualify as a fully taxable disposition for full release. In an installment sale, suspended losses are released proportionally as payments are received. On death, suspended losses in excess of the step-up in basis are deductible on the decedent's final return. Related-party sales under Section 267(b) or 707(b)(1) do not trigger release; losses remain suspended until the related party disposes of the interest to an unrelated party. (IRC Section 469(g)(1)(B), (C).)

*Sources: IRC Section 469(g); The Tax Adviser, "Disposing of Passive Activities" (Apr. 2017); IRS Passive Activity Loss Audit Technique Guide.*

### Rental Activity Presumption and the Real Estate Professional Exception

All rental activities are treated as passive regardless of the taxpayer's participation, with one critical exception: **real estate professional status** under Section 469(c)(7).

To qualify as a real estate professional, a taxpayer must satisfy **both** tests during the taxable year:

1. **More-than-half test:** More than 50% of the personal services the taxpayer performs in all trades or businesses are performed in real property trades or businesses in which the taxpayer materially participates.
2. **750-hour test:** The taxpayer performs more than 750 hours of services in real property trades or businesses in which the taxpayer materially participates.

**Real property trades or businesses** are defined in Section 469(c)(7)(C) and include: development, redevelopment, construction, reconstruction, acquisition, conversion, rental, operation, management, leasing, and brokerage.

Qualifying as a real estate professional only removes the rental activity presumption. The taxpayer must **still** demonstrate material participation in each individual rental activity (using the seven tests above) to treat losses from that activity as non-passive. A taxpayer may elect under Section 469(c)(7)(A) to aggregate all rental real estate interests as a single activity for purposes of this material participation determination. This election, once made, applies to the current and all future years and is revocable only if facts and circumstances materially change.

**Joint filers:** Spouses cannot combine hours for the two real estate professional tests (the more-than-half and 750-hour tests), but they can combine hours for the material participation test. Personal services performed as an employee generally do not count unless the taxpayer is a 5% owner under Section 416(i)(1)(B).

*Sources: IRC Section 469(c)(7); Reg. Section 1.469-9; The Tax Adviser, "Navigating the Real Estate Professional Rules" (Mar. 2017); IRS Instructions for Form 8582 (2025).*

### $25,000 Special Rental Loss Allowance

Section 469(i) provides a special allowance permitting taxpayers who **actively participate** in a rental real estate activity to deduct up to $25,000 of rental losses against non-passive income. Active participation is a lower standard than material participation -- it requires bona fide involvement in management decisions such as approving tenants, setting rental terms, or authorizing repairs. Limited partners cannot meet the active participation standard.

**Phase-out:** The $25,000 allowance is reduced by $0.50 for every $1.00 of modified adjusted gross income (MAGI) above $100,000. It is completely eliminated at $150,000 MAGI.

| MAGI | Allowance |
|------|-----------|
| $100,000 or less | Full $25,000 |
| $125,000 | $12,500 |
| $150,000 or more | $0 |

**Married filing separately (living apart all year):** Maximum allowance is $12,500, phase-out begins at $50,000 MAGI, fully eliminated at $75,000. If the spouses lived together at any time during the year, the allowance is zero.

A special rule for rehabilitation investment credits and low-income housing credits allows a $25,000 equivalent credit offset regardless of AGI (Section 469(i)(3)).

*Sources: IRC Section 469(i); IRS Instructions for Form 8582 (2025); Cary Millar, PC, "Passive Activity Loss Limitations."*

### Grouping Elections

Reg. Section 1.469-4 permits taxpayers to group one or more trade or business activities, or one or more rental activities, into a single activity if they constitute an **appropriate economic unit**. The five factors considered are:

1. Similarities and differences in types of trades or businesses
2. Extent of common control
3. Extent of common ownership
4. Geographical location
5. Interdependencies between or among the activities

**Limitation on rental/business grouping:** A rental activity may not be grouped with a trade or business activity unless (a) the rental activity is insubstantial relative to the business, (b) the business activity is insubstantial relative to the rental, or (c) each owner has the same proportionate ownership in both. (Reg. Section 1.469-4(d)(1)(i).)

**Permanence:** Once a grouping is established (including treating activities as separate, which is itself a grouping choice), the taxpayer must continue using that grouping unless a material change in facts and circumstances renders it clearly inappropriate. (Reg. Section 1.469-4(e).) The election must be made on the first return that reports the activities -- failing to group on the initial return is treated as electing separate treatment and is difficult to unwind.

*Sources: Reg. Section 1.469-4(c)-(e); KBKG Tax Insight, "Overcoming Passive Losses from Self-Rental Property Using Grouping Elections"; The Tax Adviser, "The Self-Rental Rules: Risks and Opportunities" (Jan. 2024).*

### Self-Charged Interest

Under Reg. Section 1.469-7, when a taxpayer lends money to a passthrough entity in which the taxpayer owns an interest (or vice versa), the resulting interest income that would otherwise be classified as portfolio income may be recharacterized as passive activity gross income. This prevents the mismatch where the passthrough entity's interest expense deduction is passive but the lender's corresponding interest income is non-passive portfolio income.

The applicable percentage of self-charged interest income recharacterized as passive equals the taxpayer's passive activity deductions for interest charged by the entity divided by the greater of (a) the taxpayer's total share of interest deductions from the entity or (b) the taxpayer's total interest income from the entity for the year.

A passthrough entity may elect **out** of the self-charged interest rules by attaching a statement to its return. Once made, the election applies to the current year and all subsequent years unless revoked with IRS consent. (Reg. Section 1.469-7(g).)

*Sources: Reg. Section 1.469-7; The Tax Adviser, "Interest Recharacterization Rules for Self-Charged Interest" (Aug. 2018); The Tax Adviser, "Increasing Passive Activity Loss Deductions with Self-Charged Interest" (Dec. 2010).*

## Key Thresholds and Figures

| Item | Amount / Threshold | Notes |
|------|-------------------|-------|
| Material participation -- 500-hour test | > 500 hours/year | Most commonly used test |
| Material participation -- 100-hour test | > 100 hours, not less than anyone else | Test 3 |
| Significant participation activity (SPA) | > 100 hours individual, > 500 hours aggregate across all SPAs | Test 4 |
| Facts and circumstances minimum | > 100 hours required | Below 100 hours automatically fails Test 7 |
| Five-of-ten-years test | 5 of prior 10 years | Test 5; years need not be consecutive |
| Real estate professional -- 750-hour test | > 750 hours in real property trades/businesses | Must also pass more-than-half test |
| Special rental loss allowance | Up to $25,000 | Active participation required |
| Allowance phase-out begins | $100,000 MAGI ($50,000 MFS living apart) | Reduced $0.50 per $1.00 above threshold |
| Allowance fully eliminated | $150,000 MAGI ($75,000 MFS living apart) | -- |
| MFS (living together) allowance | $0 | No allowance available |
| Suspended loss release | 100% on complete taxable disposition | To unrelated party; Section 469(g) |

These thresholds are **not** indexed for inflation and have remained unchanged since enactment in 1986.

## OBBBA Changes (if applicable)

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed into law in 2025, does **not** directly amend Section 469 or the passive activity loss rules. The passive loss limitation framework, the seven material participation tests, the real estate professional exception, the $25,000 rental allowance, and all associated thresholds remain unchanged.

However, the OBBBA references Section 469 indirectly in two contexts:

- **QBI Deduction (Section 199A):** The OBBBA's new minimum QBI deduction of $400 (for taxpayers with at least $1,000 of aggregate QBI) requires that the taxpayer "materially participate" in the qualified trade or business as defined under Section 469(h) and the material participation regulations. This reinforces the importance of meeting the Section 469 material participation tests not only for loss limitation purposes but also for QBI eligibility under the new minimum deduction rules.

- **Excess Business Loss Limitation (Section 461(l)):** The OBBBA extends the Section 461(l) excess business loss limitation through 2028. Losses that survive the Section 469 passive activity filter still face the Section 461(l) cap (currently $305,000 single / $610,000 joint for 2024, indexed annually). Practitioners must continue to apply both limitations in the correct order.

*Sources: OBBBA Sections 70101-70108; Grant Thornton, "New Law Brings Significant Tax Changes" (2025); Kitces.com, "Breaking Down the OBBBA" (2025).*

## Agent Rules

1. **Always apply loss limitation ordering.** Before applying Section 469, confirm the taxpayer has sufficient basis (Section 704(d)/1366(d)) and is at risk (Section 465). After Section 469, apply Section 461(l) excess business loss limitation.

2. **Track hours rigorously.** When evaluating material participation, require the taxpayer to identify which of the seven tests they satisfy. Document the specific test number and supporting hour totals. If relying on contemporaneous logs, note the log format and period covered.

3. **Distinguish active participation from material participation.** The $25,000 rental allowance requires active participation (a lower bar). Real estate professional status and non-passive treatment of trade or business activities require material participation (a higher bar). Do not conflate the two.

4. **Check AGI before applying the $25,000 allowance.** Always compute MAGI and apply the phase-out formula: Allowance = $25,000 - [0.50 x (MAGI - $100,000)]. If MAGI exceeds $150,000, the allowance is zero.

5. **Flag grouping elections.** If a taxpayer groups activities, note that the grouping is generally permanent. Before recommending a grouping election, consider the long-term implications -- ungrouping requires a material change in facts and circumstances and IRS consent.

6. **Identify self-rental situations.** When a taxpayer rents property to a business they control, the self-rental recharacterization rules under Reg. Section 1.469-2(f)(6) treat net rental income as non-passive. Losses remain passive unless a grouping election is made. Flag these arrangements for special handling.

7. **Track suspended losses per activity.** Maintain a schedule of suspended PALs by activity, by year, and by character. These are needed to compute release upon disposition and to properly allocate losses on Form 8582.

8. **Real estate professional status is per-taxpayer, per-year.** On a joint return, only one spouse needs to qualify. Spouses cannot combine hours for the 750-hour and more-than-half tests but can combine hours for material participation in individual rental activities.

## Common Traps

1. **Assuming rental losses are always deductible.** Rental activities are passive by default. Many taxpayers incorrectly assume that hands-on management of a rental property makes losses fully deductible. Without real estate professional status or the $25,000 allowance, rental losses are suspended.

2. **Failing to document hours.** The IRS frequently challenges material participation claims. Tax Court cases consistently penalize taxpayers who cannot produce credible records. Narrative testimony alone is rarely sufficient -- contemporaneous logs, calendars, or appointment records are strongly preferred.

3. **Confusing real estate professional qualification with material participation.** Meeting the 750-hour and more-than-half tests qualifies the taxpayer as a real estate professional, but this only lifts the rental presumption. The taxpayer must still prove material participation in each rental activity (or elect to aggregate) to treat losses as non-passive.

4. **Overlooking the grouping election deadline.** The grouping election must be made on the first tax return that reports the activities. Filing with activities treated separately is itself a grouping choice under Reg. Section 1.469-4(e). Retroactive grouping is not permitted absent a material change in facts and circumstances.

5. **Ignoring the MAGI phase-out on the $25,000 allowance.** The phase-out is steep -- $0.50 per dollar above $100,000 AGI. A taxpayer at $130,000 MAGI receives only a $10,000 allowance. Many taxpayers with moderate incomes assume they get the full $25,000 when they do not.

6. **Selling to a related party and expecting loss release.** Dispositions to related parties under Section 267(b) or 707(b)(1) do not trigger the release of suspended passive losses. The losses remain suspended until the related party sells to an unrelated party.

7. **Double-counting hours across tests.** Hours spent in a specific rental property count toward that property's material participation test and toward the 750-hour real estate professional test, but the two tests serve different purposes. Taxpayers sometimes erroneously believe that 750 hours across all properties satisfies material participation for each individual property without an aggregation election.

8. **Self-charged interest mismatch.** When a taxpayer lends to their own passthrough entity, the interest income is portfolio by default while the entity's interest expense deduction passes through as passive. Without applying the self-charged interest recharacterization rules of Reg. Section 1.469-7, the taxpayer loses the ability to offset the passive expense against the related income.
