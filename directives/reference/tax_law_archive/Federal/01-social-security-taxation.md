# Social Security Benefit Taxation
**IRC Section(s):** Section 86
**Relevant Forms:** Form 1040 (lines 6a, 6b, 6d), Form SSA-1099, IRS Publication 915
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

Under IRC Section 86, up to 85% of a taxpayer's Social Security benefits may be included in gross income and taxed as ordinary income. The taxable portion is determined by a unique income measure called **combined income** (also referred to as **provisional income**), which differs from adjusted gross income (AGI) in important ways. The combined income thresholds that trigger taxation have never been indexed for inflation since they were first enacted in 1984 (for the 50% tier) and 1993 (for the 85% tier), meaning that an increasing share of Social Security recipients are subject to benefit taxation each year as nominal incomes rise.

Social Security benefits themselves are reported on Form SSA-1099, box 5. The taxable portion is computed using Worksheet 1 in IRS Publication 915 and reported on Form 1040, line 6b. The gross benefit amount appears on line 6a.

Sources: IRS Publication 915 (2025); IRS Publication 554 (2025); Congressional Research Service, "Social Security: Calculation and History of Taxing Benefits" (RL32552).

---

## Rules

### Combined Income Formula

Combined income is calculated as follows:

```
Combined Income = AGI (excluding Social Security benefits)
                + Tax-exempt interest income
                + 50% of gross Social Security benefits
```

Specifically, the taxpayer:

1. Takes their adjusted gross income from Form 1040, line 11, **excluding** any Social Security benefits already included on line 6b.
2. Adds all tax-exempt interest income (e.g., municipal bond interest, reported on Form 1040, line 2a).
3. Adds 50% of their gross Social Security benefits from Form SSA-1099, box 5.

The resulting figure is combined income. It is this figure -- not AGI alone -- that determines how much of the Social Security benefit is taxable.

Additional items added back into the combined income calculation include: interest from qualified U.S. savings bonds used for educational expenses (which is otherwise excludable), employer-provided adoption benefits, foreign earned income or housing exclusions, and income earned in American Samoa or Puerto Rico by bona fide residents (per IRS Publication 554, 2025).

### Two-Tier Inclusion System

Section 86 uses a two-tier system to determine the taxable portion of benefits:

**Tier 1 (up to 50% taxable):** The taxable amount is the **lesser** of:
- 50% of Social Security benefits, **or**
- 50% of the amount by which combined income exceeds the first threshold ($25,000 single / $32,000 MFJ)

**Tier 2 (up to 85% taxable):** If combined income exceeds the second threshold ($34,000 single / $44,000 MFJ), the taxable amount is the **lesser** of:
- 85% of Social Security benefits, **or**
- 85% of the amount by which combined income exceeds the second threshold, **plus** the lesser of the Tier 1 amount or $4,500 (single) / $6,000 (MFJ)

The maximum taxable percentage is capped at 85%. There is no scenario under current law where 100% of Social Security benefits are taxable.

### Married Filing Separately -- Special Rule

If a taxpayer's filing status is **married filing separately** and the taxpayer **lived with their spouse at any time during the tax year**, the base amount threshold is **$0**. This effectively means that up to **85% of Social Security benefits are taxable** from the first dollar of combined income. The taxpayer skips the two-tier calculation entirely and instead multiplies the total amount on line 8 of Worksheet 1 by 85%. This is one of the harshest filing-status penalties in the tax code for Social Security recipients.

If the MFS taxpayer lived **apart** from their spouse for the **entire** tax year, they use the single-filer thresholds ($25,000 / $34,000) and must check box 6d on Form 1040.

Source: IRS Publication 915 (2025), Worksheet 1; IRS Publication 554 (2025).

## Key Thresholds and Figures

### Combined Income Thresholds (Unchanged Since 1984/1993)

| Filing Status | 0% Taxable | Up to 50% Taxable | Up to 85% Taxable |
|---|---|---|---|
| Single / Head of Household / Qualifying Surviving Spouse | Below $25,000 | $25,000 - $34,000 | Above $34,000 |
| Married Filing Jointly | Below $32,000 | $32,000 - $44,000 | Above $44,000 |
| Married Filing Separately (lived with spouse) | N/A | N/A | 85% from first dollar |
| Married Filing Separately (lived apart all year) | Below $25,000 | $25,000 - $34,000 | Above $34,000 |

### Critical Notes on Thresholds

- **Not inflation-adjusted.** The $25,000 and $32,000 thresholds have been static since 1984 (Social Security Amendments of 1983). The $34,000 and $44,000 thresholds have been static since 1993 (Omnibus Budget Reconciliation Act of 1993). Had the original 1984 thresholds been indexed to CPI, they would today be approximately $72,000 (single) and $92,000 (MFJ).
- **Bracket creep effect.** Because these thresholds are frozen while nominal incomes rise with inflation, an ever-growing share of Social Security recipients falls into the taxable tiers each year. Benefits that were nontaxable a decade ago may now be partially or fully taxable.
- **No legislative changes to Section 86 thresholds in 2025 or 2026.** Despite widespread political discussion of eliminating Social Security benefit taxation, the OBBBA (One Big Beautiful Bill Act) did not modify the combined income thresholds or the 50%/85% inclusion rates.

### Maximum Taxable Percentage

| Tier | Maximum Inclusion | IRC Section |
|---|---|---|
| Below first threshold | 0% | 86(a), 86(c) |
| Between first and second threshold | 50% | 86(a)(1) |
| Above second threshold | 85% | 86(a)(2) |

Source: IRC Section 86(a), (c); Financial Planning Association, "The Taxation of Social Security Benefits and Planning Implications."

## OBBBA Changes (if applicable)

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed into law in 2025, **did not change** the IRC Section 86 rules governing the taxation of Social Security benefits. The combined income thresholds, the two-tier inclusion structure, and the 85% maximum remain exactly as they have been since 1993. Thomson Reuters confirmed: "Social Security Tax Rules Unchanged" under the OBBBA.

However, the OBBBA introduced an **indirect** benefit for seniors:

### New Senior Tax Deduction (2025-2028)

- **Amount:** Up to $6,000 per qualifying individual ($12,000 for MFJ where both spouses are 65+).
- **Eligibility:** Taxpayer must be age 65 or older by December 31 of the tax year.
- **Phaseout:** Reduced by 6% of MAGI exceeding $75,000 (single/HOH) or $150,000 (MFJ). Completely phased out at $175,000 (single) or $350,000 (MFJ).
- **MFS exclusion:** Taxpayers filing married filing separately are **not eligible** for the senior deduction.
- **Nature:** This is a **below-the-line** deduction reducing taxable income, not AGI. It does **not** reduce combined income for Section 86 purposes.
- **Availability:** Both itemizers and standard-deduction filers may claim it.
- **Temporary:** Applies to tax years 2025 through 2028 only.
- **New form:** Reported on Schedule 1-A.

**Important distinction:** The senior deduction does not eliminate or reduce Social Security benefit taxation. It provides a separate deduction that reduces taxable income after the Section 86 calculation has already been performed. A taxpayer whose benefits are 85% taxable under Section 86 will still have 85% of their benefits included in gross income; they simply get an additional deduction against their overall taxable income.

Sources: IRS, "One, Big, Beautiful Bill Act: Tax deductions for working Americans and seniors"; Thomson Reuters, "Breaking down the OBBBA's Social Security tax deduction"; Bipartisan Policy Center, "The 2025 Tax Bill: Additional $6,000 Deduction for Seniors, Simplified."

## Agent Rules

1. **Use combined income, not AGI.** Social Security benefit taxation is determined by combined income (AGI + tax-exempt interest + 50% of SS benefits). Never use AGI alone to determine benefit taxability.

2. **Tax-exempt interest must be added back.** Municipal bond interest, despite being federally tax-exempt for income tax purposes, is included in the combined income calculation. Advising Social Security recipients to hold municipal bonds as a strategy to avoid Social Security taxation provides **no benefit** -- the interest is captured in the combined income formula regardless of its otherwise tax-exempt status.

3. **MFS with cohabitation = 85% from dollar one.** If a married taxpayer files separately and lived with their spouse at any point during the year, there is no threshold -- 85% of benefits are immediately taxable. Always verify living arrangements before applying MFS thresholds.

4. **Thresholds are static.** Do not assume that Section 86 thresholds are updated annually. They have been frozen since 1984/1993 and remain unchanged in 2025 and 2026.

5. **The OBBBA senior deduction does not reduce combined income.** The new $6,000 senior deduction is below-the-line and does not affect the Section 86 combined income calculation. Do not tell a taxpayer that the senior deduction will reduce their Social Security benefit taxation.

6. **85% is the maximum.** Never state or imply that 100% of Social Security benefits can be taxable. The statutory maximum is 85%.

7. **Lump-sum elections.** If a taxpayer received a lump-sum Social Security payment in 2025 that covers prior years, IRS Publication 915 Worksheets 2, 3, and 4 allow an alternative calculation that may produce a lower taxable amount. Flag this when lump-sum payments appear on Form SSA-1099.

## Common Traps

### Trap 1: Municipal Bond Interest Blindspot
Taxpayers and advisors often assume that tax-exempt municipal bond interest is invisible to the IRS for all purposes. For Social Security taxation, it is not. Municipal bond interest is explicitly included in the combined income formula under Section 86. A retiree holding a large municipal bond portfolio may be surprised to find that this interest pushes their combined income above the $25,000/$32,000 or $34,000/$44,000 thresholds, triggering taxation of benefits that would otherwise be tax-free.

### Trap 2: Triple Tax Cost of Additional Income
An unplanned increase in income (e.g., a Roth conversion, capital gain, or IRA distribution) can produce a cascading tax effect for Social Security recipients near the threshold boundaries. The taxpayer: (1) pays tax on the new income itself, (2) pays tax on a newly taxable portion of Social Security benefits (since higher income pushes more benefits into the taxable range), and (3) may face a higher effective marginal tax rate than their bracket alone would suggest. Effective marginal rates can exceed 40% in the phase-in zones even for taxpayers in the 12% or 22% brackets.

### Trap 3: Married Filing Separately Penalty
Taxpayers who file MFS while living with their spouse -- sometimes done to manage student loan repayments or to isolate income-driven repayment plan calculations -- face immediate 85% taxability of Social Security benefits with no threshold protection. This can be a costly oversight.

### Trap 4: Forgetting the 50% SS Benefit Add-Back
When estimating combined income, taxpayers sometimes forget that 50% of their Social Security benefits must be added to AGI and tax-exempt interest. This self-referential element of the formula means that larger Social Security benefits contribute to their own taxation.

### Trap 5: Assuming OBBBA Eliminated Social Security Taxes
There was significant political rhetoric during 2024-2025 about eliminating taxes on Social Security benefits entirely. The OBBBA did **not** do this. The senior deduction is a separate, temporary, income-limited benefit. Taxpayers who heard campaign promises may incorrectly believe their benefits are now tax-free.

### Trap 6: State-Level Taxation
Section 86 governs federal taxation only. As of 2025, nine states also tax Social Security benefits to varying degrees (Colorado, Connecticut, Minnesota, Montana, New Mexico, Rhode Island, Utah, Vermont, and West Virginia), though several have been phasing out or reducing their taxation. State rules differ significantly from federal rules and must be analyzed separately.
