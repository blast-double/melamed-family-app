# Meals and Entertainment Deductions
**IRC Section(s):** Section 274 (with references to Sections 119, 132, 162)
**Relevant Forms:** Form 1040 Schedule C (Line 24b), Form 1065, Form 1120/1120-S, Form 2106, Publication 463
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

IRC Section 274 governs the deductibility of meals, entertainment, travel, and certain fringe benefits. The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA, 2017) permanently disallowed deductions for entertainment expenses and set the stage for a phaseout of employer-provided meal deductions. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA, 2025) made the entertainment disallowance permanent, eliminated employer meal deductions effective January 1, 2026, and added narrow exceptions for specific industries. Standard business meals with clients or prospects remain 50% deductible when substantiation requirements are met.

**Legislative timeline:**
- **Pre-TCJA (before 2018):** Entertainment 50% deductible if directly related to or associated with business; meals 50% deductible.
- **TCJA (2018-2025):** Entertainment 100% nondeductible under Section 274(a)(1). Business meals remain 50% deductible. Employer "convenience" meals (Section 119) remain 50% deductible through 2025. Section 274(o) enacted with a delayed effective date of January 1, 2026.
- **CAA (2021-2022):** Temporary 100% deduction for business meals provided by restaurants (expired December 31, 2022).
- **OBBBA (2025, effective 2026):** Confirmed Section 274(o) disallowance. Added exceptions under Section 274(e)(8) for restaurants, fishing vessels, and certain other industries. Made the TCJA entertainment disallowance permanent.

*Sources: CohnReznick, "OBBB Updates Meals And Entertainment Deduction Rules"; ForvisMazars, "Convenience Meals No More: IRC Section 274(o) Updates"; IRS TD-9925; 26 USC Section 274.*

## Rules

### 1. Entertainment Expenses -- 100% Nondeductible

No deduction is allowed for any expense related to entertainment, amusement, or recreation activities or facilities under Section 274(a)(1). This is permanent law under the TCJA, confirmed by the OBBBA.

**What counts as entertainment:**
- Tickets to sporting events, concerts, theater
- Golf outings, country club dues, hunting trips
- Vacation packages, personal use of company recreational facilities
- Skyboxes and luxury suites
- Any activity generally considered entertainment, amusement, or recreation

**No "directly related" or "associated with" exception exists.** Unlike pre-TCJA law, business discussion before, during, or after entertainment does not rescue the deduction. The entire entertainment cost is nondeductible regardless of how strong the business purpose.

**Meals during entertainment:** If food and beverages are purchased separately from the entertainment (separately stated on invoice/receipt) and not lavish or extravagant, the meal portion may qualify for the 50% business meal deduction. The key is separate invoicing -- a single lump-sum ticket price that includes food cannot be split.

*Sources: IRC Section 274(a)(1); Treas. Reg. Section 1.274-11; Bloomberg Tax, "Business Meal & Entertainment Deduction Guide for 2026."*

### 2. Business Meals -- 50% Deductible

Under Section 274(k) and 274(n)(1), food and beverage expenses are generally limited to a 50% deduction. To qualify, a business meal must satisfy all of the following conditions:

1. **Not lavish or extravagant** under the circumstances (Section 274(k)(1)(A))
2. **Taxpayer or employee present** at the furnishing of the food or beverages (Section 274(k)(1)(B))
3. **Ordinary and necessary** business expense under Section 162
4. **Bona fide business purpose** -- the meal must be related to the active conduct of a trade or business
5. **Adequate substantiation** under Section 274(d) (see Substantiation section below)

**Common qualifying scenarios:**
- Meals with clients, prospects, or business associates where business is discussed
- Meals while traveling away from home on business (travel meals)
- Meals at business conventions, conferences, or league meetings
- Employee travel meals reimbursed under an accountable plan

**100% deductible exceptions** (not subject to the 50% limit):
- Expenses treated as employee compensation (Section 274(e)(2))
- Reimbursed expenses under accountable plans (Section 274(e)(3))
- Recreational/social activities for the benefit of employees -- holiday parties, company picnics (Section 274(e)(4))
- Food sold to the public in the ordinary course of business -- e.g., restaurants (Section 274(e)(7))
- Meals on fishing boats and in fish processing facilities (Section 274(n)(2)(C)(v), added by OBBBA)

*Sources: IRC Sections 274(k), 274(n); Treas. Reg. Section 1.274-12; IRS Publication 463 (2025 draft).*

### 3. Employer-Provided Meals -- Major Change in 2026

**Through December 31, 2025:**
- Meals provided for the convenience of the employer (Section 119) are 50% deductible to the employer.
- Employer-operated eating facilities (Section 132(e)(2)) are 50% deductible.
- De minimis fringe benefit meals (breakroom snacks, coffee, overtime meals) are 50% deductible.
- The Section 119 income exclusion for employees is in effect -- qualifying meals are not taxable income to the employee.

**Starting January 1, 2026 (Section 274(o)):**
- Meals provided for the convenience of the employer: **0% deductible** (100% disallowed).
- Employer-operated eating facilities: **0% deductible** (100% disallowed), including food, beverages, and facility operating costs.
- De minimis fringe benefit meals (snacks, coffee, etc.): **0% deductible** as a business deduction, though the employee-side exclusion from income may still apply.

**Employee income treatment (Section 119):** The OBBBA eliminated the employer deduction but did not repeal Section 119 itself. Meals meeting Section 119 requirements -- furnished on business premises for the employer's convenience -- remain excludable from the employee's gross income in 2025. For 2026, the employee exclusion under Section 119 is preserved, but the employer receives no corresponding deduction.

**OBBBA exceptions to Section 274(o) disallowance:**
- **Restaurants and caterers** (Section 274(e)(8)): Establishments that provide meals to customers may deduct 100% of employee meal costs. Treasury has acknowledged this exception under Treas. Reg. Section 1.274-12(c)(2)(v)(A).
- **Fishing vessels and fish processing facilities** in Alaska (Section 274(n)(2)(C)(v)).
- **Offshore oil rig and gas platform workers** and maritime crew required to be provided meals under federal law.
- **On-site cafeterias open to the public** that generate revenue through full-value sales may still qualify, but must maintain separate records for employee vs. public meals.

**Fiscal year taxpayers:** The disallowance applies to amounts paid or incurred after December 31, 2025, meaning fiscal year taxpayers straddling the effective date must prorate.

*Sources: IRC Sections 274(o), 119; Covington, "Key Provisions of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act"; HBK, "Employer-Provided Meals: Major Deduction Changes for 2026"; Hawkins Ash, "Rethinking Meals & Entertainment Deductions."*

### 4. DOT Transportation Workers -- 80% Deductible

Under Section 274(n)(3), individuals subject to the Department of Transportation's "hours of service" limits may deduct **80%** (rather than 50%) of meal expenses incurred while traveling away from home during or incident to a period subject to those limits.

**Eligible individuals:**
- Interstate truck drivers and bus drivers (CDL holders subject to FMCSA hours-of-service rules)
- Airline pilots, co-pilots, and flight engineers
- Railroad engineers and conductors
- Merchant mariners

**Per diem rates (effective October 1, 2024 through September 30, 2025):**
- CONUS (continental US): **$80/day** (deductible amount: $64/day at 80%)
- OCONUS (outside continental US): **$86/day** (deductible amount: $68.80/day at 80%)
- Partial day: **$60** CONUS / **$64.50** OCONUS (at 75% of full day)
- Incidental expenses only: **$5/day**

**Important:** Company drivers (W-2 employees) cannot claim per diem as an itemized deduction due to the TCJA suspension of unreimbursed employee business expenses (made permanent by OBBBA). Only self-employed owner-operators and independent contractors may claim the per diem deduction on Schedule C.

*Sources: IRS Notice 2024-68; IRS Publication 463 (2025 draft); ATBS, "Per Diem Tax Deduction Tips for Truck Drivers in 2026"; FreightWaves.*

## OBBBA Changes

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (Pub. L. 119-21, signed 2025) made the following changes relevant to Section 274:

1. **Section 274(o) confirmed and amended:** The TCJA-enacted disallowance of employer meal deductions became effective January 1, 2026. OBBBA amended Section 274(o) to add exceptions for expenses described in Section 274(e)(8) (restaurant/caterer employee meals) and Section 274(n)(2)(C) (fishing industry meals).

2. **Entertainment disallowance made permanent:** The TCJA's repeal of the entertainment deduction under Section 274(a)(1) is permanent. No sunset provision.

3. **Unreimbursed employee business expenses permanently suspended:** The TCJA suspension of the miscellaneous itemized deduction (2% floor) for unreimbursed employee expenses, which was set to expire after 2025, is now permanent. W-2 employees cannot deduct meals or other business expenses.

4. **Qualified bicycle commuting reimbursement eliminated:** Section 274(l) amended to permanently eliminate the deduction for qualified bicycle commuting reimbursements that was scheduled to return in 2026.

5. **New industry-specific exceptions added:**
   - Fishing boats and fish processing facilities (Section 274(n)(2)(C)(v))
   - Offshore oil/gas workers and maritime crew under federal meal requirements

*Sources: Pub. L. 119-21 Sections 70305, 70112; RSM, "How the OBBBA affects tax treatment of compensation and benefits"; Landmark CPAs, "How OBBBA and TCJA Impact 2025 Year-End Tax Planning."*

## Agent Rules

1. **Always separate meals from entertainment on receipts.** If a client event includes both dinner and entertainment (e.g., a concert), the meal must be separately invoiced to preserve the 50% deduction. A combined charge is fully nondeductible.

2. **Record the five substantiation elements contemporaneously.** Do not rely on memory or reconstruct records after the fact. The Cohan rule (estimating expenses) does not apply to meals under Section 274(d) -- inadequate records mean total disallowance.

3. **For 2026 employer meals, check the exceptions before defaulting to nondeductible.** Restaurant employers, fishing industry, and certain maritime/oil-gas employers have carve-outs.

4. **DOT per diem is only for self-employed drivers.** Never advise a W-2 company driver to claim per diem on their personal return -- the deduction for unreimbursed employee expenses is permanently suspended.

5. **Travel meals vs. local business meals have different substantiation paths.** Travel meals require departure/return dates and destination in addition to the standard five elements. Local business meals require documentation of the business relationship and purpose.

6. **Employee recreational events (holiday parties, picnics) remain 100% deductible** under Section 274(e)(4) -- do not confuse these with employer-provided meals subject to Section 274(o).

7. **Fiscal year taxpayers must prorate.** The Section 274(o) disallowance applies to amounts paid or incurred after December 31, 2025, not by tax year. Fiscal year entities straddling the date must allocate.

## Common Traps

1. **Bundled entertainment and meals.** A single receipt for a sporting event that includes food in the ticket price is 100% nondeductible. There is no way to estimate or allocate the meal portion from a bundled charge. Insist on separate billing.

2. **"Business discussion" does not save entertainment.** Pre-TCJA, taxpayers could deduct entertainment if it was "directly related to" or "associated with" business. That exception is gone. No amount of business discussion before, during, or after a golf outing makes it deductible.

3. **Lavish or extravagant meals.** The statute does not define a dollar threshold for "lavish or extravagant." The standard is contextual -- what is reasonable for the industry and circumstances. A $500 dinner for two to close a major deal may be reasonable; the same dinner as a routine Tuesday lunch likely is not. If challenged, the burden is on the taxpayer.

4. **Missing substantiation -- total disallowance.** Unlike most business expenses where the Cohan doctrine allows estimation, Section 274(d) explicitly overrides Cohan. If any of the five required elements is missing, the entire deduction is disallowed -- not reduced, disallowed. (Temp. Reg. Section 1.274-5T(a).)

5. **Employer meals in 2026 -- forgetting the change.** Many businesses have historically deducted 50% of breakroom snacks, coffee, cafeteria costs, and on-premises meals. Starting in 2026, these are 0% deductible. Accounting systems and chart-of-accounts coding must be updated.

6. **De minimis fringe confusion.** A meal can still be a tax-free de minimis fringe benefit to the employee (not included in W-2 wages) even though the employer gets zero deduction in 2026. The employee-side and employer-side rules are now decoupled.

7. **Owner-operator per diem without adequate travel records.** The 80% DOT deduction requires that meals be incurred while traveling away from the tax home during hours-of-service periods. Local drivers who start and end each day at home generally do not qualify, even if they work a full 14-hour HOS day.

8. **Reimbursed meals under non-accountable plans.** If an employer reimburses meal expenses but the arrangement does not meet accountable plan requirements (adequate accounting, return of excess), the reimbursement is treated as wages -- not as a deductible meal expense subject to Section 274.
