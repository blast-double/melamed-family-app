# International Tax: FBAR, FATCA, Foreign Income, and PFICs
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 901, 911, 1291-1298, 6038, 6038D; 31 USC Section 5314
**Relevant Forms:** FinCEN 114, Forms 8938, 2555, 1116, 5471, 8621
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

U.S. persons are taxed on worldwide income and must report foreign financial accounts and assets under multiple overlapping regimes. The two primary reporting frameworks are (1) the Report of Foreign Bank and Financial Accounts (FBAR) under the Bank Secrecy Act (31 USC 5314, FinCEN Form 114), administered by FinCEN, and (2) the Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act (FATCA, IRC Section 6038D, Form 8938), administered by the IRS. These regimes have different thresholds, different filing destinations, and separate penalty structures -- but many taxpayers must comply with both.

Beyond reporting, substantive tax provisions govern how foreign income is taxed. The Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (Section 911) allows qualifying taxpayers to exclude earned income from abroad. The Foreign Tax Credit (Section 901) provides a dollar-for-dollar offset for taxes paid to foreign governments. The Passive Foreign Investment Company (PFIC) rules (Sections 1291-1298) impose punitive taxation on foreign mutual funds and similar entities. Form 5471 reports ownership interests in Controlled Foreign Corporations (CFCs).

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed July 4, 2025, introduced significant changes to international provisions effective for tax years beginning after December 31, 2025, including rebranding GILTI to NCTI, FDII to FDDEI, a permanent BEAT rate of 10.5%, and a new 1% excise tax on outbound remittance transfers.

## Rules

### FBAR (FinCEN Form 114)

**Statutory basis:** 31 USC Section 5314; 31 CFR 1010.350.

**Who must file:** Any "U.S. person" -- citizens (including expats and dual citizens), residents, domestic entities -- who has a financial interest in or signature authority over one or more foreign financial accounts if the aggregate value of all such accounts exceeds $10,000 at any point during the calendar year. The $10,000 threshold is measured on the day the aggregate balance is highest, not on any particular date ([sdocpa.com](https://www.sdocpa.com/fbar-filing-guide/); [brighttax.com](https://brighttax.com/blog/how-to-file-fbar-online-guide-expats/)).

**Covered accounts:** Bank accounts, brokerage accounts, mutual funds, certain retirement accounts, and accounts where the filer has signature authority but no ownership interest. Balances are reported in U.S. dollars using the Treasury's official year-end exchange rate.

**Filing method:** Electronically through the BSA E-Filing System (not with the tax return). The FBAR is filed with FinCEN (Treasury Department), not the IRS.

**Due date:** April 15, with an automatic extension to October 15. No separate extension request is required.

**Penalties:**
- **Non-willful:** Up to $16,536 per violation for 2026 (inflation-adjusted from $10,000 statutory amount). Following the 2023 Supreme Court decision in *Bittner v. United States*, non-willful penalties apply per form (per annual report), not per account ([taxesforexpats.com](https://www.taxesforexpats.com/articles/fbar-fatca/fbar-penalties.html)).
- **Willful:** The greater of $165,353 or 50% of the account balance at the time of violation, per account, per year. Criminal prosecution is possible, with fines up to $500,000 and imprisonment up to 10 years in aggravated cases ([sdocpa.com](https://www.sdocpa.com/fbar-filing-guide/); [taxesforexpats.com](https://www.taxesforexpats.com/articles/fbar-fatca/fbar-penalties.html)).
- **Relief options:** Delinquent FBAR Submission Procedures, Streamlined Filing Compliance Procedures (Forms 14653 and 14654), and Voluntary Disclosure Practice.

### FATCA (Form 8938, IRC Section 6038D)

**Statutory basis:** IRC Section 6038D, enacted as part of the Hiring Incentives to Restore Employment (HIRE) Act of 2010.

**Who must file:** U.S. persons (individuals) who hold specified foreign financial assets exceeding the applicable threshold. Filed as an attachment to Form 1040 ([onlinetaxman.com](https://onlinetaxman.com/form-8938-fatca-reporting-guide-expats-2026)).

**Covered assets:** Broader than FBAR -- includes foreign financial accounts, foreign stocks and securities held outside a U.S. financial institution, interests in foreign entities, and certain foreign financial instruments.

**Filing thresholds (unchanged for 2025-2026):**

| Filing Status & Residence | Year-End Value | At Any Point During Year |
|---|---|---|
| Single / MFS, living in U.S. | $50,000 | $75,000 |
| Married Filing Jointly, living in U.S. | $100,000 | $150,000 |
| Single / MFS, living abroad | $200,000 | $300,000 |
| Married Filing Jointly, living abroad | $400,000 | $600,000 |

"Living abroad" requires meeting the bona fide residence test or physical presence test under Section 911 ([arcandledger.com](https://www.arcandledger.com/guides/fbar-fatca-guide/); [sdocpa.com](https://www.sdocpa.com/fbar-vs-form-8938/)).

**Due date:** Same as the tax return (including extensions).

**Penalties:**
- $10,000 failure-to-file penalty per year.
- If not filed within 90 days of IRS notice, additional $10,000 penalty for each 30-day period of non-compliance, up to a maximum of $50,000 in additional penalties (total up to $60,000).
- 40% accuracy-related penalty on any underpayment of tax attributable to undisclosed foreign financial assets ([arcandledger.com](https://www.arcandledger.com/guides/fbar-fatca-guide/); [onlinetaxman.com](https://onlinetaxman.com/form-8938-fatca-reporting-guide-expats-2026)).

### Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (Section 911, Form 2555)

**Exclusion amounts:**
- **2025:** $130,000 per qualifying individual
- **2026:** $132,900 per qualifying individual (confirmed by IRS, October 2025)
- Married couples where both qualify: double the per-person amount ($260,000 for 2025; $265,800 for 2026)

**Qualification tests (must meet one):**

1. **Bona fide residence test:** Bona fide resident of a foreign country for an uninterrupted period including an entire calendar year (Jan 1 - Dec 31). Facts-and-circumstances standard focused on intent, ties, and foreign tax home.

2. **Physical presence test:** Physically present in foreign countries for at least 330 full 24-hour days during any 12-month period. The 12-month window is rolling (not tied to calendar year), allowing strategic start-date positioning ([taxesforexpats.com](https://www.taxesforexpats.com/articles/tax-saving-strategies/foreign-earned-income-exclusion.html); [greenbacktaxservices.com](https://www.greenbacktaxservices.com/knowledge-center/tips-updates-foreign-earned-income-exclusion/)).

Both tests require a tax home in a foreign country (general area of main place of business or employment).

**Qualifying income:** Salaries, wages, bonuses, self-employment income earned for services performed abroad. Does NOT include investment income, pensions, or most rental income.

**Proration:** If the taxpayer qualifies for only part of the year, the exclusion is prorated: (FEIE Limit) x (Qualifying Days / 365).

**Foreign housing exclusion/deduction:** Additional amounts above the base housing amount ($20,800 for 2025, 16% of FEIE) up to a general 30% cap ($39,000 for 2025) may be excluded. Higher limits apply for IRS-listed high-cost localities.

**OBBBA impact:** The OBBBA did not alter Section 911's qualification mechanics or exclusion amounts ([taxesforexpats.com](https://www.taxesforexpats.com/articles/expat-tax-rules/bona-fide-residence-test-vs-physical-presence-test.html)).

### Foreign Tax Credit (Section 901, Form 1116)

**Mechanism:** Dollar-for-dollar credit against U.S. tax for foreign income taxes paid or accrued. Cannot exceed U.S. tax on the same foreign-source income.

**Limitation formula:** FTC Limit = U.S. Tax x (Foreign-Source Taxable Income / Worldwide Taxable Income). Computed separately per income basket.

**Income baskets:** Separate Form 1116 required for each: General category (wages, active business income); Passive category (dividends, interest, rents, royalties); Section 901(j) (sanctioned countries); NCTI category (formerly GILTI). Credits cannot cross baskets ([universaltaxprofessionals.com](https://universaltaxprofessionals.com/foreign-tax-credit/); [taxesforexpats.com](https://www.taxesforexpats.com/articles/tax-saving-strategies/foreign-tax-credit-carryover.html)).

**Carryback/carryforward:** 1-year carryback, 10-year carryforward. Applied FIFO (oldest first). Must stay within original basket. Unused credits expire after 10 years. File Form 1116 annually to preserve the carryover chain. NCTI credits have no carryover under OBBBA ([universaltaxprofessionals.com](https://universaltaxprofessionals.com/foreign-tax-credit/)).

**Election vs. deduction:** Taxpayers may deduct foreign taxes under Section 164 instead of claiming a credit. Credit is generally more favorable. Cannot claim credit and FEIE on the same income.

### PFIC Rules (Sections 1291-1298, Form 8621)

**Definition:** A foreign corporation is a Passive Foreign Investment Company if it meets either:
- **Income test:** 75% or more of gross income is passive income, OR
- **Asset test:** 50% or more of assets produce or are held for the production of passive income.

Most foreign mutual funds, ETFs, and pooled investment vehicles qualify as PFICs ([taxesforexpats.com](https://www.taxesforexpats.com/articles/investments/pfic-taxes.html)).

**Three taxation methods:**

1. **Excess distribution method (Section 1291, default):** Distributions exceeding 125% of the average distributions over the prior three years are "excess distributions," allocated ratably over the holding period, taxed at the highest marginal rate for each year, plus interest. Most punitive method.

2. **Mark-to-market election (Section 1296):** PFIC stock treated as sold at FMV on the last day of each tax year. Gains are ordinary income; losses allowed to the extent of prior MTM gains. Available only for regularly traded PFIC stock.

3. **QEF election (Section 1295):** Shareholder includes pro rata share of PFIC's ordinary earnings and net capital gain annually. Most favorable treatment but requires the PFIC to provide annual financial information, which many foreign funds do not ([irs.gov](https://www.irs.gov/instructions/i8621); [1040abroad.com](https://1040abroad.com/blog/excess-distribution-rules-for-pfics-explained/)).

**Form 8621:** Required annually for each PFIC held. A separate form must be filed for each PFIC. An exception exists for aggregate PFIC holdings valued at $25,000 or less ($50,000 for MFJ), provided no elections are in effect and no excess distributions occurred.

**Failure to file:** Extends the statute of limitations indefinitely for assessment of tax related to the PFIC investment.

### CFC Reporting (Form 5471)

**Who must file:** U.S. persons who are officers, directors, or 10%+ shareholders of certain foreign corporations, particularly CFCs. A CFC is a foreign corporation where U.S. shareholders (10%+ owners) collectively own more than 50% of total voting power or value. Five filing categories (1-5) based on nature and extent of involvement; Category 5 (CFC shareholders) is most extensive.

**Penalties:** $10,000 initial per form per year. Additional $10,000 per 30-day period after IRS notice, up to $50,000 additional (total up to $60,000 per corporation per year). Criminal penalties may apply ([greenbacktaxservices.com](https://www.greenbacktaxservices.com/knowledge-center/form-5471/); [sdocpa.com](https://www.sdocpa.com/form-5471-us-shareholders-foreign-corporations-guide/)).

## OBBBA Changes (if applicable)

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed July 4, 2025 (Public Law 119-21), made substantial changes to international corporate tax provisions effective for tax years beginning after December 31, 2025. Key changes relevant to this knowledge base:

### GILTI Renamed to NCTI (Net CFC Tested Income)
- The GILTI regime is rebranded as "Net CFC Tested Income" (NCTI), reflecting the elimination of the 10% QBAI (Qualified Business Asset Investment) return from the calculation. Capital-intensive CFCs that previously generated little or no GILTI may now produce significant NCTI inclusions.
- **Section 250 deduction reduced** from 50% to 40%, producing an effective tax rate of approximately 12.6% on NCTI (up from 10.5%).
- **Deemed-paid foreign tax credit haircut** reduced from 20% to 10% -- meaning 90% of applicable foreign taxes may be credited against NCTI (previously 80%).
- **NCTI credits have no carryover provisions** -- unused credits expire at year-end ([grantthornton.com](https://www.grantthornton.com/insights/alerts/tax/2025/legislative-updates/2026-international-tax-planning-guide); [rsmus.com](https://rsmus.com/insights/services/business-tax/global-taxation-reform-gilti-fdii-mean-for-multinationals.html); [pro.bloombergtax.com](https://pro.bloombergtax.com/insights/international-tax/foreign-derived-intangible-income/)).

### FDII Renamed to FDDEI (Foreign-Derived Deduction Eligible Income)
- Foreign Derived Intangible Income is rebranded as "Foreign-Derived Deduction Eligible Income" (FDDEI).
- **Section 250 deduction permanently set at 33.34%**, producing an effective tax rate of approximately 14% on qualifying export income (up from 13.125%).
- QBAI return eliminated from the FDDEI calculation, which can increase the base of qualifying income.
- R&D expenses are no longer allocated against DEI when computing FDDEI, effectively treating all R&D as related to U.S. income and maximizing the income eligible for the deduction ([rsmus.com](https://rsmus.com/insights/services/business-tax/global-taxation-reform-gilti-fdii-mean-for-multinationals.html); [mayerbrown.com](https://www.mayerbrown.com/en/insights/publications/2025/07/one-big-beautiful-bill-act-introduces-significant-domestic-and-international-tax-changes)).

### BEAT Rate Set at 10.5%
- The Base Erosion and Anti-Abuse Tax (BEAT) rate is permanently set at 10.5% for tax years beginning after 2025 (11.5% for certain banks and securities dealers). This supersedes the previously scheduled increase to 12.5% under the TCJA sunset.
- Pre-2025 rules for treatment of U.S. tax credits under BEAT are retained.
- BEAT applies to applicable taxpayers with average annual receipts of at least $500 million and base erosion payments comprising at least 3% of total deductions ([unclekam.com](https://unclekam.com/tax-write-offs/deductions/beat-base-erosion-anti-abuse-tax/); [taxfoundation.org](https://taxfoundation.org/blog/beat-tax-changes-obbba-section-899/); [katten.com](https://katten.com/the-OBBBA-act-key-year-end-tax-changes-for-private-wealth-clients)).

### 1% Excise Tax on Outbound Remittance Transfers
- New IRC Section 4475 imposes a **1% excise tax on remittance transfers** from senders in the U.S. to recipients in foreign countries, effective January 1, 2026.
- **Applies to transfers funded by cash, money orders, or cashier's checks.** Transfers via bank account or U.S. debit/credit card are exempt.
- Collected by remittance transfer providers (per Section 919(g), Electronic Funds Transfer Act) and remitted quarterly to the IRS via Form 720.
- No exception for U.S. citizens or residents -- applies regardless of sender's status.
- IRC Section 36C may allow a credit for remittance tax paid, but as of March 2026, no IRS guidance has been issued.
- Secondary liability attaches to providers who fail to collect at time of transfer ([rsmus.com](https://rsmus.com/insights/tax-alerts/2025/excise-tax-on-cross-border-remittances.html); [taxesforexpats.com](https://www.taxesforexpats.com/articles/expat-tax-rules/remittance-tax.html)).

### CFC Attribution Rules
- The OBBBA restores the rule limiting "downward attribution" of stock for CFC status, reducing the number of foreign corporations treated as CFCs.
- New Section 951B targets certain foreign-parented groups.
- Pro rata share rules are modified so U.S. shareholders may have Subpart F or NCTI inclusions if they own CFC stock on any day of the year, not just the last day ([katten.com](https://katten.com/the-OBBBA-act-key-year-end-tax-changes-for-private-wealth-clients)).

## Agent Rules

1. **Dual filing obligation:** When a taxpayer has foreign accounts, always evaluate both FBAR and FATCA requirements. The $10,000 FBAR threshold is far lower than FATCA thresholds -- most taxpayers who file Form 8938 must also file FBAR, but not vice versa.

2. **FBAR vs. FATCA distinction:** FBAR is filed with FinCEN (Treasury) through BSA E-Filing. Form 8938 is filed with the IRS as part of the tax return. These are separate regimes with separate penalties -- non-compliance with one does not satisfy the other.

3. **FEIE and FTC interaction:** A taxpayer cannot claim both the FEIE and the FTC on the same income. The FEIE reduces AGI; the FTC does not. Strategy depends on foreign tax rates: if the foreign rate exceeds the U.S. rate, FTC is generally more beneficial. If the foreign rate is lower, FEIE may be preferable. Excess income above the FEIE limit can be covered by the FTC.

4. **PFIC identification:** Any foreign mutual fund, ETF, or pooled investment should be treated as a potential PFIC until confirmed otherwise. Always check for PFIC status before acquisition when possible.

5. **Mexico-specific considerations:** For a taxpayer living in Mexico City, the higher FATCA thresholds for "living abroad" apply if the bona fide residence or physical presence test is met. Mexican bank accounts exceeding $10,000 in aggregate trigger FBAR. Income taxes paid to Mexico (ISR) are generally creditable under the U.S.-Mexico tax treaty and Section 901.

6. **Remittance tax awareness:** Transfers to Mexico funded by cash, money orders, or cashier's checks are subject to the 1% excise tax effective January 1, 2026. Transfers funded via bank account or U.S. debit/credit card are exempt. Advise use of bank transfers or card payments to avoid the excise tax.

## Common Traps

1. **Assuming FBAR and FATCA are the same.** They have different thresholds, different filing destinations, different covered assets, and different penalties. Filing one does not satisfy the other.

2. **Ignoring signature authority accounts.** FBAR filing is required for accounts over which the taxpayer has signature authority, even without ownership. Business accounts at foreign banks where the taxpayer is an authorized signer count toward the $10,000 aggregate.

3. **Failing to aggregate accounts for FBAR.** The $10,000 threshold is the aggregate of all foreign accounts combined, not per account. Two accounts with $6,000 each trigger FBAR.

4. **Missing the FEIE election in the first qualifying year.** The FEIE election (Form 2555) must be filed timely (including extensions) for the first year of eligibility. Missing it requires filing an amended return within one year of the original due date. Once revoked, a taxpayer cannot re-elect for five years without IRS approval.

5. **Claiming both FEIE and FTC on the same income.** Section 911(d)(6) prohibits double benefits. Income excluded under FEIE cannot also generate a foreign tax credit. The stacking rule means excluded income fills the lowest brackets first, pushing remaining income into higher brackets.

6. **Overlooking PFICs in foreign retirement or savings accounts.** Foreign pension plans, savings plans, and insurance products with investment components often qualify as PFICs. Each requires a separate Form 8621.

7. **Failing to make QEF or MTM election timely.** Elections must be made in the first year of PFIC ownership (or via deemed sale/deemed dividend election later). Defaulting into the excess distribution method is expensive.

8. **NCTI credit expiration under OBBBA.** NCTI credits cannot be carried back or forward -- excess credits expire at year-end.

9. **Confusing the remittance tax scope.** The 1% excise tax applies only to cash-funded transfers. Bank-to-bank wires and card-funded transfers are exempt.

10. **International form statute of limitations.** Failure to file Forms 5471, 8938, or 8621 keeps the statute of limitations open indefinitely for the entire return.
