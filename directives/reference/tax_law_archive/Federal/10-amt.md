# Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT)
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 55-59
**Relevant Forms:** Form 6251 (Alternative Minimum Tax -- Individuals)
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

The Alternative Minimum Tax is a parallel tax system that operates alongside the regular federal income tax. A taxpayer calculates liability under both systems and pays the **higher** of the two amounts. The AMT was originally enacted in 1969 (Tax Reform Act of 1969) to ensure that high-income taxpayers who benefit from certain deductions, exclusions, and other tax preferences pay at least a minimum amount of federal income tax. IRC Sections 56, 57, and 58 were added by the 1969 Act; the 1986 Tax Reform Act restructured and expanded the AMT; and the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) significantly increased exemption amounts and phase-out thresholds for 2018-2025. The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed July 4, 2025, made the higher TCJA exemptions permanent but materially changed the phase-out mechanics beginning in 2026.

**Sources:** IRS Revenue Procedure 2024-40 (2025 figures); IRS Revenue Procedure 2025-32 (2026 figures); Tax Foundation, "2025 Tax Brackets" and "2026 Tax Brackets"; The Tax Adviser, "Planning for the AMT" (March 2025).

## Rules

### AMT Calculation Mechanics

The AMT computation proceeds in four steps, as reflected on Form 6251:

1. **Start with regular taxable income** (Form 1040, Line 15).
2. **Add back adjustments and preference items** to arrive at Alternative Minimum Taxable Income (AMTI). These add-backs are codified in IRC Sections 56 (adjustments), 57 (tax preference items), and 58 (additional rules).
3. **Subtract the AMT exemption amount** (based on filing status and subject to phase-out at higher income levels).
4. **Apply the AMT tax rates** (26% and 28%) to the resulting net AMTI to compute the Tentative Minimum Tax (TMT).

If the TMT exceeds the taxpayer's regular tax liability, the difference is owed as AMT. If regular tax is higher, no AMT is due.

Long-term capital gains and qualified dividends are taxed at the same preferential rates under both the regular tax and the AMT (they do not get re-taxed at 26%/28%), but the gains **do** increase AMTI and can accelerate exemption phase-out.

### Key Adjustments (IRC Section 56)

Adjustment items generally require a separate AMT computation and are substituted for the amounts computed under regular tax rules. They can be positive or negative:

- **Standard deduction**: If taken for regular tax, the full amount is added back for AMT. The standard deduction is not allowed under the AMT system.
- **State and local tax (SALT) deduction**: State and local income taxes, sales taxes, real property taxes, and personal property taxes deducted as itemized deductions on Schedule A must be added back in full for AMT. SALT remains non-deductible for AMT even though the OBBBA raised the regular-tax SALT cap to $40,000 for 2025.
- **Depreciation**: For AMT, property placed in service after 1986 generally must be depreciated using the Alternative Depreciation System (ADS) with straight-line or 150% declining balance methods over longer recovery periods, rather than the General Depreciation System (GDS) with 200% declining balance used for regular tax. The difference between the two depreciation calculations is an AMT adjustment (positive in early years, negative in later years as the timing reverses). **Exception**: Bonus depreciation claimed on eligible assets (placed in service before 2027) produces no AMT adjustment.
- **Incentive stock options (ISOs)**: The bargain element (spread between fair market value at exercise and the strike price) is not included in regular taxable income upon exercise but **must** be included in AMTI. This is one of the most significant AMT triggers for employees of public and pre-IPO companies.
- **Research and experimental expenditures**: Post-2022, IRC Section 174 requires 60-month amortization (180 months for foreign research) for regular tax. No AMT adjustment is required if the taxpayer materially participates in the activity (Section 56(b)(2)(C)). Otherwise, an adjustment applies for the excess of the regular-tax deduction over what would be allowable under 10-year amortization.
- **Tax-exempt interest on private activity bonds**: Interest from certain private activity bonds issued after August 7, 1986, is excluded from regular income but added back for AMTI (with exceptions for bonds issued in 2009-2010 and certain refunding bonds).

### Tax Preference Items (IRC Section 57)

Preference items are always positive add-backs (they can never reduce AMTI):

- **Excess percentage depletion** (Section 57(a)(1)): The excess of the percentage depletion deduction over the adjusted basis of the property at year-end.
- **Intangible drilling costs** (Section 57(a)(2)): The excess IDC from productive wells over what would have been deductible under 120-month amortization, to the extent it exceeds 65% of the taxpayer's net oil/gas/geothermal income. (Exempt for independent producers, subject to a 40% AMTI reduction cap.)
- **Exclusion for gain on qualified small business stock** (Section 57(a)(7)): 7% of the excluded gain under Section 1202 is a preference item (for stock acquired after September 27, 2010, this preference was eliminated).

### Personal Exemptions

Under pre-TCJA law, personal and dependency exemptions were added back for AMT. The TCJA suspended personal exemptions for 2018-2025 and the OBBBA made this suspension permanent, so this add-back is no longer operative.

|----------|---------------------|----------------------|---------------|
| **2024** | AMTI up to $232,600 | AMTI above $232,600 | $116,300 |

### AMT Exemption Amounts

| Tax Year | Single / HOH | Married Filing Jointly | Married Filing Separately |
|----------|-------------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| **2025** | $88,100 | $137,000 | $68,500 |
| **2026** | $90,100 | $140,200 | $70,100 |

**Source:** IRS Rev. Proc. 2024-40 (2025); IRS Rev. Proc. 2025-32 (2026); NerdWallet; NTU Foundation.

### Exemption Phase-Out Thresholds

| Tax Year | Single / HOH | MFJ | Phase-Out Rate | Exemption Fully Eliminated |
|----------|-------------|-----|---------------|---------------------------|
| **2025** | $626,350 | $1,252,700 | 25% ($0.25 per $1) | Single: $978,750; MFJ: $1,800,700 |
| **2026** | $500,000 | $1,000,000 | **50%** ($0.50 per $1) | Single: $680,200; MFJ: $1,280,400 |

**Source:** Tax Foundation; Moore Colson; Venable SALT Alert; IRS Rev. Proc. 2025-32.

The 2026 phase-out ranges are substantially compressed compared to 2025. A married couple's AMT exemption is fully eliminated at approximately $1.28 million of AMTI in 2026, compared to roughly $1.8 million under 2025 rules.

## OBBBA Changes

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed into law on July 4, 2025, made several permanent changes to the AMT:

1. **Permanent higher exemptions**: The TCJA's increased AMT exemption amounts (which were set to expire after 2025) are made permanent and continue to be indexed annually for inflation.

2. **Lowered phase-out thresholds**: Beginning in 2026, the income thresholds at which the AMT exemption begins to phase out **revert to 2018 levels** (removing the inflation adjustments that had been applied for 2019-2025). For 2026, this means $500,000 for single filers and $1,000,000 for joint filers, down from $626,350 and $1,252,700 in 2025. These thresholds will be re-indexed for inflation starting in 2027.

3. **Doubled phase-out rate**: The exemption phase-out rate increases from 25 cents per dollar of AMTI above the threshold (pre-OBBBA) to **50 cents per dollar** beginning in 2026. This means the exemption is lost twice as quickly once income exceeds the threshold. An additional $100,000 of income above the MFJ threshold now eliminates $50,000 of exemption (versus $25,000 under the old rate).

4. **Increased SALT cap interaction**: The OBBBA raised the regular-tax SALT deduction cap to $40,000 for 2025 (subject to income-based reduction above certain thresholds). However, SALT remains **entirely non-deductible** for AMT purposes. This widened gap between regular tax and AMT treatment of SALT increases AMT exposure, particularly for taxpayers in high-tax states.

5. **Permanent suspension of personal exemptions**: The TCJA suspension of personal and dependency exemptions is made permanent, so this former AMT add-back remains inoperative.

**Net effect:** More taxpayers, particularly those earning between $750,000 and $1,500,000 (MFJ), will be subject to the AMT beginning in 2026 due to the lower thresholds and faster phase-out.

**Sources:** Venable SALT Alert (August 2025); Charles Schwab OBBBA analysis; Wealthspire Advisors; Moore Colson; Kirsch CPA.

## Agent Rules

- **Always run both calculations.** When estimating a client's or user's tax liability, compute both regular tax and AMT. The AMT is not optional -- taxpayers owe whichever is higher.
- **ISO exercises require AMT modeling.** Before exercising incentive stock options, model the AMT impact of the bargain element. The spread increases AMTI even though no cash is received. Consider same-day sales (disqualifying dispositions) if AMT liability would be severe relative to the paper gain.
- **Track timing vs. exclusion items separately.** AMT caused by timing items (depreciation differences, ISO exercises) generates an AMT credit under Section 53 that carries forward. AMT caused by exclusion items (SALT add-back, private activity bond interest) does **not** generate a credit. This distinction matters for multi-year planning.
- **Use 2026 thresholds for forward planning.** The OBBBA changes make 2026 materially different from 2025. Do not assume continuity in phase-out behavior.
- **SALT is the silent AMT driver.** For taxpayers in high-tax states (CA, NY, NJ, CT), the SALT add-back alone can be sufficient to trigger AMT exposure, especially when combined with the faster 2026 phase-out.
- **Depreciation: check bonus eligibility first.** Bonus depreciation on eligible property (placed in service before 2027) eliminates the AMT depreciation adjustment. Only non-bonus depreciation using GDS/MACRS accelerated methods triggers the adjustment.

## Common Traps

1. **ISO exercise without liquidity planning.** A taxpayer exercises ISOs and holds shares past year-end, triggering AMT on the bargain element. If the stock price declines before sale, the taxpayer owes AMT on "phantom" income (gains that have evaporated). The AMT credit may carry forward, but recovery depends on future tax profiles.

2. **Ignoring the 2026 phase-out cliff.** Taxpayers accustomed to retaining most of their AMT exemption at income levels of $800,000-$1,200,000 (MFJ) may be surprised when the 50% phase-out rate under OBBBA eliminates the exemption at much lower income levels. The exemption is now fully gone at $1,280,400 MFJ (down from $1,800,700 in 2025).

3. **SALT deduction creates false sense of regular-tax savings.** The higher SALT cap ($40,000 in 2025) reduces regular tax, which widens the gap between regular tax and AMT. A larger SALT deduction actually increases the probability of AMT exposure because SALT is fully added back for AMT.

4. **Failing to claim the AMT credit (Section 53).** Taxpayers who paid AMT in prior years due to timing differences (ISO exercises, depreciation adjustments) may have AMT credits that carry forward indefinitely. These credits can offset regular tax in future years when regular tax exceeds tentative minimum tax. Form 8801 (Credit for Prior Year Minimum Tax) must be filed to claim the credit. This credit is frequently overlooked.

5. **Capital gains stacking.** Large long-term capital gains and qualified dividends are taxed at preferential rates under both systems, but they still increase AMTI. This can push a taxpayer into the exemption phase-out zone, causing the exemption to shrink or disappear entirely, thereby increasing overall AMT exposure even though the gains themselves are not re-taxed at 26%/28%.

6. **Depreciation reversal surprise.** The AMT depreciation adjustment is a timing difference: positive in early years (AMT depreciation is slower), negative in later years (AMT depreciation catches up). Taxpayers who sell property before the adjustment fully reverses may lose the benefit of the reversal. Conversely, taxpayers who hold property long enough will see the adjustment reverse and may generate AMT credits.

7. **MFS penalty.** Married Filing Separately taxpayers face half the exemption amount ($70,100 for 2026) and half the rate threshold ($122,250), plus may be required to add additional amounts to AMTI under Section 55(d)(2) in certain situations. MFS status significantly increases AMT risk.
