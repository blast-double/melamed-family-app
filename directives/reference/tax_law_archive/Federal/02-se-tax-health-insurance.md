# Self-Employment Tax and Self-Employed Health Insurance Deduction
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 1401, 1402, 162(l), 164(f)
**Relevant Forms:** Schedule SE (Form 1040), Form 7206, Schedule 1 (Form 1040), Schedule C
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

Self-employed individuals bear the full burden of Social Security and Medicare taxes that would otherwise be split between employer and employee. IRC Section 1401 imposes self-employment (SE) tax; Section 1402 defines the income subject to it. Two above-the-line deductions partially offset this burden: (1) a deduction for 50% of SE tax paid (Section 164(f)), and (2) the self-employed health insurance deduction (Section 162(l)), which allows a 100% deduction of qualifying health, dental, vision, and long-term care premiums. Both deductions reduce adjusted gross income (AGI), making them available regardless of whether the taxpayer itemizes.

---

## Rules

### Self-Employment Tax (IRC Sections 1401, 1402)

**Who owes SE tax.** Any individual with net earnings from self-employment of $400 or more during the tax year. This includes sole proprietors (Schedule C), general partners (Schedule K-1, Box 14, Code A), and members of LLCs taxed as partnerships who are not limited partners. It does not apply to S-corporation distributions (only W-2 wages from the S-corp are subject to FICA).

**Computing net SE income.** Gross SE income (Schedule C net profit or K-1 SE income) is multiplied by 92.35% (i.e., 0.9235) to arrive at net earnings from self-employment. This 7.65% reduction approximates the "employer half" of FICA that W-2 employees never pay on, placing self-employed taxpayers on roughly equal footing.

**Tax rate structure.** The combined SE tax rate is 15.3%, broken into two components:

- **Social Security (OASDI):** 12.4% on net SE income up to the annual wage base.
- **Medicare (HI):** 2.9% on all net SE income with no cap.
- **Additional Medicare Tax:** 0.9% on combined SE income and wages exceeding $200,000 (Single/HOH), $250,000 (MFJ), or $125,000 (MFS). These thresholds are statutory and are **not indexed for inflation**.

**Multiple businesses.** If a taxpayer has more than one business subject to SE tax, net earnings from all businesses are combined on a single Schedule SE. Losses from one business offset income from another.

**Joint returns.** Each spouse with SE income files a separate Schedule SE. Combined SE tax is reported on Schedule 2 (Form 1040), Line 4.

### 50% SE Tax Deduction (IRC Section 164(f))

The taxpayer may deduct one-half (50%) of the total SE tax computed on Schedule SE. This deduction is claimed on Schedule 1 (Form 1040), Line 15, and reduces AGI. It is an above-the-line deduction; no itemizing is required. The deduction reflects the concept that an employer's share of FICA is deductible as a business expense -- self-employed individuals receive an analogous benefit through this adjustment.

**Calculation.** Schedule SE, Line 13 = Line 12 (total SE tax) x 0.50. This amount flows to Schedule 1, Line 15.

### Self-Employed Health Insurance Deduction (IRC Section 162(l))

**Eligible taxpayers.** The deduction is available to:

- Self-employed individuals with Schedule C or Schedule F net profit.
- Partners with net SE earnings reported on Schedule K-1 (Form 1065), Box 14, Code A.
- More-than-2% S-corporation shareholders who receive W-2 wages including health insurance premiums.
- Taxpayers using an optional method to figure net SE earnings on Schedule SE.

**Qualifying coverage.** Premiums for medical, dental, and vision insurance, plus qualifying long-term care insurance (subject to age-based limits). Coverage may extend to the taxpayer's spouse, dependents, and children under age 27 at end of the tax year (even if the child is not a dependent).

**Deduction amount.** 100% of qualifying premiums, subject to two limitations:

1. **Net profit limitation.** The deduction cannot exceed the net profit from the business under which the insurance plan is established (or, for S-corp shareholders, the shareholder's W-2 wages from that S-corp).
2. **Employer-plan eligibility rule.** No deduction is allowed for any month in which the taxpayer was eligible to participate in a health plan subsidized by any employer -- the taxpayer's own employer, a spouse's employer, or an employer of the taxpayer's dependent or under-27 child. Eligibility alone disqualifies the month, regardless of whether the taxpayer actually enrolled.

**Effect on SE tax.** The Section 162(l) deduction does NOT reduce net earnings for purposes of computing SE tax. It is purely an income tax adjustment.

**Claiming the deduction.** Reported on Schedule 1 (Form 1040), Line 17. Calculated on Form 7206 (Self-Employed Health Insurance Deduction). If premiums span multiple businesses, a separate Form 7206 is required for each business.

**Residual premiums.** Any premiums not deductible under Section 162(l) may still be claimed as an itemized medical expense on Schedule A, subject to the 7.5% AGI floor.

### S-Corporation More-Than-2% Shareholder Rules

A shareholder owning more than 2% of an S-corporation's stock (or voting power) on any day of the tax year is treated as self-employed for health insurance purposes. Family attribution rules apply: stock owned by a spouse, parent, grandparent, child, or grandchild is attributed to the shareholder.

**W-2 reporting requirements:**

| W-2 Box | Include Premiums? | Reason |
|---------|-------------------|--------|
| Box 1 (Wages) | **Yes** | Subject to federal income tax |
| Box 3 (Social Security wages) | **No** | Exempt from FICA |
| Box 5 (Medicare wages) | **No** | Exempt from FICA |
| Box 14 (Other) | Optional | Informational; label as "2% SH Health Ins" |

**Mechanics.** The S-corp either pays premiums directly or reimburses the shareholder. Either way, the premium amount must be included in the shareholder's W-2, Box 1. If the policy is in the shareholder's name and the shareholder pays premiums directly, the S-corp must reimburse the shareholder and report the amount on the W-2 -- otherwise the plan is not considered established under the business. The shareholder then deducts the premiums on Form 7206 / Schedule 1, Line 17.

| Component | Rate | Cap |
|-----------|------|-----|
| Social Security (OASDI) | 12.4% | Wage base (see above) |
| Medicare (HI) | 2.9% | No cap |
| Additional Medicare | 0.9% | Above $200K (S), $250K (MFJ), $125K (MFS) -- NOT indexed |

### Long-Term Care Premium Deduction Limits (per person, per year)

| Attained Age Before Close of Tax Year | 2025 Limit | 2026 Limit |
|----------------------------------------|------------|------------|
| 40 or younger | $480 | $500 |
| 41 to 50 | $900 | $930 |
| 51 to 60 | $1,800 | $1,860 |
| 61 to 70 | $4,810 | $4,960 |
| 71 or older | $6,020 | $6,200 |

Only tax-qualified long-term care policies (meeting IRC Section 7702B requirements) are eligible. Most hybrid/linked-benefit life insurance policies do not qualify.

## OBBBA Changes (if applicable)

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), enacted July 4, 2025, did not directly alter SE tax rates, the Section 162(l) health insurance deduction, or long-term care premium deductibility limits. However, several OBBBA provisions have indirect implications for self-employed taxpayers:

- **Income tax rates made permanent.** The seven-bracket structure (10% through 37%) from the TCJA is now permanent, preserving the income tax rates against which SE income is taxed.
- **Excess business loss limitation.** The Section 461(l) limit is made permanent with a lower threshold for 2026: $256,000 (single) / $512,000 (MFJ), down from $313,000 / $626,000 in 2025. Self-employed taxpayers with business losses should note that suspended excess losses carry forward under NOL rules.
- **Premium Tax Credit changes (2026+).** Modifications to ACA Marketplace premium tax credits may affect self-employed individuals who purchase insurance through the Marketplace. Changes to eligibility and subsidy calculations could alter the interplay between PTC and the Section 162(l) deduction.
- **HSA expansion.** OBBBA expanded HDHP safe harbor rules for telehealth services, potentially affecting self-employed taxpayers who pair an HSA with a high-deductible health plan.

## Agent Rules

1. **SE tax applies to Schedule C net profit AND K-1 SE income (Box 14, Code A).** Always check both sources when computing SE tax for taxpayers with multiple businesses.
2. **Multiply net SE income by 92.35% before applying rates.** Never apply the 15.3% rate to gross Schedule C profit.
3. **The 50% SE tax deduction (Section 164(f)) goes on Schedule 1, Line 15.** The 162(l) health insurance deduction goes on Schedule 1, Line 17. They are separate deductions.
4. **The Section 162(l) deduction does NOT reduce SE income.** It only reduces income tax. Do not subtract it when computing Schedule SE.
5. **Month-by-month eligibility test for 162(l).** If the taxpayer was eligible for an employer plan in January but not February through December, only 11 months of premiums are deductible.
6. **LTC limits are per-person.** A married couple where both spouses are age 55 can deduct up to $3,600 (2025) or $3,720 (2026) combined under Section 162(l), assuming premiums equal or exceed those amounts.
7. **S-corp shareholders: Box 1 only, never Boxes 3/5.** Premiums in Box 3 or Box 5 trigger unnecessary FICA tax. Always verify W-2 treatment before computing the deduction.
8. **Net profit limitation is per-business.** If a taxpayer has two businesses with separate health plans, each plan's deduction is limited to the net profit (or W-2 wages) from its respective business. Use separate Form 7206 for each.
9. **Additional Medicare Tax threshold is NOT indexed.** The $200K/$250K/$125K thresholds have been static since 2013. Do not adjust them for inflation.

## Common Traps

1. **Applying 15.3% to gross income.** The 92.35% multiplier must be applied first. Omitting it overstates SE tax.
2. **Deducting health insurance premiums against SE tax.** Section 162(l) reduces income tax only. The deduction cannot be used to reduce net earnings on Schedule SE.
3. **Taking Section 162(l) for months with employer-plan eligibility.** Even if the taxpayer did not enroll in a spouse's employer plan, mere eligibility in any given month disqualifies that month's premiums.
4. **Exceeding the net profit cap.** The health insurance deduction cannot exceed net profit from the specific business under which the plan is established. A taxpayer with $8,000 in net profit cannot deduct $12,000 in premiums under Section 162(l) (the excess $4,000 can go to Schedule A if itemizing).
5. **Double-counting on Schedule A.** Premiums deducted on Schedule 1, Line 17 must NOT also be included in the Schedule A medical expense calculation.
6. **S-corp W-2 errors.** Including premiums in Boxes 3 and 5 creates FICA liability. Including premiums in Box 12 (code DD) instead of Box 1 fails to establish the deduction. The S-corp must also actually pay or reimburse premiums for the plan to be "established under the business."
7. **Ignoring the Additional Medicare Tax.** High-earning self-employed taxpayers often forget the 0.9% surtax. It is calculated on Form 8959 and is NOT withheld from SE income -- it must be estimated and paid quarterly.
8. **LTC limits confused with total deduction limits.** The age-based limits apply only to long-term care premiums within the Section 162(l) deduction. Medical/dental/vision premiums are not subject to age-based caps.
9. **Family attribution for S-corp shareholders.** A taxpayer who owns 0% of an S-corp but whose spouse owns 3% is treated as a more-than-2% shareholder. Failure to apply attribution can result in incorrect W-2 and deduction treatment.
