# Trust and Fiduciary Income Taxation
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 641-679, 1(e)
**Relevant Forms:** Form 1041, Schedule K-1 (Form 1041), Form 8960, Form 7004, Form 8855
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

Trusts and estates are separate taxable entities under Subchapter J of the Internal Revenue Code (Sections 641-692). The defining feature of trust taxation is its dramatically compressed rate structure: a trust reaches the top 37% federal income tax bracket at just $15,650 of taxable income in 2025, compared to over $609,350 for a single individual filer. This compression, codified in Section 1(e), creates both a planning imperative and a trap for the unwary.

The tax system classifies trusts into several categories -- simple trusts, complex trusts, and grantor trusts -- each with distinct rules governing how income is taxed and to whom. The central mechanism is Distributable Net Income (DNI), defined in Section 643, which serves as both a cap on the trust's distribution deduction and a measure of the character of income passing through to beneficiaries.

Sources: IRS Instructions for Form 1041 (2025), irs.gov/instructions/i1041; SmartAsset, "Trust Tax Rates and Exemptions for 2026," smartasset.com/taxes/trust-tax-rates.

## Rules

### Trust Classification

**Simple Trust (Section 651-652).** A trust qualifies as a "simple trust" for any taxable year in which it:
- Is required to distribute all income currently to beneficiaries
- Makes no distributions of corpus (principal)
- Makes no charitable contributions from trust income

The trust receives a distribution deduction under Section 651 equal to the lesser of (a) income required to be distributed currently or (b) DNI (computed without tax-exempt income). Beneficiaries include their proportionate share of DNI in gross income under Section 652, retaining the character of the income as it existed in the trust (e.g., dividends remain dividends, interest remains interest). A trust may be simple in one year and complex in another depending on the terms of the governing instrument and actual distributions.

**Complex Trust (Section 661-662).** Any trust that is not a simple trust is a complex trust. Complex trusts may:
- Accumulate income rather than distributing it currently
- Make distributions of corpus to beneficiaries
- Make charitable contributions under Section 642(c)

The distribution deduction under Section 661 equals the lesser of (a) actual distributions made during the taxable year or (b) DNI. Capital gains are generally retained at the trust level unless the governing instrument or state law allocates them to income or the trustee exercises discretion to include them in DNI. Beneficiaries report their share under Section 662.

**Grantor Trust (Sections 671-679).** When the grantor retains certain powers or interests, the trust is disregarded for income tax purposes, and all items of income, deduction, and credit are reported on the grantor's individual return. A trust is a grantor trust if the grantor retains any of the following:
- A reversionary interest exceeding 5% of trust assets (Section 673)
- Power to revoke the trust and reclaim corpus (Section 676) -- this makes all revocable living trusts grantor trusts
- Power to control beneficial enjoyment (Section 674)
- Certain administrative powers that benefit the grantor (Section 675), including the power to substitute assets of equivalent value
- Power to distribute income to the grantor or grantor's spouse (Section 677)
- Trust funded by a U.S. person with a foreign trust having a U.S. beneficiary (Section 679)

If Section 678 applies, a beneficiary (rather than the grantor) may be treated as the deemed owner. The Intentionally Defective Grantor Trust (IDGT) exploits the mismatch between income tax and estate tax treatment: the grantor retains a power (typically the swap power under Section 675(4)) that causes grantor trust status for income tax purposes, while the trust assets remain outside the grantor's gross estate for estate tax purposes. The grantor's payment of income tax on trust earnings further reduces the taxable estate without constituting a gift.

Sources: Treas. Reg. Section 1.651(a)-1 through 1.652(b)-2; Freeman Law, "Internal Revenue Code's Grantor Trust Rules," freemanlaw.com/grantor-trusts; The Tax Adviser, "The Grantor Trust Rules: An Exploited Mismatch," Nov. 2021.

### Distributable Net Income (DNI) -- Section 643

DNI is the linchpin of trust taxation. It is computed as the trust's taxable income with the following modifications:
- No deduction for distributions (Sections 651, 661)
- No personal exemption
- Capital gains excluded (unless allocated to income, distributed to beneficiaries, or used for charitable purposes)
- Capital losses excluded (except in the trust's final year)
- Tax-exempt interest included (net of allocable expenses)

DNI serves two critical functions: (1) it caps the trust's distribution deduction, preventing trusts from deducting more than their economic income, and (2) it determines the amount and character of income taxable to beneficiaries. Income retains its character as it passes through -- ordinary income, qualified dividends, capital gains (if included in DNI), and tax-exempt interest maintain their respective tax treatment on the beneficiary's return.

Sources: IRC Section 643(a); Belfint Lyons & Shuman, "Complex and Simple Trusts: A High-Level Overview," revenuefiles.delaware.gov; Greenleaf Trust, "Distributable Net Income," greenleaftrust.com/missives/distributable-net-income.

### Section 645 Election

Section 645 allows a qualified revocable trust (QRT) to be treated as part of the decedent's estate for federal income tax purposes. The election is made by filing Form 8855 and provides several advantages:
- **Fiscal year selection**: Trusts normally must use a calendar year; the election permits a fiscal year
- **Estimated tax exemption**: No estimated taxes required for up to two years after death
- **S corporation eligibility**: The trust can hold S corporation stock as part of the estate without a QSST or ESBT election
- **Higher exemption amount**: The estate's $600 personal exemption versus the trust's $100 or $300
- **Charitable set-aside deduction**: Available to estates but not trusts under Section 642(c)

The election period runs for two years after the date of death if no estate tax return is required, or until six months after the final determination of estate tax liability if Form 706 is filed.

Sources: The Tax Adviser, "The Sec. 645 Election to Treat a Trust as Part of the Estate," May 2024; IRS Instructions for Form 1041 (2025).

### Charitable Remainder Trusts (Section 664)

A charitable remainder trust (CRT) -- either an annuity trust (CRAT) or unitrust (CRUT) -- is exempt from income tax under Section 664(c)(1). Distributions to non-charitable beneficiaries follow a mandatory four-tier ordering system under Section 664(b):

1. **Tier 1 -- Ordinary income**: Current and accumulated undistributed ordinary income
2. **Tier 2 -- Capital gains**: Current and accumulated undistributed capital gains (subcategorized by rate: 28% collectibles gain, unrecaptured Section 1250 gain, and standard long-term gain)
3. **Tier 3 -- Other income**: Tax-exempt interest and other non-taxable income
4. **Tier 4 -- Corpus**: Return of principal (non-taxable)

Each tier must be exhausted before distributions are deemed to come from the next tier. CRTs are not eligible to be ESBTs and cannot hold S corporation stock.

Sources: IRC Section 664(b); The Tax Adviser, "Planning with Charitable Remainder Trusts," Sept. 2025; IRS Publication on CRT Tier Rules, irs.gov/pub/irs-tege/eotopicg96.pdf.

### QSSTs and ESBTs -- S Corporation Trust Shareholders

Two trust structures are eligible to hold S corporation stock:

**Qualified Subchapter S Trust (QSST)**: Must have only one current income beneficiary, must distribute all income currently, and the beneficiary must elect QSST status. The beneficiary is treated as the owner of the S corporation stock portion and reports the S corporation income on their individual return.

**Electing Small Business Trust (ESBT)**: May have multiple beneficiaries (limited to individuals, estates, and charities). The S corporation income is taxed in a separate portion of the trust at the highest individual marginal rate (37%). No interest in an ESBT may be acquired by purchase. QSSTs, tax-exempt trusts, and CRTs are ineligible for ESBT treatment.

Sources: BDO, "Trusts as S Corporation Shareholders," Tax Adviser, May 2022; IRC Sections 1361(c)(2), 1361(d), 1361(e).

### Form 1041 Filing Requirements

- **Due date**: April 15 following the close of the calendar tax year (fiscal year trusts: 15th day of the 4th month after year-end)
- **Extension**: Automatic 5.5-month extension via Form 7004 (extends to September 30 for calendar year filers)
- **Schedule K-1**: Must be furnished to each beneficiary; reports the beneficiary's share of income, deductions, and credits
- **K-1 penalties**: $340 per failure to provide a timely or correct K-1; maximum $4,098,500 per calendar year; doubled to $680 (or 10% of reportable amounts) for intentional disregard
- **Estimated taxes**: Required if the trust expects to owe $1,000 or more in tax; filed on Form 1041-ES

Sources: IRS Instructions for Form 1041 (2025), irs.gov/instructions/i1041; IRS Schedule K-1 (Form 1041) 2025.

| | 2025 | 2026 |
|---|---|---|
| 0% rate | $0 - $3,250 | $0 - $3,300 |
| 15% rate | $3,250 - $15,900 | $3,300 - $16,250 |
| 20% rate | Over $15,900 | Over $16,250 |

### Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) -- Section 1411

| Item | 2025 | 2026 |
|---|---|---|
| NIIT rate | 3.8% | 3.8% |
| Trust/estate AGI threshold | $15,650 | $16,000 |
| Applies to | Undistributed NII above threshold | Undistributed NII above threshold |

The NIIT threshold for trusts equals the dollar amount at which the 37% bracket begins. For individuals, NIIT thresholds ($200,000 single / $250,000 MFJ) are not indexed for inflation. Grantor trusts and charitable trusts are exempt from the NIIT at the entity level, though the grantor remains individually subject.

## OBBBA Changes

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), enacted in 2025, made several changes relevant to trust and fiduciary taxation:

- **Permanent exemption increase**: The federal estate, gift, and GST exemption was permanently set at $15,000,000 per individual effective January 1, 2026, indexed for inflation beginning in 2027. This replaces the TCJA's temporary increase that was set to sunset.
- **2/37ths itemized deduction reduction**: Beginning in 2026, trusts and estates with income above the 37% bracket threshold are subject to a reduction in itemized deductions equal to 2/37ths. This can modestly erode the Section 642(c) charitable deduction at the trust level.
- **Income tax rates preserved**: The seven-bracket structure and 10%-37% rate schedule were maintained without change. Trust brackets continue to be indexed for inflation.

The permanence of the higher exemption shifts planning emphasis from transfer tax minimization toward income tax efficiency, basis step-up optimization, and trust distribution planning.

Sources: Katten, "Planning Considerations for the Rest of 2025 and Into 2026," katten.com; Farrell Fritz, "Estate Planning Outlook: 2026 Projected IRS Thresholds."

## Agent Rules

1. **Trust type determines everything.** Before analyzing any trust tax question, determine: Is it a grantor trust, a simple trust, or a complex trust? Grantor trusts are invisible for income tax -- report on the grantor's 1040. Simple and complex trusts file Form 1041.
2. **DNI is the gatekeeper.** The distribution deduction can never exceed DNI. When calculating a trust's taxable income, always compute DNI first under Section 643 before determining the distribution deduction.
3. **Character preservation.** Income distributed to beneficiaries retains its character. Track the components of DNI (ordinary income, qualified dividends, tax-exempt interest, capital gains if included) separately through to the K-1.
4. **Distribution timing matters.** For complex trusts, distributions made within 65 days of year-end can be elected to be treated as made on the last day of the prior tax year under Section 663(b) (the "65-day rule"). Always check whether this election has been or should be made.
5. **NIIT stacks on top.** At trust-level income above $15,650 (2025) / $16,000 (2026), the combined top rate on undistributed net investment income is 40.8% (37% + 3.8%). Distributing income to beneficiaries below the individual NIIT thresholds can eliminate the trust-level NIIT.
6. **Grantor trust status ends at death.** When the grantor of a revocable trust dies, the trust becomes irrevocable and begins filing Form 1041 as a non-grantor trust (unless a Section 645 election is made). This transition is a critical planning moment.
7. **S corporation stock in trusts.** Verify eligibility: grantor trusts qualify automatically; post-death trusts have a two-year window before requiring a QSST or ESBT election. A Section 645 election can extend the holding period.
8. **CRT distributions follow the four-tier rule.** Never assume a CRT distribution is all ordinary income or all return of capital. Apply the Section 664(b) ordering rules strictly.

## Common Traps

1. **Ignoring the compressed brackets.** Retaining $50,000 of income in a non-grantor trust subjects virtually all of it to the top 37% rate plus NIIT. A beneficiary in the 22% bracket would owe dramatically less on the same income. Failure to plan distributions is the single most common trust tax error.

2. **Confusing trust accounting income with DNI.** Trust accounting income (fiduciary accounting income under the governing instrument and state law) and DNI (a tax concept) are different. Capital gains may be part of one but not the other. Applying the wrong measure produces incorrect deductions and K-1 allocations.

3. **Missing the grantor trust transition.** When a grantor dies, the trust's tax status changes immediately. Trustees who continue reporting on the decedent's SSN after the date of death, or who fail to obtain an EIN for the now-separate trust entity, create reporting errors and potential penalties.

4. **Overlooking the 65-day election.** Complex trusts can make distributions in the first 65 days of the following year and elect to treat them as prior-year distributions under Section 663(b). Missing this window forfeits a year of distribution planning flexibility.

5. **NIIT on undistributed income.** The NIIT applies to trusts at the $15,650/$16,000 threshold on undistributed net investment income. Distributing investment income to beneficiaries removes it from the trust's NIIT base. Trustees who accumulate investment income without considering NIIT exposure overpay tax.

6. **K-1 reporting failures.** The $340-per-K-1 penalty for late or incorrect schedules is applied per beneficiary, per failure. A trust with five beneficiaries and two errors per K-1 could face $3,400 in penalties. Intentional disregard doubles the penalty with no cap.

7. **Capital gains trapped at entity level.** Unless the governing instrument, state law, or trustee discretion permits allocating capital gains to income or including them in distributions, capital gains remain taxed at the trust's compressed rates. Review the trust instrument and applicable state law for flexibility.

8. **Section 645 election missed.** The election must be made on the trust's first Form 1041 filed for the year of the decedent's death. A late filing forfeits the fiscal year, estimated tax exemption, and S corporation holding benefits.
