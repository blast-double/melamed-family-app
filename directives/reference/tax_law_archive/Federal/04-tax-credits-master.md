# Tax Credits Master Reference
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 21, 24, 25A, 25B, 32, 36B, 38, 41
**Relevant Forms:** Form 2441, Form 8812, Form 8863, Form 8880, Schedule EIC, Form 8962, Form 3800, Form 6765
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

Federal tax credits reduce tax liability dollar-for-dollar, making them substantially more valuable than deductions of equivalent size. Credits fall into two categories: **refundable** credits (paid out even when they exceed the taxpayer's liability) and **nonrefundable** credits (limited to the tax owed). The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed into law in July 2025, made permanent several TCJA provisions that were scheduled to sunset after 2025 and introduced significant changes to credit amounts, phase-out structures, and energy-related incentives. This document covers all major individual and business credits for tax years 2025 and 2026, incorporating OBBBA changes.

---

## Rules

### Refundable vs. Nonrefundable

- **Refundable credits** generate a refund if they exceed the taxpayer's income tax liability. Key refundable credits: EITC (fully), ACTC (partially refundable portion of the CTC), AOTC (40% refundable), and the Premium Tax Credit.
- **Nonrefundable credits** can only reduce tax liability to zero. Key nonrefundable credits: Child and Dependent Care Credit (Section 21), Lifetime Learning Credit, Saver's Credit, and the main (non-ACTC) portion of the Child Tax Credit.
- **Ordering rule**: Nonrefundable credits are applied first to reduce tax liability, then refundable credits are applied. Refundable credits that exceed remaining liability produce a refund.

### General Limitations

- Credits cannot be used to reduce self-employment tax (with limited exceptions for the EITC, which offsets income tax liability and generates refunds independently).
- Most credits require a valid Social Security Number (SSN) for both the taxpayer and qualifying individuals. The OBBBA tightened SSN requirements for the CTC specifically.
- Married Filing Separately status disqualifies taxpayers from the EITC, education credits, and the Child and Dependent Care Credit (with narrow exceptions).

## Key Thresholds and Figures

### 1. Child Tax Credit (IRC Section 24)

**Form:** 8812 | **Type:** Partially refundable

The OBBBA permanently increased the CTC from $2,000 to **$2,200 per qualifying child under age 17**, effective for tax year 2025. Beginning in 2026, the credit amount is indexed to inflation for the first time in the credit's history. The refundable portion (Additional Child Tax Credit, or ACTC) is capped at **$1,700 per child** for 2025, also inflation-adjusted thereafter. The earned income threshold for the ACTC remains $2,500.

| Parameter | 2025 | 2026 |
|-----------|------|------|
| Maximum CTC per child | $2,200 | $2,200 (inflation-adjusted) |
| Maximum ACTC (refundable portion) | $1,700 | Inflation-adjusted |
| Phase-out start (MFJ) | $400,000 | $400,000 |
| Phase-out start (all others) | $200,000 | $200,000 |
| Phase-out rate | $50 per $1,000 excess MAGI | $50 per $1,000 excess MAGI |
| Earned income floor for ACTC | $2,500 | $2,500 |

**Other Dependents Credit (ODC):** $500 per non-child dependent (e.g., older children, elderly parents). The OBBBA made this credit permanent; it was previously set to expire after 2025. Subject to the same phase-out thresholds as the CTC.

**SSN requirement (OBBBA):** Starting in 2025, at least one parent claiming the CTC must have a valid SSN. For joint filers, only one spouse needs an SSN. All qualifying children must have SSNs (this was a temporary requirement that the OBBBA made permanent).

*Sources: Tax Foundation (2025); Doeren Mayhew (2025); Kitces.com (2025); TurboTax (2025)*

### 2. Child and Dependent Care Credit (IRC Section 21)

**Form:** 2441 | **Type:** Nonrefundable

This credit offsets employment-related expenses for the care of qualifying individuals (children under 13 or disabled dependents) to enable the taxpayer to work. The OBBBA significantly enhanced the credit percentage structure beginning in 2025.

| Parameter | 2025 (OBBBA) | Pre-OBBBA |
|-----------|-------------|-----------|
| Maximum credit rate | **50%** | 35% |
| Minimum credit rate (floor) | 20% | 20% |
| Eligible expenses: 1 dependent | $3,000 | $3,000 |
| Eligible expenses: 2+ dependents | $6,000 | $6,000 |
| Maximum credit (1 dependent, lowest AGI) | $1,500 | $1,050 |
| Maximum credit (2+ dependents, lowest AGI) | $3,000 | $2,100 |

**OBBBA phase-down structure (effective 2025):**
- **50% rate** for AGI up to $15,000 (single) / $30,000 (MFJ)
- Reduced by 1 percentage point per $2,000 of AGI above $15,000, **but not below 35%**
- **35% rate** for AGI of $75,000 (single) / $150,000 (MFJ)
- Further reduced by 1 percentage point per $2,000 (single) or $4,000 (MFJ) above those thresholds, **but not below 20%**
- **20% floor** reached at AGI of $103,000 (single) / $206,000 (MFJ)

Both spouses must have earned income to claim the credit, with exceptions for full-time students and disabled spouses. The credit remains **nonrefundable**, which means low-income families with little or no income tax liability receive no benefit.

Additionally, the OBBBA increased the Dependent Care Assistance Program (DCAP / dependent care FSA) annual exclusion from $5,000 to **$7,500** per household, effective 2026.

*Sources: SmartAsset (2026); Newfront (2025); TPC (2025); WesternCPE (2025)*

### 3. American Opportunity Tax Credit (IRC Section 25A)

**Form:** 8863 | **Type:** 40% refundable

Available for the first four years of post-secondary education. Calculated as 100% of the first $2,000 plus 25% of the next $2,000 in qualified education expenses, for a maximum of **$2,500 per eligible student per year**. Up to 40% of the credit ($1,000) is refundable.

| Parameter | 2025-2026 |
|-----------|-----------|
| Maximum credit | $2,500 per eligible student |
| Refundable portion | 40% (up to $1,000) |
| Phase-out (Single/HoH) | $80,000 - $90,000 MAGI |
| Phase-out (MFJ) | $160,000 - $180,000 MAGI |
| Maximum years | First 4 years of post-secondary |
| Enrollment requirement | At least half-time |

**Qualified expenses:** Tuition, required enrollment fees, and required books/supplies/equipment. Room, board, transportation, and medical costs do not qualify.

The OBBBA did not modify the AOTC. Phase-out thresholds are **not indexed to inflation** and have remained unchanged since the credit's creation in 2009.

*Sources: IRS.gov (2025); SK Financial (2026); TaxFyle (2025)*

### 4. Lifetime Learning Credit (IRC Section 25A)

**Form:** 8863 | **Type:** Nonrefundable

The LLC provides 20% of the first $10,000 in qualified education expenses, for a maximum of **$2,000 per tax return** (not per student). No limit on the number of years it can be claimed, and there is no half-time enrollment requirement.

| Parameter | 2025-2026 |
|-----------|-----------|
| Maximum credit | $2,000 per return |
| Phase-out (Single/HoH) | $80,000 - $90,000 MAGI |
| Phase-out (MFJ) | $160,000 - $180,000 MAGI |
| Year limit | None |

A taxpayer cannot claim both the AOTC and LLC for the same student in the same year. However, the AOTC may be claimed for one student while the LLC is claimed for another student on the same return.

*Sources: IRS.gov (2025); SK Financial (2026)*

### 5. Saver's Credit / Saver's Match (IRC Section 25B)

**Form:** 8880 | **Type:** Nonrefundable (credit); direct deposit (match starting 2027)

The Saver's Credit encourages low- and moderate-income taxpayers to contribute to retirement accounts by providing a credit of 10%, 20%, or 50% of retirement contributions up to $2,000 ($4,000 for MFJ). The credit is available for 2025 and 2026. Beginning in **tax year 2027**, SECURE 2.0 replaces the Saver's Credit with a **Saver's Match**--a direct federal matching contribution deposited into the taxpayer's retirement account.

**Saver's Credit (2025-2026):**

| Credit Rate | Single | HoH | MFJ |
|-------------|--------|-----|-----|
| 50% | AGI up to $23,750 (est.) | AGI up to $35,625 (est.) | AGI up to $47,500 (est.) |
| 20% | $23,751 - $25,750 (est.) | $35,626 - $38,625 (est.) | $47,501 - $51,500 (est.) |
| 10% | $25,751 - $39,500 (est.) | $38,626 - $59,250 (est.) | $51,501 - $79,000 (est.) |
| 0% | Above $39,500 | Above $59,250 | Above $79,000 |

**Saver's Match (effective 2027):**
- Federal government matches **50% of the first $2,000** in annual retirement contributions per individual
- Maximum match: **$1,000 per person** deposited directly into the retirement account (401(k), 403(b), 457(b), or IRA)
- Phase-out ranges (subject to inflation adjustment): Single $20,500-$35,500; HoH $30,750-$53,250; MFJ $41,000-$71,000
- The match phases out gradually (eliminating the "cliff" effect of the current credit)
- The match is a **contribution**, not a tax credit, meaning it grows tax-deferred in the account

*Sources: Congressional Research Service (2025); NAPA-Net (2026); Retirement Clearinghouse (2025); JD Supra (2025)*

### 6. Premium Tax Credit (IRC Section 36B)

**Form:** 8962 | **Type:** Refundable

The PTC subsidizes health insurance premiums for individuals purchasing coverage through the ACA Marketplace. ARPA (2021) had temporarily eliminated the 400% FPL income cap and reduced applicable percentages, making subsidies larger and available to higher earners. Those enhancements were extended through 2025 but **were not extended by the OBBBA**.

**2025 (enhanced PTCs still in effect):**
- No income cap for eligibility; individuals above 400% FPL pay no more than 8.5% of income
- Lower applicable percentages across all income bands
- Individuals below 150% FPL pay 0% of income toward the benchmark plan

**2026 and beyond (reversion to standard PTC rules):**
- Eligibility limited to households with income between **100% and 400% of FPL**
- Higher applicable percentages (larger premium contributions required from enrollees)
- Indexing of applicable percentages resumes

**OBBBA PTC changes:**
- **Repayment caps eliminated** (effective for tax years beginning after December 31, 2025): Taxpayers who receive excess advance PTCs must repay the full excess amount, regardless of income. Previously, repayment was capped for those below 400% FPL.
- **Income-based SEP restriction** (effective 2026): Individuals enrolling during a special enrollment period based solely on income change (without another qualifying life event) are ineligible for PTCs.
- **Eligibility restrictions for certain non-citizens** (effective 2027): PTC eligibility narrowed to lawful permanent residents, certain Cuban immigrants, and Compact of Free Association migrants, excluding other lawfully present non-citizens.

*Sources: Congressional Research Service (2025); Applied Policy (2025); H&R Block (2025); IRS (2025); Urban Institute (2025)*

### 7. Earned Income Tax Credit (IRC Section 32)

**Form:** Schedule EIC | **Type:** Fully refundable

The EITC is the largest federal anti-poverty program delivered through the tax code. The credit phases in with earned income, reaches a maximum, plateaus, and then phases out at higher income levels.

**2025 EITC:**

| Qualifying Children | Maximum Credit | Phase-out (Single/HoH) | Phase-out (MFJ) |
|---------------------|---------------|------------------------|-----------------|
| 0 | $649 | $19,104 | $26,214 |
| 1 | $4,328 | $50,434 | $57,554 |
| 2 | $7,152 | $57,310 | $64,430 |
| 3+ | $8,046 | $61,555 | $68,675 |

**Investment income limit (2025):** $11,950

**2026 EITC:**

| Qualifying Children | Maximum Credit | Phase-out (Single/HoH) | Phase-out (MFJ) |
|---------------------|---------------|------------------------|-----------------|
| 0 | $664 | $19,540 | $26,820 |
| 1 | $4,427 | $51,593 | $58,863 |
| 2 | $7,316 | $58,629 | $65,899 |
| 3+ | $8,231 | $62,974 | $70,224 |

**Investment income limit (2026):** $12,200

**Key eligibility rules:** Must have earned income (wages, salary, self-employment); must file a return even if no tax is owed; qualifying child must meet relationship, age, residency, and joint-return tests; taxpayer must be a U.S. citizen or resident alien for the full year; MFS filers may claim only if they meet specific separation requirements.

*Sources: IRS.gov EITC Tables (2025); NerdWallet (2025); Kiplinger (2025); Bipartisan Policy Center (2025)*

### 8. General Business Credit (IRC Section 38, Form 3800)

**Form:** 3800 | **Type:** Nonrefundable (with carryover provisions)

The General Business Credit (GBC) is an umbrella credit that aggregates dozens of individual business credits into a single limitation framework. Key component credits include:

- **Research Credit (Section 41):** 20% of qualified research expenses exceeding a base amount, or 14% under the alternative simplified method. Reported on Form 6765. Note: Section 174 amortization rules (5-year domestic / 15-year foreign) apply to R&D expenditures; the OBBBA allowed retroactive expensing of 2022-2024 R&E costs beginning in 2025.
- **Work Opportunity Tax Credit (WOTC, Section 51):** Credit for hiring individuals from targeted groups (veterans, ex-felons, long-term unemployed). Generally 40% of first $6,000 in wages ($2,400 max per employee).
- **Disabled Access Credit (Section 44):** 50% of eligible access expenditures between $250 and $10,250, for a maximum credit of $5,000.
- **Employer-Provided Childcare Credit (Section 45F):** Enhanced by OBBBA beginning in 2026.

**GBC limitation:** The credit cannot exceed the excess of net income tax over the greater of the tentative minimum tax or 25% of net regular tax liability exceeding $25,000.

**Carryover rules:**
- **Carryback:** 1 year (5 years for marginal oil and gas well production credits; 3 years for certain Section 6417(b) credits)
- **Carryforward:** 20 years
- Credits are used on a FIFO basis (oldest credits first)
- Unused qualified business credits remaining after the carryforward period may be taken as a deduction

*Sources: IRS Form 3800 Instructions (2025); 26 U.S.C. Section 38; KPMG (2020)*

## OBBBA Changes

### Credits Enhanced or Extended

1. **Child Tax Credit (Section 24):** Increased from $2,000 to $2,200; indexed to inflation starting 2026; ACTC refundable portion set at $1,700; SSN requirements tightened; Other Dependents Credit made permanent.

2. **Child and Dependent Care Credit (Section 21):** Maximum applicable percentage raised from 35% to 50%; phase-down thresholds restructured with a two-tier reduction formula; DCAP exclusion increased to $7,500 (2026).

3. **Section 48D Semiconductor Credit:** Advanced manufacturing investment tax credit increased from **25% to 35%** for property placed in service after December 31, 2025. The termination date (end of 2026) was not changed, so the enhanced rate benefits projects already under construction.

### Credits Terminated or Rolled Back

4. **Section 30D New Clean Vehicle Credit:** $7,500 maximum credit **terminated for vehicles acquired after September 30, 2025**. Vehicles under a binding contract with payment made by that date remain eligible even if placed in service later.

5. **Section 25E Used Clean Vehicle Credit:** $4,000 maximum credit **terminated for vehicles acquired after September 30, 2025**.

6. **Section 45W Commercial Clean Vehicle Credit:** $7,500/$40,000 maximum credit **terminated for vehicles acquired after September 30, 2025**.

7. **Section 25C Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit:** **Terminated for property placed in service after December 31, 2025** (previously scheduled to expire after 2032).

8. **Section 25D Residential Clean Energy Credit:** **Terminated for expenditures made after December 31, 2025** (previously scheduled to phase down through 2034).

9. **Section 30C Alternative Fuel Vehicle Refueling Property Credit:** Terminated for property placed in service after **June 30, 2026**.

### Premium Tax Credit (Section 36B)

10. Enhanced ARPA subsidies (no income cap, lower applicable percentages) **not extended past 2025**. Standard ACA PTC rules revert for 2026. Repayment caps for excess advance PTCs eliminated. Income-based special enrollment period eligibility for PTCs restricted starting 2026.

## Agent Rules

1. **Credit type determines filing impact.** Always verify whether a credit is refundable, partially refundable, or nonrefundable before advising on expected refund. The EITC and PTC generate refunds; the CDCC and LLC do not.

2. **Phase-out calculations require MAGI, not AGI.** For most credits, modified adjusted gross income (MAGI) governs the phase-out. MAGI definitions vary by credit (e.g., for the AOTC, MAGI adds back foreign earned income exclusion; for the PTC, MAGI includes tax-exempt interest).

3. **CTC requires earned income for refundable portion.** The $2,500 earned income floor applies to the ACTC. Taxpayers with no earned income receive only the nonrefundable CTC (up to tax liability).

4. **Education credits are mutually exclusive per student.** Never claim both AOTC and LLC for the same student in the same year. Evaluate which produces the larger benefit.

5. **EV credit transition rules matter.** For Section 30D, "acquired" means a binding contract plus payment by September 30, 2025. Vehicles placed in service after that date may still qualify if they were acquired before the deadline. Verify the Time of Sale Report from the dealer.

6. **EITC investment income is a hard cutoff.** If disqualified income (interest, dividends, capital gains, rents/royalties not in the ordinary course of business) exceeds $11,950 (2025) or $12,200 (2026), the entire EITC is disallowed--not reduced.

7. **PTC advance payments create reconciliation risk in 2026.** With repayment caps eliminated by OBBBA, any excess advance PTC must be repaid in full. Flag this for clients receiving advance subsidies.

8. **Saver's Credit ends after 2026.** For 2027, advise clients about the Saver's Match, which requires coordination with their retirement plan administrator since the government match is deposited directly into the account.

9. **Form 3800 aggregation.** All GBC component credits must be reported through Form 3800. Do not report individual business credits directly on Form 1040 or 1120 without flowing them through the GBC framework.

## Common Traps

1. **CTC SSN trap (OBBBA).** Under the new rules, a parent filing for the CTC must have a valid SSN. Taxpayers with only an ITIN (Individual Taxpayer Identification Number) can no longer claim the CTC. Up to 2 million children with SSNs may lose eligibility because their parents lack SSNs. This is a significant change from prior law.

2. **CDCC nonrefundability.** The OBBBA raised the maximum credit rate to 50%, but the credit remains nonrefundable. Low-income families with no income tax liability receive zero benefit despite qualifying for the highest rate. Do not assume the enhancement helps all taxpayers equally.

3. **AOTC felony drug conviction disqualification.** A student with a felony drug conviction is ineligible for the AOTC (but may still qualify for the LLC). This is frequently overlooked.

4. **AOTC four-year cliff.** The credit is available only for the first four tax years of post-secondary education. A student who previously claimed four years of Hope Credit or AOTC is permanently ineligible, even if they return to school later.

5. **Double-dipping with education benefits.** Expenses paid with tax-free education benefits (scholarships, 529 distributions, employer tuition assistance) cannot also be used to claim education credits. This is the most common audit trigger for education credits.

6. **EITC and rental income interaction.** Rental income included in disqualified income (rents/royalties not in the ordinary course of business) counts toward the investment income limit. Taxpayers with even modest rental portfolios can be disqualified from the EITC entirely.

7. **PTC cliff in 2026.** With the enhanced subsidies expiring, a taxpayer at 401% FPL goes from paying 8.5% of income (2025) to receiving zero subsidy (2026). This is the sharpest cliff in the individual tax code for 2026. Advise clients to model their projected income carefully for Marketplace enrollment.

8. **GBC passive activity limitation.** Credits attributable to passive activities are subject to the passive activity rules of Section 469 and can only offset tax on passive income. Credits from an S corporation or partnership K-1 require careful analysis of the taxpayer's material participation.

9. **EV credit retroactive risk.** Taxpayers who took delivery of a clean vehicle after September 30, 2025 without a binding contract and payment by that date have no credit eligibility. The IRS has issued guidance requiring both a written binding contract and at least a nominal payment to establish the acquisition date.

10. **Section 25C/25D trap for 2025 installations.** Homeowners who contracted for solar, geothermal, or energy-efficient improvements in 2025 but whose installations were not placed in service until 2026 may lose the credit entirely. The termination applies to property "placed in service" (Section 25C) or "expenditures made" (Section 25D) after December 31, 2025.
