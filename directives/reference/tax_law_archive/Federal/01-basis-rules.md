# Basis of Property: Cost, Adjusted, Gift, and Inherited
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 1011, 1012, 1014, 1015, 1016
**Relevant Forms:** Form 8949, Schedule D, Form 4797, Form 1099-B, Form 1099-DA (digital assets, 2025+), Form 709 (gift tax), Form 706 (estate tax)
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

Basis is the starting point for computing gain or loss on the disposition of any asset. Without it, there is no way to determine taxable income from a sale, exchange, or other disposition. The IRC establishes basis rules across several sections: Section 1012 defines cost basis, Section 1016 provides the rules for adjustments, Section 1015 governs property received by gift, and Section 1014 governs property acquired from a decedent. Section 1011 ties them together by defining "adjusted basis" as the figure used to determine gain or loss under Section 1001.

The rules differ materially depending on *how* the taxpayer acquired the property -- purchase, gift, inheritance, compensation, or exchange. Misapplying basis rules is one of the most common sources of tax errors.

---

## Rules

### Cost Basis (Section 1012)

The basis of property is its **cost** -- the amount paid in cash or other property to acquire it. Cost includes the purchase price plus all acquisition costs: commissions, transfer taxes, title insurance, legal fees, recording fees, and survey costs. For real property, settlement charges that are properly capitalized (not deductible) are added to basis. (IRS Publication 551, December 2025.)

For securities, cost basis includes the purchase price plus brokerage commissions and fees. Brokers are required to track and report cost basis for "covered securities" on Form 1099-B.

### Adjusted Basis (Sections 1011, 1016)

Section 1016 requires the taxpayer to adjust the cost basis of property for certain items that occur during ownership. Adjusted basis is the figure used to compute gain or loss upon disposition.

**Increases to basis:**
- Capital improvements (additions, renovations, restorations that add value, prolong useful life, or adapt property to a new use)
- Assessments for local improvements (sidewalks, roads, sewers)
- Amounts spent to restore property after a casualty loss
- Legal fees to defend or perfect title
- Zoning costs capitalized to the property

**Decreases to basis:**
- Depreciation allowed or allowable (MACRS, straight-line) under Section 167/168
- Section 179 expense deductions
- Bonus depreciation under Section 168(k)
- Casualty or theft losses deducted (and not compensated by insurance)
- Insurance reimbursements received for casualty losses
- Credits previously taken (e.g., energy credits, rehabilitation credits)
- Exclusions from income (e.g., discharge of indebtedness under Section 108)
- Amortization of bond premium

The IRS rule for depreciation is that basis must be reduced by the amount of depreciation **allowable**, even if the taxpayer failed to claim it. (Section 1016(a)(2); IRS Publication 551.) This prevents taxpayers from skipping depreciation deductions and later claiming a higher basis on sale.

**Example (from IRS Publication 551, December 2025):** A factory purchased in 2020 for $82,600 (including fees), with $20,000 in improvements, $14,526 in depreciation taken through 2024, and a $5,000 casualty loss deducted, has an adjusted basis of $78,249 as of January 1, 2025. (Source: IRS Publication 551, December 2025, irs.gov/publications/p551.)

### Gifted Property (Section 1015)

When property is received by **gift**, the basis rules depend on the relationship between the donor's adjusted basis and the property's fair market value at the time of the gift.

**General rule -- gain basis:** The donee takes the donor's adjusted basis (carryover basis). If the donee later sells the property at a gain, the gain is computed using the donor's basis.

**Special rule -- loss basis:** If the FMV of the property at the time of the gift is **less than** the donor's adjusted basis, then for purposes of determining a **loss**, the donee's basis is the FMV at the date of the gift. This prevents the donee from deducting a loss that economically accrued while the donor held the property.

**No gain or loss zone:** If the donee sells the property for an amount between the donor's basis and the FMV at the date of the gift, the donee recognizes **neither gain nor loss**. This is the so-called "dual basis" or "split basis" rule.

**Gift tax adjustment:** If the donor paid gift tax on the transfer, the donee's basis is increased by the portion of gift tax attributable to the net appreciation in the property (the excess of FMV over donor's basis at the time of the gift). The basis increase cannot exceed the FMV at the time of the gift. (Section 1015(d); IRS Publication 551.)

**Example (from IRS Publication 551, December 2025):** A gift of property with a donor's adjusted basis of $20,000 and FMV of $50,000, with $6,220 in gift tax paid (after subtracting the $19,000 annual exclusion for 2025), produces a donee basis of $26,033. (Source: irs.gov/publications/p551.)

The 2025 annual gift tax exclusion is **$19,000** per donee ($38,000 for married couples electing gift-splitting). The 2025 unified estate and gift tax exemption is **$13,990,000**. (Source: Davis Law Carolina, Carryover and Stepped-Up Basis, February 2025.)

### Inherited Property (Section 1014)

Property acquired from a decedent generally receives a **stepped-up basis** equal to the fair market value of the property at the date of the decedent's death. This eliminates all built-in gain (or loss) that accrued during the decedent's lifetime. (Section 1014(a); Forbes, "Understanding The Step-Up In Basis Rules Upon Death," September 2024.)

The step-up applies to a broad range of asset classes: real estate, stocks, bonds, business interests, collectibles, and foreign property. The step-up applies regardless of where the property is located, as long as the beneficiary is a U.S. taxpayer. (Rev. Rul. 84-139; Patel Law Offices, March 2025.)

**Alternate valuation date:** The executor may elect to value estate assets six months after the date of death under Section 2032, which also sets the basis for income tax purposes.

**Critical exception -- Income in Respect of a Decedent (IRD):** Section 1014(c) explicitly provides that the step-up in basis does **NOT** apply to property that constitutes a right to receive IRD under Section 691. Common IRD items include:
- Traditional IRA balances
- 401(k) and other qualified plan balances
- Deferred compensation
- Installment sale obligations (unreported gain portion)
- Accrued but unpaid salary, bonuses, or commissions
- Accounts receivable of a cash-basis decedent

These items are taxable as **ordinary income** to the beneficiary when distributed. The full value of an inherited IRA or 401(k) is subject to income tax -- there is no step-up. (IRS Rev. Rul. 2005-30; Northern Trust, "Inherited IRAs and the IRD Deduction"; Section 1014(c), 26 U.S.C. 1014.)

An IRD deduction under Section 691(c) may be available if estate tax was paid on the IRD items, preventing full double taxation.

### Community Property Step-Up (Section 1014(b)(6))

In community property states (Arizona, California, Idaho, Louisiana, Nevada, New Mexico, Texas, Washington, Wisconsin), **both halves** of community property receive a step-up in basis upon the death of either spouse -- not just the decedent's half. This is a significant advantage over jointly held property in common law states, where only the decedent's half receives the step-up.

Section 1014(b)(6) applies when the property is held "under the community property laws of any State" and at least one-half of the community interest was includible in the decedent's gross estate. The surviving spouse's half is also stepped up, even though it was never part of the decedent's estate for ownership purposes. (IRC Section 1014(b)(6); Oregon State Bar, Tax Section, August 2019; CPA Journal, "Community Property Step-Up in Basis.")

Several states (Alaska, Florida, Kentucky, South Dakota, Tennessee) have enacted "opt-in" community property trust statutes. Whether these trusts qualify for the double step-up under Section 1014(b)(6) remains an open question -- the IRS has not issued formal guidance, though the issue is under active internal consideration. (Jones Foster, "Unlocking the Step-Up: Florida's Community Property Trust Act," 2025.)

### ESPP and ISO Stock Basis

**Employee Stock Purchase Plans (ESPPs):** The basis of ESPP shares is the discounted purchase price paid by the employee. However, upon disposition, additional compensation income may be recognized, which adjusts the effective basis.

- **Qualifying disposition** (held at least 2 years from grant date AND 1 year from purchase date): Ordinary income equals the lesser of (a) the discount at grant date or (b) the actual gain on sale. The remainder is capital gain. Basis for Schedule D purposes is the purchase price plus the ordinary income recognized. (Fidelity Stock Plan Services, 2025; Haven Wealth Planning, January 2025.)
- **Disqualifying disposition** (sold before meeting both holding periods): Ordinary income equals the spread between FMV on the purchase date and the purchase price (the "bargain element"). This amount is included in W-2 wages. Basis on Schedule D equals the purchase price plus the bargain element reported as compensation. (TurboTax, "Employee Stock Purchase Plans"; Orrick, January 2025.)

**Incentive Stock Options (ISOs):** No regular income tax is recognized at exercise (though the spread is an AMT preference item). If the shares are held for at least 2 years from grant and 1 year from exercise (qualifying disposition), the entire gain is long-term capital gain. If sold before meeting both holding periods (disqualifying disposition), the spread at exercise is ordinary income, and basis is adjusted to exercise price plus compensation income recognized. (Navalign, August 2025.)

### Mutual Fund Basis Methods

The IRS permits three primary methods for computing cost basis of mutual fund shares:

1. **First-In, First-Out (FIFO):** Default method. Assumes the oldest shares are sold first. Generally produces the largest gain because the oldest shares typically have the lowest cost.
2. **Specific Identification:** Taxpayer designates which specific shares (tax lots) are being sold. Offers the most flexibility for tax planning. Requires broker confirmation of the specification.
3. **Average Cost:** Available **only** for mutual funds (and certain ETFs classified as RICs). Computed by dividing total cost of all shares by total number of shares held. Once elected for a fund, it must be applied to all future sales from that fund. (T. Rowe Price; Charles Schwab; Vanguard; IRS Publication 551.)

The method must be elected before or at the time of sale. Brokers default to average cost for mutual funds and FIFO for individual stocks and ETFs.

### Cryptocurrency Basis

Digital assets are treated as property for federal tax purposes (IRS Notice 2014-21). Cost basis is the purchase price plus any exchange or network fees.

**Permitted methods:**
- **FIFO (default):** If the taxpayer does not specifically identify which units were sold, the IRS presumes the earliest-acquired units were disposed of first. FIFO has always been the default -- this is not a new 2025 rule. (Recap.io; BitcoinWell, 2025.)
- **Specific identification:** Permitted if the taxpayer maintains **contemporaneous records** identifying the specific units sold, the acquisition date, and the cost of each unit. The specific lot must be identified **before** the trade is executed, not retroactively. (ChainwiseCPA, March 2026.)

**2025 changes -- per-wallet tracking:** Beginning with the 2025 tax year, the IRS requires cost basis to be tracked on a **wallet-by-wallet** (or account-by-account) basis, rather than universally across all wallets. Consistency within a wallet/account for the tax year is required. Different wallets may use different permissible methods. (IRS Revenue Procedure 2024-28; Cryptoworth; ChainwiseCPA.)

**Form 1099-DA:** For 2025 transactions, brokers must report gross proceeds (cost basis reporting is optional). Beginning with 2026 transactions, brokers must report both gross proceeds and adjusted basis for covered digital assets acquired and held within the same broker account. (Recap.io, 2025.)

## OBBBA Changes (if applicable)

No provisions of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), as publicly reported through March 2026, directly modify Sections 1011, 1012, 1014, 1015, or 1016 basis rules. The step-up in basis under Section 1014 remains intact. The TCJA estate and gift tax exemption, which affects gift basis calculations under Section 1015(d), was set to sunset at the end of 2025 but may be extended or made permanent under pending legislation. Monitor legislative developments for any changes to the unified exemption amount and bonus depreciation schedules.

## Agent Rules

- You **cannot** calculate gain or loss without knowing adjusted basis. Before computing any gain or loss, always ask three questions:
  1. **What did the taxpayer pay?** (Cost basis under Section 1012)
  2. **What adjustments have occurred?** (Improvements, depreciation, Section 179, casualty losses, credits -- Section 1016)
  3. **Was the property gifted or inherited?** (If gifted, apply the dual-basis rule of Section 1015. If inherited, apply the step-up of Section 1014 -- but NOT for IRD assets.)
- Never assume basis equals current FMV unless the taxpayer just purchased the property or inherited it (and it is not IRD).
- For rental and business property, always confirm whether depreciation was taken (or should have been taken -- the "allowable" rule applies regardless).
- For securities, always ask which cost basis method applies (FIFO, specific identification, or average cost) before computing gain.
- For crypto, confirm whether the taxpayer has adequate records for specific identification. If not, default to FIFO.
- For ESPP/ISO shares, determine whether the disposition was qualifying or disqualifying before computing basis.

## Common Traps

1. **Step-up does NOT apply to retirement accounts.** IRAs, 401(k)s, 403(b)s, and other qualified plans are IRD under Section 691. The full value is taxable as ordinary income to the beneficiary when distributed. There is no step-up. This is the single most common misconception about inherited property.

2. **Depreciation "allowable" vs. "allowed."** Even if the taxpayer never claimed depreciation on rental or business property, the IRS reduces basis by the amount that *should have been* claimed. Failing to take depreciation costs the taxpayer twice: no deduction was taken, and basis is still reduced.

3. **Gift basis dual-rule confusion.** When FMV at the date of gift is less than the donor's basis, the donee has two different bases depending on whether the sale produces a gain or a loss. If the sale price falls between the two figures, the result is neither gain nor loss -- not a loss computed from the donor's basis.

4. **Community property requires documentation.** The double step-up under Section 1014(b)(6) only applies if the property is actually held as community property. Separate property does not qualify even in community property states. Commingling can destroy community property character.

5. **ESPP/ISO basis errors on Form 1099-B.** Brokers frequently report ESPP and ISO shares with an unadjusted cost basis (the exercise or purchase price only, without the compensation income). Taxpayers must manually adjust the basis on Form 8949 to avoid double-reporting the compensation income.

6. **Crypto basis without records.** If the taxpayer cannot substantiate specific identification, the IRS may disregard the claimed basis entirely and treat the disposition as having zero cost basis. Accuracy-related penalties of 20% may apply.

7. **Installment sale obligations are IRD.** The unreported gain portion of installment notes does not receive a step-up at death. The heir who collects payments must report the deferred gain as ordinary income or capital gain, depending on the character of the original sale.
