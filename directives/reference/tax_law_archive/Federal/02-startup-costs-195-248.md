# Startup Costs, Organizational Costs, and Syndication Fees
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 195, 248, 709
**Relevant Forms:** Form 4562 (Amortization), Schedule C / Form 1065 / Form 1120 / Form 1120-S
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

Costs incurred to investigate, create, or launch a new business are not immediately deductible as ordinary business expenses under Section 162 because the taxpayer is not yet "carrying on" a trade or business. Congress enacted three parallel provisions to allow recovery of these costs:

- **Section 195** -- Startup expenditures (all entity types)
- **Section 248** -- Organizational expenditures (corporations, including S corporations)
- **Section 709** -- Organizational and syndication costs (partnerships, including LLCs taxed as partnerships)

All three provisions share an identical deduction structure: elect to deduct up to $5,000 in the first year the business begins, with the remainder amortized ratably over 180 months (15 years). The $5,000 first-year deduction phases out dollar-for-dollar when total qualifying costs exceed $50,000, and is completely eliminated at $55,000.

---

## Rules

### Section 195 -- Startup Expenditures

**What qualifies.** A startup expenditure is any amount that (26 USC 195(c)(1)):

1. Is paid or incurred in connection with investigating the creation or acquisition of an active trade or business, or creating an active trade or business; AND
2. Would be deductible under Section 162 as an ordinary and necessary business expense if it were paid or incurred after the business began operations.

**Common examples:**
- Market research and analysis of potential markets
- Pre-opening advertising and promotional costs
- Employee training and wages during pre-opening period
- Travel costs to investigate business opportunities
- Professional fees (consultants, attorneys, accountants) for business planning
- Rent and utilities during pre-opening setup

**What does NOT qualify under Section 195:**
- Costs of acquiring specific assets (capitalized separately under Sections 263, 263A, or 197)
- Interest, taxes, or research/experimental expenditures (governed by their own IRC provisions)
- Costs that would not be deductible under Section 162 even after the business began (e.g., fines, political contributions)

**When the business "begins."** The active trade or business begins when the taxpayer starts the activities for which the business was organized -- i.e., when it is in a position to begin generating revenue. Mere preparatory activity (forming entities, printing business cards, attending courses) does not constitute beginning business operations (see *Eason v. Commissioner*).

### Section 248 -- Corporate Organizational Expenditures

**What qualifies.** Organizational expenditures are costs that are (26 USC 248(b); Treas. Reg. 1.248-1(b)):

1. Incident to the creation of the corporation
2. Chargeable to capital account
3. Of a character that would be amortizable over the life of the corporation if its charter limited its life

**Common examples:**
- Legal fees to draft articles of incorporation, bylaws, and organizational minutes
- State filing and incorporation fees
- Fees for temporary directors and organizational meetings
- Accounting fees for setting up the initial corporate books

**What does NOT qualify under Section 248:**
- Costs of issuing or selling stock (commissions, underwriting fees, printing stock certificates) -- these are capital costs that reduce paid-in capital
- Costs of transferring assets to the corporation
- Costs related to reorganization (capitalized under Section 368 rules)

### Section 709 -- Partnership Organizational and Syndication Costs

**Organizational costs** follow the same rules as Section 248, adapted to partnerships (26 USC 709(b)(3); Treas. Reg. 1.709-2(a)):

1. Incident to the creation of the partnership
2. Chargeable to capital account
3. Of a character that, if expended incident to a partnership having an ascertainable life, would be amortizable over that life

**Common examples:**
- Legal fees for drafting the partnership or operating agreement
- Filing fees paid to the state of organization
- Accounting fees for establishing the partnership's books
- Fees for required regulatory filings

**Syndication costs** (costs of selling, marketing, or promoting partnership interests) are permanently capitalized under Section 709(a) and can NEVER be deducted or amortized -- not even upon liquidation of the partnership (Treas. Reg. 1.709-1(b)(3)(i)). Examples include brokerage fees, registration fees, printing costs of offering materials, and legal/accounting fees related to the issuance and marketing of partnership interests.

## Key Thresholds and Figures

| Parameter | Section 195 | Section 248 | Section 709 |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| First-year deduction | Up to $5,000 | Up to $5,000 | Up to $5,000 |
| Phase-out begins | $50,000 total costs | $50,000 total costs | $50,000 total costs |
| Phase-out rate | $1-for-$1 | $1-for-$1 | $1-for-$1 |
| First-year deduction fully eliminated | $55,000+ | $55,000+ | $55,000+ |
| Amortization period for remainder | 180 months | 180 months | 180 months |
| Amortization starts | Month business begins active operations | Month corporation begins business | Month partnership begins business |
| Maximum combined first-year deduction | $5,000 (Sec. 195) + $5,000 (Sec. 248 or 709) = **$10,000** | -- | -- |

**Phase-out formula:**

```
First-year deduction = MAX(0, $5,000 - (Total qualifying costs - $50,000))
Monthly amortization = (Total qualifying costs - First-year deduction) / 180
First-year total = First-year deduction + (Monthly amortization x months in operation)
```

**Worked example:** A corporation incurs $53,000 in startup costs (Sec. 195) and $41,000 in organizational costs (Sec. 248) and begins business on July 1, 2025.

- Sec. 195 first-year deduction: $5,000 - ($53,000 - $50,000) = $2,000
- Sec. 195 amortization: ($53,000 - $2,000) / 180 = $283.33/month x 6 months = $1,700
- Sec. 195 total Year 1: $2,000 + $1,700 = **$3,700**
- Sec. 248 first-year deduction: $5,000 (under $50,000 threshold, no phase-out)
- Sec. 248 amortization: ($41,000 - $5,000) / 180 = $200/month x 6 months = $1,200
- Sec. 248 total Year 1: $5,000 + $1,200 = **$6,200**
- Combined Year 1 deduction: **$9,900**

### Election Mechanics

- **No separate statement required.** The election is deemed made by deducting the costs on the taxpayer's first return for the year business begins (Treas. Reg. 1.195-1(b); Treas. Reg. 1.248-1(c); Treas. Reg. 1.709-1(b)(2)).
- **Deemed election.** Unless the taxpayer clearly capitalizes the costs on the return, the IRS presumes the election has been made.
- **Irrevocable.** Once made, the election cannot be revoked without IRS consent.
- **Forgoing the election.** A taxpayer may choose to capitalize all costs and forgo the deduction/amortization entirely. The capitalized costs are recovered only upon disposition or liquidation.

### Disposition / Abandonment Rules

**Business disposed of or liquidated (Secs. 195(b)(2), 248(a)(2), 709(b)(2)):** If the trade or business is completely disposed of before the end of the 180-month amortization period, the entire remaining unamortized balance is deductible as a loss under Section 165.

**Startup abandoned before business begins:** If the taxpayer incurs investigation and startup costs but never actually begins business, the costs are treated as a capital loss in the year the effort is abandoned -- not an ordinary deduction under Section 195 (IRS Publication 535). The costs cannot be amortized because the business never began operations, and the Section 195 election (which requires business to begin) was never triggered.

## OBBBA Changes

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (Pub. L. 119-21, signed July 4, 2025) did not directly amend Sections 195, 248, or 709. The $5,000 / $50,000 / 180-month structure remains unchanged.

**Indirect relevance:** The OBBBA's amendment to Section 174 (research and experimental expenditures) via new Section 174A is cross-referenced in Section 195(c)(1), which excludes R&E expenditures from the definition of startup costs. The OBBBA restored immediate expensing for domestic R&E costs (effective for tax years beginning after December 31, 2024), meaning costs that qualify as R&E under Section 174/174A should be claimed there rather than treated as startup costs under Section 195. Foreign R&E continues to be amortized over 15 years.

The OBBBA also permanently extended 100% bonus depreciation, which affects asset acquisition costs that are excluded from Sections 195/248/709 and capitalized separately.

## Agent Rules

1. **Classify costs carefully.** Before applying Sections 195/248/709, verify that the cost (a) is not deductible under another provision (Section 162 post-opening, Section 174/174A for R&E, Section 263A for inventory), and (b) is not an asset acquisition cost (capitalized separately).
2. **Track the $50,000 threshold per section.** The phase-out applies independently to Section 195 costs and to Section 248/709 costs. A taxpayer can claim up to $10,000 in combined first-year deductions ($5,000 startup + $5,000 organizational) if both categories stay under their respective $50,000 thresholds.
3. **Identify the month business begins.** Amortization starts in that month, not the month costs are incurred. First-year amortization is prorated for the number of months the business operates during the tax year.
4. **Never amortize syndication costs.** Partnership syndication costs (Section 709(a)) are permanently capitalized. They cannot be deducted even upon partnership liquidation.
5. **Distinguish pre-opening from post-opening.** The same type of expense (e.g., advertising) is a startup cost under Section 195 if incurred before the business begins, but an ordinary Section 162 deduction if incurred after. Misclassification means either losing the immediate deduction or incorrectly amortizing a currently deductible expense.
6. **Watch for abandoned startups.** If the business never starts, Section 195 does not apply. Costs become a capital loss -- not an ordinary loss, not amortizable.
7. **Form 4562 for amortization.** Report amortization on Part VI of Form 4562. The amortization start date is the month business begins, the period is 180 months, and the code section is 195, 248, or 709.
8. **Partnerships pass through.** Startup and organizational deductions/amortization are computed at the partnership level and reported to partners on Schedule K-1 (Form 1065).

## Common Traps

1. **Confusing startup costs with organizational costs.** They are governed by different code sections and tracked separately. A taxpayer can claim $5,000 for each category. Lumping them together means forfeiting up to $5,000 in first-year deductions.

2. **Exceeding $50,000 unknowingly.** The dollar-for-dollar phase-out is steep. At $55,000 in costs, the entire $5,000 first-year deduction is lost. Planning opportunity: defer discretionary pre-opening costs until after the business begins to keep Section 195 costs below the threshold while deducting those same costs as ordinary Section 162 expenses.

3. **Treating pre-opening asset purchases as startup costs.** Equipment, furniture, vehicles, and other depreciable assets acquired before the business begins are capitalized and depreciated under their own rules (Sections 167, 168, 179). They are not startup costs under Section 195.

4. **Deducting syndication costs.** Partnership syndication costs can never be deducted or amortized. They are capitalized permanently and reduce gain (or increase loss) only when the partnership interest is disposed of. Attempting to deduct them as organizational costs or management fees will be disallowed (*Egolf v. Commissioner*, 87 T.C. 34 (1986)).

5. **Forgetting partial-year proration.** In the first year, amortization covers only the months from business inception through December. A business that opens in November gets only 2 months of amortization plus the first-year deduction. Failing to prorate overstates the deduction.

6. **Missing the abandoned-startup distinction.** If the business never begins, Section 195 does not apply at all. The costs are a capital loss, subject to the $3,000 annual net capital loss limitation for individuals. Taxpayers sometimes incorrectly claim an ordinary loss.

7. **Stock issuance costs in a corporation.** Costs of issuing stock (SEC registration, underwriter commissions, printing) are NOT organizational costs under Section 248. They reduce paid-in capital and are not deductible or amortizable.

8. **Expansion vs. new business.** Costs to investigate expanding an existing business into a substantially similar line of business may be currently deductible under Section 162. Only costs to investigate or create a new, unrelated trade or business fall under Section 195 (Rev. Rul. 99-23).

## Cross-References

- **Section 162** -- Trade or business expenses (ordinary deductions available once business begins)
- **Section 165** -- Losses (governs deduction of unamortized costs upon disposition/abandonment)
- **Section 174 / 174A** -- Research and experimental expenditures (excluded from Section 195; OBBBA restored immediate domestic expensing)
- **Section 179** -- Expensing of depreciable assets (alternative to depreciation for asset purchases; not a startup cost)
- **Section 197** -- Amortization of goodwill and certain intangibles (15-year period aligns with 180-month startup amortization)
- **Section 263** -- Capital expenditures (governs costs that must be capitalized rather than deducted)
- **Section 351** -- Transfer of property to a corporation (asset transfer costs are not organizational costs under Section 248)
