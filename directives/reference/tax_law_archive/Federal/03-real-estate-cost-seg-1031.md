# Real Estate: Cost Segregation and Section 1031 Like-Kind Exchanges
**IRC Section(s):** Sections 168(k), 1031, 1245, 1250
**Relevant Forms:** Form 4562, Form 8824, Form 3115
**Tax Years Covered:** 2025-2026
**Last Updated:** March 2026

---

## Overview

Cost segregation and Section 1031 like-kind exchanges are two of the most powerful tax deferral and acceleration strategies available to real estate investors. Cost segregation reclassifies building components into shorter recovery periods to accelerate depreciation deductions, while Section 1031 allows indefinite deferral of capital gains on the exchange of qualifying real property. These strategies interact in important ways: cost segregation increases current deductions but creates Section 1245 recapture exposure that must be managed upon sale, and a 1031 exchange can defer that recapture -- but only if the replacement property contains sufficient personal property to absorb the recapture.

---

## Rules

### Cost Segregation

**What it is.** A cost segregation study is an engineering-based analysis that reclassifies building components from their default MACRS recovery period (27.5 years for residential rental, 39 years for nonresidential real property) into shorter-lived asset categories. The study identifies components that qualify as Section 1245 tangible personal property (5-year or 7-year) or land improvements (15-year) rather than Section 1250 real property. [HCVT, "Viability of Cost Segregation Studies for Tax Year 2025"; EisnerAmper, "Five Essential Cost Segregation Updates for Tax Year 2025"]

**Common reclassifications include:**

| Component | Recovery Period | MACRS Class |
|-----------|----------------|-------------|
| Electrical systems serving specific equipment | 5 years | Personal property (1245) |
| Carpeting | 5 years | Personal property (1245) |
| Decorative millwork, cabinetry | 7 years | Personal property (1245) |
| Special plumbing (e.g., restaurant grease traps) | 7 years | Personal property (1245) |
| Parking lots, driveways | 15 years | Land improvement |
| Fencing | 15 years | Land improvement |
| Landscaping, irrigation | 15 years | Land improvement |
| Sidewalks, curbing | 15 years | Land improvement |

**Average basis reallocation** by property type:
- Residential rental: 20-40%
- Apartment building: 20-50%
- Office building: 10-40%
- Warehouse: 10-30%
- Hotel: 25-45%

[HCVT, "Viability of Cost Segregation Studies for Tax Year 2025"]

**Interaction with bonus depreciation.** Reclassified 5-year, 7-year, and 15-year property is eligible for bonus depreciation under IRC Section 168(k). Under the OBBBA (see below), 100% bonus depreciation is now permanently available for qualifying property acquired and placed in service after January 19, 2025. This means the full cost of reclassified components can be expensed in the year the property is placed in service. [Veritax Advisors, "IRS Notice 2026-11: New Guidance on Bonus Depreciation"; Grant Thornton, "OBBBA offers new ways to accelerate depreciation"]

**Look-back studies (Rev. Proc. 2011-14 / Rev. Proc. 2015-13).** Taxpayers who placed property in service in prior years without performing a cost segregation study can file a "look-back" study and claim catch-up depreciation in the current year -- without amending prior returns. The mechanism is a change in accounting method filed on Form 3115 under the automatic consent procedures. The Section 481(a) adjustment captures the cumulative difference between depreciation actually claimed and what would have been claimed had cost segregation been applied from the start. This entire adjustment is recognized in the year of change. [R.E. Cost Seg, "Look-Back Studies: Catching Up on 10 Years of Missed Deductions"; IRS, Rev. Proc. 2011-14; The Tax Adviser, "Updated Procedures for Changing Accounting Methods"]

**Recapture risk.** Components reclassified as Section 1245 personal property are subject to ordinary income recapture upon sale. All depreciation taken on Section 1245 property is recaptured as ordinary income (taxed at the taxpayer's marginal rate, up to 37%). This contrasts with Section 1250 real property, where unrecaptured depreciation is taxed at a maximum rate of 25%. Proper valuation of Section 1245 components at the time of sale can mitigate recapture: components like old carpeting or dated fixtures may have minimal fair market value, permitting a low allocation and reduced recapture. [EisnerAmper, "Tax Depreciation Recapture for Real Estate"; The Tax Adviser, "Avoiding Cost Segregation Recapture Tax"; Thomson Reuters, "Depreciation recapture tax: Overview and FAQs"]

### Section 1031 Like-Kind Exchanges

**Core rule.** IRC Section 1031(a)(1) provides that no gain or loss is recognized on the exchange of real property held for productive use in a trade or business or for investment, if exchanged solely for like-kind real property also held for productive use or investment. [IRS, Instructions for Form 8824 (2025)]

**Like-kind definition.** Post-TCJA, Section 1031 applies only to real property. Personal property and intangible property are excluded. Any U.S. real property is like-kind with any other U.S. real property -- a single-family rental can be exchanged for a commercial building, vacant land, or a multifamily complex. Foreign real property is not like-kind with U.S. real property. [IRS Form 8824 Instructions (2025); DoorLoop, "IRS 1031 Exchange Rules for 2026"]

**Timeline requirements:**
- **Day 0:** Relinquished property closes; exchange proceeds go to a Qualified Intermediary (QI). The taxpayer must not have constructive receipt of funds.
- **Day 45:** Deadline to identify replacement properties in writing. The identification must be signed and delivered to the QI.
- **Day 180:** Deadline to close on replacement property. This runs concurrently with (not after) the 45-day period. If the taxpayer's tax return is due before day 180, the exchange deadline is the earlier of day 180 or the tax filing deadline (including extensions).

[Bonaventure, "1031 Exchange Deadlines: Master the 45 and 180 Day Rules"; JTC Group, "The 1031 Exchange 45-Day Identification Deadline"; IPX1031, "Delayed 1031 Exchange -- Timelines, Deadlines and Identification"]

**Identification rules (choose one):**
1. **Three-property rule:** Identify up to 3 replacement properties regardless of value.
2. **200% rule:** Identify any number of properties whose combined FMV does not exceed 200% of the relinquished property's sale price.
3. **95% rule:** Identify any number of properties of any value, but acquire at least 95% of the total identified value. Failure to meet this threshold disqualifies the entire exchange.

[Bonaventure; JTC Group; IPX1031]

**Boot.** Any cash or non-like-kind property received in the exchange triggers gain recognition to the extent of the boot. Mortgage relief (reduction in debt from relinquished to replacement property) is treated as boot. To fully defer gain, the taxpayer must acquire replacement property of equal or greater value and assume equal or greater debt. [1031 Specialists, "1031 Exchange Boot: Navigating Tax Compliance and Reporting Requirements"]

**Basis.** The basis in the replacement property carries over from the relinquished property, adjusted for any boot paid or received. This carryover basis preserves the deferred gain -- the tax is deferred, not eliminated, until a taxable disposition occurs.

**Related party rule (Section 1031(f)).** Both parties must hold replacement properties for at least two years. If either disposes within two years, the exchange is disqualified and gain recognized as of the disposition date. Exceptions: death, involuntary conversion (Section 1033), or non-tax-avoidance purpose. Interposing an unrelated QI does not circumvent the rule. [IPX1031, "Related Party Exchanges"; The Tax Adviser, "Sec. 1031 Related-Party Exchanges and Basis Shifting"]

**Reverse exchanges (Rev. Proc. 2000-37).** When replacement property must be acquired before the relinquished property sells, an Exchange Accommodation Titleholder (EAT) "parks" title under a Qualified Exchange Accommodation Arrangement (QEAA) for a maximum of 180 days. The same 45/180-day deadlines apply. The EAT is the tax owner but bears no economic risk; a nominal net lease ($1/year) transfers operations back to the taxpayer. [1031 CORP, "Reverse Exchanges"; IPX1031, "The Safe Harbor Reverse Exchange FAQs"]

## OBBBA Changes

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed July 4, 2025, made the following changes relevant to cost segregation and like-kind exchanges:

1. **Permanent 100% bonus depreciation.** The OBBBA permanently restores 100% first-year bonus depreciation under IRC Section 168(k) for qualified property acquired and placed in service after January 19, 2025. This reverses the TCJA phase-down schedule (which had reduced bonus to 40% for 2025 and would have eliminated it by 2027). For the first taxable year ending after January 19, 2025, taxpayers may elect a reduced 40% bonus rate (60% for long-production-period property) instead of 100%. [Grant Thornton; BDO, "Expanded Depreciation Expensing Under the OBBBA"; RSM, "Restored 100% bonus depreciation"]

2. **Qualified Production Property (QPP).** The OBBBA creates a new category: portions of U.S. nonresidential real property directly used for qualified production activities. For a limited window (construction commencing between 1/20/2025 and 12/31/2028, placed in service before 1/1/2031), QPP is eligible for 100% bonus depreciation -- including base building assets that would otherwise be 39-year property. This is the first time building shell/core assets have been eligible for immediate expensing. [EisnerAmper, "Five Essential Cost Segregation Updates for Tax Year 2025"]

3. **Enhanced Section 179.** The OBBBA raises the Section 179 expensing limit to $2,500,000 for 2025 (up from the previously scheduled TCJA amount), with a phaseout threshold of $4,000,000. These amounts are adjusted for inflation annually. [TGC CPA, "How the OBBBA Changes the Depreciation Game"]

4. **Section 1031 preserved.** The OBBBA preserved Section 1031 without the $500,000 deferral cap that had been proposed in earlier legislative drafts. Like-kind exchanges remain fully available with no dollar limitation. [AmeriSave, "7 Smart Ways to Use a 1031 Exchange in 2026"]

5. **IRS Notice 2026-11.** Published January 14, 2026, this notice provides interim guidance on the OBBBA's bonus depreciation provisions, including the 10% safe-harbor test for determining whether self-constructed property qualifies under the TCJA phase-down or the OBBBA permanent rate, and rules for the component election. [Veritax Advisors; EisnerAmper, "Bonus Depreciation in 2025: TCJA vs. OBBBA"]

## Agent Rules

1. **Always ask the client for the property acquisition date.** The applicable bonus depreciation rate depends on whether the property was acquired before or after January 20, 2025. Properties acquired before that date are subject to the TCJA 40% phase-down for 2025; properties acquired on or after that date qualify for permanent 100% bonus.

2. **Flag recapture exposure before recommending cost segregation.** If the client is likely to sell the property within 5-7 years, quantify the Section 1245 ordinary income recapture that will offset the benefit of accelerated depreciation. Recapture is taxed at ordinary rates (up to 37%), not capital gains rates.

3. **Check for look-back study eligibility on existing properties.** If a client owns property placed in service in a prior year without a cost segregation study, recommend a look-back study with Form 3115 filing. This is automatic consent -- no amended returns needed, no IRS pre-approval required. The catch-up deduction is taken entirely in the current year.

4. **For 1031 exchanges, verify the 45/180-day deadlines immediately.** Calendar both dates upon learning of the relinquished property closing. These are hard deadlines with no extensions (except for federally declared disasters under Rev. Proc. 2018-58). Weekends and holidays count.

5. **Ask about related-party transactions.** If the buyer or seller of either property is a related party (family members, controlled entities per Sections 267(b) and 707(b)(1)), flag the two-year holding requirement. An exchange can be retroactively disqualified if either party disposes within two years.

6. **Quantify boot before the exchange closes.** Any mortgage relief or cash received triggers gain recognition. Model the full debt structure of both relinquished and replacement properties to identify boot exposure.

7. **Coordinate cost segregation with 1031 exchanges.** When a property that had a cost segregation study is exchanged via 1031, the Section 1245 personal property components form a separate exchange group. To avoid recapture, the replacement property must contain at least as much Section 1245 property as the relinquished property. Recommend a cost segregation study on the replacement property before closing to verify this.

8. **Never treat 1031 deferral as elimination.** The carryover basis in the replacement property preserves the deferred gain. The tax is due upon eventual taxable disposition. Model the total deferred gain for the client's records.

## Common Traps

1. **Constructive receipt kills the exchange.** If the taxpayer accesses exchange proceeds at any point, the exchange fails. All funds must be held by the QI with proper restrictions on access.

2. **Tax return due date truncates the 180-day period.** Always file an extension to preserve the full 180 days when the exchange straddles year-end.

3. **The 95% rule is a trap.** Missing the 95% acquisition threshold by any amount disqualifies the entire exchange. In practice, use the three-property or 200% rule.

4. **Mortgage relief is boot.** Trading down in debt triggers gain recognition on the differential -- the most commonly overlooked boot source.

5. **State nonconformity on bonus depreciation.** Several states do not conform to federal bonus depreciation. Check state rules before projecting cost segregation savings.

6. **Passive activity loss limitations.** Rental losses are passive and limited to $25,000/year (AGI under $100,000, phased out at $150,000). Exception: real estate professionals who materially participate.

7. **Personal property exclusion post-TCJA.** Section 1031 no longer covers personal property. Cost-segregated 1245 components require a separate exchange group with matching personal property in the replacement.

8. **Reverse exchange financing.** Lenders may resist financing when the EAT holds title. Secure commitments before structuring a reverse exchange.

9. **Look-back studies require Form 3115.** Catch-up depreciation must be claimed via accounting method change on Form 3115 -- not simply on Form 4562.
