## Overview

California itemized deductions diverge from federal law in several critical ways. The state uses Schedule CA (540), Part II, to adjust federal Schedule A amounts for California purposes. Taxpayers who did not itemize on their federal return may still itemize for California by completing federal Schedule A and then adjusting on Schedule CA. These differences can produce substantially different deduction totals at the state level compared to the federal return, making careful line-by-line analysis essential.

For tax year 2025, the California standard deduction is $5,706 (single/MFS) or $11,412 (MFJ/HoH/QSS), far lower than the federal amounts. This lower standard deduction means California taxpayers are more likely to benefit from itemizing, even when taking the federal standard deduction.

## California Rule

California computes itemized deductions by starting with federal Schedule A amounts and then making specific adjustments in Schedule CA (540), Part II. The key categories and California-specific rules are detailed below.

### State and Local Taxes (SALT)

This is the single most important area of divergence between California and federal itemized deductions.

**California state income taxes are NOT deductible on the California return.** R&TC Section 17220 specifically disallows the deduction for California income taxes paid. Taxpayers must subtract any California income tax claimed on federal Schedule A, line 5a, as an adjustment on Schedule CA (540), Part II, line 5a, column B.

**Real property taxes** are fully deductible on the California return with no cap. The federal $10,000 SALT cap (IRC Section 164(b)(6), enacted under TCJA and continued under OBBBA at $40,000) does **not** apply for California purposes. California never conformed to the TCJA SALT limitation and does not conform to the OBBBA's modified $40,000 cap. Taxpayers who were limited to $10,000 (or $40,000 under OBBBA) on their federal return can deduct the full amount of real property taxes on their California return.

**Other states' income taxes** paid are deductible on the California return without limitation. If a California resident pays income tax to another state (e.g., New York, Texas franchise tax), those amounts are fully deductible as itemized deductions on the California return.

**Key adjustment:** Taxpayers must add back the federal SALT cap limitation and subtract California income taxes. The net effect is typically a large Column B subtraction on Schedule CA.

### Mortgage Interest

California conforms, via SB 711, to the $750,000 mortgage acquisition indebtedness limit (for loans originated after December 15, 2017) as enacted by the TCJA. Prior to SB 711, California maintained the pre-TCJA $1,000,000 limit. However, the FTB deductions page currently lists the CA limit as $1,000,000 for home purchases, reflecting that existing pre-12/15/2017 mortgages are grandfathered at the $1,000,000 threshold.

**Home equity interest** is deductible only if the proceeds are used to buy, build, or substantially improve the taxpayer's qualified residence. Home equity loans used for other purposes (e.g., paying off credit card debt, vacation expenses) do not generate deductible interest at either the federal or California level, conforming to the TCJA change.

Interest on a second home (qualified residence) remains deductible under the same rules, subject to the combined acquisition indebtedness limit.

### Charitable Contributions

California uses a **50% of AGI limitation** for cash contributions to public charities. This is lower than the TCJA-era federal 60% limit. SB 711 did not conform to the TCJA increase from 50% to 60% (TCJA Section 11023(a)); the FTB bill analysis explicitly lists this as a non-conformity item.

**Capital gain property** contributions to public charities are subject to a **30% of AGI limit**, consistent with the pre-TCJA federal rules that California has historically followed.

**Five-year carryforward** applies for contributions exceeding the AGI percentage limits, consistent with IRC Section 170(d).

**OBBBA charitable changes — California does NOT conform.** The OBBBA introduced several changes to charitable deduction rules, including a 0.5% (later clarified as 1%) floor on corporate charitable deductions and a 35% rate cap on the tax benefit of individual charitable deductions. California does not conform to any OBBBA provisions, as the OBBBA was enacted July 4, 2025, and California's conformity date under SB 711 is January 1, 2025. The OBBBA's above-the-line deduction for non-itemizer charitable contributions ($1,000/$2,000) is also not available on the California return.

### Medical and Dental Expenses

California **conforms** to the 7.5% of AGI floor for medical and dental expenses. This is calculated using the taxpayer's California AGI (which may differ from federal AGI due to California-specific adjustments on Schedule CA, Part I). The 7.5% floor was made permanent under the Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2021, and California conforms via its IRC conformity date.

### Miscellaneous Itemized Deductions — CRITICAL NON-CONFORMITY

**California did NOT conform to the TCJA's suspension of miscellaneous itemized deductions subject to the 2% floor.** This is one of the most significant differences between California and federal itemized deductions.

Under federal law (post-TCJA and continued under OBBBA), miscellaneous itemized deductions subject to the 2% floor are not deductible. California retained these deductions.

**Deductible at the 2% of California AGI floor:**

- **Unreimbursed employee expenses** — including work-related travel, uniforms, tools, union dues, professional organization dues, continuing education expenses, and home office expenses for employees (not self-employed individuals, who deduct on Schedule C). This is a major benefit for California employees who work from home but are not self-employed.
- **Investment advisory and management fees** — fees paid to financial advisors, investment managers, and custodians for managing taxable accounts.
- **Tax preparation fees** — fees paid to CPAs, enrolled agents, tax attorneys, and tax software for preparation of individual tax returns.
- **Safe deposit box rental** — when used to store investment-related documents or taxable securities.
- **Other qualifying expenses** — legal fees for tax advice, investment-related publications, and other expenses directly connected to the production of income.

Taxpayers must enter these amounts on Schedule CA (540), Part II, lines 19–25, in column C (additions). The total of these expenses exceeding 2% of California AGI is then deductible.

### Casualty and Theft Losses

California **conforms** to the TCJA requirement that personal casualty losses are deductible only if attributable to a federally declared disaster. However, California also expands this to include **state-declared disasters** (disasters declared by the Governor of California). This is a meaningful expansion, as some California disasters (e.g., localized wildfires, mudslides) may receive a state declaration but not a federal one.

Casualty losses are subject to the $100 per-casualty floor and the 10% of AGI threshold, consistent with federal rules.

### Gambling Losses

California allows gambling losses as a deduction to the extent of gambling winnings. Note that for tax years on or after 2026, the federal OBBBA limits gambling loss deductions to 90% of winnings; California does **not** conform to this OBBBA change.

## High-Income Limitation (Pease-Like Limitation)

California imposes an itemized deduction phaseout for high-income taxpayers, similar to the former federal "Pease limitation" (which was repealed by TCJA at the federal level). Under R&TC Section 17076, California reduces itemized deductions by the **lesser of**:

- **6% of the excess** of California AGI over the threshold amount, **or**
- **80% of itemized deductions** (excluding medical expenses, investment interest, casualty/theft losses, and gambling losses to the extent of gambling winnings).

**2025 AGI Thresholds for Itemized Deduction Limitation:**

| Filing Status | Threshold |
|---|---|
| Single / MFS | $252,203 |
| Head of Household | $378,310 |
| MFJ / QSS | $504,411 |

This limitation is calculated on Schedule CA (540), Part II, line 29, using the Itemized Deductions Worksheet in the instructions. Taxpayers with AGI above these thresholds will see a reduction in their California itemized deductions.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

California does not conform to any provision of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (P.L. 119-21), enacted July 4, 2025. The OBBBA falls after California's SB 711 conformity date of January 1, 2025. Key OBBBA itemized deduction provisions that do NOT apply for California:

1. **SALT cap increase to $40,000** — Irrelevant to California, which already has no SALT cap on the state return.
2. **Charitable deduction rate cap of 35%** — Does not apply to California.
3. **Charitable 0.5%/1% floor for corporations** — Does not apply to California.
4. **Non-itemizer charitable deduction ($1,000/$2,000)** — Not available on California return.
5. **Gambling loss limitation to 90% of winnings** — California retains full deductibility up to winnings.
6. **No tax on tips/overtime/car loan interest deductions** — These above-the-line deductions are not available on California return; California-source tips/overtime remain fully taxable.

## Agent Rules

When computing California itemized deductions, the agent must focus on three major differences from the federal return:

1. **No SALT cap.** Remove the federal SALT cap entirely. Real property taxes and other states' income taxes are fully deductible. However, California's own income taxes are never deductible on the California return.

2. **Miscellaneous itemized deductions survive at 2%.** Gather all unreimbursed employee expenses, investment advisory fees, tax preparation fees, and other qualifying expenses. Apply the 2% of CA AGI floor. This is a unique California deduction opportunity not available federally.

3. **California income taxes are not deductible on the CA return.** Always subtract any California income tax amounts included in the federal SALT deduction.

4. **Charitable contributions use 50% AGI limit** for cash (not 60%). Do not apply any OBBBA charitable changes.

5. **Apply the Pease-like limitation** for taxpayers with CA AGI above the thresholds. This federal-era limitation was repealed at the federal level but survives in California.

6. **Check whether the taxpayer should itemize for California even if they took the standard deduction federally.** The low California standard deduction ($5,706/$11,412) means many taxpayers benefit from California itemization even when federal itemization was not worthwhile.

## Practical Workflow: Computing California Itemized Deductions

When preparing a California return, follow this step-by-step workflow to correctly compute itemized deductions:

**Step 1: Determine whether to itemize for California.** Compare the taxpayer's likely California itemized deductions to the California standard deduction ($5,706 single / $11,412 MFJ). Because the California standard deduction is much lower than the federal standard deduction, many taxpayers who take the federal standard deduction should still itemize for California. If the taxpayer did not itemize federally, check the box on Schedule CA (540), Part II, then complete federal Schedule A as if itemizing, and use those amounts as the starting point.

**Step 2: Start with federal Schedule A amounts.** Enter each line of federal Schedule A in Column A of Schedule CA (540), Part II. These are the baseline federal amounts.

**Step 3: Remove the SALT cap adjustment.** On line 5 (taxes), remove the federal SALT cap. Add back any amount that was disallowed by the $10,000 or $40,000 SALT cap in Column B (subtractions). Then subtract California income taxes paid from the total, as these are not deductible on the California return. The net effect: full real property taxes deductible, other states' income taxes deductible, California income taxes removed.

**Step 4: Adjust mortgage interest if needed.** If the taxpayer has pre-12/15/2017 mortgage debt between $750,000 and $1,000,000, California allows the higher $1,000,000 grandfathered limit. Recalculate interest deduction using California limits. For post-12/15/2017 loans, the $750,000 limit applies to both federal and California (post-SB 711).

**Step 5: Adjust charitable contributions.** If the federal return used the 60% AGI cash contribution limit, recalculate using the California 50% AGI limit. Carry forward excess contributions for up to 5 years.

**Step 6: Compute miscellaneous itemized deductions.** This is the California-specific step that has no federal counterpart. Gather all unreimbursed employee expenses, investment advisory fees, tax preparation fees, safe deposit box costs, and other qualifying expenses. Enter them on lines 19-21 of Schedule CA, Part II. Compute the 2% of CA AGI floor on line 24 and subtract. The net amount (line 25) is added to total itemized deductions.

**Step 7: Apply the Pease-like limitation.** If the taxpayer's federal AGI exceeds the threshold ($252,203 single / $504,411 MFJ for 2025), complete the Itemized Deductions Worksheet to reduce deductions by the lesser of 6% of excess AGI or 80% of deductions (excluding medical, investment interest, casualty losses, and gambling losses).

**Step 8: Enter the result on Form 540, line 18.** The larger of the computed California itemized deductions or the California standard deduction goes on line 18.

## Common Traps

1. **Forgetting to remove CA income taxes from the SALT deduction.** Taxpayers carry over their federal Schedule A, line 5a amount without removing California taxes. This overstates the California deduction.

2. **Applying the federal SALT cap on the CA return.** The $10,000/$40,000 SALT cap does not exist for California. Taxpayers who were capped federally must add back the limitation and deduct the full property tax amount.

3. **Missing the 2% miscellaneous deduction.** Because these deductions are suspended federally, taxpayers (and even tax software) may overlook them entirely. The California return requires separate tracking of these expenses.

4. **Using 60% AGI for charitable cash contributions.** California's limit is 50%, not 60%. Excess contributions carry forward but the base calculation must use 50%.

5. **Failing to apply the Pease-like limitation.** High-income taxpayers must reduce deductions — this is unique to California as the federal Pease limitation was repealed under TCJA.

6. **Not itemizing for California when taking the federal standard deduction.** Check the box on Schedule CA (540), Part II, and complete both federal Schedule A and Schedule CA.

7. **Applying OBBBA provisions to California.** None of the OBBBA's itemized deduction changes apply to California for any tax year.

8. **Investment interest expense.** The investment interest deduction is limited to net investment income. California computes this limit using California amounts, which may differ from federal (e.g., if California treats certain income differently). The California investment interest deduction may not match the federal amount.

9. **Gambling losses for California lottery.** California lottery winnings are excluded from California taxable income. If the taxpayer reduced gambling income for California lottery winnings, the gambling losses included in federal itemized deductions must also be reduced proportionally on Schedule CA, Part II, line 16.
