## Overview

California conforms to the federal framework for classifying gains and losses on the sale of business property under IRC Sections 1231, 1245, and 1250, including the depreciation recapture rules. However, two critical differences make California gain calculations diverge from federal: (1) California does not allow bonus depreciation under IRC 168(k), and (2) California caps the Section 179 deduction at $25,000 (compared to the federal limit of $2,500,000 for 2025). Because California depreciation is smaller, the California adjusted basis of business assets is typically higher than the federal basis, resulting in a lower gain (or larger loss) on sale for California purposes. Additionally, all recaptured gain and Section 1231 gain is taxed at ordinary income rates in California -- there is no preferential long-term capital gains rate.

## California Rule

### Depreciation Differences

1. **No bonus depreciation.** California has never conformed to IRC 168(k) bonus depreciation. Any bonus depreciation claimed on the federal return must be added back on the California return, and the asset must be depreciated over its full recovery period using standard MACRS rules for California purposes.

2. **Section 179 capped at $25,000.** For California, the maximum IRC Section 179 expense deduction is $25,000. This begins to phase out when total Section 179 property placed in service exceeds $200,000, and is fully phased out at $225,000. The federal limits for 2025 are $2,500,000 (phaseout starting at $4,000,000).

3. **MACRS recovery periods conform.** California generally conforms to the MACRS recovery periods and methods (straight-line, declining balance) under IRC 168 as of the January 1, 2025 conformity date, with the exception of bonus depreciation.

4. **Dual depreciation schedules required.** Because of the bonus depreciation and Section 179 differences, taxpayers must maintain separate depreciation schedules for federal and California purposes. Form FTB 3885A (individuals) or Form FTB 3885 (corporations) tracks these differences.

### Recapture Framework

5. **Section 1245 recapture -- conforms.** When Section 1245 property (tangible personal property such as machinery, equipment, vehicles, furniture) is sold at a gain, the gain is recaptured as ordinary income to the extent of depreciation previously taken. California conforms to this rule, but the recapture amount is calculated using California depreciation (which is lower than federal due to the absence of bonus depreciation).

6. **Section 1250 recapture -- conforms in structure, diverges in rate.** When Section 1250 property (depreciable real property such as buildings) is sold at a gain, the gain attributable to depreciation in excess of straight-line is recaptured as ordinary income. For post-1986 property depreciated using straight-line, the Section 1250 recapture is typically zero or minimal. However, the concept of "unrecaptured Section 1250 gain" (taxed at a maximum 25% rate federally) has no separate rate treatment in California. All such gain is taxed at ordinary CA rates (up to 13.3%).

7. **Section 1231 gain -- no preferential rate.** Federally, net Section 1231 gain is treated as long-term capital gain (taxed at 0%/15%/20%). In California, net Section 1231 gain is included in taxable income and taxed at ordinary rates. There is no rate benefit for Section 1231 characterization on the California return.

8. **Section 1231 loss -- conforms.** Net Section 1231 loss is treated as ordinary loss for both federal and California purposes. The 5-year lookback rule for recharacterizing Section 1231 gains as ordinary income (to the extent of prior Section 1231 losses) also applies in California.

## Conformity Analysis

### Depreciation

**IRC RULE:** IRC 168(k) provides 100% bonus depreciation for qualified property placed in service after September 27, 2017 (restored by OBBBA after temporary phase-down under TCJA). IRC 179 allows up to $2,500,000 of immediate expensing for 2025, phasing out dollar-for-dollar when total acquisitions exceed $4,000,000.

**CALIFORNIA RULE:** R&TC 17024.5 conforms to IRC 168 generally but explicitly decouples from IRC 168(k) (bonus depreciation). R&TC 17024.5 limits IRC 179 to $25,000 (phaseout at $200,000). Any excess federal deduction over the California limit must be added back and depreciated over the asset's recovery period for California purposes.

**Controlling Authority:** R&TC 17024.5; FTB 3885A Instructions (2025); SB 711 (Conformity Act of 2025 -- does not change bonus depreciation non-conformity).

**Schedule CA Impact:** Column B addition -- add back the difference between federal bonus depreciation (or excess Section 179) and California depreciation for the year. Column C subtraction -- subtract the California depreciation that exceeds the federal amount in later years (as the California depreciation catches up).

### Recapture on Sale

**IRC RULE:** IRC 1245 recaptures all depreciation as ordinary income on sale of personal property. IRC 1250 recaptures excess depreciation on real property. Unrecaptured Section 1250 gain is taxed at a maximum 25% rate. Net Section 1231 gain is taxed at LTCG rates (0%/15%/20%).

**CALIFORNIA RULE:** California conforms to the recapture mechanics (what is recaptured) but taxes ALL recaptured gain and Section 1231 gain at ordinary income rates (up to 13.3%). The federal 25% unrecaptured Section 1250 rate and the preferential LTCG rates have no California equivalent.

**Controlling Authority:** R&TC 17024.5; R&TC 18100-18175.

**Schedule CA Impact:** Adjustment required to the extent that the California gain differs from the federal gain due to basis differences. If California basis is higher (less depreciation taken), the California gain on sale will be lower than the federal gain.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

The OBBBA restored 100% bonus depreciation under IRC 168(k) retroactively for property placed in service after December 31, 2022. California does not conform to this restoration. California had already not conformed to bonus depreciation in any prior version, so the OBBBA change does not alter California's position. Any bonus depreciation claimed on the federal return -- whether under the original TCJA provision or the OBBBA restoration -- must be reversed on the California return.

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE:** On EVERY business asset sale, calculate the California gain separately from the federal gain. The California adjusted basis will be higher than the federal basis if bonus depreciation or excess Section 179 was claimed federally. Use California depreciation (Form FTB 3885A records) to determine the correct California basis.

> **AGENT RULE:** Maintain dual depreciation schedules at all times -- one for federal and one for California. The difference typically reverses over the life of the asset, but any sale before the asset is fully depreciated will produce different gain amounts.

> **AGENT RULE:** Do NOT apply the federal 25% unrecaptured Section 1250 rate for California. All recaptured depreciation on real property is taxed at ordinary CA rates.

> **AGENT RULE:** When a taxpayer claims bonus depreciation on the federal return, calculate the Schedule CA Column B addition (add-back) as: Federal bonus depreciation minus California first-year depreciation (standard MACRS + up to $25,000 Section 179). In subsequent years, compute the Column C subtraction as: California annual depreciation minus federal annual depreciation (which is $0 for fully-expensed assets).

## Common Traps

> **TRAP:** Forgetting to recalculate California basis on sale. If a $500,000 piece of equipment was fully expensed federally (100% bonus depreciation, basis = $0), but only $25,000 was deducted for California (with the remainder being depreciated annually), the California basis at the time of sale may be $200,000+. The federal gain on sale is $500,000 but the California gain may only be $300,000. Reporting the full federal gain on the California return overstates the California tax.

> **TRAP:** Section 179 phaseout surprise. The California phaseout starts at $200,000 of total Section 179 property, compared to $4,000,000 federally. A business placing $250,000 of equipment in service gets $0 Section 179 in California (fully phased out) but up to $250,000 in Section 179 federally.

> **TRAP:** Assuming Section 1231 gain gets capital gains treatment in California. It does not. A taxpayer selling a business asset held for more than one year at a gain does not benefit from LTCG rates on the California return. The gain is taxed at up to 13.3%.

> **TRAP:** Timing differences create cash flow surprises. In the year of purchase, federal taxable income drops dramatically due to bonus depreciation, but California taxable income remains high. This can cause unexpected California tax bills and estimated tax penalties if California estimated payments are not adjusted upward.
