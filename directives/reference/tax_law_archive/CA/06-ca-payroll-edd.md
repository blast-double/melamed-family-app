## Overview

California's payroll tax system is administered by the Employment Development Department (EDD) and consists of four separate programs: Unemployment Insurance (UI), Employment Training Tax (ETT), State Disability Insurance (SDI), and Personal Income Tax (PIT) withholding. Employers bear the cost of UI and ETT, while employees pay SDI through wage withholding. AB 5 (codifying the Dynamex ABC test) governs worker classification and carries steep penalties for misclassification.

This document covers the current rates, wage bases, reporting requirements, and misclassification risks for California employers.

## California Rule

### State Disability Insurance (SDI)

SDI provides short-term disability and paid family leave benefits to eligible California workers. It is funded entirely by employee contributions through wage withholding.

| Tax Year | SDI Rate | Taxable Wage Base |
|---|---|---|
| **2025** | **1.2%** | No cap (all wages subject) |
| **2026** | **1.3%** | No cap (all wages subject) |

**Critical change (effective January 1, 2024):** SB 951 removed the SDI taxable wage ceiling. Previously, SDI was capped at a specific wage limit (e.g., $153,164 in 2023). Starting in 2024, ALL wages are subject to SDI contributions with no ceiling. This represents a significant increase in SDI costs for high-earning employees.

The SDI rate is set annually by a statutory formula designed to maintain the DI Fund balance (CUIC Section 984(a)(1)). The rate increased from 0.9% (2023) to 1.1% (2024) to 1.2% (2025) to 1.3% (2026).

Maximum weekly benefit amount for 2026: $1,765.

### Unemployment Insurance (UI)

UI provides temporary partial wage replacement to workers who are unemployed through no fault of their own. It is funded by employer contributions.

| Tax Year | UI Rate Range | Taxable Wage Base | Schedule |
|---|---|---|---|
| **2025** | **1.5% - 6.2%** | $7,000 per employee | Schedule F+ |
| **2026** | **1.5% - 6.2%** | $7,000 per employee | Schedule F+ |

Key details:

- **New employers** are assigned a rate of **3.4%** for a period of two to three years until they meet the criteria under CUIC Section 982(b) to receive an experience-rated rate.
- **Schedule F+** is Schedule F plus a 15% emergency surcharge, rounded to the nearest tenth. This has been the applicable schedule for both 2025 and 2026.
- **Maximum UI tax per employee**: $434/year (6.2% x $7,000).
- The rate is determined by the employer's experience rating (reserve account balance relative to taxable wages).

**FUTA interaction:** California employers also pay Federal Unemployment Tax (FUTA) at 6.0%, with a normal 5.4% credit for state UI taxes paid. However, because California has an outstanding federal UI loan, the FUTA credit is currently reduced by 1.2% for the 2025 tax year (resulting in a net FUTA rate of 1.8% instead of 0.6%). This means California employers pay an extra $84 per employee for FUTA in 2025. The credit reduction increases by 0.3% each year until the loan is repaid.

### Employment Training Tax (ETT)

ETT funds training programs for California workers in targeted industries.

| Tax Year | ETT Rate | Taxable Wage Base |
|---|---|---|
| **2025** | **0.1%** | $7,000 per employee |
| **2026** | **0.1%** | $7,000 per employee |

ETT is employer-only. Maximum cost: $7 per employee per year.

### Personal Income Tax (PIT) Withholding

Employers must withhold California PIT from employee wages based on the employee's withholding allowance certificate (DE 4) and the applicable withholding schedules published annually by the EDD. PIT withholding is not a payroll "tax" in the traditional sense but is a mandatory employer obligation.

### Summary of Employer Costs per Employee (2026)

| Tax | Rate | Wage Base | Max Annual Cost | Paid By |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| SDI | 1.3% | No cap | Uncapped | Employee |
| UI | 1.5%-6.2% | $7,000 | $434 | Employer |
| ETT | 0.1% | $7,000 | $7 | Employer |
| PIT Withholding | Varies | All wages | Varies | Employee (withheld by employer) |

## Quarterly Reporting

### Form DE 9 / DE 9C

Employers must file quarterly returns reporting wages paid and payroll taxes due.

| Quarter | Period | Due Date | Delinquent If Not Filed By |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1st | January - March | April 1 | April 30 |
| 2nd | April - June | July 1 | July 31 |
| 3rd | July - September | October 1 | November 2 |
| 4th | October - December | January 1 | February 1 |

- **DE 9**: Quarterly Contribution Return and Report of Wages (summary page).
- **DE 9C**: Quarterly Contribution Return and Report of Wages (individual employee detail).
- Deposit requirements vary based on the accumulated withholding amount (next-day, semi-weekly, monthly, or quarterly).

## AB 5 and Worker Classification

### The ABC Test

AB 5 (effective January 1, 2020) codified the California Supreme Court's 2018 Dynamex decision and expanded the ABC test to apply under the Labor Code and Unemployment Insurance Code. Under the ABC test, a worker is presumed to be an employee unless the hiring entity demonstrates all three conditions:

**A.** The worker is free from the control and direction of the hiring entity in connection with the performance of the work, both under the contract and in fact.

**B.** The worker performs work that is outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business.

**C.** The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business of the same nature as the work performed.

All three prongs must be satisfied for the worker to be classified as an independent contractor. Failure on any single prong results in employee classification.

### Exemptions

AB 5 exempts certain professions from the ABC test, reverting to the multi-factor Borello test for these workers:
- Licensed physicians, surgeons, dentists, podiatrists, psychologists, veterinarians
- Licensed attorneys, architects, engineers, accountants
- Licensed insurance agents, securities broker-dealers, investment advisers
- Direct sales salespersons
- Commercial fishermen
- Certain other specified professions (added by subsequent legislation AB 2257)

### Misclassification Penalties

Labor Code Section 226.8 provides severe civil penalties for willful misclassification of employees as independent contractors:

| Violation Type | Penalty Range |
|---|---|
| **Willful misclassification** | $5,000 - $15,000 per violation |
| **Pattern or practice of misclassification** | $10,000 - $25,000 per violation |

"Willful misclassification" is defined as voluntarily and knowingly misclassifying an employee as an independent contractor (Labor Code Section 226.8(j)).

In addition to these civil penalties:
- EDD may assess a **15% penalty** on deficient payroll taxes for negligent or intentional disregard of reporting requirements (CUIC).
- Workers may bring claims under the **Private Attorneys General Act (PAGA)**, allowing private individuals to sue on behalf of themselves and other aggrieved employees and collect 25% of the civil penalties plus attorney fees.
- Misclassified workers are entitled to all back wages, overtime, meal/rest break penalties, and other Labor Code remedies.

## Agent Rules

1. **Calculate total employer payroll cost**: Sum UI + ETT + FUTA (with credit reduction) for each employee. SDI is employee-paid but must be properly withheld and remitted.
2. **Flag SDI wage ceiling removal**: For high-earning employees, the removal of the SDI wage ceiling (effective 2024) represents a material increase in withholding. An employee earning $500,000 pays $6,500 in SDI at the 1.3% rate (2026) vs. approximately $1,378 under the old capped system.
3. **AB 5 risk assessment**: Before classifying any worker as an independent contractor, apply the ABC test. If any prong fails, the worker must be classified as an employee. When in doubt, classify as employee.
4. **Quarterly filing compliance**: DE 9/DE 9C filings are due quarterly. Late filing triggers penalties and interest.
5. **FUTA credit reduction monitoring**: Track the outstanding federal UI loan balance. The FUTA credit reduction increases annually until the loan is repaid, adding incremental cost per employee each year.

## Common Traps

1. **SDI on all wages -- no cap.** Starting 2024, there is no SDI wage ceiling. Employers accustomed to the old capped system may fail to withhold SDI on wages above the former ceiling, creating a deficiency.

2. **ABC test "B" prong is strict.** The most frequently failed prong is "B" -- the worker performs work outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business. A software company hiring a freelance software developer fails prong B because the work is within the company's usual course of business, regardless of the worker's independence.

3. **PAGA exposure multiplies risk.** Misclassification penalties are assessed per violation (per worker). A company with 50 misclassified workers faces potential penalties of $250,000 to $1,250,000 under the pattern/practice standard, plus back wages, benefits, and attorney fees.

4. **S-Corp shareholder-employees.** S corporation shareholders who perform services must be treated as employees and run through payroll. They cannot be classified as independent contractors to avoid payroll taxes.

5. **Deposit timing.** California deposit requirements do not always match federal deposit schedules. Employers must track both state and federal deposit requirements separately to avoid penalties.
