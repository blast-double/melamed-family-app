## Overview

California imposes a variety of entity-level taxes and fees that differ substantially from the federal tax treatment of business entities. Every LLC, LP, LLP, and corporation doing business in California or organized under California law is subject to a minimum annual franchise tax of $800. LLCs face an additional annual fee based on total California-source income (gross receipts). The choice of business entity has significant California tax consequences that diverge from federal treatment, most notably because California does not allow the Qualified Business Income (QBI) deduction under IRC Section 199A.

This document provides a comprehensive comparison of entity types and their California tax obligations, with particular focus on the LLC annual tax and fee structure.

## California Rule

### Minimum Franchise Tax -- $800 Annual

Every LLC, LP, LLP, and corporation that is organized, registered, or doing business in California must pay an annual minimum franchise tax of $800 (R&TC Sections 17941, 23153). This tax is due by the 15th day of the 4th month after the beginning of the taxable year (April 15 for calendar-year filers).

Key rules:

- **LLCs** (including SMLLCs and multi-member LLCs): $800 annual tax under R&TC Section 17941. Due even if the LLC is inactive or operating at a loss. The tax must be paid each year until a certificate of cancellation is filed with the Secretary of State.
- **Corporations** (C-corps and S-corps): $800 minimum franchise tax under R&TC Section 23153. Applies whether active, inactive, or operating at a loss.
- **Limited Partnerships and LLPs**: $800 annual tax. Same rules as LLCs.

### First-Year Exemption -- EXPIRED

Assembly Bill 85 (AB 85) provided a first-year exemption from the $800 annual tax for LLCs, LPs, and LLPs that organized or registered with the California Secretary of State on or after January 1, 2021, and before January 1, 2024. **This exemption has expired.** Entities formed on or after January 1, 2024, owe the full $800 in their first year.

For corporations, a separate first-year exemption still applies: newly incorporated or qualified corporations on or after January 1, 2020, are not required to pay the minimum franchise tax in their first taxable year (R&TC Section 23222). Note that the corporate first-year exemption has no stated sunset date as of March 2026.

There is also a 15-day exception: entities are not subject to the annual tax if the taxable year is 15 days or fewer AND the entity did not conduct business in California during that period.

### LLC Annual Fee (R&TC Section 17942)

In addition to the $800 minimum annual tax, LLCs are subject to an annual fee based on total income from all sources derived from or attributable to California. This fee is based on **total income (gross receipts plus cost of goods sold)**, NOT net income or profit. The fee schedule is:

| Total California Income (Rounded) | LLC Fee |
|---|---|
| Less than $250,000 | $0 |
| $250,000 -- $499,999 | $900 |
| $500,000 -- $999,999 | $2,500 |
| $1,000,000 -- $4,999,999 | $6,000 |
| $5,000,000 or more | $11,790 |

The estimated fee must be paid by the 15th day of the 6th month of the current tax year (June 15 for calendar-year filers) using Form FTB 3536. The actual fee is reconciled when the LLC files its annual return (Form 568). If the estimated fee is underpaid, penalties and interest apply.

**Combined LLC obligations example**: An LLC with $2 million in California gross income owes $800 (annual tax) + $6,000 (LLC fee) = **$6,800 total**. An LLC with $6 million in gross income owes $800 + $11,790 = **$12,590 total**.

### S Corporation Tax Detail

S corporations pay the greater of the minimum franchise tax ($800) OR 1.5% of net income (R&TC Section 23802). Financial institution S corporations pay 3.5%. Income and losses pass through to shareholders via Schedule K-1 (100S). The entity-level 1.5% tax has no federal equivalent.

### C Corporation Tax Detail

C corporations pay the greater of the minimum franchise tax ($800) OR 8.84% of net income (R&TC Section 23151). Financial institutions pay 10.84%. The corporation is taxed at the entity level and shareholders are taxed again on distributions (double taxation).

## LLC Fee Calculation -- Detailed Mechanics

The LLC fee under R&TC Section 17942 requires careful calculation because it is based on "total income from all sources derived from or attributable to California." This definition differs from net income, adjusted gross income, or taxable income. Understanding what is included is critical.

### Definition of Total Income for LLC Fee Purposes

Per FTB Pub. 3556 and the Form 568 instructions, "total income" means gross income as defined in R&TC Section 24271, plus the cost of goods sold paid or incurred in connection with the trade or business of the taxpayer. Specifically:

- **Gross receipts** from sales of goods and services attributable to California
- **Cost of goods sold** is added back (not subtracted) -- this is counterintuitive but correct. The fee is on total revenue before COGS deduction for most businesses.
- **Rental income**, interest, dividends, and other income attributable to California sources
- For multi-state LLCs, the fee applies only to California-source income, determined using existing sourcing and apportionment rules (Schedule R)

### LLC Fee vs. LLC Tax -- Two Separate Obligations

These are two distinct payments with different due dates:

| Obligation | Amount | Due Date | Form | Authority |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Annual LLC Tax** | $800 (flat) | April 15 (calendar year) | FTB 3522 | R&TC 17941 |
| **LLC Fee** (estimated) | $0-$11,790 (based on gross income) | June 15 (calendar year) | FTB 3536 | R&TC 17942 |
| **LLC Fee** (final) | Reconciled on annual return | Return due date | Form 568 | R&TC 17942 |

The $800 annual tax and the LLC fee are paid separately and should not be submitted together. The FTB specifically instructs taxpayers not to send the $800 annual tax with Form 568.

### Estimated Fee Calculation and Penalties

LLCs must estimate their current-year total California income and pay the corresponding LLC fee by June 15. If the estimate is too low:

- **Underpayment penalty**: Assessed on the difference between the estimated fee paid and the actual fee owed.
- **Interest**: Accrues from the due date to the date of payment.
- The penalty applies even if the underpayment was reasonable or based on a good-faith estimate.

**Best practice:** Estimate conservatively by using the higher income tier when uncertain. Overpayments are applied as credits on the LLC return.

### 15-Day Exception

LLCs are not subject to the annual tax and fee if both of the following apply:
- The taxable year was 15 days or fewer, AND
- The LLC did not conduct any business in California during the 15-day period.

This exception is narrow and rarely applicable to operating businesses.

## Check-the-Box Classification

### Conformity with Federal Entity Classification

California follows federal entity classification rules. Under R&TC Section 23038, an LLC must have the same classification for both California and federal tax purposes. If an LLC elects to be treated as a corporation (or S corporation) via federal Form 8832, that classification applies for California purposes as well.

Key implications:

- **SMLLC as disregarded entity**: A single-member LLC that does not elect corporate classification is disregarded for income tax purposes (same as federal). However, it is still subject to the $800 annual tax and LLC fee as a separate entity for California franchise tax purposes.
- **Multi-member LLC as partnership**: The default classification for a multi-member LLC. The LLC files Form 568 and issues Schedule K-1 (568) to members.
- **LLC electing C-Corp treatment**: Files Form 100. Subject to 8.84% corporate rate and $800 minimum. No longer subject to LLC fee (R&TC 17942 applies only to LLCs not treated as corporations).
- **LLC electing S-Corp treatment**: Files Form 100S. Subject to 1.5% entity-level tax and $800 minimum. No longer subject to LLC fee. Requires valid federal S election first, then the California S election follows automatically.

### State-Level Classification Change

A change in federal classification (e.g., from partnership to S corporation via Form 8832 and Form 2553) automatically changes the California classification. No separate California election is needed. However, the timing of the change and any built-in gains implications must be considered.

## Conformity Analysis

### QBI Deduction -- California Does NOT Conform

This is the single most important non-conformity for business entity selection. California does not conform to IRC Section 199A (the Qualified Business Income deduction). Under OBBBA, the federal QBI deduction was made permanent and increased to 23%. California provides no comparable deduction. This means:

- A sole proprietor, LLC member, S corporation shareholder, or partner who claims a federal QBI deduction of up to 23% of qualified business income receives **zero** deduction on the California return.
- The California taxable income for passthrough entity owners is therefore higher than federal taxable income by the amount of the QBI deduction.
- This non-conformity applies to all entity types. No business entity in California benefits from QBI.
- For a taxpayer in the top California bracket (13.3%), the QBI non-conformity adds approximately 3.1% to the effective tax rate on qualified business income (13.3% x 23% QBI deduction = 3.06% additional CA tax).

SB 711 (effective 2025) updated California's general conformity date to January 1, 2025, but explicitly continues non-conformity with IRC Section 199A.

### Entity Selection Scenarios

**Scenario 1: High-Revenue, Low-Margin Business ($5M revenue, $100K net income)**
- As LLC: $800 + $11,790 fee = $12,590 entity cost + individual tax on $100K passthrough
- As S-Corp: $800 minimum (1.5% of $100K = $1,500, so $1,500 entity tax) + individual tax on passthrough
- S-Corp saves $11,090 in entity-level costs. Strong candidate for conversion.

**Scenario 2: Low-Revenue Service Business ($200K revenue, $150K net income)**
- As LLC: $800 + $0 fee = $800 entity cost + individual tax on $150K passthrough
- As S-Corp: $800 minimum (1.5% of $150K = $2,250, so $2,250 entity tax) + individual tax on passthrough
- LLC is cheaper by $1,450 in entity-level costs. LLC is preferred.

**Scenario 3: Moderate-Revenue Business ($1M revenue, $300K net income)**
- As LLC: $800 + $6,000 fee = $6,800 entity cost + individual tax on passthrough
- As S-Corp: 1.5% of $300K = $4,500 entity tax + individual tax on passthrough
- S-Corp saves $2,300. Consider conversion, but factor in reasonable compensation and payroll costs.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed July 4, 2025, made the federal QBI deduction permanent and increased it from 20% to 23%. California's SB 711 explicitly decouples from IRC Section 199A. This non-conformity creates a growing gap between federal and California taxable income for passthrough entity owners.

The OBBBA also increased the federal SALT deduction cap from $10,000 to $40,000 (for tax years 2025-2029), which may reduce the benefit of California's PTE elective tax for some taxpayers. However, the SALT cap returns to $10,000 for 2030 and beyond, maintaining the ongoing value of entity-level tax strategies.

## Agent Rules

1. **Always calculate the combined CA tax burden**: For any LLC, compute $800 annual tax + LLC fee based on projected gross receipts. The LLC fee alone can exceed $11,000 for high-revenue businesses.
2. **Compare entity types using a comprehensive model**: When advising on entity selection, compare the total CA tax cost across entity types, including entity-level taxes, owner-level taxes, LLC fees, and the absence of QBI.
3. **Do not assume federal QBI savings apply to California**: Any analysis that references QBI must explicitly note the California exclusion.
4. **Flag the LLC fee gross receipts trap**: Always verify whether the LLC fee is being calculated on total income (gross receipts) rather than net income. This is the most common error.
5. **Track first-year exemption status**: The LLC/LP/LLP first-year exemption expired January 1, 2024. The corporate first-year exemption remains active (no current sunset).

## Common Traps

1. **LLC fee is based on gross revenue, not profit.** An LLC with $5 million in revenue and a $100,000 net loss still owes the $11,790 LLC fee plus the $800 annual tax. Total: $12,590 even though the business lost money.

2. **The LLC fee applies to SMLLCs and multi-member LLCs equally.** A single-member LLC treated as a disregarded entity for tax purposes still pays both the $800 annual tax and the LLC fee. The disregarded entity treatment does not eliminate these California-specific obligations.

3. **First-year exemption expiration.** The AB 85 first-year exemption for LLCs, LPs, and LLPs expired after December 31, 2023. Entities formed in 2024 and later owe $800 in year one. Many online resources still reference this exemption without noting the expiration.

4. **No California QBI deduction -- entity selection impact.** Because California does not allow the QBI deduction, the relative tax advantage of passthrough entities over C corporations is smaller in California than at the federal level. Entity selection analysis that relies heavily on QBI savings is misleading for California tax purposes.

5. **Estimated LLC fee penalty.** If the LLC fee estimated payment (due June 15) is less than the actual fee owed, the FTB assesses penalties and interest on the underpayment. Estimate conservatively.

6. **$800 due until cancellation.** The $800 annual tax accrues every year until the entity formally files a certificate of cancellation with the Secretary of State. Simply ceasing operations does not stop the tax from accruing.

7. **S-Corp election does not eliminate minimum tax.** Converting an LLC to S-Corp status eliminates the LLC fee but introduces the 1.5% entity-level tax. For low-income businesses, the net effect may be negligible; for high-revenue, low-margin businesses, the S-Corp structure may save significant LLC fee costs.
