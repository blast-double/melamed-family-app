# Social Security Benefits Exclusion -- California
*Last Edited: 2026-03-26*

**R&TC Section(s):** Section 17087 (Social Security and Railroad Retirement), Section 17083 (Unemployment Compensation)
**Parallel IRC Section:** IRC Section 86 (Taxation of Social Security Benefits)
**California Conformity Status:** Does Not Conform -- California provides a FULL exclusion of Social Security benefits from state taxable income under R&TC 17087, which is more favorable than federal law. Federal law taxes up to 85% of benefits under the combined income formula of IRC 86.

## Overview

California fully excludes all Social Security benefits from state taxable income. This exclusion, codified in R&TC Section 17087, is absolute and unconditional -- it applies regardless of the taxpayer's income level, filing status, or any other factor. Federal law, by contrast, taxes up to 85% of Social Security benefits based on a "combined income" formula under IRC Section 86. California's exclusion also covers Railroad Retirement Tier 1 and Tier 2 benefits. Separately, California excludes unemployment insurance benefits from state income under R&TC Section 17083, whereas federal law includes them in gross income.

## California Rule

### Social Security Benefits -- Full Exclusion

1. **100% exclusion from California gross income.** Social Security retirement benefits, Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) benefits, and survivor benefits are fully excluded from California taxable income. There is no partial inclusion at any income level.

2. **No combined income test.** The federal IRC 86 "combined income" formula (AGI + tax-exempt interest + 50% of Social Security benefits) that determines what percentage of benefits is federally taxable does NOT apply in California. The answer in California is always: 0% of benefits are taxable.

3. **Statutory authority.** R&TC Section 17087 provides the exclusion. This section has been in effect for decades and has no sunset date.

4. **Scope of the exclusion.** The following are all fully excluded from California gross income:
   - Social Security retirement benefits (Old-Age)
   - Social Security disability benefits (SSDI)
   - Social Security survivor benefits
   - Supplemental Security Income (SSI) -- also excluded federally
   - Lump-sum retroactive Social Security payments

### Railroad Retirement Benefits -- Excluded

5. **Tier 1 benefits -- excluded.** Railroad Retirement Tier 1 benefits (the Social Security equivalent portion) are excluded from California income under R&TC 17087, consistent with the Social Security exclusion.

6. **Tier 2 benefits -- excluded.** Railroad Retirement Tier 2 benefits (the pension equivalent portion, above the Social Security equivalent) are also excluded from California income. This is more favorable than federal treatment, where Tier 2 benefits are fully taxable as pension income.

### Unemployment Compensation -- Excluded

7. **California excludes unemployment insurance benefits.** Under R&TC Section 17083, benefits from California's unemployment insurance program are excluded from state gross income. This is a significant departure from federal law, where unemployment compensation is fully included in gross income under IRC 85.

8. **This exclusion covers state UI benefits.** It applies to California Employment Development Department (EDD) unemployment insurance payments. The 2016-17 Tax Expenditure Report confirms this exclusion covers 1.3 million returns and 2 million taxpayers as of the 2013 tax year.

### Schedule CA Adjustment

9. **Schedule CA, Column C subtraction.** Because the California return starts with federal AGI, which includes the taxable portion of Social Security benefits, the taxpayer must subtract 100% of the amount on federal Form 1040, Line 6b (Taxable Social Security Benefits) on Schedule CA (540), Column C.

10. **The subtraction is the FULL federal taxable amount.** Even if only 50% of benefits are taxable federally, the Column C subtraction is 100% of whatever was included in federal AGI on Line 6b. The effect is to remove all Social Security income from California taxable income.

11. **Unemployment compensation subtraction.** Similarly, the full amount of unemployment compensation included in federal AGI must be subtracted on Schedule CA Column C, as California excludes it under R&TC 17083.

## Conformity Analysis

### Social Security

**IRC RULE:** Under IRC 86, Social Security benefits are partially taxable based on "combined income" (also called "provisional income"):
- **Single filers:** If combined income is between $25,000 and $34,000, up to 50% of benefits are taxable. If combined income exceeds $34,000, up to 85% of benefits are taxable.
- **Married filing jointly:** If combined income is between $32,000 and $44,000, up to 50% of benefits are taxable. If combined income exceeds $44,000, up to 85% of benefits are taxable.
- **Married filing separately (lived with spouse):** Up to 85% of benefits are taxable with no base amount.

**CALIFORNIA RULE:** R&TC 17087 provides a complete exclusion of Social Security and Railroad Retirement benefits from California gross income. No combined income test. No partial inclusion. No income threshold. The exclusion is absolute.

**Controlling Authority:** R&TC 17087 (Social Security and Railroad Retirement); R&TC 17083 (Unemployment Compensation).

**Schedule CA Impact:** Column C subtraction -- subtract 100% of the federal Form 1040, Line 6b amount (taxable Social Security benefits). Also subtract 100% of unemployment compensation included in federal AGI.

### Unemployment Compensation

**IRC RULE:** IRC 85 includes unemployment compensation in gross income. The full amount of unemployment benefits received is taxable on the federal return (except for the temporary ARPA exclusion of $10,200 for 2020 only, which has expired).

**CALIFORNIA RULE:** R&TC 17083 fully excludes unemployment insurance benefits from California gross income. This is a standalone California provision that differs from federal treatment.

**Controlling Authority:** R&TC 17083.

**Schedule CA Impact:** Column C subtraction -- subtract 100% of unemployment compensation from federal AGI.

## Key Thresholds and Figures

| Item | Federal | California |
|------|---------|------------|
| Social Security -- taxable percentage | 0%, 50%, or 85% (income-dependent) | 0% -- fully excluded |
| Social Security -- combined income threshold (single) | $25,000 (50% taxable); $34,000 (85% taxable) | N/A -- no threshold |
| Social Security -- combined income threshold (MFJ) | $32,000 (50% taxable); $44,000 (85% taxable) | N/A -- no threshold |
| Railroad Retirement Tier 1 | Same as Social Security | Fully excluded |
| Railroad Retirement Tier 2 | Fully taxable as pension | Fully excluded |
| Unemployment compensation | Fully taxable | Fully excluded (R&TC 17083) |
| SSI (Supplemental Security Income) | Not taxable | Not taxable (conforms) |

### Revenue Impact

Per the California Department of Finance 2023-24 Tax Expenditure Report:
- Social Security exclusion: 2.2 million returns (3.4 million taxpayers) claimed this exclusion for tax year 2020.
- This is the second-largest personal income tax exclusion in California by number of returns affected.
- State General Fund revenue loss ranges from approximately $2.2 billion to $2.6 billion annually (2021-2026 estimates).

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE:** ALWAYS subtract the FULL amount of federal Form 1040 Line 6b (taxable Social Security benefits) on Schedule CA Column C. No calculation needed. No income test. No phase-in. The exclusion is unconditional and complete.

> **AGENT RULE:** Do NOT apply any federal "combined income" formula for California purposes. The answer is always: 0% of Social Security benefits are taxable in California, regardless of the taxpayer's total income.

> **AGENT RULE:** For Railroad Retirement benefits, exclude BOTH Tier 1 and Tier 2 from California income. Tier 2 is fully taxable federally but fully excluded in California.

> **AGENT RULE:** For unemployment compensation, subtract the full amount included in federal AGI on Schedule CA Column C. California excludes all unemployment insurance benefits under R&TC 17083.

> **AGENT RULE:** When projecting California tax liability for retirees, remember that Social Security income has zero California tax impact. Only other retirement income (pensions, 401(k)/IRA distributions, investment income) is taxable at California rates.

## Common Traps

> **TRAP:** Do NOT apply any federal combined income test for California. The answer is always 0% taxable. A high-income retiree with $300,000 of total income including $40,000 in Social Security still excludes 100% of the $40,000 from California income. The federal combined income thresholds are irrelevant for California.

> **TRAP:** Do NOT confuse the Schedule CA subtraction amount with the total Social Security received. The subtraction is the amount from federal Form 1040, Line 6b (the TAXABLE portion that was included in federal AGI), NOT the total amount received (Form 1040, Line 6a). If the taxpayer has low enough federal income that no Social Security was taxable federally, there is no subtraction needed on Schedule CA (because nothing was included in federal AGI to begin with).

> **TRAP:** Railroad Retirement Tier 2 is commonly reported as taxable pension income on the federal return (it appears on Form RRB-1099-R). This amount must be subtracted on Schedule CA for California. Many tax preparers miss this because they think only the "Social Security equivalent" (Tier 1) qualifies for exclusion.

> **TRAP:** SSI (Supplemental Security Income) is already excluded from gross income at the federal level and does not appear on Form 1040 at all. No adjustment is needed on Schedule CA for SSI. Do not create a phantom subtraction.

> **TRAP:** Unemployment compensation exclusion is a California-only benefit. If a taxpayer moves from California to another state mid-year, the unemployment benefits received while a California resident are excluded from California income, but benefits received after moving may be taxable in the new state (depending on that state's rules).
