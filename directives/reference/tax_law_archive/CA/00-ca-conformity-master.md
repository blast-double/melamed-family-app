## Overview

California uses a "static" or "fixed-date" conformity model: the R&TC incorporates the federal Internal Revenue Code by reference as of a legislatively enacted specified date. SB 711 (the Conformity Act of 2025), signed October 1, 2025, advanced that date from January 1, 2015 to January 1, 2025 — the first update in a decade. This document is the comprehensive reference for what California conforms to, what it newly conforms to under SB 711, what it continues to reject despite the date update, and what it cannot conform to because the OBBBA was enacted after the conformity window.

## California Rule: Conformity Date History

California has periodically updated its IRC conformity date through legislation:

| Legislation | Specified Date | Effective For TY Beginning |
|-------------|---------------|---------------------------|
| AB 115 (Chapter 691, Statutes of 2005) | January 1, 2005 | On or after January 1, 2005 |
| SB 410 (Chapter 14, Statutes of 2010) | January 1, 2009 | On or after January 1, 2010 |
| AB 154 (Chapter 359, Statutes of 2015) | January 1, 2015 | On or after January 1, 2015 |
| **SB 711 (Statutes of 2025)** | **January 1, 2025** | **On or after January 1, 2025** |

The gap between AB 154 and SB 711 represents ten years of accumulated federal tax changes that went unaddressed by California's conformity statute. During this period, the TCJA (P.L. 115-97, December 2017), CARES Act (P.L. 116-136, March 2020), Consolidated Appropriations Act 2021 (P.L. 116-260), American Rescue Plan Act (P.L. 117-2), Inflation Reduction Act (P.L. 117-169), SECURE Act (P.L. 116-94), and SECURE 2.0 Act (within CAA 2023, P.L. 117-328) all took effect federally without automatic California adoption.

SB 711 addresses more than 1,000 substantive IRC changes enacted during that decade. California adopted many of them through the conformity date advance but expressly decoupled from others.

## Conformity Analysis

**IRC RULE:** The IRC is a unified code amended continuously by Congress. All provisions are effective as enacted.

**CALIFORNIA RULE:** R&TC Section 17024.5(a)(1)(Q) (PIT) and Section 23051.5 (CTL) define "Internal Revenue Code" by reference to the IRC as of January 1, 2025. California then specifically decouples from enumerated provisions.

**Controlling Authority:** SB 711 (Chapter XXX, Statutes of 2025); R&TC Sections 17024.5, 23051.5

**Schedule CA Impact:** Every area of non-conformity requires a Column B subtraction or Column C addition on Schedule CA (540) or Schedule CA (540NR).

## Items Newly Conformed Under SB 711

The following significant changes are now adopted by California for taxable years beginning on or after January 1, 2025, as a result of advancing the conformity date:

### Individual / PIT Provisions

1. **Alimony (post-2018 agreements):** California now conforms to the TCJA change making alimony non-deductible by the payor and non-includable by the recipient for divorce or separation instruments executed after December 31, 2018. Prior to SB 711, California still followed pre-TCJA rules (deductible by payor, includable by recipient).

2. **Moving expense deduction suspension:** California conforms to the TCJA suspension of the moving expense deduction for tax years 2018-2025 (except for active-duty military). Previously, California still allowed the deduction.

3. **Bicycle commuting reimbursement suspension:** California conforms to the TCJA suspension of the exclusion for qualified bicycle commuting reimbursements.

4. **Qualified moving expense reimbursement suspension:** California conforms to the TCJA suspension of the exclusion for qualified moving expense reimbursements (except for active-duty military).

5. **Like-kind exchanges limited to real property:** California now conforms to IRC Section 1031 as amended by TCJA, limiting like-kind exchanges to real property only. Personal property exchanges no longer qualify. SB 711 also adds a sunset provision for California's prior modified conformity (AGI limitation) on like-kind exchanges.

6. **Wagering loss limitation:** California conforms to the TCJA modification limiting all deductions for expenses incurred in wagering transactions (not just wagering losses) to the extent of wagering winnings.

7. **Limitation on luxury automobile depreciation (Section 280F):** California conforms to TCJA modifications with specific California adjustments.

8. **Real property recovery periods (Section 168):** California conforms to the TCJA change providing a 15-year recovery period for qualified improvement property (QIP) with straight-line method.

9. **SECURE Act conformity:** California generally conforms to retirement plan changes under the SECURE Act and SECURE 2.0 Act, including changes to required minimum distribution ages and other retirement provisions.

10. **Technical corrections:** Various technical corrections from the Tax Technical Corrections Act of 2018 and subsequent technical corrections acts are incorporated.

### Corporate / CTL Provisions

1. **Credit for prior year minimum tax liability:** California conforms to TCJA Section 12002(a) regarding the corporate AMT credit.
2. **REIT prohibited transaction tax:** California imposes a 12.5% tax on REIT prohibited transaction income.
3. **Original issue discount timing rules:** California conforms to allow OID timing rules instead of applicable financial statement income inclusion rules for certain payment fees.

### R&D Credit Changes Under SB 711

SB 711 introduces the Alternative Simplified Credit (ASC) method for computing the California research credit, replacing the Alternative Incremental Research Credit (AIRC):

- **ASC rate:** 3% of qualified research expenses (QREs) exceeding 50% of the average QREs for the prior three years.
- **Reduced rate:** 1.3% of current-year QREs if no QREs existed in any of the prior three years.
- **Election:** Must be made on an originally filed return. Switching methods requires FTB consent.
- **Comparison to federal ASC rates:** Federal rates are 14% and 6% respectively — California rates are significantly lower.

## Items That Remain Non-Conformed Despite Date Update

Despite advancing the conformity date to January 1, 2025, SB 711 specifically decouples from the following TCJA and other provisions:

### Depreciation and Expensing

| Provision | IRC Section | Federal Rule | California Rule | R&TC Authority |
|-----------|------------|--------------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Bonus depreciation** | 168(k) | 100% first-year deduction (OBBBA restored) | Not allowed; use MACRS without bonus | R&TC Section 17250 et seq. |
| **Section 179 expensing** | 179 | $2,500,000 limit / $4,000,000 phaseout (OBBBA) | $25,000 limit / $200,000 phaseout | R&TC Section 17255 |
| **Luxury automobile limits (Section 280F)** | 280F | TCJA-increased limits | CA has own limits | R&TC Section 17268 |

### Deductions and Exclusions

| Provision | IRC Section | Federal Rule | California Rule | R&TC Authority |
|-----------|------------|--------------|-----------------|---------------|
| **QBI deduction** | 199A | 20% deduction for qualified business income (permanent per OBBBA) | Not allowed | No R&TC incorporation |
| **Business interest limitation** | 163(j) | 30% of adjusted taxable income (EBITDA basis per OBBBA) | California does not apply the Section 163(j) limitation | R&TC Section 24344(e); R&TC Section 24365(d) |
| **R&E expenditure capitalization** | 174 | Must capitalize and amortize over 5 years (domestic) or 15 years (foreign) | Fully deductible in year incurred | R&TC maintains pre-TCJA treatment |
| **Excess business loss limitation** | 461(l) | Limits business losses to $313,000 single / $626,000 MFJ (2025, indexed) | California does not conform | No R&TC incorporation |
| **Personal casualty losses** | 165 | Limited to federally declared disasters (TCJA) | California maintains own disaster loss rules | R&TC Sections 17207, 17207.13 |

### Individual Tax Structure

| Provision | IRC Section | Federal Rule | California Rule |
|-----------|------------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Standard deduction** | 63(c) | $16,100 single / $32,200 MFJ (OBBBA) | $5,706 single / $11,412 MFJ (CA independent) |
| **Personal exemption** | 151 | Suspended (TCJA/OBBBA) | CA uses exemption credits: $153 per filer, $475 per dependent |
| **Misc. itemized deductions** | 67 | Suspended (TCJA/OBBBA) | Retained with 2% AGI floor |
| **SALT deduction cap** | 164 | $40,000 cap (OBBBA) | No cap for CA itemized deductions |
| **Mortgage interest (acquisition debt)** | 163(h) | $750,000 limit (TCJA/OBBBA) | $1,000,000 limit (pre-TCJA amount) |

### Capital Gains and Investment Income

| Provision | IRC Section | Federal Rule | California Rule |
|-----------|------------|--------------|-----------------|
| **QSBS exclusion** | 1202 | Up to 100% exclusion; $75M cap per OBBBA | 50% exclusion under CA rules |
| **Capital gains rates** | 1(h) | Preferential 0%/15%/20% | Taxed as ordinary income (up to 13.3%) |
| **NIIT (Net Investment Income Tax)** | 1411 | 3.8% surtax | Does not apply for CA |

### Corporate Provisions

| Provision | IRC Section | Federal Rule | California Rule |
|-----------|------------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Corporate AMT (CAMT)** | 55-59 (as amended by IRA) | 15% on adjusted financial statement income | Does not apply for CA |
| **GILTI** | 951A | Inclusion of global intangible low-taxed income | Not incorporated by reference |
| **FDII** | 250 | Deduction for foreign-derived intangible income | Not incorporated by reference |
| **Subpart F** | 951-965 | CFC income inclusions | California historically decouples |

### NOL Treatment

California maintains its own NOL rules independent of federal changes:
- California decoupled from TCJA and CARES Act NOL changes.
- California has imposed periodic NOL suspension periods.
- CA NOL carryforward periods and limitations differ from federal rules.
- R&TC Sections 24416 et seq. govern corporate NOLs; R&TC Sections 17276 et seq. govern individual NOLs.

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE:** When preparing a California return for TY 2025 or 2026, first identify every federal deduction, credit, or exclusion claimed on the 1040. For each one, verify California conformity status using this document. If non-conformed, compute the Schedule CA Column B subtraction or Column C addition.

> **AGENT RULE:** For any federal provision enacted after January 1, 2025 (including all OBBBA provisions), assume California does NOT conform unless you can cite a specific R&TC section or subsequent legislation that adopts it.

> **AGENT RULE:** When a taxpayer claims bonus depreciation on their federal return (Form 4562), compute the California depreciation separately using MACRS without bonus and add the difference back as a Schedule CA Column C addition.

> **AGENT RULE:** When a taxpayer claims a QBI deduction (Form 8995 or 8995-A) on their federal return, add the full QBI deduction amount back as a Schedule CA Column C addition. California does not allow any QBI deduction.

> **AGENT RULE:** When a taxpayer claims an OBBBA-specific deduction (tip income, overtime pay, auto loan interest, senior deduction), add the full amount back as a Schedule CA Column C addition.

> **AGENT RULE:** For Section 174 R&E expenditures, note the asymmetry: federal law requires capitalization and amortization, but California allows full deduction. This creates a Schedule CA Column B subtraction for the difference between the federal amortized amount and the full expense.

## Common Traps

> **TRAP:** Assuming SB 711 automatically conforms California to everything enacted between 2015 and 2025. SB 711 includes extensive specific decoupling provisions. The date advance is the default, but exceptions are numerous and significant.

> **TRAP:** Confusing the Section 163(j) rules. Federally, business interest is limited to 30% of adjusted taxable income. California does NOT apply Section 163(j) at all — full business interest is deductible for California purposes. This is a California-favorable difference that requires a Schedule CA Column B subtraction.

> **TRAP:** Applying OBBBA Section 179 limits ($2.5M) for California purposes. California's Section 179 limit remains at $25,000 with a $200,000 phaseout. The difference can be over $2.4 million in denied deductions.

> **TRAP:** For alimony, the new conformity applies only to divorce instruments executed after December 31, 2018. Instruments executed on or before that date still follow prior law (deductible by payor, includable by recipient) for both federal and California purposes.

> **TRAP:** The R&D credit method change (from AIRC to ASC) under SB 711 must be elected on the originally filed return. A taxpayer who fails to elect on the original return cannot switch to ASC on an amended return without FTB consent.
