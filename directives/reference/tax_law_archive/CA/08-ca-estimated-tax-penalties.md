## Overview

California imposes penalties and interest for failure to file, failure to pay, underpayment of estimated tax, and various accuracy-related deficiencies. The estimated tax system operates independently from the federal system, with a notably lower threshold for when estimated payments are required ($500 for California vs. $1,000 for federal). California's penalty structure broadly parallels federal penalties but with important differences in thresholds, rates, and computation methods.

---

## California Rule

### Estimated Tax Requirements

California residents, nonresidents with California-source income, and part-year residents must make estimated tax payments if their expected California tax liability will be **$500 or more** for the tax year after subtracting withholding and credits. This threshold is significantly lower than the federal $1,000 threshold.

### Due Dates

California estimated tax payments follow this schedule:

| Installment | Due Date | Percentage of Annual Payment |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | April 15 | 30% |
| 2nd | June 15 | 40% |
| 3rd | September 15 | 0% (no payment due) |
| 4th | January 15 | 30% |

Note the asymmetric payment schedule: California requires 30% with the first installment, 40% with the second, no third installment, and 30% with the fourth. This differs from the federal equal-quarter system (25% each).

When a due date falls on a weekend or legal holiday, the deadline extends to the next business day.

### Safe Harbors

Taxpayers can avoid the underpayment of estimated tax penalty by meeting either of these safe harbors:

1. **90% of current year's tax** — Pay at least 90% of the tax shown on the current year's return through estimated payments and withholding.

2. **100% of prior year's tax** — Pay at least 100% of the tax shown on the prior year's return (including AMT). **This increases to 110%** if the prior year's California AGI exceeded $150,000 ($75,000 if MFS).

**High-income exception:** Taxpayers with 2025 California AGI equal to or greater than **$1,000,000** ($500,000 if MFS) **cannot use the prior year safe harbor**. They must base estimated payments on 90% of their current year's actual tax liability. This is a critical trap for taxpayers with volatile income (e.g., from stock sales, business distributions, or real estate transactions).

### Farmers and Fishermen

If at least two-thirds of the taxpayer's 2024 or 2025 gross income is from farming or fishing, special rules apply: a single estimated tax payment may be made by January 15, or the entire tax may be paid with the return filed by March 1.

### Late Filing vs. Late Payment

If both penalties apply simultaneously, the late filing penalty is reduced by the late payment penalty for the same month, effectively capping the combined monthly rate. The maximum combined penalty is 47.5% of unpaid tax (25% late filing + 22.5% late payment over 45 months).

If the return is filed on or before the extended due date, the delinquent filing penalty does not apply, but the late payment penalty applies if tax was not paid by the original due date.

## Interest

**Rate:** California uses the federal underpayment rate as established under IRC Section 6621. The rate is adjusted quarterly and is based on the federal short-term rate plus 3 percentage points.

**Computation:** Interest is **compounded daily**, not monthly. This differs from some states that compound monthly or use simple interest.

**Key rule:** Interest is **not tolled** (stopped) during an appeal before the Office of Tax Appeals (OTA). Interest continues to accrue on any disputed amount throughout the protest and appeal process. This means that even if the taxpayer ultimately prevails on the underlying tax issue, significant interest may have accrued if the dispute takes years to resolve.

Interest applies to both underpayments and overpayments, though the rates may differ (the overpayment rate is typically lower than the underpayment rate for non-corporate taxpayers).

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

The OBBBA does not make material changes to the federal estimated tax penalty structure. California's non-conformity to OBBBA has minimal direct impact on the penalty framework. However, because OBBBA changes (tip/overtime exclusions, SALT cap changes, bonus depreciation) may alter federal tax liability without corresponding California changes, taxpayers must compute California estimated taxes independently from federal estimates.

## One-Time Penalty Abatement

Under R&TC Section 19132.5 (added by AB 194), individual taxpayers may be eligible for a **one-time cancellation** of the failure-to-file (19131) and failure-to-pay (19132) penalties. Requirements:

- Once-in-a-lifetime per taxpayer
- Available only to individuals (not fiduciaries, estates, or trusts)
- Applicable for taxable years beginning on or after January 1, 2022
- Taxpayer must be compliant with all filing requirements
- All outstanding liabilities (excluding the penalties being abated) must be paid or on an installment plan
- Can be requested in writing, verbally, or through MyFTB

This is separate from and in addition to reasonable cause penalty abatement.

## Agent Rules

1. **California's $500 threshold is lower than federal's $1,000.** More taxpayers must make estimated payments to California than to the IRS. Any taxpayer expecting $500+ in California tax (after withholding and credits) must make estimated payments.

2. **The payment schedule is asymmetric.** Do not assume equal quarterly payments. The 30%/40%/0%/30% schedule means the June 15 payment is the largest single installment.

3. **High-income taxpayers ($1M+) cannot use prior year safe harbor.** For taxpayers with volatile income above $1,000,000 CA AGI, the only safe harbor is 90% of the current year's tax. This requires ongoing tax projection throughout the year.

4. **Interest accrues during OTA appeals.** When advising on protest and appeal strategy, factor in the interest cost of extended disputes. Even a successful appeal may result in significant interest charges if the process takes years.

5. **Include AMT in estimated tax calculations.** The safe harbor tests use total tax including AMT. Do not overlook AMT when computing the prior year safe harbor amount.

6. **PTE tax is computed separately.** The PTE elective tax liability is not included in the computation of the individual's estimated tax under R&TC Section 19136. However, the PTE tax credit does reduce the estimated payment computation for qualified taxpayers.

---

## Common Traps

1. **Using the federal $1,000 threshold for California.** California's $500 threshold catches many taxpayers who do not need to make federal estimated payments but do need to make California payments.

2. **Assuming equal quarterly payments.** The 30%/40%/0%/30% schedule means underpaying the June 15 installment triggers a larger penalty than underpaying other installments.

3. **Using prior year safe harbor when AGI exceeds $1,000,000.** High-income taxpayers must project current year tax. Relying on 110% of prior year tax is not a valid safe harbor when CA AGI reaches $1,000,000.

4. **Forgetting the Behavioral Health Services Tax (formerly Mental Health Services Tax).** Taxpayers with taxable income over $1,000,000 are subject to an additional 1% tax. This must be included in estimated tax calculations. The PTE credit cannot offset the BHS Tax.

5. **Not requesting one-time penalty abatement.** Eligible taxpayers may be unaware of the AB 194 one-time abatement, which can eliminate filing and payment penalties for a single tax year.

6. **Ignoring interest during disputes.** Interest compounds daily and does not stop during OTA appeals. The cost of a multi-year dispute can be significant.
