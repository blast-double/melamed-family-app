## Overview

California does **not** impose a self-employment tax. The self-employment tax (Social Security + Medicare) is exclusively a federal tax under IRC Sections 1401--1402. However, California imposes its own payroll tax system on wages paid to employees, administered by the Employment Development Department (EDD). This system includes State Disability Insurance (SDI), Unemployment Insurance (UI), Employment Training Tax (ETT), and Personal Income Tax (PIT) withholding. Since January 1, 2024, SDI has no wage cap, meaning all wages are subject to SDI contributions. California also applies a stricter worker classification test (the ABC test under AB 5/Dynamex) than the federal common-law test.

## California Rule

### No California Self-Employment Tax

1. **Self-employment tax is federal only.** There is no California equivalent of IRC Sections 1401--1402.
2. Sole proprietors, partners, and LLC members pay SE tax on their federal return but have no corresponding California SE tax liability.
3. The deductible portion of SE tax (50% of SE tax on Form 1040, Schedule 1) is a **federal-only** deduction. California does not allow a corresponding deduction because California does not impose the tax.
4. **Schedule CA adjustment:** The 50% SE tax deduction claimed federally may need to be added back on Schedule CA if the taxpayer itemizes. However, under R&TC Section 17072 (conformity to IRC Section 62), California generally conforms to the above-the-line deduction for SE tax.

|-----|----------|-----------|-----------|-------------------|-----------|

### SDI -- Wage Cap Elimination (SB 951)

1. Effective **January 1, 2024**, Senate Bill 951 removed the taxable wage limit for SDI contributions
2. Prior to 2024, SDI had an annual wage cap (e.g., $153,164 in 2023). SDI was only withheld on wages up to this cap.
3. Now, **all wages** are subject to SDI withholding, regardless of income level
4. Impact on high-wage employees:
   - A W-2 employee earning $500,000 pays: $6,000 SDI in 2025 (1.2%) / $6,500 in 2026 (1.3%)
   - Under the old system with a ~$153K cap: max was ~$1,378 (2023)
   - This is a **significant increase** for employees earning above the former cap
5. The SDI program funds both Disability Insurance (DI) and Paid Family Leave (PFL)

### UI Rate Schedule

1. California uses an experience-rated system for UI contributions
2. The 2025 and 2026 rate schedule is **Schedule F+** (Schedule F plus a 15% emergency surcharge, rounded to the nearest tenth)
3. Rates range from **1.5% to 6.2%** depending on the employer's experience rating
4. New employers are typically assigned a rate of 3.4% for the first 2--3 years
5. The taxable wage limit is **$7,000 per employee per calendar year**
6. Exception: Employers subject to CUIC Section 977(c) (SUTA dumping) pay the highest rate plus an additional 2%

## AB 5 / Dynamex ABC Test -- Worker Classification

### The ABC Test

California's AB 5 (effective January 1, 2020, codifying *Dynamex Operations West, Inc. v. Superior Court*) established a presumption that a worker is an **employee**, not an independent contractor. To classify a worker as an independent contractor, the hiring entity must demonstrate **all three** prongs:

1. **(A) Freedom from control:** The worker is free from the control and direction of the hiring entity in the performance of the work, both under the contract and in fact
2. **(B) Outside usual course of business:** The worker performs work that is outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business
3. **(C) Independent trade/occupation:** The worker is customarily engaged in an independently established trade, occupation, or business of the same nature as the work performed

**All three prongs must be satisfied.** Failure on **any single prong** results in classification as an employee.

### Comparison to Federal Test

| Feature | California (AB 5 / ABC Test) | Federal (Common Law / 20-Factor) |
|---------|------------------------------|----------------------------------|
| Presumption | Worker is an employee | Depends on facts and circumstances |
| Burden of proof | On the hiring entity | On the IRS |
| Prongs | All 3 must be met | Multi-factor balancing test |
| Strictness | Very strict | More flexible |
| Prong B test | Work must be outside usual business | No equivalent bright-line test |

### Misclassification Penalties

| Violation Type | Per-Violation Penalty | Authority |
|---------------|----------------------|-----------|
| Willful misclassification | $5,000 -- $15,000 | Labor Code Section 226.8 |
| Pattern/practice of willful misclassification | $10,000 -- $25,000 | Labor Code Section 226.8 |
| Wage statement violations | $250 -- $1,000 (up to $4,000) | Labor Code Section 226 |
| PAGA penalties (default) | $100 first violation, $200 subsequent, per pay period | Labor Code Section 2699 |

Additional consequences include: back wages, unpaid overtime, unpaid payroll taxes, interest, workers' compensation penalties, and attorney's fees.

### AB 5 Exemptions

Certain professions and industries have exemptions from the ABC test and instead use the older Borello multi-factor test. These include (but are not limited to):
- Licensed insurance agents, physicians, dentists, lawyers, architects, accountants
- Real estate agents (licensed)
- Direct sales persons
- Certain freelance writers, photographers, and artists (with limitations)

The exemptions are narrowly construed and require meeting specific statutory criteria.

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE:** California does NOT have a self-employment tax. When computing California tax liability, never include SE tax. The 15.3% SE tax is federal only.

> **AGENT RULE:** SDI has NO wage cap since January 1, 2024. When computing payroll costs for California employees, apply the SDI rate to **all wages** -- not just the first $X of wages. Use 1.2% for 2025, 1.3% for 2026.

> **AGENT RULE:** When evaluating whether a worker is an employee or independent contractor in California, apply the **ABC test**, not the federal 20-factor common-law test. The California test is significantly stricter. Prong B (outside usual course of business) is the most commonly failed prong.

> **AGENT RULE:** For multi-state employers, California payroll taxes apply to wages paid for services performed in California, regardless of where the employer is located. Employees working remotely in California are subject to California withholding and SDI.

## Common Traps

> **TRAP:** Assuming California has a self-employment tax. It does not. Self-employed individuals in California owe federal SE tax but no state-level equivalent. Do not model a California SE tax in financial projections.

> **TRAP:** Applying a wage cap to SDI calculations. The cap was eliminated effective January 1, 2024 (SB 951). Any payroll system still capping SDI at the old limit is computing incorrectly and will face EDD penalties.

> **TRAP:** Using the federal 20-factor test to classify workers in California. California requires the stricter ABC test under AB 5. A worker who passes the federal test may still be classified as an employee under California law.

> **TRAP:** Misclassification penalties are per-violation, not aggregate. A company that misclassifies 10 workers faces $50,000--$250,000 in civil penalties alone (before back wages, taxes, and attorney's fees). Pattern/practice findings escalate to $10,000--$25,000 per violation.

> **TRAP:** Ignoring Prong B of the ABC test. Even if a worker sets their own hours and uses their own tools (Prong A), if the work is within the hiring entity's usual course of business (Prong B fails), the worker is an employee. This is the most common failure point for technology and gig companies.
