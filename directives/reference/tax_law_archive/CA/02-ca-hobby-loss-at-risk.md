## Overview

California conforms to the federal hobby loss rules under IRC Section 183 and the at-risk limitation rules under IRC Section 465. Under the hobby loss rules, if an activity is not engaged in for profit, income from the activity is taxable but deductions (expenses) are nondeductible. Under the at-risk rules, a taxpayer's deductible loss from a business or income-producing activity is limited to the amount the taxpayer has "at risk" in the activity. Both rules are applied using California AGI (not federal AGI) for any income-based thresholds or phase-outs. California also conforms to the passive activity loss rules under IRC Section 469, with one significant modification: rental real estate activities of taxpayers engaged in a real property business are still treated as passive in California.

## California Rule

### Hobby Loss Rules -- IRC Section 183

1. **Conforms:** California conforms to IRC Section 183 through R&TC Section 17551 (block conformity to Subchapter E of the IRC)
2. **Presumption of profit motive:** An activity is presumed to be for profit if it produces a net profit in at least **3 out of 5** consecutive tax years (2 out of 7 for horse breeding, training, showing, or racing)
3. **If the activity is a hobby (not for profit):**
   - Income from the activity is **taxable** (reported on Schedule 1 or applicable schedule)
   - Expenses are **nondeductible** -- California conforms to the TCJA elimination of hobby expense deductions. Under pre-TCJA law, hobby expenses were deductible as miscellaneous itemized deductions up to hobby income. Under OBBBA, the elimination of miscellaneous itemized deductions is permanent. California's position: hobby expenses are nondeductible because the activity is not a trade or business, regardless of the miscellaneous itemized deduction rules.
4. **Nine-factor test:** The IRS and FTB use the same nine factors to determine profit motive:
   - Manner in which the taxpayer carries on the activity
   - Expertise of the taxpayer or advisors
   - Time and effort expended
   - Expectation that assets may appreciate
   - Success in similar or related activities
   - History of income or losses from the activity
   - Amount of occasional profits
   - Financial status of the taxpayer
   - Elements of personal pleasure or recreation

### At-Risk Rules -- IRC Section 465

1. **Conforms:** California conforms to IRC Section 465 at-risk limitations through R&TC Section 17551
2. **At-risk amount:** A taxpayer's deductible loss from a business activity is limited to the total amount the taxpayer has at risk in that activity at the close of the tax year
3. **Amounts at risk include:**
   - Cash contributed to the activity
   - Adjusted basis of property contributed
   - Amounts borrowed for use in the activity for which the taxpayer is personally liable (recourse debt)
   - Amounts borrowed secured by property not used in the activity
4. **Amounts NOT at risk include:**
   - Nonrecourse borrowing (except qualified nonrecourse financing for real estate)
   - Amounts protected against loss by guarantees, stop-loss agreements, or other similar arrangements
   - Amounts borrowed from persons with an interest in the activity (related-party nonrecourse debt)
5. **Qualified nonrecourse financing exception:** For real estate activities, qualified nonrecourse financing from certain institutional lenders is treated as an amount at risk. California conforms.
6. **Ordering:** Losses are first subject to at-risk rules, then to passive activity loss rules (IRC Section 469)

### Passive Activity Loss Rules -- IRC Section 469 (Conformity with Modification)

1. California generally conforms to IRC Section 469 passive activity loss rules
2. **Key California modification:** Rental real estate activities of taxpayers engaged in a **real property business** are still treated as **passive activities** in California, even if the taxpayer qualifies as a real estate professional under IRC Section 469(c)(7)
3. **$25,000 rental loss allowance:** California conforms to the $25,000 special allowance for rental real estate activities in which the taxpayer actively participates. The allowance is phased out between **$100,000 and $150,000** of **California modified AGI** (not federal AGI).
4. **Phase-out computation:** The $25,000 allowance is reduced by 50% of the excess of California modified AGI over $100,000, reaching zero at $150,000

## Conformity Analysis

**IRC RULE:** Hobby income taxable, hobby expenses nondeductible (IRC Section 183). Losses limited to at-risk amount (IRC Section 465). Passive losses limited to passive income (IRC Section 469).

**CALIFORNIA RULE:** Identical to federal for Sections 183 and 465. Conforms to Section 469 with modification: rental real estate of real property businesses remains passive in California. Phase-outs use California AGI.

**Controlling Authority:** R&TC Section 17551 (conformity to IRC Subchapter E -- accounting periods and methods, which encompasses Sections 183 and 465); R&TC Section 17551.5 (passive activity rules with California modifications).

**Schedule CA Impact:** Generally no adjustment for hobby loss or at-risk rules, as California conforms. Adjustment may be needed for the passive activity rules if the taxpayer is a real estate professional (federal allows active treatment; California does not). Phase-out differences between federal AGI and California AGI may also create adjustments for the $25,000 rental loss allowance.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity (Where Applicable)

The OBBBA (Section 70601) modifies and permanently extends the excess business loss limitation under IRC Section 461(l). While this does not directly amend Sections 183 or 465, the interaction matters:

1. **Federal:** Hobby losses are not "excess business losses" because the activity is not a trade or business. Section 461(l) does not apply to hobbies.
2. **California:** Same treatment. R&TC Section 17560.5 (California's version of excess business loss limitation) applies only to trades or businesses.
3. The OBBBA did not amend IRC Section 183 or 465. No direct non-conformity issue for hobby loss or at-risk rules.

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE:** When an activity generates consistent losses, apply the nine-factor test to determine if it is a hobby or a trade/business. If it is a hobby, income is taxable on both returns but expenses are nondeductible on both returns. No Schedule CA adjustment needed.

> **AGENT RULE:** For the $25,000 rental real estate loss allowance phase-out, use **California AGI** (which may differ from federal AGI due to conformity differences). Recalculate the phase-out using the California-specific amount.

> **AGENT RULE:** If the taxpayer qualifies as a real estate professional under IRC Section 469(c)(7), rental activity is treated as **active** on the federal return but remains **passive** on the California return. This creates a Schedule CA addition (disallowing the California passive loss that was allowed federally as an active loss).

> **AGENT RULE:** Apply losses in the correct order: (1) at-risk limitation (Section 465), then (2) passive activity limitation (Section 469), then (3) excess business loss limitation (Section 461(l) / R&TC 17560.5 for California). A loss that fails at an earlier stage never reaches the later stages.

## Common Traps

> **TRAP:** Assuming a real estate professional can deduct rental losses against active income in California. California does **not** conform to the real estate professional exception under IRC Section 469(c)(7). Rental activities remain passive in California regardless of the taxpayer's real estate professional status.

> **TRAP:** Using federal AGI instead of California AGI for the $25,000 rental loss allowance phase-out. The phase-out starts at $100,000 of **California** modified AGI. Depreciation add-backs and other conformity differences can cause California AGI to differ significantly from federal AGI, changing the phase-out calculation.

> **TRAP:** Deducting hobby expenses on the California return because California did not conform to the TCJA miscellaneous itemized deduction suspension. This reasoning is incorrect. Hobby expenses are nondeductible because the activity is not a trade or business, regardless of the itemized deduction rules. The nondeductibility comes from Section 183, not Section 67(g).

> **TRAP:** Confusing at-risk limitations with passive activity limitations. They are separate rules applied in a specific order. A taxpayer may have sufficient at-risk basis but still have the loss disallowed under the passive activity rules. Both computations must be performed.

> **TRAP:** Forgetting to adjust at-risk basis for California-specific items. If California depreciation differs from federal (due to bonus depreciation non-conformity), the at-risk basis recovery can differ. Ensure at-risk calculations use California-specific amounts.
