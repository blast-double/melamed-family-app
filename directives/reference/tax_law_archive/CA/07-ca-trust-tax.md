## Overview

California's trust taxation is governed by a residency-based framework that looks to the residency of the trustee(s), the residency of the non-contingent beneficiary(ies), and whether the trust has California-source income. The tax consequences vary dramatically depending on these factors. Trusts are taxed at the same rates as individuals (up to 12.3%, plus the 1% Mental Health Services tax on income over $1 million, for a maximum rate of 13.3%), and the compressed trust income tax brackets mean trusts reach the highest marginal rates much faster than individuals.

California has taken an aggressive stance against tax planning strategies that attempt to move trust income outside California, most notably through the anti-ING trust statute (R&TC Section 17082, effective January 1, 2023) and FTB Legal Ruling 2019-02 regarding the taxation of trusts with California-resident non-contingent beneficiaries.

## California Rule

### Resident vs. Nonresident Trust

The determination of whether a trust is a "resident" or "nonresident" for California tax purposes depends on the residency of the fiduciaries (trustees) and the non-contingent beneficiaries. California does not follow a single bright-line rule; instead, it applies an apportionment methodology based on the proportion of California-resident trustees and beneficiaries.

**Key principles:**

1. **All trustees and all non-contingent beneficiaries are California residents**: The trust is fully taxable in California on all income from all sources.

2. **All trustees and all non-contingent beneficiaries are outside California, AND no California-source income**: Generally, no California filing requirement and no California tax.

3. **Mixed residency (some trustees or beneficiaries in California, some outside)**: The trust uses Schedule G (Form 541) to apportion income between California and non-California sources based on the proportion of California-resident trustees and beneficiaries.

4. **California-source income**: Regardless of trustee/beneficiary residency, California-source income (rental income from California property, California business income, etc.) is always taxable by California.

### Apportionment Under Schedule G

Form 541, Schedule G provides the apportionment methodology for trusts with mixed residency:

- **Column A**: California-source income (always taxable in CA regardless of residency)
- **Column B**: Non-California-source income
- **Column C**: Apportioned income based on ratio of CA trustees to total trustees
- **Column E**: Apportioned income based on ratio of CA non-contingent beneficiaries to total non-contingent beneficiaries
- **Column F**: Total income reportable to California (sum of Column A + Column C + Column E)

### Tax Rates

Trusts and estates are subject to the same 9-bracket California income tax rate structure as individuals, ranging from 1% to 12.3%. The 1% Mental Health Services (MHS) tax applies to trust taxable income over $1 million, bringing the maximum effective rate to **13.3%**.

However, trusts have highly compressed brackets. For 2025, trusts reach the 12.3% bracket at approximately $742,953 of taxable income (using the single filer schedule). The MHS tax adds 1% above $1 million. Note that the 2025 Form 541 instructions reference the applicable tax rate schedule for trusts, which uses the same rates as single filers.

**Behavioral Health Services Tax (formerly MHS Tax)**: Beginning with the 2025 Form 541, line 27 references the "Behavioral Health Services Tax" -- the renamed Mental Health Services Tax. The substance has not changed: 1% additional tax on taxable income over $1 million.

### Grantor Trusts

California conforms to the federal grantor trust rules under IRC Sections 671-679 (via R&TC Section 17731). If a trust is a grantor trust for federal purposes, it is a grantor trust for California purposes. The grantor (not the trust) reports and pays tax on the trust's income.

For California residents who are grantors of a grantor trust, all trust income is taxable in California regardless of where the trust is domiciled, where the trustee is located, or where the assets are situated.

### Form 541 Filing Requirements

Form 541 must be filed when the trust or estate has:
- A California-resident fiduciary (trustee), OR
- A California-resident non-contingent beneficiary, OR
- California-source income

**Do NOT file Form 541** if there are no California fiduciaries, no California non-contingent beneficiaries, and no California-source income.

**Due date**: April 15 for calendar-year trusts, with a 5-month automatic extension (to September 15). Estimated tax payments are due quarterly.

## FTB Legal Ruling 2019-02 and Non-Contingent Beneficiaries

### The Proportionate Allocation Rule

FTB Legal Ruling 2019-02 addresses the taxation of irrevocable trusts with California-resident non-contingent beneficiaries. Under this ruling, if any non-contingent beneficiary of a trust is a California resident, California taxes a proportionate share of the trust's non-California-source income based on the ratio of California-resident non-contingent beneficiaries to total non-contingent beneficiaries.

This ruling is significant because it prevents trusts from escaping California taxation simply by appointing out-of-state trustees when the economic beneficiaries are California residents.

### Definition of Non-Contingent Beneficiary

A non-contingent beneficiary is a beneficiary whose interest in the trust is not subject to a condition precedent. If a beneficiary has a present right to receive distributions (whether or not distributions are actually made), that beneficiary is non-contingent. Remainder beneficiaries whose interests are subject to conditions (e.g., surviving to a certain age) are generally contingent.

## NING Trusts -- Do NOT Work for California Residents

### Background

Nevada Incomplete-Gift Non-Grantor (NING) trusts and Delaware Incomplete-Gift Non-Grantor (DING) trusts were marketed as strategies for California residents to avoid state income taxes by transferring assets to a non-grantor trust in a state with no income tax (Nevada, Delaware, etc.). Because the gift was "incomplete" for federal transfer tax purposes, no gift tax was due, and because the trust was a non-grantor trust, the income was not taxed to the grantor at the federal level.

### R&TC Section 17082 -- Anti-ING Statute (Effective January 1, 2023)

California enacted R&TC Section 17082 to close the ING trust loophole. Under this provision:

- The income of an incomplete gift non-grantor trust must be included in the qualified taxpayer's (grantor's) California gross income, to the extent the trust were treated as a grantor trust under R&TC Section 17731.
- An "incomplete gift non-grantor trust" is defined as a trust that (A) does not qualify as a grantor trust under IRC Sections 671-679, and (B) the qualified taxpayer's transfer of assets to the trust is treated as an incomplete gift under IRC Section 2511.
- The statute effectively treats ING trusts as grantor trusts for California tax purposes, regardless of their federal classification.

**Exception:** ING trust income is not included in the grantor's gross income if (1) the fiduciary timely files Form 541 and makes an irrevocable election to be treated as a resident non-grantor trust, (2) the trust is a non-grantor trust, and (3) 90% or more of the trust's distributable net income is distributed to a charitable organization under IRC Section 501(c)(3).

### Practical Effect

For California residents, NING and DING trusts no longer provide any California income tax benefit. The grantor's California tax liability is the same whether assets are held in the ING trust or held directly. The only remaining use cases are for federal tax planning or for grantors who are not California residents.

Non-resident grantors of ING trusts have a California filing requirement only if the ING trust has California-source taxable income.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

The OBBBA does not directly alter California's trust taxation framework. However:

- The OBBBA's permanent extension of the QBI deduction (IRC Section 199A at 23%) does NOT apply to California trusts. Trusts that pass through QBI to beneficiaries do not receive a California deduction.
- The increased federal estate tax exemption ($15 million in 2026) affects trust funding strategies but has no California estate tax impact (since California has no estate tax).
- The SALT cap changes (increased to $40,000 for 2025-2029) may affect whether trust beneficiaries need the PTE elective tax. Trusts themselves are not PTE-eligible entities, but their beneficiaries' overall tax situation may be affected.

## Agent Rules

1. **A trust with CA-resident non-contingent beneficiaries cannot escape CA tax.** Regardless of where the trust is domiciled or who serves as trustee, if any non-contingent beneficiary is a California resident, California taxes a proportionate share of the trust's income (FTB Legal Ruling 2019-02).
2. **NING/DING trusts do not work for California resident grantors.** R&TC Section 17082 treats ING trusts as grantor trusts for California purposes. Do not recommend ING trust strategies for California residents seeking state income tax savings.
3. **Always check trustee AND beneficiary residency.** California trust taxation depends on both. A change in trustee or beneficiary residency can change the trust's California tax exposure.
4. **Apply the compressed brackets.** Trust income reaches the highest California tax rate much faster than individual income. Model the tax impact of retaining income in the trust vs. distributing to beneficiaries.
5. **File Schedule G for mixed-residency trusts.** If the trust has any combination of resident and nonresident trustees or beneficiaries, Schedule G must be completed to properly apportion income.

## Common Traps

1. **NING trusts -- still recommended by out-of-state advisors.** Advisors in Nevada and Delaware may still recommend NING/DING trusts to California clients without awareness of R&TC Section 17082. These strategies have been fully neutralized for California residents since January 1, 2023.

2. **Contingent vs. non-contingent beneficiary classification.** The California tax exposure depends critically on whether beneficiaries are "non-contingent." Misclassifying a non-contingent beneficiary as contingent can result in under-reporting and penalties. The FTB's interpretation is that a beneficiary with a present right to receive distributions is non-contingent, even if no distributions are actually made.

3. **Grantor trust status change.** If a grantor trust ceases to be a grantor trust (e.g., due to the grantor's death or release of a grantor trust power), the trust becomes a separate taxpayer. This change in status can trigger unexpected California tax obligations, particularly if the trust has been operating without filing Form 541 as a grantor trust.

4. **Accumulation distributions from non-filing trusts.** If a trust with a California-resident non-contingent beneficiary has not been filing California returns and paying California tax as income was accumulated, the full amount of any accumulation distribution is taxable to the beneficiary in the year received. Federal Schedule J (1041) adjustments are required for California purposes.

5. **MHS tax on trust income over $1 million.** The 1% Behavioral Health Services Tax applies to trust taxable income over $1 million. This tax cannot be offset by the PTE elective tax credit or many other credits. High-income trusts must account for this additional layer.

6. **Moving trustee or beneficiary out of California does not retroactively eliminate CA tax.** A change in residency applies prospectively. Income accumulated while the trustee or beneficiary was a California resident remains subject to California tax for that period.
