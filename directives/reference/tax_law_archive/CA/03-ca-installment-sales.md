## Overview

California generally conforms to the federal installment sale method under IRC Section 453. The installment method allows a seller to recognize gain proportionally as payments are received, rather than recognizing the entire gain in the year of sale. This is the default method for any sale where at least one payment is received after the close of the taxable year of the sale, unless the taxpayer elects out.

While the general mechanics are the same, California introduces important computational differences because the California adjusted basis of property frequently differs from the federal adjusted basis. This occurs because California does not conform to bonus depreciation (IRC Section 168(k)), has different Section 179 limits, and has its own depreciation adjustments. When the basis differs, the California gross profit ratio -- the percentage of each payment that is treated as gain -- will differ from the federal ratio, requiring a separate California computation on Form FTB 3805E.

## California Rule

### General Installment Method Conformity

California law is the same as federal law concerning installment sales, as stated in the FTB 3805E instructions: "Generally, California law is the same as federal law concerning installment sales." The rules conform through R&TC Sections 17551 (general accounting methods), 17560, and 24667 (installment sales for corporations). Specific conformity points include:

**Eligible transactions.** A sale qualifies for the installment method if at least one payment will be received after the close of the tax year of sale. Dealer dispositions (inventory, personal property sold by dealers) are excluded, consistent with federal law.

**Gross profit ratio.** The gross profit (selling price minus adjusted basis) divided by the contract price determines the percentage of each payment recognized as gain. For California, this ratio must be computed using the California adjusted basis, which may differ from the federal adjusted basis.

**Ordinary income recapture.** Depreciation recapture under IRC Sections 1245, 1250, 179, and 291 must be reported in full in the year of sale, even if no payments are received. California conforms, but the recapture amount may differ because California depreciation differs from federal depreciation.

**Electing out.** A taxpayer may elect out of the installment method under IRC Section 453(d) by reporting the entire gain in the year of sale on Schedule D-1. California conforms to this election. An election made for federal purposes is binding for California purposes under R&TC Section 17024.5.

**Related-party sales.** If property (other than marketable securities) is sold to a related party using the installment method, the seller must complete Form FTB 3805E for the year of sale and for two years following, even if no payment is received. If the related party disposes of the property within two years, the remaining deferred gain is accelerated. California conforms to these rules.

### Section 453A Interest on Deferred Tax (Large Obligations)

California conforms to IRC Section 453A, which imposes interest on the deferred tax from installment obligations where both of the following apply:

1. The property sold has a sales price exceeding $150,000.
2. The taxpayer's total outstanding installment obligations from dispositions during the year (and in prior years still outstanding) exceed $5,000,000 at the close of the year.

**Key California difference:** The interest is computed using the maximum California tax rate rather than the maximum federal rate. The FTB 3805E instructions direct taxpayers to "Substitute the maximum rate of tax imposed under R&TC Section 17041, 23151, 23186, or 23802, whichever applies, for the maximum rate of tax under IRC Section 1 or 11."

For individuals, the maximum California rate is 12.3% (plus the 1% Mental Health Services Tax on taxable income exceeding $1 million, for a total of 13.3%). For C corporations, the rate is 8.84%.

**Reporting:** The interest is not figured on Form FTB 3805E. It is computed separately per IRC Section 453A and reported as an additional tax on the California return. Individuals report the amount on Form 540, line 63 (or Form 540NR, line 73), writing "IRC Section 453A interest" on the dotted line.

**Deductibility:** Corporations may deduct the Section 453A interest in the year it is paid or accrued. Individuals and other taxpayers may not deduct this interest.

### Section 453(l) Interest on Timeshares and Residential Lots

California conforms to IRC Section 453(l), which requires interest on the deferred tax from all installment obligations arising from the disposition of timeshares and residential lots, regardless of the $5 million threshold. The interest computation follows the same rules as Section 453A, substituting the California maximum tax rate.

### Installment Sales of California Real Property -- Withholding

When California real property is sold on an installment basis, withholding under Form 593 applies to the principal portion of each installment payment (excluding interest). The withholding options are:

- **Total Sales Price Method:** 3 1/3% (0.0333) of the principal portion of each payment.
- **Alternative Withholding Calculation:** The gain portion of each payment multiplied by the applicable maximum tax rate (e.g., 12.3% for individuals).

The buyer is responsible for withholding, filing Form 593, and remitting payment with Form 593-V by the 20th day of the month following each installment payment. The seller may elect out of installment method withholding on future payments by reporting the entire gain in the year of sale and requesting FTB release the buyer from withholding obligations.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

The installment sale rules under IRC Section 453 were not materially changed by the OBBBA. California's conformity to installment sale rules is generally current. The primary area where differences arise remains the basis computation (due to bonus depreciation non-conformity) rather than the installment method mechanics themselves.

## Agent Rules

1. **Always compute the California gross profit ratio independently from the federal ratio.** If the property was subject to different depreciation treatment (bonus depreciation federally, no bonus depreciation for California), the California adjusted basis will be higher, resulting in a lower gross profit ratio and less gain recognized per payment for California. This is the most critical compliance point.

2. **Use the California maximum tax rate (not the federal rate) when computing Section 453A interest.** For individuals, the relevant rate is 12.3% under R&TC Section 17041 (or 13.3% including the Mental Health Services Tax for taxpayers with over $1M in taxable income). For corporations, use 8.84% under R&TC Section 23151.

3. **Track installment sale withholding on Form 593 for California real property.** The buyer must withhold on the principal portion of each installment payment and remit to FTB by the 20th of the following month. Ensure the correct withholding method (3 1/3% or alternative) is applied consistently.

4. **Report ordinary income recapture in full in the year of sale, using California depreciation amounts.** If California depreciation differs from federal, the recapture amount will differ. This is common when bonus depreciation was claimed federally.

5. **For related-party installment sales, file Form FTB 3805E for 3 consecutive years** (year of sale plus two years), even if no payments are received in a particular year.

## Common Traps

1. **Using the federal gross profit ratio on the California return.** This is the most frequent error. When California basis exceeds federal basis (because less depreciation was claimed for California), the California gross profit ratio will be lower. Each payment will include less gain for California, and the Schedule CA (540) adjustment must reflect this difference.

2. **Failing to adjust the Section 453A interest computation for the California rate.** Taxpayers who compute 453A interest using the federal maximum rate of 37% instead of California's 12.3% will significantly overstate the California interest charge. The computation must be done separately for each jurisdiction.

3. **Neglecting installment payment withholding obligations (buyer side).** The buyer of California real property in an installment sale must withhold on every principal payment and file Form 593 with each payment. Many buyers are unaware of this continuing obligation after escrow closes.

4. **Not tracking California-specific installment sale basis when the property had a cost segregation study.** Cost segregation creates different depreciation schedules for federal versus California. The cumulative depreciation difference directly affects the California gross profit ratio for the installment sale.

5. **Ignoring the interaction between installment sales and passive activity rules.** Gain recognized on installment payments from a passive activity is passive income for California (remember: rental real estate is always passive for California). This affects the passive activity computation on Form FTB 3801 each year payments are received.

6. **Forgetting that the elect-out decision is binding across jurisdictions.** If the taxpayer elects out of the installment method on the federal return under IRC 453(d), this election is binding for California under R&TC Section 17024.5. The entire gain must be reported in the year of sale for California as well, and withholding on the full sales price (not installment payments) applies.
