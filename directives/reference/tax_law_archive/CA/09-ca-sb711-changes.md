## Overview

Senate Bill 711 (the "Conformity Act of 2025"), signed by Governor Gavin Newsom on October 1, 2025, updated California's Internal Revenue Code (IRC) conformity date from January 1, 2015, to January 1, 2025 — the first conformity update in a decade. This means California now incorporates IRC changes enacted from 2015 through December 31, 2024. However, California continues to decouple from numerous major provisions of the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA, P.L. 115-97, enacted December 22, 2017) and does not conform to any provision of the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA, P.L. 119-21, enacted July 4, 2025), which falls after the new conformity date.

The FTB's own bill analysis noted that "more than 1,000 substantive changes" were made to the IRC since 2015. SB 711 selectively incorporates many of these changes while explicitly decoupling from dozens of others.

---

## 1. Conformity Date Change: 2015 to 2025

### What Changed

R&TC Section 17024.5(a)(1)(Q) (personal income tax) and Section 23051.5 (corporate tax) were amended to reference the IRC "as of January 1, 2025." Previously, these sections referenced the IRC "as of January 1, 2015."

### Practical Meaning

- California tax law now automatically incorporates IRC provisions as amended through December 31, 2024, **to the extent those provisions were already incorporated by reference** in the R&TC.
- If California previously had standalone law for a topic (not incorporating the IRC by reference), the conformity date change alone does not create new conformity.
- SB 711 makes over 100 specific statutory changes to either conform, modify, or explicitly not conform to specific IRC provisions.
- The net result is a mixture: some long-standing Schedule CA adjustments are eliminated, while others are preserved or newly created.

## 2. Items Newly Conformed (Removes Prior Schedule CA Adjustments)

These items previously required adjustments on Schedule CA because California was stuck at the 2015 conformity date. With SB 711, these adjustments are no longer necessary:

### Alimony (SB 711 Sections 15, 28, 45, 57)
- **Pre-SB 711:** California followed the pre-2019 federal rule — alimony was deductible by the payor and includable by the recipient for all agreements, regardless of execution date.
- **Post-SB 711:** For divorce or separation agreements executed on or after January 1, 2026, or existing agreements modified after December 31, 2025 (if the modification expressly provides that SB 711 amendments apply), alimony is no longer deductible by the payor and is not included in the recipient's income. This aligns with the federal TCJA change, effective one year later for California.
- **Schedule CA adjustment eliminated** for new agreements after 2025.

### Moving Expenses
- California now conforms to the TCJA suspension of the moving expense deduction for non-military taxpayers. Prior to SB 711, California still allowed the moving expense deduction. The adjustment on Schedule CA for moving expenses is no longer needed (except for active-duty military, where the deduction is still available both federally and for California).

### Bicycle Commuting Exclusion
- The TCJA suspended the employer-provided bicycle commuting fringe benefit exclusion. California now conforms. The Schedule CA adjustment for this item is eliminated.

### Section 1031 Like-Kind Exchange — Personal Property
- The TCJA limited Section 1031 exchanges to real property only (eliminating exchanges of personal property such as equipment, vehicles, and artwork). California now conforms to this limitation. Previously, California still allowed personal property exchanges under the pre-2018 rules.

### Technical Corrections and Miscellaneous
- Numerous technical corrections from the Tax Technical Corrections Act, Bipartisan Budget Act provisions, SECURE Act, SECURE 2.0 Act, CAA 2021, and other post-2015 legislation are incorporated.
- Partnership audit rules (BBA regime) conformity
- Clarification of tax basis of life insurance contracts
- Partnership interest built-in loss rules
- Partner's distributive share rules
- Deduction for fines and penalties (limits deductions per TCJA)
- Denial of deduction for settlements with NDAs related to sexual harassment/abuse
- Local lobbying expense deduction elimination
- Various information reporting provisions

## 3. Items Still Non-Conformed (TCJA Provisions)

Despite the 10-year conformity date advancement, California continues to decouple from many major TCJA provisions. These items still require Schedule CA adjustments:

### Section 168(k) — Bonus Depreciation
California does **not** conform to 100% (or any rate of) bonus depreciation. Taxpayers must compute depreciation using California's pre-TCJA rules (generally MACRS without bonus). This creates ongoing basis differences between federal and California returns and affects gain/loss on disposition.

### Section 179 — Increased Expensing
California does **not** conform to the TCJA increase in Section 179 expensing limits. California maintains its own lower Section 179 limits (California Section 179 limit: $25,000 with phaseout beginning at $200,000 for 2025).

### Section 199A — Qualified Business Income Deduction
California does **not** allow the 20% qualified business income deduction for pass-through entities and sole proprietors. This deduction does not exist on the California return.

### Section 163(j) — Business Interest Expense Limitation
California does **not** conform to the TCJA's limitation on business interest expense deductions (30% of ATI/EBIT). SB 711 specifically decoupled for corporate income tax purposes. SB 1435 (proposed 2026) would clarify decoupling for personal income tax purposes as well.

### Standard Deduction
California has its own standalone standard deduction ($5,706 single; $11,412 MFJ for 2025), far lower than the federal amount. California does not conform to the TCJA's increased federal standard deduction.

### Personal Exemption
California retains its personal exemption credit system. The federal personal exemption was reduced to $0 by the TCJA. California continues to allow exemption credits under its own rules.

### Miscellaneous Itemized Deductions (Section 67)
California does **not** conform to the TCJA's suspension of miscellaneous itemized deductions subject to the 2% AGI floor. These remain deductible on the California return.

### Section 1202 — QSBS Exclusion
California does **not** conform to the 100% qualified small business stock gain exclusion. California limits the QSBS exclusion to 50% of eligible gain, with further limitations.

### Section 461(l) — Excess Business Loss Limitation
California has its own standalone provision under R&TC Section 17560.5 (FTB 3461) that parallels but differs from the federal excess business loss limitation. The amounts are: $313,000 single; $626,000 MFJ for 2025.

### Net Operating Loss (NOL) Provisions
California does not conform to TCJA/CARES Act NOL changes. California maintains standalone NOL rules under R&TC Section 24416/17276, including:
- NOL suspension for 2024-2026 (for taxpayers with taxable income over $1,000,000)
- California carryover and carryback rules differ from federal

### Corporate AMT
California does not conform to the IRA's 15% corporate AMT on AFSI (IRC Section 56A). California applies its own 6.65% corporate AMT under pre-existing rules.

### Section 174 — R&E Expenditures
California does not conform to the TCJA's mandatory capitalization and amortization of research and experimental expenditures. California retains the pre-TCJA expensing treatment for both domestic and foreign R&E costs. This is a major planning opportunity — California allows immediate deduction while federal requires 5-year (domestic) or 15-year (foreign) amortization.

### GILTI / FDII
California does not incorporate IRC Section 951A (GILTI) or the FDII deduction. California's historic policy of decoupling from Subpart F (IRC Sections 951-965) continues.

### Health Savings Accounts (HSAs)
California does not conform to HSA treatment. Contributions to HSAs are not deductible for California, and HSA earnings are taxable.

### Mortgage Interest ($750,000 Cap)
California conforms to the $750,000 mortgage acquisition indebtedness limit via SB 711 for loans originated after December 15, 2017. Pre-existing loans remain at $1,000,000.

## 5. R&D Credit ASC Changes

SB 711 revises the California research credit calculation methods (R&TC Section 17052.12 / 23609):

- **Alternative Simplified Credit (ASC) now available:** For tax years beginning on or after January 1, 2025, California taxpayers may elect the ASC method.
- **California ASC rate:** 3% (compared to federal 14%)
- **Reduced ASC rate:** 1.3% for taxpayers with no qualified research expenditures in one of the three prior tax years (compared to federal 6%)
- **Alternative Incremental Research Credit (AIRC)** method is **no longer available**
- California taxpayers are not required to capitalize and amortize R&E expenditures under Section 174/174A. California retains the pre-TCJA expensing treatment for both domestic and foreign R&E costs.

---

## 6. Banking / Financial Institution Apportionment Changes

SB 711 includes amendments to the apportionment rules for banks and financial institutions doing business in California. These changes were enacted alongside the general conformity update but address California-specific apportionment methodology rather than IRC conformity.

Key changes include modifications to how financial institutions apportion income for California franchise tax purposes, affecting the calculation of California-source taxable income for banks, savings institutions, and financial corporations. The changes align California's treatment with evolving financial services industry structures.

### Impact on Tax Return Preparation

For every OBBBA provision that California does not conform to, Schedule CA adjustments may be required. The most common adjustments for individual returns:

1. **Tips/overtime/car loan interest:** These federal above-the-line deductions must be added back on Schedule CA, Part I, as California does not allow them.
2. **SALT cap:** The federal $40,000 cap must be backed out; California allows full property tax deduction but disallows CA income taxes.
3. **Bonus depreciation:** Any federal bonus depreciation must be reversed; California depreciation uses MACRS without bonus.
4. **Section 179:** Federal Section 179 amounts above California's $25,000 limit must be adjusted.
5. **Gambling losses:** For 2026+, the federal 90% limitation must be reversed; California allows full deductibility up to winnings.

## Agent Rules

1. **The conformity date is January 1, 2025 — not July 4, 2025.** OBBBA provisions are completely excluded. Do not apply any OBBBA change to any California return for any tax year.

2. **Review Schedule CA adjustments.** Many prior-year adjustments are eliminated. Alimony, moving expenses, personal property exchanges, and various technical provisions no longer require Schedule CA adjustments for 2025+ tax years.

3. **Many TCJA non-conformities remain.** Do not assume that SB 711 conformed to all TCJA provisions. Bonus depreciation, Section 199A, Section 163(j), standard deduction, personal exemptions, miscellaneous itemized deductions, and many other items still require Schedule CA adjustments.

4. **R&D is a planning opportunity.** California allows expensing of both domestic and foreign R&E costs while federal requires amortization. The new ASC method at 3% provides an additional credit calculation option.

5. **Track basis differences.** Non-conformity to bonus depreciation, Section 179, and Section 174 amortization creates ongoing basis differences. These affect gain/loss calculations on asset disposition and must be tracked throughout the asset's life.

6. **OBBBA creates many new Schedule CA adjustments.** For 2025 and later tax years, the number of required Schedule CA adjustments has significantly increased due to OBBBA provisions. Each OBBBA deduction or exclusion claimed on the federal return that California does not conform to requires a corresponding addition on Schedule CA.

---

## Common Traps

1. **Assuming SB 711 means full federal conformity.** It does not. The bill explicitly decouples from dozens of significant federal provisions.

2. **Applying OBBBA provisions to California.** The OBBBA falls entirely outside the conformity window. No OBBBA provision applies to any California return.

3. **Continuing to make Schedule CA adjustments for newly conformed items.** Alimony (for new agreements after 2025), moving expenses, and personal property exchanges no longer require adjustments.

4. **Overlooking the R&D credit method change.** The ASC is now available but at a much lower rate (3% vs. federal 14%). The AIRC method is no longer available.

5. **Not updating depreciation schedules.** While California still does not allow bonus depreciation, the conformity date change may affect depreciation for certain asset classes or methods that were modified by post-2015 legislation other than the TCJA.

6. **Confusing SB 711 and SB 132.** SB 711 is the conformity act. SB 132 is the PTE extension act. Both were enacted in 2025 but address different topics.
