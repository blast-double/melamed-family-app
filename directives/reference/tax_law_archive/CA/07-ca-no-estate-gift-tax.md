## Overview

California has no state estate tax, no state inheritance tax, no state gift tax, and no state generation-skipping transfer (GST) tax. These taxes were repealed by voter initiative in 1982 (Proposition 5 repealed the inheritance tax; Proposition 6 prohibited future enactment of any estate, gift, or inheritance taxes). The repeal is enshrined in R&TC Section 13301 and can only be reversed by a statewide voter initiative. California residents are subject only to federal estate, gift, and GST taxes administered by the IRS.

This means estate and wealth transfer planning for California residents focuses entirely on federal transfer tax obligations and California income tax consequences of inherited assets.

## California Rule

### No State Estate Tax

R&TC Section 13301 provides:

> "Neither the state nor any political subdivision of the state shall impose any gift, inheritance, succession, legacy, income, or estate tax, or any other tax, on gifts or on the estate or inheritance of any person or on or by reason of any transfer occurring by reason of a death."

This prohibition was enacted by California voters on June 8, 1982, through Proposition 6 (and companion Proposition 5 which repealed the then-existing inheritance tax). The statute further provides that the Legislature cannot amend or repeal this prohibition except by an enactment approved by a majority of voters in a statewide election.

California previously had a "pick-up tax" (also known as a "sponge tax") that was equal to the state death tax credit allowed under the federal estate tax. The federal Economic Growth and Tax Relief Reconciliation Act of 2001 (EGTRRA) phased out the state death tax credit and replaced it with a deduction, effectively eliminating California's pick-up tax revenue without requiring any change to California law.

### No State Gift Tax

California imposes no tax on lifetime gifts. There is no California equivalent to the federal gift tax (IRC Sections 2501-2524). Gifts of any amount can be made between California residents without state tax consequence (though federal gift tax rules still apply).

### No State Generation-Skipping Transfer Tax

California does not impose a GST tax. Only the federal GST tax (IRC Sections 2601-2664) applies to transfers that skip generations.

### Inheritance Is Not California Taxable Income

California conforms to IRC Section 102, which excludes gifts and inheritances from the recipient's gross income. An inheritance received by a California resident is not subject to California personal income tax. This applies to:
- Cash bequests
- Property received by inheritance
- Life insurance proceeds received by reason of death

### Estate Income After Death IS Taxable

While the inheritance itself is not taxable, income earned by a decedent's estate after death is subject to California income tax. The estate is a separate taxpayer and files Form 541 (California Fiduciary Income Tax Return).

- If the decedent was a California resident at death, the entire income of the estate must be reported on Form 541.
- If the decedent was a nonresident at death, only income derived from California sources is reported.
- The estate is subject to the same California income tax rates as individuals (1% to 12.3%) plus the 1% Mental Health Services (MHS) tax on income over $1 million.

The estate's income distribution deduction reduces the estate's taxable income and shifts the tax to beneficiaries who receive distributions.

## Federal Estate and Gift Tax -- Applies to California Residents

Since California imposes no transfer taxes, the entire planning focus is on federal obligations.

### Federal Estate Tax (2025-2026)

| Item | 2025 | 2026 |
|---|---|---|
| **Basic Exclusion Amount** | $13,990,000 | $15,000,000 |
| **Married Couple (Portability)** | $27,980,000 | $30,000,000 |
| **Top Estate Tax Rate** | 40% | 40% |
| **Filing Threshold** | $13,990,000 | $15,000,000 |
| **Annual Gift Exclusion** | $19,000 per donee | $19,000 per donee |

The OBBBA, signed July 4, 2025, increased the basic exclusion amount to $15,000,000 for 2026 (IRC Section 2010(c)(3) as amended). This replaces the previously anticipated sunset to approximately $7 million (adjusted for inflation) that was scheduled under the TCJA.

- **Form 706** (United States Estate and Generation-Skipping Transfer Tax Return) is required for decedents whose gross estate plus adjusted taxable gifts exceeds the basic exclusion amount.
- **Form 709** (United States Gift and Generation-Skipping Transfer Tax Return) is required for gifts exceeding the annual exclusion ($19,000 per donee in 2025 and 2026).
- **Portability**: A surviving spouse can elect to use a deceased spouse's unused exclusion amount (DSUE) by timely filing Form 706.

### Community Property Full Step-Up in Basis

California is a community property state. Under IRC Section 1014(b)(6), when one spouse dies, **both halves** of community property receive a stepped-up basis to fair market value at the date of death. This is a significant advantage over separate property states, where only the decedent's share of jointly held property receives a step-up.

**Example:** A married couple holds community property stock with a total basis of $200,000 and a fair market value of $1,000,000 at the first spouse's death. In California (community property), the entire $1,000,000 becomes the new basis. In a separate property state, only the decedent's half ($500,000) would receive a step-up, resulting in a basis of $600,000 ($100,000 original basis for surviving spouse's half + $500,000 stepped-up basis for decedent's half).

This full step-up eliminates embedded capital gains on community property at the first death, which has profound income tax consequences for the surviving spouse.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

The OBBBA impacts California residents primarily through the federal estate and gift tax provisions, not through California-specific taxes (since California has none). Key OBBBA changes:

- The basic exclusion amount is set at $15,000,000 for 2026, with inflation adjustments in subsequent years. This eliminates the anticipated TCJA sunset.
- The increased exclusion provides more room for lifetime gifts without triggering federal gift tax.
- California's lack of a state estate tax means there is no state-level "clawback" risk associated with making large lifetime gifts.

## Agent Rules

1. **Focus planning entirely on federal transfer taxes and CA income tax**: Since California has no estate, gift, or GST taxes, all transfer tax planning should target federal obligations.
2. **Maximize community property step-up**: Ensure that appreciated assets held by married couples are properly characterized as community property to obtain the full double step-up under IRC Section 1014(b)(6).
3. **File Form 706 for portability even if no tax is owed**: If the first spouse's estate is below the exemption, a timely Form 706 should still be filed to elect portability and preserve the DSUE for the surviving spouse.
4. **Track estate income separately on Form 541**: Post-death income of the estate is taxable in California. Ensure timely filing of Form 541 and proper allocation of income between the estate and beneficiaries.
5. **Monitor S.B. 378 and similar legislative proposals**: There have been legislative proposals (e.g., S.B. 378) to reimpose a California estate tax. While these have not passed and would require a voter initiative to overcome Proposition 6, they should be monitored.

## Common Traps

1. **Confusing "no estate tax" with "no income tax on estate income."** California has no estate tax, but estate income after the date of death is fully taxable on Form 541 at individual rates (up to 13.3% including MHS tax). Beneficiaries receiving distributions of estate income must report it on their personal returns.

2. **Failing to characterize community property correctly.** The full step-up in basis under IRC Section 1014(b)(6) only applies to community property. Property held as separate property or in a separate property state receives only a half step-up. Proper titling and documentation of community property is essential.

3. **Missing the portability election deadline.** Form 706 must be filed within 9 months of death (with a 6-month extension available) to elect portability. If the surviving spouse does not file a timely Form 706, the DSUE is lost permanently.

4. **Assuming California will never have an estate tax.** While Proposition 6 requires a voter initiative to reimpose estate taxes, legislative proposals have been introduced. The possibility, however remote, should be acknowledged in long-term planning.

5. **Forgetting federal gift tax reporting.** Because California has no gift tax, taxpayers may forget that federal Form 709 is still required for gifts exceeding $19,000 per donee per year. The form is due April 15 of the year following the gift.
