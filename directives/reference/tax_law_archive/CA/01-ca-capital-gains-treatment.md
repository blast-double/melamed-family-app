## Overview

California taxes all capital gains -- both short-term and long-term -- at ordinary income tax rates ranging from 1% to 13.3%. There is no preferential rate for long-term capital gains as exists under federal law. California conforms to federal rules for computing capital gains and losses (including holding period tracking, wash sale rules, and capital loss limitations) but applies its own rate structure to the resulting income. This creates one of the largest federal-state tax divergences for high-income taxpayers and startup founders, particularly in the context of QSBS under IRC 1202.

## California Rule

1. **No preferential capital gains rates.** California does not provide special tax rates for net capital gain. All capital gains -- short-term and long-term -- are included in taxable income and taxed at the same progressive rates as ordinary income (1% to 12.3%, plus the 1% Mental Health Services Tax surcharge on income exceeding $1,000,000, for a maximum rate of 13.3%).

2. **Holding period still tracked.** The distinction between short-term and long-term holding periods is maintained for purposes of computing gain/loss character on the federal return. However, the holding period has no rate impact on the California return.

3. **Capital loss limitation -- conforms to IRC 1211.** Capital losses in excess of capital gains are deductible against ordinary income up to $3,000 per year ($1,500 for married filing separately). Unused capital losses carry forward indefinitely. This conforms to federal law.

4. **Wash sale rule -- conforms to IRC 1091.** California conforms to the federal wash sale rule. A loss from the sale of stock or securities is disallowed if substantially identical stock or securities are acquired within 30 days before or after the sale. The disallowed loss is added to the basis of the replacement securities.

5. **Capital loss carryforward.** Unused capital losses carry forward to subsequent tax years. There is no carryback for capital losses. This conforms to IRC 1212.

## Conformity Analysis

### General Capital Gains Rate Treatment

**IRC RULE:** Under IRC 1(h), net capital gain from assets held more than one year is taxed at preferential rates of 0%, 15%, or 20%, depending on taxable income. The 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) may also apply, bringing the effective maximum federal rate to 23.8%.

**CALIFORNIA RULE:** R&TC 17024.5 conforms to federal capital gain/loss computation rules but does NOT conform to the preferential rate structure of IRC 1(h). All gains are taxed at ordinary rates up to 13.3%.

**Controlling Authority:** R&TC 17024.5(a)(1)(Q) (conformity date of January 1, 2025 per SB 711); FTB Summary of Federal Income Tax Changes (explicit statement that California does not provide special tax rates for net capital gain).

**Schedule CA Impact:** No adjustment needed for the gain amount itself. The divergence is handled by California's own tax rate schedule applied to total taxable income.

### QSBS Exclusion (IRC 1202)

**IRC RULE:** IRC 1202 provides up to a 100% exclusion of gain from the sale of Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS) held for more than 5 years, up to the greater of $10 million or 10x the taxpayer's adjusted basis. The OBBBA (P.L. 119-21, enacted July 4, 2025) expanded this: for stock acquired after July 4, 2025, the per-issuer cap increased to $15 million (inflation-adjusted after 2026), the aggregate gross assets threshold increased to $75 million, and a tiered holding period was introduced (50% exclusion at 3 years, 75% at 4 years, 100% at 5 years).

**CALIFORNIA RULE:** California does NOT conform to IRC 1202. R&TC 18152.5 previously provided a partial (50%) QSBS exclusion but was repealed effective January 1, 2013. No QSBS exclusion of any kind exists in California. The full gain from QSBS sales is taxable at ordinary CA rates (up to 13.3%). California also does not conform to any OBBBA expansion of Section 1202.

**Controlling Authority:** R&TC 18152.5 (repealed 2013); SB 209 (2013 repeal legislation); FTB Summary of Federal Income Tax Changes confirming non-conformity to OBBBA Section 70431.

**Schedule CA Impact:** Column B addition -- if the taxpayer excluded gain on the federal return under IRC 1202, the full excluded amount must be added back on Schedule CA, Column B.

### Like-Kind Exchanges (IRC 1031)

**IRC RULE:** IRC 1031 allows deferral of gain on exchange of real property held for productive use or investment for like-kind real property. The TCJA (2017) limited this to real property only (eliminating personal property exchanges).

**CALIFORNIA RULE:** California conforms to IRC 1031, including the real property limitation, effective for exchanges initiated after January 10, 2019 (per SB 711 conformity update). However, California imposes an additional filing requirement: Form FTB 3840 must be filed when California real property is exchanged for out-of-state replacement property. This form must be filed annually until the deferred California-source gain is recognized.

**Controlling Authority:** R&TC 18032; R&TC 24953; SB 711 (Conformity Act of 2025).

**Schedule CA Impact:** No adjustment needed for valid like-kind exchanges. However, California preserves the source of the gain at the time of the original exchange, regardless of when recognition occurs.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (P.L. 119-21), enacted July 4, 2025, made significant changes to IRC 1202 (QSBS):

1. **Per-issuer gain cap increased from $10M to $15M** (inflation-adjusted after 2026).
2. **Aggregate gross assets threshold increased from $50M to $75M** (inflation-adjusted after 2026).
3. **Tiered holding period introduced:** 50% exclusion at 3 years, 75% at 4 years, 100% at 5+ years (for stock acquired after July 4, 2025).

California's conformity date of January 1, 2025 (per SB 711, signed October 1, 2025) does not reach the OBBBA, which was enacted on July 4, 2025. Therefore, California does not conform to ANY of these OBBBA changes. Since California already did not conform to IRC 1202 in any version, the practical effect is the same: no QSBS exclusion exists in California for any tax year.

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE:** When processing a capital gain or loss for a California return, NEVER apply a preferential rate. All gains are taxed at ordinary income rates. The holding period is irrelevant for rate purposes on the CA return.

> **AGENT RULE:** When a federal return shows a QSBS exclusion under IRC 1202, ALWAYS add back the full excluded amount on Schedule CA, Column B. The entire gain is taxable in California.

> **AGENT RULE:** For like-kind exchanges involving California real property exchanged for out-of-state property, flag the requirement to file Form FTB 3840 in the year of exchange AND every subsequent year until the deferred CA-source gain is recognized.

> **AGENT RULE:** When calculating combined federal + California tax on long-term capital gains, use the applicable federal LTCG rate PLUS the California ordinary rate. For a high-income taxpayer, this can reach 23.8% federal + 13.3% CA = 37.1% combined.

## Common Traps

> **TRAP:** QSBS is the single largest federal-California divergence for startup founders. A founder who excludes $10M of gain federally under IRC 1202 (paying $0 federal tax) still owes approximately $1,330,000 in California tax (13.3% x $10M). Under the new OBBBA rules with a $15M cap, the California tax exposure on federally excluded gain rises to approximately $1,995,000.

> **TRAP:** Do NOT assume California's lack of a LTCG rate means capital gains are taxed at the top 13.3% rate for all taxpayers. California uses progressive brackets. A taxpayer with $100,000 of capital gain and no other income pays substantially less than 13.3% effective rate. Apply the full bracket schedule.

> **TRAP:** Like-kind exchange compliance -- the FTB 3840 annual filing requirement for out-of-state replacement property is easily overlooked. Failure to file triggers FTB inquiry. The filing obligation continues until the California-source deferred gain is fully recognized, even if the replacement property is subsequently exchanged again.

> **TRAP:** Wash sale losses disallowed under IRC 1091 are disallowed for both federal AND California purposes. Do not attempt to claim a wash sale loss on the California return that was disallowed federally.
