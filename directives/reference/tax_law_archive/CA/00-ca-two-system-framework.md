## Overview

California imposes its own personal income tax (PIT) and corporate franchise/income tax independently of and in addition to the federal income tax. A California taxpayer computes two entirely separate tax liabilities — federal and state — each governed by its own code, rates, forms, and administering agency. The California system starts with federal adjusted gross income (AGI) and then bridges to California AGI using Schedule CA, which adds back items California does not allow and subtracts items California excludes from income. Understanding this two-system architecture is the foundational prerequisite for every other California tax topic.

## The Two Systems

### Federal System

1. **Governing law:** Internal Revenue Code (IRC), Title 26 USC.
2. **Administering agency:** Internal Revenue Service (IRS).
3. **Individual return:** Form 1040 / 1040-SR.
4. **Corporate return:** Form 1120 / 1120-S.
5. **Tax rates (individual):** 10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, 37% (2025 brackets per OBBBA).
6. **Tax rates (corporate):** 21% flat rate (IRC Section 11(b)).

### California System

1. **Governing law:** California Revenue and Taxation Code (R&TC), Part 10 (Personal Income Tax Law) and Part 11 (Corporation Tax Law).
2. **Administering agency:** Franchise Tax Board (FTB).
3. **Individual return:** Form 540 (residents), Form 540NR (nonresidents/part-year).
4. **Corporate return:** Form 100 (corporations), Form 100S (S corporations).
5. **Tax rates (individual):** Nine brackets from 1% to 12.3%, plus 1% Behavioral Health Services Tax (formerly Mental Health Services Tax) on taxable income exceeding $1,000,000, for an effective top rate of 13.3%.
6. **Tax rates (corporate):** 8.84% (C corporations), 1.5% minimum franchise tax for S corporations.

### Independent Liabilities

- The two liabilities are computed independently. Paying federal tax does not reduce California tax.
- California does **not** allow a deduction for federal income taxes paid. This is a common misconception. R&TC Section 17220 expressly disallows it.
- Conversely, state income taxes paid are deductible on the federal return (as an itemized deduction on Schedule A), subject to the SALT cap under current federal law.

## California Rule: The Conformity Mechanism

California uses a "static conformity" approach. R&TC Section 17024.5 defines the term "Internal Revenue Code" for PIT purposes by reference to the federal IRC as of a legislatively specified date. R&TC Section 23051.5 does the same for Corporation Tax Law.

### How Conformity Works

1. The California R&TC incorporates many IRC definitions, rules, and computational methods by reference (e.g., R&TC Section 17071 incorporates IRC Section 61 — gross income defined).
2. These incorporations are frozen as of the "specified date." Any federal changes enacted after that date do not apply in California unless the legislature acts.
3. California selectively decouples from specific IRC provisions even within the conformity window by enacting express exceptions in the R&TC.

### SB 711: The Conformity Act of 2025

- **Signed:** October 1, 2025, by Governor Newsom.
- **Prior specified date:** January 1, 2015 (set by AB 154, Chapter 359, Statutes of 2015).
- **New specified date:** January 1, 2025.
- **Effective for:** Taxable years beginning on or after January 1, 2025.
- **Effect:** California now conforms to IRC changes made from January 1, 2015 through December 31, 2024, to the extent those IRC sections were already incorporated by reference, except as otherwise provided in SB 711.

### OBBBA: Non-Conformity

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (P.L. 119-21, OBBBA) was enacted on July 4, 2025. Because SB 711 sets the conformity date at January 1, 2025, all OBBBA provisions fall outside the conformity window. California does **not** conform to any OBBBA provision unless future legislation specifically adopts it.

## Schedule CA as a Bridge

Form 540 begins with federal AGI (Form 1040, line 11b) and uses Schedule CA (540) to convert it to California AGI. Schedule CA has three columns:

| Column | Purpose | Direction |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| **A — Federal Amounts** | Taxable amounts from federal return | Starting point |
| **B — Subtractions** | Income included federally but excluded by California | Reduces CA income |
| **C — Additions** | Income excluded federally but included by California, or deductions taken federally but not allowed by California | Increases CA income |

**Formula:**
```
Federal AGI
- Schedule CA Column B subtractions
+ Schedule CA Column C additions
= California AGI (Form 540, line 17)
```

California AGI then flows to taxable income after the California standard deduction or itemized deductions and is taxed at California rates.

## Conformity Analysis

**IRC RULE:** Federal tax law is codified in the Internal Revenue Code (Title 26 USC), amended by Congress.

**CALIFORNIA RULE:** R&TC Sections 17024.5 and 23051.5 incorporate the IRC by reference as of a specified date (now January 1, 2025 per SB 711). California selectively decouples from provisions it does not wish to adopt.

**Controlling Authority:** R&TC Section 17024.5(a)(1)(Q) (as amended by SB 711); R&TC Section 23051.5

**Schedule CA Impact:** Schedule CA (540) is the bridge mechanism. Column B subtractions and Column C additions reconcile every difference between federal and California treatment.

## Major Areas of Non-Conformity

The following table summarizes the most significant areas where California and federal law diverge for 2025-2026:

| Topic | Federal Rule | California Rule | Schedule CA Impact |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Bonus Depreciation (Section 168(k))** | 100% first-year deduction (OBBBA restored) | Not allowed; use CA depreciation schedules | Column C addition for excess depreciation |
| **Section 179 Expensing** | $2,500,000 limit / $4,000,000 phaseout (OBBBA) | $25,000 limit / $200,000 phaseout | Column C addition for excess over CA limit |
| **QBI Deduction (Section 199A)** | 20% deduction for qualified business income (permanent per OBBBA) | Not allowed | Column C addition |
| **SALT Deduction Cap** | $40,000 cap (OBBBA) | No cap; full CA itemized SALT deduction allowed (but no deduction for CA state taxes on CA return) | Column B subtraction for SALT above federal cap |
| **Capital Gains Rates** | Preferential 0%/15%/20% rates | Taxed as ordinary income at up to 13.3% | No adjustment needed (same income, different rates) |
| **QSBS Exclusion (Section 1202)** | Up to 100% exclusion ($75M cap per OBBBA) | Partial exclusion only (50% under CA rules) | Column C addition for excess exclusion |
| **Net Operating Loss** | Federal NOL rules (80% limit, indefinite carryforward) | CA own NOL rules with different carryback/carryforward | Column B/C adjustments as applicable |
| **Social Security Benefits** | Up to 85% taxable | 100% excluded from CA income | Column B subtraction |
| **Misc. Itemized Deductions** | Suspended (TCJA/OBBBA) | Retained with 2% AGI floor | Column B subtraction |
| **Employee Home Office** | Not deductible (W-2 employees, TCJA/OBBBA) | Deductible for CA if unreimbursed | Column B subtraction |
| **Standard Deduction** | $16,100 single / $32,200 MFJ (OBBBA) | $5,706 single / $11,412 MFJ | No Schedule CA adjustment (computed independently) |
| **Personal Exemption** | Suspended (TCJA/OBBBA) | CA uses exemption credits ($153/$475) instead | No Schedule CA adjustment (computed independently) |

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

**OBBBA PROVISION:** The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (P.L. 119-21) enacted permanent TCJA brackets, increased standard deduction, $2,200 CTC, 100% bonus depreciation, $2.5M Section 179, permanent QBI deduction, $40K SALT cap, tip/overtime/auto/senior deductions, $15M estate exemption, and numerous other provisions.

**CALIFORNIA STATUS:** Does not conform. SB 711 set California's conformity date at January 1, 2025. OBBBA was enacted July 4, 2025 — after the conformity date. California decouples from all OBBBA provisions until the legislature passes future conformity legislation.

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE:** When computing California tax liability, NEVER assume that a federal deduction or exclusion automatically applies for California purposes. Always check R&TC conformity status. Start from federal AGI and apply Schedule CA adjustments for every area of non-conformity before computing California tax.

> **AGENT RULE:** When a taxpayer reports bonus depreciation, QBI deduction, OBBBA-specific deductions (tip, overtime, auto loan interest, senior deduction), or any post-January-1-2025 federal provision on their federal return, add the full amount back as a Schedule CA Column C addition for California purposes.

> **AGENT RULE:** When a taxpayer receives Social Security benefits, subtract 100% of the federally taxable amount on Schedule CA Column B. California excludes all Social Security from income.

> **AGENT RULE:** When evaluating any federal tax change, first determine: (1) Was it enacted before January 1, 2025? If yes, check if SB 711 specifically decoupled from it. (2) Was it enacted on or after January 1, 2025 (e.g., OBBBA)? If yes, California does not conform unless future legislation says otherwise.

## Common Traps

> **TRAP:** Assuming federal tax paid reduces California tax. It does not. R&TC Section 17220 disallows any deduction for federal income taxes on the California return.

> **TRAP:** Using the federal standard deduction amount on the California return. California's standard deduction ($5,706 single / $11,412 MFJ for 2025) is dramatically lower than the federal amount. Always use California-specific figures.

> **TRAP:** Assuming OBBBA provisions apply in California because they are "current law." OBBBA was enacted July 4, 2025 — after California's January 1, 2025 conformity date. No OBBBA provision applies for California purposes.

> **TRAP:** Forgetting that California taxes capital gains as ordinary income. The federal preferential rates of 0%/15%/20% do not exist for California. A taxpayer in the 12.3% bracket with $1M+ income effectively pays 13.3% on long-term capital gains.
