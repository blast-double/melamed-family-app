## Overview

California has never conformed to federal bonus depreciation under IRC Section 168(k) and maintains its own, significantly lower, Section 179 expensing limits. This creates one of the most significant and persistent California/federal differences for business taxpayers. Every asset placed in service that claims bonus depreciation or enhanced Section 179 federally requires an adjustment on Schedule CA and separate depreciation tracking on Form 3885A (individuals) or Form 3885 (corporations). SB 711 (signed October 1, 2025) updated California's conformity date to January 1, 2025, but explicitly maintained the decoupling from Sections 168(k) and 179 enhancements.

## California Rule

### Bonus Depreciation -- IRC Section 168(k)

1. California has **never** allowed the additional first-year depreciation deduction under IRC Section 168(k) -- for any property, in any year, at any percentage.
2. This predates the TCJA. California did not conform to the 30% bonus (2001), the 50% bonus (2003), the 100% bonus (TCJA 2017), or the OBBBA permanent 100% bonus (2025).
3. The OBBBA (P.L. 119-21, Section 70301) made 100% bonus depreciation permanent for property acquired after January 19, 2025. California does **not** conform. FTB's Summary of Federal Income Tax Changes confirms: Section 70301 -- "Full expensing for certain business property" -- **No** conformity. Relevant R&TC: 17201, 17250, 24349.
4. Any bonus depreciation claimed federally must be **added back in full** on Schedule CA, Column B (addition), and California depreciation must be computed separately using MACRS without the 168(k) allowance.

### Section 179 Expensing

1. California's Section 179 limit is **$25,000** (R&TC Section 17255 for PITL; R&TC Section 24356 for CTL).
2. The phase-out threshold begins at **$200,000** of total Section 179 property placed in service during the tax year.
3. The $25,000 limit is reduced dollar-for-dollar once total Section 179 property exceeds $200,000, reaching zero at $225,000.
4. These amounts are **not indexed for inflation** and have remained static for years.
5. California does **not** allow the Section 179 election for off-the-shelf computer software.
6. California does **not** conform to the expanded definition of Section 179 property for certain depreciable tangible personal property related to furnishing lodging or qualified real property improvements.

### MACRS Conformity

1. California **does conform** to MACRS recovery periods for property placed in service on or after January 1, 1987.
2. The difference is solely in first-year treatment: no bonus depreciation and the lower Section 179 cap.
3. California uses the same depreciation methods (200% DB, 150% DB, straight-line) and conventions (half-year, mid-quarter) as federal.

### Qualified Improvement Property (QIP)

1. QIP has a 15-year MACRS recovery period under both federal and California law.
2. However, federal bonus depreciation on QIP is **not** allowed for California purposes.
3. QIP must be depreciated over 15 years using straight-line or accelerated methods, without the additional first-year deduction.

## Conformity Analysis

**IRC RULE:** Under the OBBBA, 100% bonus depreciation under IRC Section 168(k) is permanent for qualified property acquired after January 19, 2025. Section 179 federal limit is $2,500,000 with a $4,000,000 phase-out threshold (OBBBA Section 70306).

**CALIFORNIA RULE:** No bonus depreciation. Section 179 capped at $25,000/$200,000 (R&TC Sections 17255, 24356). MACRS recovery periods conform.

**Controlling Authority:** R&TC Sections 17250 (decouples from 168(k) for PITL), 24349 (decouples for CTL), 17255 (PITL 179 limit), 24356 (CTL 179 limit). SB 711 Section 120 (2025 conformity update -- maintains decoupling).

**Schedule CA Impact:** Add back 100% of any federal bonus depreciation in Column B (addition). Add back the difference between federal Section 179 ($2,500,000 max) and California Section 179 ($25,000 max) in Column B. Subtract California-only MACRS depreciation in Column C (subtraction) for years where California depreciation exceeds the remaining federal depreciation (typically years 2+ of an asset's life, because the federal asset is already fully expensed).

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

The OBBBA (P.L. 119-21) made three significant changes to depreciation that California does **not** conform to:

1. **Section 70301 -- Full Expensing (Permanent 100% Bonus):** Makes IRC Section 168(k) 100% bonus permanent for property acquired after January 19, 2025. California: No conformity. R&TC 17201, 17250, 24349.
2. **Section 70306 -- Enhanced Section 179:** Increases the Section 179 limit to $2,500,000 with a $4,000,000 phase-out for tax years beginning after December 31, 2024. California: No conformity. R&TC 17201, 17255, 24356.
3. **Section 70307 -- Special Depreciation for Qualified Production Property:** IRC Section 168(n) -- 100% depreciation election. California: No conformity. R&TC 17201, 17250, 24349.

## Worked Example

**Scenario:** A sole proprietor places a $100,000 machine in service in 2025. >50% business use. Not a luxury auto.

| Item | Federal | California | Schedule CA Adj. |
|------|---------|------------|-----------------|
| Bonus Depreciation (100%) | $100,000 | $0 | -- |
| Section 179 (if elected instead) | $100,000 | $25,000 | -- |
| MACRS Year 1 (7-yr, 200% DB, HY) | $0 (fully expensed) | $14,290 | -- |
| **Total Year 1 Deduction** | **$100,000** | **$14,290** (179) or **$14,290** (MACRS only) | -- |
| Column B Addition | -- | -- | **$85,710** (add back) |

If Section 179 elected on both returns: Federal deducts $100,000; California deducts $25,000 plus $10,715 MACRS on remaining $75,000 = $35,715 total. Column B add-back = $64,285.

**Net effect:** California taxable income is $64,285 to $85,710 **higher** than federal in Year 1. This difference reverses over the asset's remaining MACRS life through Column C subtractions.

The total deductions are identical over the asset's life. The California adjustments net to zero across all years. The timing difference creates a cash flow disadvantage for California taxpayers in Year 1 and a gradual reversal in Years 2--8.

**Scenario:** A business purchases a passenger automobile (not a truck/van) for $60,000 in 2025 with 100% business use.

The luxury auto depreciation difference persists for many more years in California because the lower annual limits spread the recovery over a much longer period. A $60,000 vehicle fully depreciated by Year 5 federally (with bonus) would take approximately 22+ years to fully depreciate under California limits.

## Listed Property and Luxury Automobiles -- IRC Section 280F

1. Listed property must meet the **>50% business use** requirement in the year placed in service to qualify for MACRS accelerated depreciation and Section 179.
2. California does **not** conform to federal modifications to the Section 280F luxury auto depreciation limits.
3. For passenger automobiles placed in service in 2025:
   - Federal Year 1 with bonus: **$20,200**
   - Federal Year 1 without bonus: **$12,200**
   - California Year 1: **$3,860** (no bonus add-on, no enhanced 280F)
4. The California limits are published annually in the Form 3885 instructions and are significantly lower than federal limits because California applies the pre-TCJA Section 280F framework without any bonus depreciation increment.

### Form 3885A/3885 Tracking Requirement

1. Taxpayers **must** file Form 3885A (individuals) or Form 3885 (corporations) whenever California depreciation differs from federal.
2. The form tracks California cost basis, recovery period, method, and annual depreciation for each asset.
3. Assets must be tracked for their **entire recovery period** -- differences created in Year 1 do not resolve until the asset is fully depreciated or disposed of.

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE:** When computing California depreciation for any asset, **always** strip out IRC Section 168(k) bonus depreciation and recompute using MACRS only. Apply the $25,000/$200,000 Section 179 limit. For luxury autos, use the California-specific 280F limits published in the Form 3885 instructions for the year placed in service. Track all differences on Form 3885A/3885.

> **AGENT RULE:** Any federal Section 179 deduction exceeding $25,000 must be added back on Schedule CA Column B. The excess is recovered through California MACRS depreciation over the asset's remaining life.

> **AGENT RULE:** When a taxpayer shows a federal loss driven by large Section 179 or bonus depreciation, recompute California income using the $25,000 cap and no bonus. The taxpayer may have **California taxable income** even when reporting a federal loss.

## Common Traps

> **TRAP:** A taxpayer claims $500,000 Section 179 federally on equipment purchases. California allows only $25,000. The $475,000 add-back creates California taxable income even though the federal return shows a loss. This is the most common California depreciation surprise.

> **TRAP:** Failing to track California basis separately. Once bonus depreciation is added back, the California cost basis diverges from federal. The asset must be depreciated on two separate schedules for its entire life. Disposing of the asset without reconciling basis creates incorrect gain/loss.

> **TRAP:** Luxury auto limits -- applying the federal $20,200 Year 1 limit (with bonus) to a California return. California Year 1 is $3,860 for passenger autos. The difference is $16,340 per vehicle in Year 1 alone.

> **TRAP:** Assuming QIP qualifies for accelerated treatment in California. While the 15-year recovery period conforms, federal bonus depreciation on QIP does not carry over. QIP must be depreciated over 15 years without any additional first-year deduction.

> **TRAP:** Off-the-shelf software expensed under Section 179 federally. California does not allow Section 179 for off-the-shelf computer software. The entire deduction must be added back and amortized separately for California.
