# Cryptocurrency Taxation -- California
*Last Edited: 2026-03-26*

**R&TC Section(s):** Section 17024.5 (general IRC conformity)
**Parallel IRC Section:** IRC Sections 61, 1001, 1012, 1014, 1015, 1091; IRS Notice 2014-21; Rev. Rul. 2019-24
**California Conformity Status:** Conforms -- California conforms to the federal treatment of cryptocurrency as property per R&TC 17024.5 and the IRC as of January 1, 2025. All federal taxable events (sales, exchanges, mining, staking, airdrops, DeFi yields) are taxable in California. However, because California does not provide preferential capital gains rates, all crypto gains -- including those held long-term -- are taxed at ordinary income rates up to 13.3%.

## Overview

California conforms to the federal treatment of cryptocurrency as property under IRS Notice 2014-21 and subsequent guidance. All transactions involving cryptocurrency -- including sales, exchanges, payments for goods/services, mining, staking, airdrops, and DeFi activities -- are taxable events that follow the same rules as other property transactions. The critical California distinction is that all cryptocurrency gains, including long-term gains, are taxed at ordinary income rates (up to 13.3%) rather than the preferential federal long-term capital gains rates (0%/15%/20%). This results in a significantly higher combined tax burden for California residents who hold crypto as a long-term investment.

## California Rule

### Classification as Property

1. **Cryptocurrency is property, not currency.** Following IRS Notice 2014-21, California treats cryptocurrency (Bitcoin, Ethereum, and all other virtual currencies) as intangible personal property. It is not treated as foreign currency for tax purposes.

2. **General tax principles for property apply.** All rules applicable to property transactions under the IRC apply to cryptocurrency: basis determination, holding period, gain/loss computation, wash sale rules, and gifting/inheritance rules.

### Taxable Events

3. **All federal taxable events are California taxable events.** These include:
   - **Selling crypto for fiat currency** (USD, MXN, etc.) -- gain/loss recognized
   - **Exchanging one crypto for another** (e.g., BTC for ETH) -- gain/loss recognized
   - **Using crypto to pay for goods/services** -- gain/loss recognized (measured against basis)
   - **Mining cryptocurrency** -- ordinary income at FMV when received
   - **Staking rewards** -- ordinary income at FMV when received (subject to evolving IRS guidance)
   - **Airdrops** -- ordinary income at FMV when received (per Rev. Rul. 2019-24)
   - **DeFi lending/liquidity provision yields** -- ordinary income at FMV when received
   - **Hard forks resulting in new tokens** -- ordinary income at FMV when dominion and control established (per Rev. Rul. 2019-24)

4. **Non-taxable events.** The following are NOT taxable:
   - Buying crypto with fiat currency (no gain/loss until disposition)
   - Transferring crypto between wallets you own (no change in ownership)
   - Gifting crypto (gift basis rules apply under IRC 1015; no gain to donor unless selling to fund a gift)

### Tax Rates -- The Critical California Difference

5. **No long-term capital gains preference in California.** Federally, crypto held for more than one year qualifies for LTCG rates of 0%, 15%, or 20% (plus potential 3.8% NIIT). In California, there is no preferential rate. All crypto gains -- short-term and long-term -- are taxed at ordinary income rates from 1% to 13.3%.

6. **Combined rate impact.** For a high-income California resident:
   - **Short-term crypto gain:** Up to 37% federal + 13.3% CA = 50.3% combined (before NIIT)
   - **Long-term crypto gain:** Up to 20% federal + 3.8% NIIT + 13.3% CA = 37.1% combined
   - **Mining/staking income (ordinary):** Up to 37% federal + 13.3% CA = 50.3% combined

### Basis and Cost Method

7. **Basis determination -- conforms.** The basis of cryptocurrency is the cost (amount paid in USD at the time of acquisition), including any fees or commissions paid to acquire the crypto. California conforms to IRC 1012.

8. **Cost basis method -- FIFO default, specific identification permitted.** The IRS default method is FIFO (First In, First Out). Taxpayers may use specific identification if they can adequately identify the specific units sold or exchanged. California conforms to these methods.

9. **Adequate identification.** For specific identification, the taxpayer must identify the specific lot sold at the time of the transaction and maintain records documenting the identification. If identification is not made, FIFO applies by default.

### Sourcing Rules

10. **Cryptocurrency is intangible personal property.** For sourcing purposes, gains from the sale of intangible personal property are sourced to the taxpayer's state of residence (domicile) at the time of the sale.

11. **California residents.** A California resident is taxed on all income from all sources, including cryptocurrency gains from transactions on any exchange worldwide. Source is irrelevant for residents.

12. **Nonresidents.** A nonresident of California generally is NOT taxed by California on cryptocurrency gains because crypto is intangible personal property sourced to the state of residence. Exception: If the cryptocurrency is used in a trade or business carried on in California (e.g., a crypto mining operation physically located in California), the income may be California-source.

13. **Part-year residents.** Crypto gains realized during the period of California residency are taxable by California. Gains realized during the nonresident period are not California-source (unless from a CA trade or business).

### Wash Sale Rules

14. **Cryptocurrency and wash sales.** The IRS has historically treated the IRC 1091 wash sale rule as applying only to "stock or securities." There has been debate about whether cryptocurrency qualifies as a "security" for wash sale purposes. As of 2025, the IRS expanded broker reporting requirements for digital assets under IRC 6045 (effective for 2025 transactions). California conforms to IRC 1091 as of the January 1, 2025 conformity date.

15. **Practical guidance.** Whether or not the wash sale rule technically applies to a specific crypto transaction, California will follow the federal treatment. If the IRS disallows a loss under the wash sale rule, California will likewise disallow it. Taxpayers should not rely on a perceived wash sale loophole for crypto.

### Reporting

16. **Form 8949 and Schedule D.** Crypto sales and exchanges are reported on Form 8949 (detail of each transaction) and summarized on Schedule D. California uses the same form structure.

17. **1099-DA / 1099-B reporting.** Beginning with 2025 transactions, digital asset brokers (centralized exchanges) must report transactions to the IRS on Form 1099-DA (or 1099-B). California conforms to these reporting requirements.

## Conformity Analysis

**IRC RULE:** IRS Notice 2014-21 and Rev. Rul. 2019-24 establish that cryptocurrency is property. All property transaction rules (basis, holding period, gain/loss, wash sales) apply. LTCG rates (0%/15%/20%) apply to crypto held more than one year.

**CALIFORNIA RULE:** R&TC 17024.5 conforms to the IRC as of January 1, 2025. Cryptocurrency is property. All federal taxable events are California taxable events. However, California does not apply preferential LTCG rates -- all crypto gains are taxed at ordinary rates (1% to 13.3%).

**Controlling Authority:** R&TC 17024.5(a)(1)(Q) (conformity date of January 1, 2025 per SB 711); IRS Notice 2014-21; Rev. Rul. 2019-24.

**Schedule CA Impact:** Generally, no adjustment is needed for the amount of crypto gain or loss. The difference is in the rate applied, which is handled by California's own tax rate schedule. If any federal crypto-related provision was enacted after January 1, 2025 (in the OBBBA), California does not conform -- but the OBBBA did not contain material crypto-specific provisions.

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE:** Treat ALL cryptocurrency transactions as property transactions. Apply the same basis, holding period, and gain/loss rules as for stocks or other capital assets. Never treat crypto as currency for California tax purposes.

> **AGENT RULE:** When calculating California tax on crypto gains, apply ordinary income rates regardless of holding period. Do NOT apply any LTCG rate for California. The holding period distinction matters only for the federal return.

> **AGENT RULE:** Source crypto gains to the taxpayer's state of residence at the time of the transaction. For California residents, all crypto gains are CA taxable regardless of where the exchange is located. For nonresidents, crypto gains are generally NOT California-source.

> **AGENT RULE:** Maintain consistent cost basis methodology (FIFO or specific identification) for both federal and California returns. Do not use different methods for the two returns.

> **AGENT RULE:** For mining and staking income, report as ordinary income at FMV on the date of receipt. This is NOT capital gain -- it is ordinary income for both federal and California purposes.

## Common Traps

> **TRAP:** Assuming long-term crypto holdings get favorable California treatment. A taxpayer who held Bitcoin for 3 years and sold at a $500,000 gain pays only 20% federal LTCG rate ($100,000 federal, ignoring NIIT) but up to 13.3% California ordinary rate ($66,500 California). The California tax is significant and often overlooked in planning.

> **TRAP:** Crypto-to-crypto exchanges are taxable. Exchanging BTC for ETH is a realization event. The taxpayer must compute gain/loss based on the FMV of the received crypto minus the basis of the surrendered crypto. Many taxpayers mistakenly believe only cash-out events are taxable.

> **TRAP:** DeFi income characterization. Yield from DeFi lending, liquidity pools, and staking is ordinary income, not capital gain. It is taxed at the highest rates for both federal (37%) and California (13.3%) purposes. Do not mischaracterize as capital gain.

> **TRAP:** Nonresident crypto gains. A former California resident who moved to a no-income-tax state (Texas, Nevada, Florida) and then sells crypto is NOT subject to California tax on the gain -- the gain is sourced to the state of residence at the time of the sale. However, the FTB actively audits residency changes. If the move is not genuine, the FTB will assert California residency and tax the gain.

> **TRAP:** Lost or stolen crypto. There is currently no casualty or theft loss deduction available for personal-use crypto losses for federal purposes (personal casualty losses limited to federally declared disasters). California conforms to this limitation.
