## Overview

California imposes nine progressive income tax brackets ranging from 1% to 12.3%, plus a 1% Behavioral Health Services Tax (BHST, formerly Mental Health Services Tax) on taxable income exceeding $1,000,000. The effective top marginal rate is 13.3% — the highest state income tax rate in the United States. California taxes all income types (including long-term capital gains and qualified dividends) at ordinary rates with no preferential treatment. Brackets are indexed annually using the California Consumer Price Index (CCPI).

## Behavioral Health Services Tax (1% Surcharge)

### History and Legal Basis

- **Enacted:** Proposition 63 (Mental Health Services Act), approved by voters November 2004.
- **Renamed:** For taxable years beginning on or after January 1, 2025, renamed to Behavioral Health Services Tax per the Behavioral Health Services Act.
- **R&TC authority:** R&TC Section 17043.5.

### Rules

1. Applies to taxable income exceeding **$1,000,000** ($500,000 if Married/RDP Filing Separately).
2. Rate: **1%** of the excess over $1,000,000.
3. The $1,000,000 threshold is **NOT indexed for inflation**. It has remained static since 2004. As nominal incomes rise, an increasing number of taxpayers are subject to this surcharge.
4. Computed on **Form 540, line 62** (or Form 540NR, line 72).
5. Cannot be reduced by credits.

### Computation

```
Taxable income (Form 540, line 19)
Less: $1,000,000 ($500,000 if MFS)
Equals: Excess amount
Multiply by: 1% (0.01)
Equals: Behavioral Health Services Tax
```

### Effective Top Rate

The combination of the 12.3% top bracket rate plus the 1% BHST surcharge produces an effective top marginal rate of **13.3%** on taxable income above $1,000,000. This is the highest state income tax rate in the United States.

## Capital Gains: Taxed at Ordinary Rates

This is one of the most consequential differences between California and federal tax law.

### Federal Rule

Long-term capital gains (assets held more than one year) and qualified dividends are taxed at preferential rates of 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income. Additionally, the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) applies to taxpayers with MAGI exceeding $250,000 (MFJ).

### California Rule

- **NO preferential rate** for long-term capital gains.
- **NO preferential rate** for qualified dividends.
- All capital gains and dividends are taxed at the same ordinary rates as wages, business income, and other income.
- A taxpayer with $2,000,000 in long-term capital gains pays the 12.3% bracket rate + 1% BHST = **13.3%** on the portion above $1M, compared to the federal 20% + 3.8% NIIT = 23.8%.
- California does NOT impose the 3.8% Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT).
- California does NOT impose the 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax on earned income.

### Combined Federal + California Impact

For a high-income taxpayer (MFJ) with long-term capital gains:

| Layer | Rate |
|-------|------|
| Federal capital gains rate | 20.0% |
| Federal NIIT | 3.8% |
| California rate (above $1M) | 13.3% |
| **Combined marginal rate** | **37.1%** |

Note: California taxes are deductible for federal purposes (subject to SALT cap), which provides some offset. But the combined rate for California residents on investment income is among the highest in the nation.

## Standard Deduction

California's standard deduction is set independently and is dramatically lower than the federal amount:

| Filing Status | 2025 CA | 2025 Federal (OBBBA) | Difference |
|--------------|---------|----------------------|------------|
| Single | $5,706 | $16,100 | -$10,394 |
| Married/RDP Filing Jointly | $11,412 | $32,200 | -$20,788 |
| Married/RDP Filing Separately | $5,706 | $16,100 | -$10,394 |
| Head of Household | $11,412 | $32,200 | -$20,788 |
| Qualifying Surviving Spouse/RDP | $11,412 | $32,200 | -$20,788 |

Because California's standard deduction is so much lower, many taxpayers who use the federal standard deduction will benefit from itemizing on their California return.

### Dependent Standard Deduction

If someone else can claim the taxpayer as a dependent, the California standard deduction is the greater of:
- $1,350 (minimum), OR
- The taxpayer's earned income (up to the applicable standard deduction amount)

## Personal Exemption Credits

California uses exemption **credits** (direct reduction of tax), not exemption **deductions**:

| Type | 2025 Amount |
|------|-------------|
| Personal exemption credit (Single, MFS, HoH) | $153 per filer |
| Personal exemption credit (MFJ, QSS) | $306 total ($153 each) |
| Senior exemption credit (65+) | Additional $153 per qualifying person |
| Blind exemption credit | Additional $153 per qualifying person |
| Dependent exemption credit | $475 per dependent |

### Phaseout

Exemption credits are reduced for high-income taxpayers. The phaseout begins when federal AGI exceeds:

| Filing Status | Phaseout Begins At |
|--------------|-------------------|
| Single | $252,203 |
| MFJ / QSS | $504,411 |
| HoH | $378,310 |
| MFS | $252,203 |

Each exemption credit is reduced by $6 for every $2,500 ($1,250 if MFS) of AGI above the threshold.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

**OBBBA PROVISION:** OBBBA made permanent the TCJA individual tax brackets (10%-37%), increased the standard deduction, and created new deductions (tips, overtime, auto loan interest, senior bonus). It also maintained the suspension of personal exemptions.

**CALIFORNIA STATUS:** Does not conform. California maintains its own bracket structure (1%-12.3%), its own standard deduction ($5,706/$11,412), and its own personal exemption credit system. OBBBA provisions have no effect on California rate calculations.

## Conformity Analysis

**IRC RULE:** Seven brackets (10%-37% under OBBBA); preferential capital gains rates (0%/15%/20%); NIIT (3.8%); Additional Medicare Tax (0.9%).

**CALIFORNIA RULE:** Nine brackets (1%-12.3%) plus 1% BHST; all income taxed at ordinary rates; no NIIT; no Additional Medicare Tax.

**Controlling Authority:** R&TC Sections 17041, 17043, 17043.5

**Schedule CA Impact:** No adjustment needed for the rate difference itself. The rate difference is applied at the computation stage, not through Schedule CA adjustments.

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE:** When computing California tax, use the correct Tax Rate Schedule: Schedule X for Single or MFS, Schedule Y for MFJ or QSS, Schedule Z for HoH. Never use federal brackets.

> **AGENT RULE:** For any taxpayer with California taxable income exceeding $1,000,000 ($500,000 MFS), add the 1% Behavioral Health Services Tax on the excess. This is computed separately on Form 540, line 62 and is NOT reducible by credits.

> **AGENT RULE:** When a taxpayer has long-term capital gains or qualified dividends, apply the ordinary California rate schedule. Do NOT apply the federal 0%/15%/20% preferential rates. The California tax on $500,000 of long-term capital gains for a single filer with no other income is approximately $38,500 (using Schedule X), not $75,000 (15% federal rate).

> **AGENT RULE:** When comparing a taxpayer's federal and California effective tax rates, remember that California does not impose the 3.8% NIIT or the 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax. For W-2 wage earners above $200,000/$250,000, the effective California rate is lower than it appears because these federal surcharges do not exist at the state level.

> **AGENT RULE:** Always evaluate whether the taxpayer benefits from itemizing on their California return even if they use the standard deduction federally. With California's standard deduction at $5,706 (single), even modest itemized deductions (e.g., $6,000 in mortgage interest alone) make itemizing advantageous.

## Common Traps

> **TRAP:** Applying 0%/15%/20% capital gains rates on a California return. California has NO preferential rate. All gains are ordinary income. This is the single most impactful rate difference between federal and California tax for investors.

> **TRAP:** Assuming the $1,000,000 BHST threshold is indexed for inflation. It is not. The threshold has been $1,000,000 since Proposition 63 in 2004. With 20+ years of inflation, many more taxpayers now exceed this threshold than when it was enacted.

> **TRAP:** Using the federal standard deduction on a California return. California's standard deduction ($5,706 single) is roughly one-third of the federal amount ($16,100 single under OBBBA). Using the wrong figure dramatically understates California taxable income.

> **TRAP:** Failing to account for the nine brackets when estimating California tax. The gap between the 9.3% bracket and the 10.3% bracket is large (e.g., $72,724 to $371,479 for single filers). Most middle-to-upper-middle-income taxpayers fall entirely in the 9.3% bracket, making their effective California rate much lower than the top marginal rate would suggest.

> **TRAP:** Comparing California's 13.3% top rate to other states without considering that California does not impose the NIIT or Additional Medicare Tax. The total combined state+federal burden should be compared, not just the state rate in isolation.
