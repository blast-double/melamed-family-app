## Overview

Cost basis rules in California generally conform to federal law under IRC 1012 (cost basis), IRC 1014 (inherited basis with step-up to fair market value at death), and IRC 1015 (gift basis with carryover). However, the adjusted basis of depreciable property diverges because California does not allow bonus depreciation and caps Section 179 at $25,000. This creates a persistent dual-basis tracking requirement. California's status as a community property state provides a significant advantage under IRC 1014(b)(6): both halves of community property receive a full step-up in basis at the first spouse's death. California's non-conformity to HSAs under IRC 223 creates a separate basis tracking issue for HSA-related assets.

## California Rule

### Cost Basis (IRC 1012) -- Conforms

1. **General rule.** The basis of property is its cost. This applies to purchased assets, including stocks, real estate, and business equipment. California conforms fully.

2. **Basis adjustments for improvements.** The cost basis is increased by the cost of capital improvements and decreased by returns of capital, casualty losses claimed, and depreciation allowed or allowable. California conforms to these adjustment rules but uses its own depreciation amounts.

### Adjusted Basis -- Diverges Due to Depreciation

3. **Bonus depreciation non-conformity.** California does not conform to IRC 168(k). An asset that was fully expensed with 100% bonus depreciation federally (adjusted basis = $0 for federal purposes) retains a higher California basis equal to cost minus California-allowed depreciation (standard MACRS only).

4. **Section 179 limitation.** California caps the Section 179 deduction at $25,000 (phaseout beginning at $200,000 of qualifying property). The federal limit is $2,500,000 for 2025. Any excess federal Section 179 over the California limit increases the California basis relative to federal.

5. **Practical impact.** For a business asset placed in service in 2025:
   - Federal basis after year 1: potentially $0 (if fully expensed via bonus depreciation or Section 179)
   - California basis after year 1: Cost minus $25,000 Section 179 minus one year of standard MACRS depreciation
   - The California basis is materially higher, resulting in lower gain (or higher loss) on subsequent sale

### Gift Basis (IRC 1015) -- Conforms

6. **Carryover basis on gifts.** The donee's basis in property received by gift is generally the donor's adjusted basis (for purposes of computing gain). If the FMV at the time of the gift is less than the donor's basis, the donee's basis for computing loss is the FMV. California conforms fully to IRC 1015.

7. **Gift tax adjustment.** If gift tax was paid, the donee's basis is increased by a portion of the gift tax attributable to the net appreciation. California conforms.

### Inherited Basis (IRC 1014) -- Conforms (Including Community Property Full Step-Up)

8. **Step-up to FMV at death.** The basis of property acquired from a decedent is the fair market value (FMV) at the date of the decedent's death (or alternate valuation date if elected on the estate tax return). California conforms fully to IRC 1014.

9. **Community property full step-up -- IRC 1014(b)(6).** This is a critical California advantage. In community property states (including California), BOTH the decedent's half AND the surviving spouse's half of community property receive a step-up to FMV at the first spouse's death. This is codified in IRC 1014(b)(6), and California's community property laws ensure eligibility.

   - **Example:** Spouses purchased a home as community property for $400,000 (each spouse's basis = $200,000). At the first spouse's death, the home is worth $1,200,000. Both halves step up to $600,000 each. The surviving spouse's basis in the entire property becomes $1,200,000 -- not $800,000 (as would be the case in a common law state where only the decedent's half steps up).

10. **Separate property -- only decedent's share steps up.** If the property is the decedent's separate property, the full amount steps up. If it is the surviving spouse's separate property, there is no step-up (the surviving spouse did not acquire it from the decedent).

### Income in Respect of a Decedent (IRD) -- Step-Up Does NOT Apply

11. **IRD exception.** Under IRC 1014(c), the step-up in basis does NOT apply to items that constitute "income in respect of a decedent" (IRD). These include:
    - Traditional IRA balances
    - 401(k) and other qualified retirement plan balances
    - Deferred compensation
    - Installment sale receivables
    - Accrued but unpaid income

12. **California conforms to the IRD exception.** The surviving spouse or heir who receives IRD items recognizes the income when received, at their own tax rate. No step-up applies.

### HSA Basis -- California Does NOT Recognize HSAs

13. **Federal HSA treatment.** Under IRC 223, contributions to a Health Savings Account are deductible (or excluded from income if employer-contributed), earnings grow tax-free, and distributions for qualified medical expenses are tax-free. This creates a basis of $0 for tax purposes on earnings, and no income recognition on qualified distributions.

14. **California non-conformity.** California does NOT conform to IRC 223. As a result:
    - Employer HSA contributions are included in California wages (PIT wages).
    - Employee HSA contributions are not deductible on the California return.
    - HSA earnings (interest, dividends, capital gains inside the HSA) are taxable in California in the year earned.
    - Qualified distributions are NOT excluded from California gross income.

15. **Basis implication.** Because California taxes HSA contributions and earnings, the California basis in the HSA investments is higher than the federal basis. Distributions that are tax-free federally may still be partially tax-free in California to the extent they represent previously-taxed contributions and earnings (return of basis). This requires careful tracking.

## Conformity Analysis

**IRC RULE:** IRC 1012 (cost basis), IRC 1014 (step-up at death), IRC 1014(b)(6) (community property full step-up), IRC 1015 (gift carryover basis), IRC 168(k) (bonus depreciation reducing basis), IRC 179 (expensing reducing basis), IRC 223 (HSA tax-free treatment).

**CALIFORNIA RULE:** R&TC 17024.5 conforms to IRC 1012, 1014, 1014(b)(6), and 1015. Does NOT conform to IRC 168(k) (no bonus depreciation), limits IRC 179 to $25,000, and does NOT conform to IRC 223 (HSAs). The net effect is that California adjusted basis is frequently higher than federal adjusted basis for depreciable assets and HSA assets.

**Controlling Authority:** R&TC 17024.5(a)(1)(Q) (January 1, 2025 conformity date per SB 711); R&TC 18036-18038 (basis rules); FTB Bill Analyses for AB 781 and SB 230 (confirming HSA non-conformity through at least 2026).

**Schedule CA Impact:**
- Depreciation basis differences: Column B addition (add back excess federal depreciation in early years); Column C subtraction (California depreciation exceeds federal in later years)
- HSA contributions: Column B addition (add back federal HSA deduction)
- HSA earnings: Column B addition (add back any federally excluded HSA earnings)
- HSA distributions: Potentially Column C subtraction (to the extent of previously-taxed California basis being returned)

## Key Thresholds and Figures

| Item | Federal | California |
|------|---------|------------|
| Cost basis (IRC 1012) | Conforms | Conforms |
| Inherited basis step-up (IRC 1014) | FMV at death | Conforms |
| Community property full step-up (IRC 1014(b)(6)) | Both halves step up | Conforms -- critical CA advantage |
| Gift basis carryover (IRC 1015) | Donor's adjusted basis | Conforms |
| Bonus depreciation effect on basis | Reduces basis by 100% of cost | No bonus depreciation -- basis reduced only by standard MACRS |
| Section 179 effect on basis | Reduces basis up to $2,500,000 | Reduces basis up to $25,000 |
| HSA contribution basis | Not recognized (tax-free) | Recognized (previously taxed) |
| IRD items (IRA, 401k, deferred comp) | No step-up | No step-up (conforms) |

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE:** Maintain two basis schedules -- federal and California -- for EVERY depreciable asset. The California basis will be higher than the federal basis if bonus depreciation or excess Section 179 was claimed. Use the California basis for computing gain/loss on the California return.

> **AGENT RULE:** On inherited property in California, always check whether the property was community property or separate property. If community property, apply the full step-up to BOTH halves under IRC 1014(b)(6). This is the single most valuable basis rule for California married taxpayers.

> **AGENT RULE:** Never apply a step-up in basis to IRD items (IRAs, 401(k)s, deferred compensation, installment sale receivables). These are taxable when received by the heir, at the heir's ordinary income tax rate, for both federal and California purposes.

> **AGENT RULE:** For HSA holders, track California-specific basis in the HSA. Because California taxes contributions and earnings, the California basis is higher. Qualified distributions that are tax-free federally may be partially or wholly taxable in California, offset by the higher California basis (return of after-tax contributions).

## Common Traps

> **TRAP:** Assuming the federal adjusted basis of an asset is the same as the California adjusted basis. If ANY bonus depreciation or excess Section 179 was claimed in prior years, the California basis is higher. Using the federal basis on the California return understates the basis and overstates the gain.

> **TRAP:** Forgetting the community property full step-up. In a common law state, only the decedent's half of jointly-held property steps up. In California, BOTH halves of community property step up under IRC 1014(b)(6). Failing to claim the full step-up on the surviving spouse's half results in an inflated gain and excess tax on subsequent sale.

> **TRAP:** HSA "double taxation" myth vs. reality. Because California taxes HSA contributions and earnings, and also taxes distributions, some practitioners fear a double-tax. In practice, the higher California basis should prevent double taxation -- but only if the taxpayer maintains accurate records of previously-taxed California amounts. Without records, the taxpayer may inadvertently pay tax on the same dollars twice.

> **TRAP:** Converting community property to separate property before death destroys the IRC 1014(b)(6) full step-up advantage. If spouses transmute community property to one spouse's separate property (via prenuptial agreement, postnuptial agreement, or titling change), only the decedent's interest steps up at death. This is particularly costly for highly appreciated California real estate.
