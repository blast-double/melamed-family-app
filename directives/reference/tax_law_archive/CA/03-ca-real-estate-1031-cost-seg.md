## Overview

This document covers three related California real estate tax topics: (1) IRC Section 1031 like-kind exchanges and the California clawback mechanism for out-of-state replacement property, (2) cost segregation studies and the impact of California's non-conformity to bonus depreciation, and (3) real estate withholding requirements under Form 593. These areas present the most common compliance challenges for California real estate investors and require careful attention to California-specific rules that diverge from federal treatment.

## California Rule

### Section 1031 Like-Kind Exchanges

California generally conforms to IRC Section 1031 for like-kind exchanges of real property. The TCJA amendment limiting 1031 exchanges to real property (eliminating personal property exchanges) applies in California for exchanges initiated after January 10, 2019. The core federal rules that California conforms to include:

- **Real property only.** Only real property held for productive use in a trade or business or for investment qualifies. Personal property exchanges no longer qualify.
- **45-day identification period.** The taxpayer must identify replacement property within 45 days of transferring the relinquished property.
- **180-day exchange period.** The exchange must be completed within 180 days of the transfer or by the due date of the return (with extensions), whichever is earlier.
- **Related-party 2-year holding rule.** If the exchange is between related parties, neither party may dispose of the property within 2 years, or the deferred gain is recognized. California conforms.
- **Qualified intermediary rules.** California conforms to the QI safe-harbor requirements.

**The California Clawback -- Form FTB 3840.** When a taxpayer exchanges California real property for out-of-state replacement property under IRC Section 1031, California preserves its right to tax the deferred gain through an annual information return requirement. Under R&TC Sections 18032 and 24953 (effective for taxable years beginning on or after January 1, 2014):

- The taxpayer must file Form FTB 3840 in the year the exchange occurs.
- The taxpayer must file Form FTB 3840 every subsequent year until the California-source deferred gain or loss is recognized on a California tax return.
- The filing obligation applies to all taxpayers regardless of residency status or commercial domicile.
- The filing obligation does not cease if the out-of-state replacement property is later exchanged for another property in a subsequent 1031 exchange. The obligation continues until the original California-source deferred gain is finally recognized.
- The filing obligation ends when: (a) the deferred gain or loss is recognized on a California return, (b) the property is transferred through inheritance (stepped-up basis eliminates the deferred gain), or (c) the property is donated to a nonprofit.

**Penalty for failure to file FTB 3840.** If the taxpayer fails to file, the FTB may estimate net income from available information (including the full amount of deferred gain) and propose to assess tax, interest, and penalties. The FTB has conducted active compliance campaigns, sending letters to taxpayers identified as potentially having FTB 3840 filing requirements.

### Cost Segregation

A cost segregation study reclassifies building components from 27.5-year (residential) or 39-year (commercial) property into shorter-lived asset classes such as 5-year, 7-year, or 15-year property. This accelerates depreciation deductions.

California conforms to the reclassification of property into shorter MACRS recovery periods. However, California does not conform to federal bonus depreciation under IRC Section 168(k). This means:

- **Reclassified property gets shorter MACRS recovery periods** -- California allows depreciation over 5, 7, or 15 years for components identified by cost segregation.
- **No bonus depreciation on reclassified property** -- The first-year 60% (2024), or any other applicable bonus rate, does not apply for California. The reclassified property must be depreciated using regular MACRS methods (typically 200% declining balance for 5-year and 7-year property, 150% declining balance for 15-year property).
- **No increased Section 179 expensing** -- California does not conform to the TCJA increase in Section 179 deduction limits. California maintains its own lower Section 179 threshold (the pre-TCJA amount, adjusted).
- **California depreciation basis may differ** -- Because of the above differences, the adjusted basis of property will differ between federal and California returns. This difference must be tracked on Form FTB 3885A, Depreciation and Amortization Adjustments.

The practical result: a cost segregation study still provides a California benefit through shorter recovery periods, but the benefit is substantially smaller than the federal benefit because the large first-year bonus deduction is not available.

### Real Estate Withholding -- Form 593

California requires withholding on the sale or transfer of California real property under R&TC Section 18662. The withholding is a prepayment of income tax.

**Default withholding rate:** 3 1/3% (0.0333) of the total sales price.

**Alternative withholding calculation:** The seller may elect to compute withholding based on the gain on sale multiplied by the applicable maximum tax rate:

| Entity Type | Maximum Tax Rate |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Individual | 12.3% |
| Non-California Partnership | 12.3% |
| Trust | 12.3% |
| S Corporation | 13.8% |
| Corporation | 8.84% |
| Bank/Financial Corporation | 10.84% |
| Financial S Corporation | 15.8% |

The alternative calculation is often lower than the 3 1/3% of sales price and requires the seller to complete Part VI and Part VII of Form 593 and sign the certification.

**Filing and remittance:** The real estate escrow person (REEP) or buyer must file Form 593 and remit the withheld amount to the FTB. For transactions closing through escrow, the REEP handles this within 20 days of the close of the month in which the transaction closes. For installment sales, the buyer withholds on the principal portion of each installment payment.

**Exemptions from withholding** include (among others): the seller's last known street address is in California (principal residence exclusion does not automatically exempt), the total sales price is $100,000 or less, the property is the seller's principal residence (IRC Section 121 applies but additional certification required), the sale results in a loss, or a 1031 exchange is completed.

**Important: Section 121 does not automatically exempt from withholding.** Even when the $250,000/$500,000 home sale exclusion applies, the seller must certify the exemption on Form 593. The REEP is required to withhold unless a valid exemption certificate is provided.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

California's October 2025 conformity update (SB 711) updated the specified conformity date but continued to decouple from bonus depreciation under IRC Section 168(k) and increased Section 179 expensing. If OBBBA makes permanent the 100% bonus depreciation provisions, California will not conform to those changes unless separate legislation is enacted. The continued California non-conformity to bonus depreciation directly impacts the value of cost segregation studies for California tax purposes.

## Agent Rules

1. **Any 1031 exchange of California real property into out-of-state replacement property requires annual FTB 3840 filings indefinitely.** This obligation continues until the California-source deferred gain is fully recognized. Do not assume the obligation ends when the replacement property is later exchanged in a subsequent 1031.

2. **Track California depreciation basis separately from federal.** Because California does not allow bonus depreciation, the adjusted basis of real property will diverge between federal and California. This affects gain calculations on future sales or exchanges and requires Form FTB 3885A adjustments.

3. **For cost segregation, compute the California benefit using MACRS straight-line or declining-balance methods only.** Do not apply any bonus depreciation percentage. The California benefit from cost segregation is limited to the acceleration from shorter recovery periods (e.g., 5-year instead of 39-year).

4. **On any sale of California real property, verify withholding requirements on Form 593.** Even when an exemption applies (e.g., principal residence under Section 121, 1031 exchange), the seller must certify the exemption. Failure to withhold or certify results in penalties.

5. **For installment sales of California real property, withholding applies to each principal payment.** The buyer must file Form 593 and remit withholding by the 20th of the month following each payment. Use the total sales price method (3 1/3%) or the alternative calculation.

## Common Traps

1. **Forgetting annual FTB 3840 filings after a 1031 exchange into out-of-state property.** This is the most common compliance failure. The FTB actively enforces this requirement and has sent compliance letters to identified non-filers. The obligation persists even if the taxpayer leaves California.

2. **Assuming cost segregation provides the same first-year benefit for California as federal.** Because California does not allow bonus depreciation, a cost segregation study that produces a large federal deduction (e.g., 60% of reclassified components in year one) will produce a much smaller California deduction (just the normal first-year MACRS allowance on shorter-lived property).

3. **Failing to account for California basis differences in gain calculations.** When selling property that had cost segregation applied, the California adjusted basis will be higher than the federal basis (because less total depreciation was claimed for California). This means the California gain may be lower, but the depreciation recapture amounts will also differ.

4. **Assuming Section 121 home sale exclusion automatically exempts from Form 593 withholding.** It does not. The seller must certify the exemption on Form 593. Without the certification, the REEP must withhold.

5. **Missing the FTB 3840 obligation for chain exchanges.** If a taxpayer exchanges California property for Texas property, then later exchanges the Texas property for Florida property, the FTB 3840 obligation on the original California deferred gain continues through the entire chain until the gain is recognized.

6. **Using the wrong withholding rate for the entity type.** Different entities have different maximum tax rates for the alternative withholding calculation. An S corporation uses 13.8%, not 12.3%. Misapplying the rate results in incorrect withholding.
