## Overview

California conforms to the federal treatment of startup costs under IRC Section 195 and organizational costs under IRC Section 248. Taxpayers may deduct up to $5,000 of startup costs in the year the business begins active trade or business, with the remainder amortized over 180 months. The same rules apply to organizational costs. This is an area of full conformity -- no Schedule CA adjustment is needed for startup and organizational cost deductions. Additionally, California historically provided a new business NOL carryback for losses incurred in the first three years of operation, though this provision is no longer available for current tax years.

## California Rule

### Startup Costs -- IRC Section 195

1. **First-year deduction:** Up to **$5,000** of startup costs may be deducted in the tax year the active trade or business begins
2. **Phase-out:** The $5,000 deduction is reduced (but not below zero) by the amount total startup costs exceed **$50,000**
3. **Amortization of remainder:** Costs not deducted in the first year are amortized ratably over **180 months** (15 years), beginning with the month the active trade or business begins
4. **What qualifies as a startup cost:**
   - Costs of investigating the creation or acquisition of a business
   - Costs of creating a business (before beginning operations)
   - Costs that would be deductible as trade or business expenses if paid in connection with an existing business
5. **Election:** The deduction is deemed elected in the year the business begins. An affirmative election to capitalize all costs (forgoing the $5,000 deduction) must be made on a timely filed return.

### Organizational Costs -- IRC Section 248 (Corporations) / IRC Section 709 (Partnerships)

1. **Corporations (IRC Section 248):** Up to **$5,000** of organizational costs may be deducted in the first tax year the corporation begins business; remainder amortized over 180 months. Phase-out begins at $50,000.
2. **Partnerships (IRC Section 709):** Same $5,000/$50,000/180-month treatment for partnership organizational costs.
3. **What qualifies as organizational costs:**
   - Legal services for drafting the charter, bylaws, or partnership agreement
   - Accounting services incident to organization
   - State filing fees and incorporation/formation fees
4. **What does NOT qualify:**
   - Costs of issuing or selling stock/partnership interests
   - Costs related to transferring assets to the entity
   - Syndication costs (must be capitalized; no amortization)

### California Conformity Details

1. California conforms to IRC Section 195 through R&TC Section 17201 (PITL -- block conformity to IRC as of January 1, 2025 per SB 711) and to IRC Section 248 through R&TC Section 24365 (CTL -- standalone conformity)
2. FTB Publication 984 confirms: startup cost deduction is "Same as federal" -- $5,000 first year, phase-out at $50,000
3. SB 711's conformity update to January 1, 2025 has no practical impact on startup costs, as the underlying IRC Sections 195, 248, and 709 were not changed by the TCJA or OBBBA

## Conformity Analysis

**IRC RULE:** $5,000 first-year deduction for startup costs (phased out above $50,000 total); remainder amortized over 180 months. Same for organizational costs.

**CALIFORNIA RULE:** Identical to federal. Full conformity.

**Controlling Authority:** R&TC Section 17201 (PITL block conformity); R&TC Section 24365 (CTL specific conformity to IRC 248); R&TC Section 17024.5 (specified date of conformity).

**Schedule CA Impact:** None. No adjustment required because California conforms fully.

## New Business NOL Carryback (Historical)

California historically provided a special NOL carryback for new businesses that was an exception to the general no-carryback rule:

1. **Availability:** For NOLs incurred in tax years 2013--2018, a taxpayer operating a "new business" could carry back NOLs incurred in the first 3 years of the business to the 2 preceding tax years
2. **Carryback percentage:** 100% for NOLs incurred in tax years beginning on or after January 1, 2015
3. **Current status:** The NOL carryback is **no longer available** for tax years after 2018. FTB confirms: "The NOL carryback is no longer available."
4. **New business definition (R&TC Section 17276(e)):**
   - A trade or business activity that is first commenced in California on or after January 1, 1994
   - Net increase in the number of jobs in California during the first 2 tax years (jobs test)
   - At least 80% of total employees perform at least 50% of their services in California

### New Business Extended Carryforward (Historical)

For tax years beginning before January 1, 2000, new businesses received extended carryforward periods:
- 8 years for NOLs from the first tax year
- 7 years for NOLs from the second tax year
- 6 years for NOLs from the third tax year

These extended carryforward provisions have largely been superseded by the general 20-year carryforward period.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity (Where Applicable)

The OBBBA (Section 70302) modified IRC Section 195 through conforming changes related to the new R&E expensing provisions under Section 174A. However, these conforming changes do not substantively alter the startup cost deduction itself. California does not conform to OBBBA generally, but the non-conformity has no practical impact on the startup cost deduction because the $5,000/$50,000/180-month framework was not changed.

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE:** Startup costs and organizational costs are an area of **full conformity**. No Schedule CA adjustment is needed. If the federal return correctly applies IRC Section 195/248/709, the California amounts are the same.

> **AGENT RULE:** The new business NOL carryback is no longer available for current tax years. Do not advise taxpayers that they can carry back NOLs from a new business. The last eligible tax year was 2018.

> **AGENT RULE:** Syndication costs (costs of marketing and selling partnership or LLC interests) are **never** deductible or amortizable -- they must be capitalized permanently. This applies equally on both federal and California returns.

> **AGENT RULE:** If a taxpayer has startup costs exceeding $50,000, the first-year deduction is reduced dollar-for-dollar. At $55,000 or more in startup costs, the $5,000 deduction is fully phased out, and the entire amount must be amortized over 180 months.

## Common Traps

> **TRAP:** Confusing startup costs with Section 179 or bonus depreciation. Startup costs are intangible investigation and creation costs, not depreciable tangible property. They follow Section 195, not Section 168 or 179.

> **TRAP:** Deducting startup costs before the business begins active operations. The deduction is only available in the tax year the active trade or business begins. Costs incurred in an investigation phase before the business commences are capitalized until that date.

> **TRAP:** Assuming the new business NOL carryback is still available. It expired after 2018. Taxpayers starting new businesses in 2025 or later have no carryback option -- only the standard 20-year carryforward.

> **TRAP:** Including syndication costs in the organizational cost amortization. Syndication costs are specifically excluded from Section 248/709 and must be capitalized permanently. They are not amortizable under any provision.

> **TRAP:** Failing to begin the 180-month amortization period in the month the business begins operations. The clock starts in the month of commencement, not the month the costs were incurred or the beginning of the tax year.
