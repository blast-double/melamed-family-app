## Overview

California S corporations are subject to a unique dual-layer tax regime: the entity pays a 1.5% tax on California net income (with a minimum of $800), and the income also passes through to shareholders who report it on their personal California returns. This entity-level tax has no federal equivalent and represents a significant additional cost of S corporation status in California. California generally follows the federal S election automatically, but imposes its own tax rates, does not allow the QBI deduction, and applies a longer recognition period for built-in gains than current federal law.

## California Rule

### S Election -- Follows Federal Automatically

A valid federal S election is automatically recognized for California purposes under R&TC Section 23801. A separate California S election is not required. However, a corporation can opt out of California S status by filing Form FTB 3560 to terminate or revoke the election for California purposes only.

If a corporation has a valid federal S election, it must be treated as an S corporation for California purposes (for taxable years beginning on or after January 1, 2002). A corporation cannot be a federal S corporation and a California C corporation.

### Tax Rate: 1.5% on Net Income (Minimum $800)

California S corporations pay the greater of:

- The **minimum franchise tax of $800**, OR
- **1.5% of California net income**

Financial institution S corporations pay the greater of $800 or **3.5%** of net income (R&TC Section 23802).

This entity-level tax is computed on net income calculated in the same manner as for C corporations, with certain modifications specific to S corporation treatment. The tax is due with the return on the 15th day of the 3rd month after the close of the taxable year (March 15 for calendar-year filers).

**Key distinction:** The 1.5% entity-level tax is in addition to the personal income tax that shareholders pay on their distributive share of S corporation income. There is no federal equivalent -- at the federal level, an S corporation generally pays no entity-level income tax (except for built-in gains and excess net passive income).

### Reasonable Compensation

California follows federal rules regarding reasonable compensation for S corporation shareholder-employees. The FTB may recharacterize distributions as wages if a shareholder-employee receives unreasonably low compensation while taking large distributions. This mirrors IRS enforcement priorities and applies the same substance-over-form analysis.

Shareholders who perform services for the S corporation must receive reasonable compensation before taking distributions. Failure to pay reasonable wages can result in reclassification of distributions as wages, triggering payroll tax liability and penalties.

### No QBI Deduction for Shareholders

California does not conform to IRC Section 199A. S corporation shareholders cannot claim a QBI deduction on their California return, even though they may claim one on their federal return. This increases the effective California tax rate on S corporation income relative to the federal rate.

## Built-In Gains Tax

### Overview

When a C corporation elects to be an S corporation, certain items of gain or loss recognized during the recognition period are subject to the C corporation tax rate (8.84%) instead of the S corporation tax rate (1.5%). This is the built-in gains (BIG) tax under IRC Section 1374, as modified by R&TC Section 23809.

### California Recognition Period: 10 Years

**This is a critical non-conformity.** Under current federal law, the built-in gains recognition period is 5 years (made permanent by the American Taxpayer Relief Act of 2012). California does NOT conform to the 5-year period and instead applies a **10-year recognition period** (120 months) following the C corporation's election to become an S corporation.

The recognition period begins on the first day the corporation is an S corporation. A short tax year does not reduce the recognition period.

### BIG Tax Rate

Built-in gains are taxed at the highest California corporate tax rate:
- **8.84%** for non-financial corporations
- **10.84%** for financial corporations (8.84% + 2% surcharge)

### Items Subject to BIG Tax

The following items from C corporation years are treated as built-in gains subject to the higher rate:

- Accounts receivable of cash-basis taxpayers
- Long-term contract deferred income
- Deferred income from installment sales
- Recapture of depreciation
- Income from unreplaced LIFO inventory
- Any other income items recognized during the recognition period that were economically accrued in C corporation years

### Credits Against BIG Tax

R&TC Section 23809(b) does not allow credit carryovers to reduce the California built-in gains tax. This is stricter than federal treatment, which does permit certain credits to offset BIG tax liability.

### Excess Net Passive Income Tax

Under R&TC Section 23811 (conforming to IRC Section 1375), S corporations with accumulated C corporation earnings and profits that have passive investment income exceeding 25% of gross receipts are subject to an additional tax on excess net passive income. The tax rate is 8.84%. R&TC Section 23811(c) disallows credits against this tax.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

The OBBBA made the federal QBI deduction permanent at 23% (up from 20%). California explicitly decouples from this provision under SB 711. For S corporation shareholders, this means:

- Federal taxable income is reduced by up to 23% of QBI
- California taxable income receives no such reduction
- The effective California tax rate on S corporation passthrough income is therefore materially higher relative to the federal rate

This non-conformity is permanent under current California law and requires an explicit legislative act to change.

## Agent Rules

1. **Always model S-Corp vs. LLC vs. C-Corp**: Compare the 1.5% CA entity-level tax + individual tax on passthrough vs. the LLC fee structure vs. the 8.84% C-Corp rate + dividend tax. Include the QBI non-conformity in the analysis.
2. **Flag the 10-year BIG recognition period**: When advising on C-to-S conversions, always note that California imposes a 10-year recognition period even though federal uses 5 years. This is a common oversight.
3. **Verify reasonable compensation**: The FTB follows IRS guidance on reasonable compensation. If a shareholder-employee takes distributions significantly exceeding salary, flag for review.
4. **Model PTE elective tax for every S-Corp**: See `06-ca-pte-elective-tax.md`. The PTE election is almost always beneficial for S-corps with owners affected by the SALT cap.
5. **QSub annual tax**: A qualified subchapter S subsidiary (QSub) is subject to an $800 annual tax, which is paid by the S corporation parent (R&TC Section 23800.5(a)(1)(B)).

## Common Traps

1. **Double-tax surprise**: New S corporation owners are often surprised to learn that California imposes a 1.5% entity-level tax in addition to the personal income tax on passthrough income. No other major state imposes a comparable mandatory entity-level tax on S corporations (though some states offer elective PTE taxes).

2. **BIG tax -- 10 years, not 5**: Tax professionals accustomed to the federal 5-year BIG recognition period may miss that California uses 10 years. A C-to-S conversion that is "safe" federally (assets held more than 5 years) may still trigger BIG tax in California for years 6 through 10.

3. **No credits against BIG tax**: Unlike the federal BIG tax, California does not allow credit carryovers (including net operating losses from C corporation years) to offset the built-in gains tax. The full 8.84% applies without credit offset.

4. **Accumulated E&P and passive income**: S corporations with C corporation accumulated earnings and profits must monitor the 25% passive income threshold. Exceeding this threshold triggers the ENPI tax and, for three consecutive years, can result in termination of the S election.

5. **Apportionment for multi-state S-Corps**: S corporations with income inside and outside California must use Schedule R to apportion income. The 1.5% tax applies only to California-apportioned net income, but shareholders report their share of total income on personal returns (with a credit for taxes paid to other states).
