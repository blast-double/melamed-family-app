## Overview

California's passive activity loss (PAL) rules generally conform to IRC Section 469, which limits the deductibility of losses from passive activities to the extent of passive income. Losses in excess of passive income are suspended and carried forward to future years. However, California has one critical area of non-conformity: it does not recognize the real estate professional exception under IRC Section 469(c)(7). This single difference has significant implications for real estate investors who qualify as real estate professionals for federal purposes but cannot use that status to reclassify rental activities as nonpassive on their California return.

The PAL rules apply to individuals, estates, trusts, closely held C corporations, and personal service corporations. Passive activities include any trade or business in which the taxpayer does not materially participate and any rental activity (with limited exceptions). California computes passive activity limitations using Form FTB 3801 and reports California-specific adjustments through Schedule CA (540).

## California Rule

California generally conforms to the federal PAL framework under IRC Section 469 through its incorporation by reference in R&TC Section 17561. This means the following federal rules apply for California purposes:

**Material Participation Tests.** California conforms to all seven material participation tests under Temporary Treasury Regulation Section 1.469-5T. A taxpayer materially participates in an activity if they satisfy any one of the following:

1. Participation of more than 500 hours during the tax year.
2. The taxpayer's participation constitutes substantially all of the participation in the activity.
3. Participation of more than 100 hours, and no other individual participates more.
4. The activity is a significant participation activity (more than 100 hours), and aggregate participation in all significant participation activities exceeds 500 hours.
5. The taxpayer materially participated in the activity for any 5 of the 10 preceding tax years.
6. The activity is a personal service activity and the taxpayer materially participated for any 3 preceding tax years.
7. Based on all facts and circumstances, the taxpayer participates on a regular, continuous, and substantial basis.

**$25,000 Rental Loss Allowance.** California conforms to the special $25,000 allowance for rental real estate losses under IRC Section 469(i). Taxpayers who actively participate in rental real estate activities may deduct up to $25,000 of net rental losses against nonpassive income. However, this allowance phases out at the rate of 50 cents for each dollar of AGI exceeding $100,000, reaching zero at $150,000 AGI. The critical difference is that California uses California AGI (not federal AGI) for this phase-out calculation. Since California AGI may differ from federal AGI due to various conformity adjustments, the phase-out threshold can produce different results on the state return.

**Suspended Loss Release on Disposition.** California conforms to the rule that fully suspended passive losses are released and become deductible upon a complete disposition of the activity in a fully taxable transaction to an unrelated party.

**Grouping Elections.** California generally conforms to the federal grouping rules under Treasury Regulation Section 1.469-4. Taxpayers may group one or more trade or business activities or rental activities as a single activity if the activities form an appropriate economic unit. However, rental activities cannot be grouped with trade or business activities (except in certain limited circumstances involving the real estate professional election, which California does not recognize).

## Conformity Analysis

California's conformity with the federal PAL rules is broad but contains one major non-conformity:

**Real Estate Professional Exception -- Does Not Conform.** R&TC Section 17561(a) explicitly states: "Section 469(c)(7) of the Internal Revenue Code, relating to special rules for taxpayers in real property business, shall not apply." This means that for California purposes, all rental real estate activities are treated as passive activities regardless of the taxpayer's qualification as a real estate professional for federal purposes. Taxpayers who materially participate in rental activities and meet the 750-hour and more-than-half-of-services tests under IRC Section 469(c)(7) can treat rental income and losses as nonpassive on their federal return, but they cannot do so on their California return.

This creates a situation where a taxpayer's federal Schedule E may show nonpassive rental losses fully deductible against wages and other income, while the California return must still treat those same losses as passive. This requires Schedule CA (540) adjustments and a separate computation on Form FTB 3801.

As stated in the FTB 3801 instructions: "If you have an activity that is nonpassive for federal purposes and passive for California purposes (as in the case of rental real estate professionals), use the actual federal amounts allowed in column (d) of the California Adjustment Worksheets."

**Low-Income Housing Credit Exception.** For taxable years beginning on or after January 1, 2020, the dollar limitation for the offset for rental real estate activities does not apply to the low-income housing credit program under R&TC Section 17561(d)(1). This is reported on Form FTB 3801-CR.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) provisions do not directly modify IRC Section 469 passive activity rules. California's existing non-conformity regarding the real estate professional exception under IRC 469(c)(7) remains the primary area of divergence. Any future federal changes to PAL rules should be evaluated against California's specified conformity date.

## Agent Rules

1. **Always use California AGI for the $25,000 rental loss allowance phase-out.** Do not use federal AGI. California AGI may differ from federal AGI due to numerous conformity adjustments (e.g., depreciation differences, state tax deduction addback, HSA adjustments). Calculate the phase-out independently.

2. **Never apply the real estate professional exception on a California return.** Even if the taxpayer qualifies as a real estate professional federally and has fully deductible rental losses on the federal return, those rental activities remain passive for California. Always compute Form FTB 3801 separately.

3. **Track California-specific suspended losses independently.** Because California depreciation methods differ from federal (no bonus depreciation, different Section 179 limits), the California passive loss amount will often differ from the federal amount. Maintain separate suspended loss tracking for California.

4. **Rental activities cannot be grouped with nonrental activities for California.** Since California does not recognize IRC 469(c)(7), the grouping election that allows real estate professionals to treat rental and nonrental activities as a single activity is not available for California.

5. **Form FTB 3801 is required whenever there is a difference between federal and California passive activity treatment.** This includes situations where an activity is nonpassive federally (due to REPS) but passive for California.

## Common Traps

1. **Using federal AGI instead of California AGI for the rental loss phase-out.** This is the most common error. California adjustments to AGI (depreciation differences, HSA addbacks, state tax refund exclusions) can push the taxpayer above or below the phase-out range differently than on the federal return.

2. **Assuming real estate professional status carries over to California.** Many tax preparers automatically apply the REPS designation to the California return. This is incorrect and can lead to significant underreporting of California tax.

3. **Failing to file Form FTB 3801 when federal and California passive activity amounts differ.** Even when the taxpayer has no passive losses on the federal return (because REPS makes them nonpassive), they may have passive losses on the California return that require Form FTB 3801.

4. **Overlooking the California depreciation basis difference.** Since California does not conform to bonus depreciation, the rental activity's net income or loss will differ between federal and California. This affects the passive activity computation and the amount of suspended losses.

5. **Short-term rental loophole ambiguity.** While California clearly does not conform to IRC 469(c)(7) for real estate professionals, the treatment of short-term rentals under Treasury Regulation 1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii)(A) (the 7-day average rental exception) is less clear. California does not explicitly address this regulation, which creates uncertainty for taxpayers relying on the STR loophole.

6. **Ignoring the low-income housing credit exception.** Since 2020, the $25,000 rental activity dollar limitation does not apply to the low-income housing credit. Taxpayers with LIHC allocations should claim credits on Form FTB 3801-CR without the dollar limitation.
