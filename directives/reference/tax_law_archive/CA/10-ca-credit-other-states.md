## Overview

California residents who earn income that is also taxed by another state face the problem of double taxation. The Other State Tax Credit (OSTC), claimed on Schedule S, provides relief by allowing California residents to credit income taxes paid to other states against their California tax liability. The credit ensures that the same income is not effectively taxed twice — once by the other state and once by California. The credit is strictly a matter of legislative grace and is construed narrowly, with specific requirements varying by the taxpayer's residency status and entity type.

---

## California Rule

### Eligibility by Taxpayer Type

The OSTC rules differ based on the type of taxpayer:

**California Resident Individuals (R&TC Section 18001):**
An OSTC is allowed for net income taxes imposed by and paid to another state on income that is also taxed by California, where that income is **derived from sources within the other state** as determined by California's own sourcing rules. The key limitation: the double-taxed income must be sourced to the other state under California law (not just the other state's law) for a California resident individual to claim the credit.

**California Resident Partners/Members (R&TC Section 18006):**
A California resident who is a partner in a partnership or member of an LLC that pays tax to another state may claim the OSTC for the taxpayer's pro rata share of net income taxes paid to the other state by the entity, **as if those taxes had been paid directly by the taxpayer**.

**California Resident Estates and Trusts (R&TC Section 18004):**
If a California resident estate or trust is also a resident of another state, the OSTC is available for net income taxes paid to the other state. Importantly, the dual-resident trust OSTC does **not** require the income to be sourced to the other state under California law — it applies whether the income is sourced to California or the other state. This is broader than the individual OSTC.

**California Resident Beneficiaries (R&TC Section 18005):**
A California resident beneficiary of an estate or trust may claim the OSTC for their share of income taxes paid by the estate or trust to another state.

### Credit Computation

The OSTC is computed on Schedule S and equals the **lesser of**:

1. **Net income tax actually paid to the other state** on the double-taxed income, **or**
2. **The California tax that would be imposed on the same income** (computed by applying the California effective tax rate to the double-taxed income)

The credit is computed separately for each state to which taxes were paid. If the taxpayer paid taxes to multiple other states, a separate Schedule S computation is performed for each state.

### "Net Tax" Definition

For OSTC purposes, "net income tax" means the tax liability after applying credits available in the other state but before any penalties or interest. Only actual income taxes qualify — franchise taxes, gross receipts taxes, and other non-income-based taxes generally do not qualify for the OSTC.

---

## Key Limitations

### Cannot Offset the Behavioral Health Services Tax (BHS Tax)

The OSTC **cannot reduce or offset** the 1% Behavioral Health Services Tax (formerly Mental Health Services Tax) on taxable income over $1,000,000. This means high-income taxpayers will always owe the BHS Tax regardless of how much tax they pay to other states.

### Cannot Offset Corporate Franchise Minimum Tax

For corporate taxpayers, the OSTC cannot reduce the $800 minimum franchise tax.

### S Corporation / Partnership Pass-Through

When an S corporation or partnership pays tax to another state, the credit flows through to the individual owners. The owners claim the credit on their personal Schedule S based on their pro rata share of the entity-level taxes paid.

### Timing

The credit is claimed in the tax year in which the other state's tax is paid or accrued (consistent with the taxpayer's overall accounting method). For estimated taxes paid to another state, the credit is claimed in the year the tax relates to, not necessarily when the estimated payment was made.

### Reciprocity

California does not have tax reciprocity agreements with any state. California residents working in another state must file in both states and claim the OSTC on their California return.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

The OBBBA does not directly affect the California OSTC. However, because OBBBA changes may alter other states' tax treatment of certain income (e.g., if another state conforms to OBBBA tip/overtime exclusions while California does not), the amount of double-taxed income and the resulting OSTC computation may be indirectly affected. Additionally, the OBBBA's $40,000 SALT cap does not affect the California OSTC computation, as the OSTC is a credit against California tax, not a deduction.

## PTE Credit Interaction

The relationship between the PTE elective tax credit (Form 3804-CR) and the OSTC requires careful analysis in multi-state situations:

- If a PTE operates in multiple states and elects into both California's PTE regime and another state's PTE regime, the owner may be entitled to an OSTC for the taxes paid to the other state's PTE regime
- The OSTC is applied after the PTE credit in the credit ordering sequence
- Both the PTE credit and the OSTC are nonrefundable and neither can offset the BHS Tax

---

## Agent Rules

1. **The OSTC is available only to California residents.** Nonresidents are taxed only on California-source income and do not face double taxation in the same way.

2. **Income must be sourced to the other state under California's rules** for individual residents. The credit is not available simply because another state taxes the income — California's sourcing rules must also source the income to the other state.

3. **The credit cannot exceed California's tax on the same income.** This prevents the OSTC from being used to reduce California tax below what California would impose on the double-taxed income.

4. **No carryforward.** Unlike many other credits, the OSTC has no carryforward. If the credit exceeds the current year's liability (after BHS Tax exclusion), the excess is lost.

5. **BHS Tax is always owed.** For taxpayers with income over $1,000,000, the 1% BHS Tax cannot be offset by the OSTC or the PTE credit.

6. **S-corp/partnership credits flow through.** Ensure entity-level taxes paid to other states are properly allocated on K-1s so owners can claim the OSTC.

---

## Common Traps

1. **Claiming OSTC for non-income taxes.** Franchise taxes, gross receipts taxes, and other non-income-based taxes do not qualify. Only net income taxes qualify.

2. **Overlooking the sourcing requirement.** California's sourcing rules may differ from the other state's rules. The income must be sourced to the other state under California law for a resident individual to claim the credit.

3. **Expecting the OSTC to offset BHS Tax.** The 1% tax on income over $1,000,000 is not reduced by the OSTC.

4. **Not filing Schedule S.** The OSTC requires filing Schedule S with Form 540. Simply noting the tax paid on another line is insufficient.

5. **Double-counting PTE credits and OSTC.** When both PTE credits and OSTC are involved in a multi-state situation, careful ordering and allocation is required to avoid double benefit.

6. **Assuming carryforward exists.** There is no carryforward for unused OSTC. Plan estimated payments and credit utilization accordingly.
