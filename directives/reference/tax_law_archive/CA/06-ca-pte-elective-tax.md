## Overview

California's Pass-Through Entity (PTE) Elective Tax is a workaround to the federal $10,000 State and Local Tax (SALT) deduction cap imposed by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA) of 2017. Originally enacted by AB 150 (effective for tax years beginning January 1, 2021), the PTE elective tax allows qualifying passthrough entities to pay California income tax at the entity level (9.3% of qualified net income). The entity deducts this tax payment for federal purposes as a business expense (not subject to the SALT cap per IRS Notice 2020-75), and the individual owners claim a nonrefundable credit on their California returns.

SB 132, signed into law in 2025, extends the PTE elective tax through tax year 2030 and relaxes the previously strict June 15 prepayment requirement.

## California Rule

### Eligible Entities

The PTE elective tax is available to:

- **S corporations**
- **Partnerships** (including LLCs taxed as partnerships)
- **LLCs taxed as partnerships**

The election is NOT available to:
- Sole proprietorships
- C corporations
- Single-member LLCs (disregarded entities)
- Publicly traded partnerships

### Qualified Taxpayers

The credit from the PTE elective tax may be claimed by "qualified taxpayers," defined as:
- Individuals
- Trusts
- Estates
- Certain disregarded single-member LLCs owned by individuals

Corporate partners/shareholders are NOT qualified taxpayers and cannot benefit from the PTE credit.

### Tax Rate and Calculation

The PTE elective tax rate is **9.3%** of the qualified entity's **qualified net income**. Qualified net income is the sum of the pro rata or distributive share and guaranteed payments of each qualified taxpayer's income subject to California personal income tax.

The election is made on the entity's timely filed original or superseding return by filing a completed Form 3804 and including the PTE elective tax amount on the designated line of the entity's return.

### Payment Timeline

#### Tax Years 2022-2025

| Due Date | Payment Requirement |
|---|---|
| **June 15** (of taxable year) | Payment 1: Greater of $1,000 OR 50% of the PTE elective tax paid for the prior taxable year |
| **Original return due date** (without regard to extensions) | Payment 2: Remaining balance |

For tax years 2022-2025, failure to make the required June 15 payment (or making an insufficient payment) **disqualifies the entity from making the PTE election entirely**.

#### Tax Years 2026-2030 (SB 132 Changes)

| Due Date | Payment Requirement |
|---|---|
| **June 15** (of taxable year) | Payment 1: Greater of $1,000 OR 50% of the PTE elective tax paid for the prior taxable year |
| **Original return due date** (without regard to extensions) | Payment 2: Remaining balance |

**Critical SB 132 change:** For tax years 2026-2030, failure to make the June 15 payment **no longer disqualifies** the entity from making the election. However, if the payment is late or insufficient, the qualified taxpayers' PTE elective tax credit is **reduced by 12.5%** of their pro rata share of the unpaid amount that was due on June 15.

**Example:** An entity is required to prepay $20,000 by June 15, 2026, but pays nothing. The entity can still make the PTE election. However, the total credit available to the owners is permanently reduced by $2,500 ($20,000 x 12.5%), allocated pro rata among the owners.

### Extension Through 2030

SB 132 extends the availability of the PTE elective tax for taxable years 2026 through 2030. The election remains contingent on the continued existence of the federal SALT deduction limitation under IRC Section 164(b)(6). If Congress fully repeals the SALT limitation, California's PTE program terminates in the same year. However, unused PTE credits from prior years would still carry forward for up to five years.

## Tax Credit Mechanics

### Form 3804-CR

Qualified taxpayers claim the PTE elective tax credit on Form 3804-CR, attached to their personal California return (Form 540 or 540NR).

Key credit rules:
- **Nonrefundable**: The credit can only reduce California tax liability to zero; excess credit is not refunded.
- **5-year carryforward**: Unused credits can be carried forward for up to 5 years. The carryforward period is not impacted by any repeal or expiration of the PTE tax credit statute.
- **Cannot offset the Mental Health Services (MHS) Tax**: The PTE credit cannot be used to reduce the 1% MHS tax on income over $1 million. This is a critical limitation for high-income taxpayers.

### Federal Treatment

The PTE elective tax paid by the entity is treated as a deductible state income tax at the entity level under IRS Notice 2020-75. This means:

1. The entity deducts the PTE tax when computing its non-separately stated income or loss for the tax year.
2. This reduces the K-1 income passed through to owners.
3. The deduction occurs at the entity level and is therefore **not subject to the individual SALT cap**.
4. Owners effectively get a full federal deduction for the California tax paid, bypassing the SALT limitation.

The net federal benefit equals the owner's marginal federal tax rate multiplied by the PTE tax paid on their behalf.

## Conformity Analysis

The PTE elective tax is a California-specific mechanism with no direct federal analogue. The federal interaction occurs through:

- **IRS Notice 2020-75**: Confirmed that entity-level state income taxes paid by partnerships and S corporations are deductible at the entity level and not subject to the individual SALT cap. Treasury has not yet issued formal regulations, but taxpayers may rely on the Notice for payments made on or after November 9, 2020.
- **OBBBA SALT cap changes**: The OBBBA increased the SALT cap from $10,000 to $40,000 for tax years 2025-2029 (subject to phase-out for high earners). For 2030 and beyond, the cap returns to $10,000. This means the PTE election may provide reduced benefit during 2025-2029 for some taxpayers (those whose total SALT is under $40,000), but remains valuable for most California taxpayers with significant state tax liability and is essential again starting in 2030.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

The OBBBA's increase of the SALT cap to $40,000 (2025-2029) reduces but does not eliminate the benefit of the PTE elective tax for many California taxpayers. Consider:

- A married couple with $200,000 in California income tax liability: the $40,000 SALT cap still leaves $160,000 undeductible. The PTE election provides a full deduction for the entity-level tax.
- A single filer with $30,000 in total SALT: the PTE election provides less incremental benefit under the $40,000 cap but remains relevant if state income taxes alone approach or exceed the cap.
- For 2030 and beyond (SALT cap returns to $10,000), the PTE election regains its full pre-OBBBA value.

The Grant Thornton analysis notes that California's extension of the PTE tax through 2030 was unlocked by the OBBBA's extension of the federal SALT deduction cap through 2030.

## Agent Rules

1. **Model PTE election for every CA S-corp and partnership.** Compare the taxpayer's total federal and California tax liability with and without the PTE election. The analysis must include the reduction in K-1 income, the California credit, and the MHS tax limitation.
2. **Always check MHS tax exposure.** If the taxpayer has California taxable income over $1 million, the PTE credit cannot offset the 1% MHS tax. Estimate the residual MHS liability separately.
3. **June 15 prepayment is critical for 2025.** For tax year 2025, an insufficient June 15 payment kills the election entirely. For 2026+, the penalty is 12.5% of the shortfall but the election survives.
4. **Track the OBBBA SALT cap.** The $40,000 SALT cap (2025-2029) reduces but does not eliminate PTE benefit for high-income California taxpayers. Recalculate annually.
5. **Verify qualified taxpayer status.** Corporate partners/shareholders cannot claim the credit. Only individuals, trusts, and estates qualify.

## Common Traps

1. **PTE credit cannot offset MHS Tax.** A taxpayer with $2 million in PTE income generates approximately $186,000 in PTE tax credit (9.3% x $2M). However, the 1% MHS tax on income over $1M ($10,000) cannot be offset by the PTE credit. This residual liability is often overlooked in PTE benefit calculations.

2. **June 15 prepayment -- 2025 vs. 2026 rules differ.** For 2025, a late or insufficient June 15 payment permanently disqualifies the election. For 2026-2030, the election survives but the credit is reduced by 12.5% of the shortfall. Advisors must know which rule applies for the current tax year.

3. **PTE election must be on timely filed return.** The election is made on the entity's timely filed original or superseding return. A late-filed return cannot make a retroactive PTE election.

4. **Other State Tax Credit interaction.** For tax years 2022-2025, qualified taxpayers claiming the Other State Tax Credit (OSTC) must increase their "net tax payable" by the amount of PTE tax credit that reduced net tax before applying the OSTC. This prevents double-dipping.

5. **Overpayment of PTE does not create a refund at the owner level.** The credit is nonrefundable. If PTE tax paid exceeds the owner's California tax liability, the excess carries forward (up to 5 years) but is not refunded.
