## Overview

California maintains its own system of personal and business tax credits, many of which have no federal equivalent, and many federal credits have no California equivalent. This document serves as a comprehensive reference for the major California credits for tax years 2025-2026, including the SB 167 business credit cap, refundable credits (CalEITC, YCTC, FYTC), nonrefundable credits (Renter's Credit, Child and Dependent Care, Low-Income Housing), the PTE Elective Tax Credit, and the Film/TV Production Credit Program 4.0. It also catalogs the major federal credits that have no California counterpart, which is essential for understanding why a California taxpayer's credit position can be dramatically different from their federal position.

## California Rule

### SB 167 Business Credit Cap ($5 Million/Year, 2024-2026)

Senate Bill 167, signed June 27, 2024, imposes a cap on business credits for taxable years beginning on or after January 1, 2024, and before January 1, 2027. Under R&TC Section 17039.4 (PITL) and R&TC Section 23036.4 (CTL):

- The total of all **business credits** otherwise allowable for the taxable year shall not reduce the "net tax" by more than **$5,000,000** per taxpayer (or per combined reporting group for CTL purposes).
- **Credits excluded from the cap:** The Low-Income Housing Credit (R&TC Sections 17058 / 23610.5) is exempt from the $5 million limitation. Certain personal income tax credits (CalEITC, YCTC, FYTC, Renter's Credit, dependent exemption credits) are also excluded because they are not "business credits."
- **Carryforward extension:** The carryover period for any credit that is not allowed due to the $5 million limitation is extended by the number of taxable years the credit was not allowed.
- **Refundable credit election:** Taxpayers who cannot fully use business credits due to the cap may make an irrevocable election (on the original, timely filed return) to receive a refundable credit for the excess amount. The refund is paid in five equal annual installments beginning three years after the election. This means the first refund payment would not arrive until 2027 for a 2024 election.
- **Film credits and sales tax offset:** The election to apply qualified motion picture credits against sales and use taxes is also subject to the $5 million limitation.

**SB 175 Revenue Trigger.** Senate Bill 175 (enacted June 29, 2024) provides a suspension clause: if the California Director of Finance determines by May 14 of any year that General Fund revenues are sufficient without the NOL suspension and credit limitation, the restrictions do not apply for that tax year. As of March 2026, the SB 175 trigger has not been activated for 2024 or 2025.

### CalEITC -- California Earned Income Tax Credit (R&TC Section 17052)

CalEITC is California's own earned income tax credit, separate from the federal EITC. It is **refundable** and claimed on Form FTB 3514.

**2025 Tax Year Parameters:**

| Children | Maximum Credit | Earned Income Limit |
|----------|---------------|-------------------|
| 0 | $302 | $32,900 |
| 1 | $2,016 | $32,900 |
| 2 | $3,339 | $32,900 |
| 3+ | $3,756 | $32,900 |

**Key features:**
- Uses **California-specific** income thresholds and credit tables (not the federal EITC tables).
- Requires earned income -- at least $1 of earned income to qualify.
- Both SSN and ITIN holders are eligible (for tax years beginning on or after January 1, 2020).
- Self-employment income qualifies.
- California AGI is used for the computation, not federal AGI.
- Investment income limit applies (conforms to federal EITC rules with California modifications).
- The credit is independent of federal EITC -- a taxpayer may qualify for one, both, or neither.

### YCTC -- Young Child Tax Credit (R&TC Section 17052.1)

YCTC provides up to **$1,189** per eligible tax return for tax year 2025. It is **refundable** and claimed on Form FTB 3514.

**Eligibility requirements:**
- Must qualify for CalEITC (with one exception noted below).
- Must have a qualifying child under age 6 at the end of the tax year.
- California earned income of $32,900 or less.
- The credit phases out as earned income exceeds $27,425 and completely phases out at $32,901.

**Zero earned income exception (since 2022):** For tax years 2022 forward, YCTC eligibility is expanded to individuals with earned income of zero dollars or less (net loss situation), provided: total net losses do not exceed $35,640 in the current taxable year, and total wages/salaries/tips/other employee compensation do not exceed $35,640. Net loss includes all current-year losses, not just the portion utilizable on the return.

### FYTC -- Foster Youth Tax Credit (R&TC Section 17052.2)

FYTC provides up to **$1,189** per eligible taxpayer for tax year 2025. It is **refundable** and claimed on Form FTB 3514. If both spouses/RDPs qualify, the credit can be up to $2,378.

**Eligibility requirements:**
- Must qualify for CalEITC.
- Age 18 to 25 at the end of the tax year.
- Was in foster care while age 13 or older and placed through the California foster care system.
- Must claim the credit on Form FTB 3514 and satisfy foster care verification.
- The credit phases out as earned income exceeds $27,425 and completely phases out at $32,901.

### Renter's Credit (R&TC Section 17053.5)

The Renter's Credit is a **nonrefundable** credit of $60 or $120 for qualified renters.

**2025 Tax Year Parameters:**

| Filing Status | Credit Amount | CA AGI Limit |
|--------------|--------------|-------------|
| Single / MFS | $60 | $53,994 |
| MFJ / HOH / QW | $120 | $107,987 |

**Key features:**
- Credit amounts are fixed and not adjusted for inflation.
- AGI limits are adjusted annually for inflation.
- Must have rented and occupied a California premises as principal residence for at least 50% of the tax year.
- The premises must not be exempt from property tax.
- The taxpayer must not be claimed as a dependent by another person.
- No comparable federal credit exists.

### Child and Dependent Care Expenses Credit (R&TC Section 17052.6)

This **nonrefundable** credit is claimed on Form FTB 3506. It is computed as a percentage of the federal credit (Form 2441) based on California AGI.

**How it works:**
1. Compute the federal child and dependent care credit amount using federal Form 2441 methodology (qualified expenses up to $3,000 for one qualifying person / $6,000 for two or more).
2. Determine the applicable California percentage based on federal AGI using the table in the FTB 3506 instructions.
3. The California credit equals the federal credit amount multiplied by the California percentage.

The California percentage ranges from 50% (for lowest AGI) down to 0% (for highest AGI). The specific AGI breakpoints are published annually in the Form FTB 3506 instructions. Care must be provided in California. For nonresidents and part-year residents, only California-source earned income qualifies.

### Research Credit

See separate document `02-ca-research-credit.md` for full details. The California research credit (R&TC Section 17052.12 / 23609) is subject to the SB 167 $5 million business credit cap for 2024-2026. It has an indefinite carryforward period. California does not conform to certain federal changes, and the credit computation uses California-specific rules (including the recently enacted ASC method starting 2025).

### Film/TV Production Credit -- Program 4.0 (R&TC Sections 17053.98.1 / 23698.1)

Program 4.0, enacted under Senate Bill 132, is a $3.75 billion program running through June 30, 2030, with $750 million allocated per fiscal year. It applies to productions beginning on or after July 1, 2025.

**Key features:**
- **Refundable election.** A qualified taxpayer may make a one-time, irrevocable election to have certified tax credits refunded. The total refundable amount is 90% of the certified credits exceeding the taxpayer's tax liability in the first year. This amount is paid out over five consecutive taxable years (20% per year). The election must be made on an original, timely filed return for the year the credit certificate is issued.
- **Tax credit rates:** Range from 20% to 40% of qualified expenditures depending on project type (feature films, new TV series, relocating TV series, independent films, etc.) with various uplifts.
- **Non-transferable (with exceptions).** Credits for independent films with budgets of $10 million or less are transferable. Other credits are non-transferable but refundable under the election.
- **Purchasers cannot elect refundability.** A taxpayer that purchases a credit from an independent producer cannot make the refundable election.
- **Subject to SB 167 cap.** Film credits are subject to the $5 million business credit cap for 2024-2026, though the refundable election and sales tax offset provide alternative utilization mechanisms.

### Low-Income Housing Credit (R&TC Sections 17058 / 23610.5)

The California Low-Income Housing Tax Credit (LIHTC) is a **nonrefundable** credit allocated by the California Tax Credit Allocation Committee (CTCAC).

**Key features:**
- **Exempt from SB 167 $5M cap.** The LIHC is specifically excluded from the $5 million business credit limitation under SB 167. This is the primary business credit not subject to the cap.
- **4-year carryforward.** Unused credits may be carried forward for 4 years.
- **Computed under Section 42 of the IRC** with California-specific modifications under R&TC Section 17058.
- **Passive activity credit limitations.** LIHC is subject to passive activity credit limitations on Form FTB 3801-CR, but the dollar limitation for the rental real estate activity offset does not apply to LIHC for taxable years beginning on or after January 1, 2020, per R&TC Section 17561(d)(1).
- **Annual aggregate allocation** determined by the CTCAC based on a formula starting at $70 million (2001) indexed for inflation.

### PTE Elective Tax Credit (Form FTB 3804-CR)

See separate document `06-ca-pte-elective-tax.md` for full details. Key points for the credits master:

- **Nonrefundable** credit against personal income tax.
- **5-year carryforward** for unused credits.
- **Cannot reduce the 1% Mental Health Services (MHS) Tax** under R&TC Section 17043(c)(1). The PTE credit reduces regular "net tax" but not the additional 1% tax on taxable income exceeding $1 million.
- **Operative through 2025** (taxable years beginning before January 1, 2026), but the 5-year carryforward period extends beyond the repeal date.
- **Rate:** 9.3% of the qualified entity's qualified net income.
- **Interaction with Other State Tax Credit (OSTC):** For 2022-2025, taxpayers must increase "net tax payable" by the PTE credit amount before computing the OSTC.

## Conformity Analysis

### Federal Credits with NO California Equivalent

The following major federal credits have no California counterpart. Taxpayers who benefit from these credits federally receive no corresponding California benefit:

| Federal Credit | IRC Section | California Status |
|---------------|-------------|-------------------|
| Child Tax Credit (CTC) / Additional CTC | IRC 24 | No CA equivalent. California has no child tax credit. |
| Premium Tax Credit (ACA marketplace) | IRC 36B | No CA equivalent. |
| Saver's Credit (Retirement Savings) | IRC 25B | No CA equivalent. |
| American Opportunity Tax Credit (AOTC) | IRC 25A(b) | No CA equivalent. |
| Lifetime Learning Credit (LLC) | IRC 25A(c) | No CA equivalent. |
| Clean Vehicle Credit (EV Credit) | IRC 30D | No CA equivalent (separate CA programs exist outside the tax code). |
| Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit | IRC 25C | No CA equivalent. |
| Residential Clean Energy Credit | IRC 25D | No CA equivalent. |
| Adoption Credit | IRC 23 | No CA equivalent. |
| Foreign Tax Credit | IRC 901 | California has Other State Tax Credit (OSTC) for taxes paid to other states; no credit for foreign taxes. |

This non-conformity means a taxpayer with significant federal credits (e.g., CTC reducing federal tax by $2,000-$4,000 per child) receives no corresponding reduction on their California return. This is a major reason why effective California tax rates can exceed what taxpayers expect.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

California does not conform to the OBBBA changes to federal credits. Notably:

- **No CTC conformity.** Any OBBBA expansion of the federal Child Tax Credit does not flow through to California, which has no CTC equivalent.
- **No education credit conformity.** AOTC/LLC changes under OBBBA do not affect California.
- **No clean energy credit conformity.** California has separately decoupled from Inflation Reduction Act clean energy credits and does not conform to any OBBBA modifications.
- **Research credit:** See `02-ca-research-credit.md` for OBBBA impact analysis. California's research credit has its own rules and is subject to the SB 167 cap.

## Agent Rules

1. **CalEITC and YCTC use California-specific income thresholds.** Do not apply federal EITC tables or limits. The earned income limit for CalEITC/YCTC is $32,900 for 2025 regardless of filing status or number of children (unlike the federal EITC which has different limits by category). Compute using Form FTB 3514 and the California credit table.

2. **The SB 167 $5M business credit cap applies to the aggregate of all business credits.** This includes research credits, film credits (to the extent not refunded), and any other business credits or their carryovers. The low-income housing credit is the only business credit excluded from the cap. Non-business credits (CalEITC, YCTC, FYTC, Renter's Credit, exemption credits) are not subject to the cap.

3. **PTE Elective Tax Credit cannot reduce the Mental Health Services Tax.** When computing a taxpayer's California tax, apply the PTE credit to reduce net tax (the regular tax plus AMT, less exemption credits), but it cannot reduce the 1% MHS tax on taxable income above $1 million. This creates a floor below which the credit cannot reduce total California tax.

4. **The Film Credit 4.0 refundable election is irrevocable and must be made on the original, timely filed return.** Once elected, the taxpayer receives 90% of the excess credit over 5 years (20% per year) and cannot hold any amount to use against future tax liability outside the refundability schedule. Purchased credits are not eligible for the refundable election.

5. **Verify independently that no California equivalent exists before assuming a federal credit flows through.** The absence of California CTC, education credits, Saver's Credit, premium tax credit, and EV credits is the most common source of taxpayer surprise when comparing federal and California effective tax rates.

6. **For the Child/Dependent Care credit, the California credit is a percentage of the federal credit.** The percentage is determined by the taxpayer's federal AGI using the table in the Form FTB 3506 instructions. Care must be provided in California. The California credit is nonrefundable.

7. **CalEITC/YCTC can be claimed for up to 4 prior years.** Taxpayers may file or amend returns to claim CalEITC for prior years within the statute of limitations.

## Common Traps

1. **Applying federal EITC amounts and tables to the California return.** CalEITC has its own credit tables with different maximum amounts and different income thresholds. The federal EITC maximum for 3+ children in 2025 is $8,046; the CalEITC maximum is $3,756. These are two separate credits with different calculations.

2. **Assuming the Child Tax Credit exists for California.** There is no California Child Tax Credit. Taxpayers with children who receive $2,000-$4,000+ in federal CTC per child receive $0 for this credit on their California return. This is often the single largest difference between federal and California tax positions for families.

3. **Not accounting for the SB 167 $5M cap when projecting California tax liability.** Taxpayers with large R&D credits, film credits, or other business credits that historically eliminated California tax may be surprised to find their credits capped at $5 million for 2024-2026. The carryforward extension and refundable election provide partial relief but on a delayed basis.

4. **Forgetting that the PTE credit cannot offset the MHS Tax.** High-income taxpayers (over $1M taxable income) who expect the PTE credit to eliminate their entire California liability will still owe at least the 1% MHS Tax on income above $1M.

5. **Overlooking the YCTC zero-earned-income expansion.** Since 2022, taxpayers with net losses (but no more than $35,640) can qualify for YCTC even with zero earned income, provided they would otherwise meet CalEITC requirements.

6. **Missing the Renter's Credit because it is small.** At $60 or $120, many tax preparers overlook this credit. While the amount is minimal, it is available to a broad population and should be claimed when the AGI limits are met.

7. **Not making the SB 167 refundable credit election on the original, timely filed return.** The election to receive refundable credit installments for excess business credits above $5M must be made on the original return. It cannot be made on an amended return filed after the due date. Missing this deadline forfeits the refundable option for that year.

8. **Assuming the Low-Income Housing Credit is subject to the SB 167 cap.** The LIHC is specifically excluded from the $5 million business credit limitation. Taxpayers with LIHC allocations should claim the full credit amount without reduction.
