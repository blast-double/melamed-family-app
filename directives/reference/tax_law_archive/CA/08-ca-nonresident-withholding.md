## Overview

California requires withholding on payments of California-source income to nonresidents. There are two primary withholding regimes: (1) the general 7% nonresident withholding on services, rents, royalties, and distributions, and (2) the real estate withholding at 3 1/3% of sales price (or an alternative rate based on computed gain) on sales of California real property. The real estate withholding applies regardless of the seller's residency status — both residents and nonresidents are subject to it. The payer or withholding agent is liable if required withholding is not remitted.

---

## California Rule

### General Nonresident Withholding (7%)

R&TC Section 18662 requires withholding at a rate of **7%** on California-source payments to nonresidents. This applies to:

- **Services performed in California** — Payments to nonresident independent contractors for services rendered in California. This includes consulting, professional services, entertainment, and any other work performed within the state.
- **Rents and royalties** — Gross payments to nonresident owners of California real or personal property. Property managers collecting rent on behalf of nonresident property owners must withhold.
- **Gambling winnings** — California-source gambling winnings paid to nonresidents.
- **Pass-through entity distributions** — Distributions of California-source income to nonresident partners (partnerships), members (LLCs), shareholders (S corporations), and beneficiaries (estates and trusts).
- **Endorsement income** — Payments for endorsements connected to California.

**Who is a nonresident for withholding purposes:**
- Individuals who are not California residents
- Corporations not qualified through the California Secretary of State to do business in California or having no permanent place of business in California
- Partnerships or LLCs with no permanent place of business in California
- Trusts without a California resident grantor, beneficiary, or trustee
- Estates where the decedent was not a California resident

**Reporting:** Withholding agents use Form 592 to report general nonresident withholding. For pass-through entities withholding on owners, Form 592-PTE is used. The withholding agent provides Form 592-B to the payee as a statement of the amount withheld. Payees attach Form 592-B to their California tax return to claim the credit.

**Payment schedule:** Withholding is remitted in four quarterly periods, similar to estimated tax payments, using Form 592-V (voucher).

### Reduced Withholding and Waivers

- **Form 589** — Nonresident payees can request a reduced withholding rate if their effective California tax rate is less than 7%. The request must be approved by the FTB prior to payment.
- **Form 588** — Nonresident payees can request a complete withholding waiver in certain circumstances.
- **Form 590** — California residents and certain exempt entities file this to certify exemption from withholding.

### Backup Withholding

Backup withholding at **7%** applies when federal backup withholding is required (with certain limited exceptions). There are no reductions or waivers for backup withholding, and no minimum threshold. Backup withholding supersedes all other types of withholding. Dividends, interest, and financial institution loan fund releases are exempt from California backup withholding.

### Foreign (Non-U.S.) Partner Withholding

R&TC Section 18666 conforms to IRC Section 1446 for withholding on foreign (non-U.S.) partners and members with allocable amounts of effectively connected income to a California trade or business. The withholding rates for foreign partners are:

| Entity Type | Rate |
|---|---|
| Noncorporate partners | 12.3% |
| Corporate partners | 8.84% |
| Foreign bank and financial institution partners | 10.84% |

Foreign partners cannot file Form 588 (waiver) but may file Form 589 (reduced withholding).

## Real Estate Withholding (Form 593)

### General Rule

R&TC Section 18662(e) requires withholding on every disposition (sale, transfer, or exchange) of California real property. The withholding is a prepayment of income tax, not an additional tax.

**Default rate:** **3 1/3% of the total sales price.**

**Alternative rate:** The seller may elect (on Form 593) to have withholding calculated at the **applicable California tax rate on the computed gain** rather than 3 1/3% of the sales price. For individuals, this rate is up to **12.3%** (plus 1% Mental Health Services Tax if applicable) applied to the recognized gain. This alternative may result in lower withholding when the gain is small relative to the sales price, or higher withholding when the gain is large.

### Key Rules

1. **Applies to ALL sellers regardless of residency.** Unlike the general 7% nonresident withholding, real estate withholding applies to both California residents and nonresidents. This is a critical distinction that many taxpayers and practitioners miss.

2. **Section 121 exclusion does NOT prevent withholding.** Even if the seller qualifies for the IRC Section 121 principal residence exclusion ($250,000/$500,000), withholding is still required unless the seller certifies an exemption on Form 593. The seller must actively claim the exemption — it is not automatic. If the seller fails to certify, the escrow agent must withhold.

3. **Filed by the real estate escrow person (settlement agent).** The escrow company or settlement agent is responsible for computing the withholding, collecting it from the seller's proceeds, remitting it to the FTB, and filing Form 593 within **20 days** of the close of escrow.

4. **Installment sales.** Withholding applies to each installment payment. The escrow agent or buyer must withhold on each payment as it is made.

5. **Like-kind exchanges (Section 1031).** Withholding may be deferred for qualifying like-kind exchanges of California real property. If the replacement property is outside California, Form FTB 3840 must be filed.

### Exemptions from Real Estate Withholding

Sellers may certify exemptions from withholding on Form 593 if any of the following apply:

- The property is the seller's principal residence (Section 121 exclusion)
- The sale results in a loss
- The seller is a corporation qualified to do business in California
- The property is being transferred to an IRC Section 1031 intermediary
- The property is being foreclosed
- The seller is a tax-exempt entity
- The seller is a government agency

The exemption must be certified on Form 593 prior to close of escrow.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

The OBBBA does not directly affect California's nonresident withholding regime. However, OBBBA provisions that change the character or amount of California-source income (e.g., bonus depreciation affecting gain calculations on property sales) may indirectly affect the alternative withholding calculation on Form 593. California's non-conformity to OBBBA depreciation rules means the California gain on a property sale may differ from the federal gain.

## Agent Rules

1. **Form 593 applies to BOTH resident and nonresident sellers.** This is the most commonly misunderstood aspect of California real estate withholding. Unlike the general 7% withholding (which applies only to nonresidents), real estate withholding applies to every seller of California real property regardless of residency.

2. **Section 121 exclusion does NOT prevent withholding.** The seller must affirmatively certify the exemption on Form 593. If they do not, the escrow agent must withhold. Advise sellers to work with their escrow agent to complete the exemption certification before closing.

3. **Payer liability.** The withholding agent (payer of CA-source income or real estate escrow person) is personally liable for any withholding that should have been collected but was not. This liability attaches to the payer, not just the payee.

4. **Pass-through entity withholding is reported on Form 592-PTE.** Do not use Form 592 for withholding on entity owners; use Form 592-PTE for pass-through entity withholding on nonresident owners.

5. **Property managers must withhold.** A California property manager collecting rent on behalf of a nonresident property owner must withhold 7% from the gross rent payment and remit to the FTB. The management fee may be deducted before computing the 7%.

---

## Common Traps

1. **Assuming real estate withholding applies only to nonresidents.** It applies to all sellers. Residents must certify their exemption on Form 593 or face withholding.

2. **Relying on the Section 121 exclusion without certifying on Form 593.** The exclusion does not automatically exempt the transaction from withholding. Active certification is required.

3. **Missing the 20-day filing deadline for Form 593.** The escrow agent must file Form 593 and remit the withholding within 20 days of closing. Late filing results in penalties.

4. **Not withholding on property management payments.** Property managers who collect rent for nonresident owners must withhold 7% and remit to the FTB.

5. **Using the wrong form for pass-through entity withholding.** Form 592 is for payments to non-owners. Form 592-PTE is for withholding on entity owners (partners, members, shareholders).

6. **Ignoring the alternative withholding election on Form 593.** When the gain is small relative to the sales price, the alternative rate computation (tax rate on gain) may result in substantially lower withholding than the default 3 1/3% of sales price.
