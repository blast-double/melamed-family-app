## Overview

California filing status and residency determination are foundational to computing the correct tax liability. Filing status affects tax rates, standard deduction amounts, and credit eligibility. Residency status determines what income California can tax: residents owe tax on worldwide income, while nonresidents owe tax only on California-source income. Part-year residents are taxed on worldwide income during their resident period and California-source income during their nonresident period. California is also a community property state, which has significant implications for married couples, particularly when spouses have different residency statuses.

## California Rule: Filing Status

California recognizes five filing statuses that mirror the federal statuses, plus the extension to Registered Domestic Partners (RDPs):

### 1. Single (Filing Status 1)

An unmarried individual who does not qualify for Head of Household or Qualifying Surviving Spouse status. Same definition as federal.

### 2. Married/RDP Filing Jointly (Filing Status 2)

Married individuals or RDPs who file a joint return. Both spouses/RDPs report combined income and deductions.

### 3. Married/RDP Filing Separately (Filing Status 3)

Married individuals or RDPs who elect to file separate returns. Each reports only their own income (subject to community property allocation rules).

### 4. Head of Household (Filing Status 4)

An unmarried individual (or married individual meeting certain requirements) who maintains a home for a qualifying person. California requires taxpayers to file FTB 3532 (Head of Household Filing Status Schedule) to document eligibility. Beginning in taxable year 2018, the FTB denies HoH status if this form is not attached to the return.

### 5. Qualifying Surviving Spouse/RDP (Filing Status 5)

A surviving spouse or RDP who has not remarried and who maintains a home for a dependent child. Available for the two tax years following the year of the spouse's/RDP's death.

### Registered Domestic Partners (RDPs)

California treats RDPs identically to married couples for state income tax purposes (R&TC Section 17021.7). RDPs must file their California returns using Filing Status 2 or 3. This can create significant differences from the federal return, where the RDP may file as single, requiring complete recalculation of income allocation under community property rules.

## California Rule: Residency

Residency status determines the scope of California's taxing authority over an individual's income.

### Resident (R&TC Section 17014)

A "resident" is any individual who is:
- **Present in California for other than a temporary or transitory purpose**, OR
- **Domiciled in California but outside the state for a temporary or transitory purpose.**

Residents are taxed on their **entire worldwide income**, regardless of where it is earned. They file Form 540.

### Nonresident (R&TC Section 17015)

A "nonresident" is any individual who is not a resident of California. Nonresidents are taxed only on **income from California sources**. They file Form 540NR.

### Part-Year Resident

A "part-year resident" is any individual who is a California resident for part of the year and a nonresident for part of the year. During the resident period, they are taxed on worldwide income. During the nonresident period, they are taxed only on California-source income. They file Form 540NR.

### Domicile vs. Residence

These are distinct concepts under California law:

- **Domicile** (R&TC Section 17014(c); CCR Section 17014(c)): The place where an individual has their true, fixed, permanent home and principal establishment, to which they intend to return whenever absent. Every person has exactly one domicile at all times. Domicile is changed only by (1) physical presence in a new location and (2) intent to remain there permanently.

- **Residence** for tax purposes: Broader than domicile. A person can be a California tax resident without being domiciled there (if present for other than a temporary or transitory purpose) or can be domiciled in California but not a tax resident (under the safe harbor rule).

### The FTB Publication 1031 Closest Connections Test

The FTB uses a facts-and-circumstances analysis to determine residency, considering the totality of "closest connections." Key factors include:

1. **Amount of time spent** in California vs. other locations
2. **Location of spouse/RDP and children**
3. **Location of principal residence** (where you spend the most time)
4. **State issuing your driver's license**
5. **State where vehicles are registered**
6. **State where you are registered to vote**
7. **Location of your professional licenses** and business activities
8. **Location of bank accounts** and financial advisors
9. **Location of your social ties** (church, clubs, organizations)
10. **Location of real property** and rental activity
11. **Location of doctors, dentists, and other professionals**
12. **Mailing address** used for taxes, investments, and financial statements

No single factor is determinative. The FTB weighs all factors together. California aggressively conducts residency audits, particularly for high-income individuals who claim to have changed domicile.

### Safe Harbor Rule (R&TC Section 17014(d))

A special rule exists for individuals domiciled in California who are working outside the state. The "546-day safe harbor" provides that a California-domiciled individual is treated as a **nonresident** if ALL of the following are met:

1. The individual is outside California under an **employment-related contract** for an uninterrupted period of at least **546 consecutive days** (approximately 18 months).
2. The individual spends no more than **45 days in California** during any taxable year covered by the employment contract.
3. The individual does not have more than **$200,000 in intangible income** (interest, dividends, capital gains from stocks/bonds) during any taxable year covered by the contract.
4. The principal purpose of the absence is **not to avoid** California personal income tax.

The spouse/RDP of the qualifying individual is also treated as a nonresident while accompanying them for the 546-day period.

**Important:** Return visits to California that do not exceed 45 days total per taxable year are considered temporary. Exceeding 45 days breaks the safe harbor for that year.

### The Nine-Month Presumption

Although not an absolute rule, the FTB and California courts have applied a presumption that an individual present in California for more than **nine months** of the taxable year is a resident. This creates a strong (but rebuttable) presumption of resident status.

## California Rule: Income Sourcing for Nonresidents

When a nonresident or part-year resident earns income, California must determine whether the income is from a "California source." The sourcing rules vary by income type:

### Wages and Compensation

- **Rule:** Sourced to the state where the **services are physically performed**.
- If a nonresident works partially in California and partially elsewhere, wages must be allocated (typically by days worked in CA / total days worked).
- Remote work performed outside California for a California employer is generally NOT California-source income for the employee (California does not have a "convenience of the employer" rule).

### Business Income (Sole Proprietor / Partnership / S Corp)

- **Apportioning businesses:** If the trade or business has income from activities both within and outside California, income is apportioned using a **single sales factor** formula (market-based sourcing for services and intangibles; destination for tangible goods).
- **Non-apportioning businesses:** If the business operates only in California, 100% is California-source.
- **Market-based sourcing (CCR Section 25136-2):** For sales other than tangible personal property, income is sourced to the location of the taxpayer's market (i.e., where the customer receives the benefit of the service).

### Rental Income

- **Real property:** Sourced to the **location of the property**. Rental income from California real estate is always California-source income, regardless of the owner's residency.
- **Tangible personal property:** Sourced to the location where the property is used.

### Capital Gains

- **Real property:** Gain on sale of California real property is always California-source, regardless of the seller's residency.
- **Intangible personal property (stocks, bonds, partnership interests):** Generally sourced to the **owner's state of residence** at the time of sale, NOT the location of the issuer. Exception: If the intangible has acquired a "business situs" in California (e.g., used in a California trade or business), the gain may be California-source.
- **Goodwill and business asset sales:** Sourced based on the apportionment rules of the underlying business.

### Partnership and S Corporation Income

- Nonresident partners/shareholders are taxed on their share of the entity's California-source income.
- Schedule K-1 (565/568/100S) reports the California-source column (Column E) for nonresidents.
- Partnerships with California and non-California activities must apportion using Schedule R.

### Interest and Dividends

- **General rule for nonresidents:** Interest and dividends from intangible personal property are NOT California-source income unless the property has acquired a business situs in California.
- **Exception:** Interest/dividends from a business operating in California may be California-source.

## Nonresident Withholding

California requires withholding on payments of California-source income to nonresidents:

| Payment Type | Withholding Rate | Form |
|-------------|-----------------|------|
| Payments to nonresident independent contractors (services in CA) | 7% | Form 592 |
| Rents/royalties to nonresidents | 7% | Form 592 |
| Pass-through entity distributions to nonresident partners/members/shareholders | 7% (domestic) | Form 592-PTE |
| Foreign (non-U.S.) noncorporate partners | 12.3% | Form 592-PTE |
| Foreign corporate partners | 8.84% | Form 592-PTE |
| Foreign bank/financial institution partners | 10.84% | Form 592-PTE |
| Real estate sales by nonresidents | 3 1/3% of sales price OR optional gain election | Form 593 |

Withholding reported on Form 592-B (annual statement to payee). Taxpayer claims withholding as a credit on their California return.

**Exemptions from withholding:**
- Total CA-source payments to the nonresident are $1,500 or less for the calendar year
- Payee submits Form 590 (Withholding Exemption Certificate)
- Payment is for goods (not services)
- Services are not performed in California
- FTB issues a waiver (Form 588)

## Community Property Rules

California is a community property state. Community property rules apply to all married couples and RDPs domiciled in California.

### Basic Rules

- **Community income** includes wages, salaries, and other income earned by either spouse during the marriage while domiciled in a community property state. Each spouse owns 50% of community income.
- **Separate property** includes income from assets owned before marriage, gifts, and inheritances received individually.
- **Allocation:** If spouses file separately, each reports 50% of community income plus 100% of their own separate property income.

### Mixed-Residency Couples

When one spouse is a California resident and the other is a nonresident:
- The California resident spouse is taxed on all worldwide income, including their 50% share of community income.
- The nonresident spouse is taxed only on California-source income.
- If both spouses earn California-source income while domiciled in a community property state, the community income is split equally. Both spouses have California-source income and do NOT qualify for the nonresident spouse exception.

### Full Step-Up in Basis at Death

In community property states, when one spouse dies, the surviving spouse receives a full step-up in basis on **both halves** of community property (not just the decedent's half). This eliminates capital gains tax on appreciation that occurred during the marriage. This applies under both federal and California law (IRC Section 1014(b)(6)).

- Applies to all community property: real estate, investment accounts, business interests.
- Title designation matters: Community Property with Right of Survivorship (CPWROS) provides both the full step-up and automatic transfer without probate. Joint tenancy only steps up the decedent's half.

## Conformity Analysis

**IRC RULE:** Filing statuses defined in IRC Sections 1, 2, 7703. Federal residency is based on citizenship and lawful permanent resident status for international purposes.

**CALIFORNIA RULE:** R&TC Sections 17014-17015.5 define residency using domicile and presence concepts unique to California. Filing statuses mirror federal with RDP extension.

**Controlling Authority:** R&TC Sections 17014, 17015, 17021.7; FTB Publication 1031; CCR Sections 17014-17015

**Schedule CA Impact:** No adjustment needed for filing status differences. Residency differences affect which form is filed (540 vs. 540NR) and which income is included.

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE:** When determining residency, gather all closest-connection factors from FTB Pub 1031 before concluding. No single factor (such as driver's license state or time spent) is dispositive. Apply the totality-of-circumstances test.

> **AGENT RULE:** For the 546-day safe harbor, verify ALL four conditions: (1) 546+ consecutive days outside CA under employment contract, (2) no more than 45 CA days per tax year, (3) no more than $200K intangible income, (4) no tax-avoidance purpose. Failure of ANY one condition eliminates the safe harbor.

> **AGENT RULE:** When a nonresident sells California real property, withholding of 3 1/3% of the total sales price is required (Form 593). The seller claims this as a credit against their actual CA tax liability on Form 540NR.

> **AGENT RULE:** For mixed-residency married couples in a community property state, split community income 50/50. The nonresident spouse still has California-source income from their share of community wages earned in California. Do not assume the nonresident spouse has no CA filing obligation.

> **AGENT RULE:** When sourcing income for a nonresident, always apply the correct category-specific rule: wages by physical location of work, business income by market-based sourcing, rental income by property location, capital gains by type of asset (real property vs. intangibles).

## Common Traps

> **TRAP:** Assuming that leaving California automatically ends resident status. California uses domicile as a key factor. If a taxpayer maintains their closest connections to California (family, home, driver's license, voter registration), the FTB may still classify them as a resident even if they spend significant time elsewhere.

> **TRAP:** Exceeding 45 days in California during a safe harbor year. Even one extra day can break the safe harbor for that entire tax year, subjecting the individual to worldwide taxation as a California resident.

> **TRAP:** Applying federal "convenience of the employer" logic to California. California does NOT have this rule. If a nonresident works remotely from outside California for a California employer, the income is generally sourced to the employee's physical location, not the employer's.

> **TRAP:** Failing to file FTB 3532 when claiming Head of Household status. Beginning in TY 2018, the FTB denies HoH status if this form is not attached. This can result in higher tax rates and loss of the higher standard deduction.

> **TRAP:** Ignoring community property allocation when spouses file separately or have different residency statuses. California requires 50/50 allocation of community income regardless of which spouse earned it. This can create surprising filing obligations for a nonresident spouse.

> **TRAP:** Assuming capital gains from selling stock are California-source because the company is headquartered in California. Gains from intangible property (stocks, bonds) are generally sourced to the seller's state of residence, not the issuer's location. A Nevada resident selling Apple stock owes no California tax on the gain, even though Apple is a California company.
