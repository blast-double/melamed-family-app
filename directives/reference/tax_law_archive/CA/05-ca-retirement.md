## Overview

California's treatment of retirement plan contributions and distributions generally conforms to federal law, with three critical exceptions: (1) California imposes its own 2.5% additional tax on early distributions (combined 12.5% with the federal 10% penalty); (2) California does not conform to HSA provisions -- contributions are not deductible, earnings are taxable, and employer contributions are included in income; and (3) California conforms to SECURE 2.0 but did not conform to the original SECURE Act of 2019.

## California Rule

### Contributions

**401(k), 403(b), 457(b) Plans.** California conforms to the federal rules for elective deferrals, including SECURE 2.0 changes. Under R&TC Section 17501, California conforms by reference to Subchapter D of the IRC. R&TC Section 17501(b) provides that federal changes to Part I of Subchapter D automatically apply without regard to taxable year. For 2025, the 401(k)/403(b) deferral limit is $23,500 ($7,500 catch-up age 50+; $11,250 SECURE 2.0 enhanced catch-up age 60-63). California conforms to all these limits.

**Traditional and Roth IRAs.** California conforms to federal IRA contribution limits ($7,000; $1,000 catch-up age 50+) and deductibility rules under IRC Section 408, including Roth income phase-outs and traditional IRA deduction phase-outs.

**SEP/SIMPLE IRAs.** California conforms. SEP limit: lesser of 25% of compensation or $70,000. SIMPLE deferral limit: $16,500 ($3,500 catch-up age 50+).

**Health Savings Accounts (HSAs) -- DOES NOT CONFORM.** This is the most significant area of non-conformity in the retirement/savings space. California has never conformed to IRC Section 223. As a result:

- **Contributions are not deductible.** The federal deduction for HSA contributions must be added back on Schedule CA (540). Employer contributions to HSAs, which are excluded from federal W-2 wages, must be added to California AGI (reported on Schedule CA, line 1/column C, from W-2 box 12 code W).
- **Earnings are taxable.** Interest earned and taxable dividends earned on HSA accounts are taxable in the year earned for California. This requires adjustments on Schedule CA lines 2 and 3.
- **Distributions for qualified medical expenses are generally tax-free for California** because of the California basis in the account (since contributions were not deducted). However, the amount taxable under federal law, less interest and dividend income previously taxed by California, is not taxable by California.
- **IRA-to-HSA rollovers.** Federal one-time rollover (IRC 408(d)(9)) does not apply for California. The rollover is taxable and subject to the 2.5% additional tax.
- **529-to-Roth IRA rollovers.** SECURE 2.0 provision does not apply for California. Such rollovers are taxable and subject to a 2.5% additional tax.

**Ongoing legislation.** HSA conformity bills (AB 727 in 2022, AB 781 in 2025) have been introduced but as of March 2026, none have been enacted.

### Distributions

**Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs).** California conforms to the SECURE 2.0 Act RMD ages:
- **Age 73** for individuals who reach age 72 after December 31, 2022, and age 73 before January 1, 2033.
- **Age 75** for individuals who reach age 73 after December 31, 2032.

California conformed to SECURE 2.0 provisions generally but did not conform to the original SECURE Act of 2019. The CAA, 2023 resolved this, bringing California into conformity with the current RMD framework.

**10-Year Inherited IRA Rule.** California's conformity to SECURE 2.0 means the 10-year distribution requirement for most non-spouse beneficiaries applies for California purposes.

**Roth Conversions.** Fully taxable for both federal and California. The converted amount is included in California AGI in the year of conversion. No 10% federal penalty or 2.5% California additional tax applies to conversions.

**Backdoor Roth IRA.** California conforms to the pro-rata rule under IRC Section 408(d)(2). Federal Form 8606 applies. California follows the same computation, but the California basis may differ if prior-year California adjustments affected IRA contributions.

### The California 2.5% Additional Early Distribution Tax

**R&TC Section 17085(c)** imposes a California additional tax on early distributions from qualified retirement plans. This tax is **separate from and in addition to** the 10% federal penalty under IRC Section 72(t).

**The rate is 2.5%** of the amount includible in income (not 10%).

**Combined effect:** A taxpayer who takes an early distribution that is subject to both penalties pays:
- 10% federal early distribution penalty (Form 5329)
- 2.5% California additional tax (Form FTB 3805P)
- **Total penalty: 12.5%** (plus regular income tax at both federal and state rates)

**Exceptions to the California 2.5% tax** generally parallel federal exceptions to the 10% penalty, but each must be verified independently for California. The California exceptions apply "as applicable for federal income tax purposes for the same taxable year" per R&TC Section 17085(c). Common exceptions include:

| Exception | Federal 10% | CA 2.5% |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| Age 59.5 or older | Exempt | Exempt |
| Death of account owner | Exempt | Exempt |
| Disability (IRC 72(m)(7)) | Exempt | Exempt |
| Substantially Equal Periodic Payments (72(t)(2)(A)(iv)) | Exempt | Exempt |
| Separation from service at age 55+ (qualified plans only) | Exempt | Exempt |
| Qualified public safety employees (age 50+) | Exempt | Exempt (auto-conforms) |
| First-time homebuyer ($10K IRA) | Exempt | Exempt |
| Higher education expenses (IRA) | Exempt | Exempt |
| Medical expenses exceeding 7.5% AGI | Exempt | Exempt |
| SECURE 2.0 emergency expenses ($1K) | Exempt | Verify individually |
| SECURE 2.0 domestic abuse distributions | Exempt | Verify individually |
| SECURE 2.0 terminal illness | Exempt | Verify individually |

**ABLE account distributions.** California conforms to the federal rules for ABLE (Achieving a Better Life Experience) accounts but imposes a 2.5% additional tax (rather than the federal 10%) on distributions not used for qualified disability expenses.

### Pension Income Taxation

- **CalPERS/CalSTRS pensions** are fully taxable to California residents. Federal law (4 USC Section 114) prohibits states from taxing retirement income of nonresidents, so former residents who relocate are not subject to California tax on these pensions.
- **Social Security benefits** are fully excluded from California income regardless of income level.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

California auto-conforms to qualified plan changes via R&TC Section 17501(b). However, OBBBA changes to HSAs (IRC 223), the federal 10% early distribution penalty rate, or Social Security taxation would not affect California -- HSA non-conformity persists, the 2.5% rate is fixed in R&TC 17085(c), and California fully excludes Social Security.

## Agent Rules

1. **Early distributions trigger BOTH the 10% federal penalty AND the 2.5% California additional tax, for a combined 12.5% total.** These are separate taxes imposed by separate sovereigns. Do not net them. Report the federal penalty on Form 5329 and the California additional tax on Form FTB 3805P.

2. **HSA contributions, earnings, and employer contributions must all be added back on Schedule CA (540).** This requires multiple adjustments: add back the HSA deduction (Schedule CA line 13, column C), add back employer contributions (Schedule CA line 1, column C from W-2 box 12 code W), add back interest earned (Schedule CA line 2, column C), and add back dividends earned (Schedule CA line 3, column C).

3. **Verify each early distribution exception independently for California.** While the exceptions generally parallel federal, each SECURE 2.0 new exception should be independently confirmed for California conformity. Do not assume all new federal exceptions automatically apply.

4. **Social Security benefits are fully excluded from California income regardless of income level.** No Schedule CA adjustment is needed because the benefits are already excluded from California AGI. Federal AGI is the starting point for the California return, and Social Security benefits included in federal AGI must be subtracted on Schedule CA.

5. **IRA-to-HSA and 529-to-Roth rollovers are taxable for California.** These transactions are tax-free federally but require income addition on Schedule CA and are subject to the 2.5% additional tax on Form FTB 3805P.

6. **Former California residents receiving CalPERS/CalSTRS pensions after relocating out of state are not subject to California tax on that income.** Federal law (4 USC Section 114) prohibits source-state taxation of retirement income of nonresidents.

## Common Traps

1. **Forgetting the California 2.5% additional tax on early distributions.** Many taxpayers and preparers focus only on the federal 10% penalty and overlook the separate California 2.5% tax, leaving the California Form FTB 3805P unfiled and the additional tax unpaid.

2. **Not adjusting for HSA contributions and earnings on Schedule CA.** Because HSAs are federally deducted and excluded, the California adjustments are easy to overlook. Every HSA participant needs Schedule CA adjustments for contributions, employer contributions, and earnings every year.

3. **Applying federal Social Security taxation rules to California.** California fully excludes Social Security benefits -- do not replicate the federal up-to-85% inclusion.

4. **Overlooking the Roth conversion impact on California AGI.** A large conversion increases California AGI, affecting the $25,000 rental loss phase-out, Renter's Credit AGI limit, and other AGI-based thresholds.

5. **Treating IRA-to-HSA and 529-to-Roth rollovers as tax-free for California.** These are federally tax-free but create California taxable income and trigger the 2.5% additional tax.

6. **Not tracking California HSA basis.** Because contributions are not deducted, the taxpayer has California basis in the HSA. This basis reduces California taxable distributions. Without tracking, the taxpayer may double-pay California tax.
