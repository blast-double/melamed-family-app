## Overview

California is the most aggressive state in the nation when it comes to residency audits. The Franchise Tax Board (FTB) maintains dedicated high-wealth audit teams specifically focused on taxpayers who claim to have left California or who assert nonresident status while maintaining significant California connections. Given California's top marginal rate of 13.3% (12.3% + 1% Behavioral Health Services Tax on income over $1,000,000), the stakes for residency determinations are enormous. A single high-income taxpayer who is reclassified from nonresident to resident can owe millions in back taxes, penalties, and interest.

Residency is primarily a question of fact determined by examining all the circumstances of the taxpayer's particular situation. There is no single bright-line test.

---

## California Rule

### Statutory Definition (R&TC Section 17014)

California defines a "resident" as:

1. **Every individual who is in this state for other than a temporary or transitory purpose.** This captures individuals who are physically present in California for a substantial period, even if they do not consider California their permanent home.

2. **Every individual domiciled in this state who is outside the state for a temporary or transitory purpose.** This captures individuals who maintain their permanent home (domicile) in California but are temporarily living elsewhere — for example, on a work assignment, extended vacation, or sabbatical.

A **nonresident** is any individual who is not a resident. A **part-year resident** is any individual who is a California resident for part of the year and a nonresident for part of the year.

### Domicile vs. Residence

**Domicile** is the place where an individual has a true, fixed, and permanent home and to which the individual intends to return whenever absent. A person can have only one domicile at a time. Domicile is established by physical presence in a location plus the intent to make that location one's permanent home.

**Residence** for California tax purposes is broader than domicile. A person can be a California "resident" under the statutory definition even without being domiciled in California, if they are present in the state for other than a temporary or transitory purpose.

---

## Burden of Proof

The burden of proof in a California residency dispute falls on the **taxpayer** to demonstrate that they are not a California resident. This is a critical point: the FTB does not need to prove that the taxpayer is a resident. The taxpayer must prove, by **clear and convincing evidence**, that they are not.

This is a higher standard than the typical "preponderance of the evidence" used in most civil tax disputes. The clear and convincing evidence standard requires that the taxpayer's evidence be highly and substantially more probable to be true than not.

## Safe Harbor

FTB Publication 1031 describes a safe harbor available for certain individuals leaving California under employment-related contracts:

An individual domiciled in California who is outside California under an employment-related contract for an **uninterrupted period of at least 546 consecutive days** will be considered a nonresident, **unless** any of the following conditions is met:

1. The individual has **intangible income exceeding $200,000** in any taxable year during the employment contract
2. The principal purpose of the absence is to **avoid personal income tax**
3. Return visits to California exceed **45 days** in any taxable year covered by the contract

Key points about the safe harbor:
- The 546-day period must be **uninterrupted** — separate contracts cannot be combined
- Return visits exceeding 45 days during any single tax year break the safe harbor
- The safe harbor provides a rebuttable presumption of nonresidence, not absolute protection
- The safe harbor only applies to employment-related absences, not voluntary relocations

Individuals not covered by the safe harbor must determine residency status based on the totality of facts and circumstances.

## Evidence Examined in Residency Audits

The FTB conducts thorough investigations using extensive digital and physical evidence. The following categories of evidence are routinely examined:

### Digital and Electronic Evidence
- **Cell phone records** — Location data, tower connections, GPS data. The FTB may subpoena cell phone carriers for location records showing where the taxpayer's phone was physically located throughout the year.
- **Credit card and debit card records** — Transaction locations showing where purchases were made, providing a daily record of physical presence.
- **Airlines and travel records** — Flight itineraries, boarding pass records, loyalty program data, TSA PreCheck records.
- **Social media** — Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn, and other social media posts with location tags, photos at California locations, check-ins, and geo-tagged content.
- **E-ZPass / FasTrak / toll records** — Toll road usage records proving presence on California highways.
- **Amazon / online shopping** — Delivery addresses for packages, particularly when deliveries are made to California addresses.

### Physical and Institutional Evidence
- **School enrollment** — Where the taxpayer's children attend school. California school enrollment is very strong evidence of California residency.
- **Medical and dental records** — Where the taxpayer receives healthcare, prescriptions filled, doctor visit locations.
- **Charitable contributions** — Donations to California-based charities, attendance at California galas and fundraisers.
- **Gym and club memberships** — Active memberships at California gyms, country clubs, social clubs.
- **Voter registration** — Registration and voting history in California.
- **Driver's license** — Whether the taxpayer holds a California driver's license (or vehicle registration).
- **Professional licenses** — California bar membership, medical licenses, real estate licenses.
- **Bank accounts** — Accounts at California bank branches, safe deposit boxes.
- **Real property** — Ownership or rental of California residences, particularly the relative size and condition of California vs. non-California homes.
- **Pet records** — Veterinary records for pets (the FTB has been known to subpoena vet records to establish physical presence).
- **Employment connections** — Where the taxpayer works, office location, business meetings in California.

## Departure Year: Establishing Nonresidence

For taxpayers intending to leave California permanently, the departure year is the most critical and most frequently audited period. The following must be established:

### Sever All California Connections
- Sell or permanently rent out the California home (do not maintain it as a secondary residence)
- Cancel California gym memberships, club memberships, and social organization memberships
- Transfer children to schools in the new state
- Establish medical and dental providers in the new state
- Update driver's license and vehicle registration to the new state
- Change voter registration to the new state
- Update bank accounts (primary accounts should be in the new state)
- File change of address with the post office
- Update all financial accounts, insurance policies, and professional associations

### Establish New State Connections
- Purchase or lease a primary residence in the new state
- Obtain the new state's driver's license
- Register to vote in the new state
- Join community organizations, religious institutions, and social clubs in the new state
- Establish professional and business connections in the new state

### Document Daily Location
- Maintain a **contemporaneous daily log** of physical location for at least **four years** after the departure year
- Keep all receipts, boarding passes, hotel records, and other documentation proving daily whereabouts
- The four-year period aligns with the California statute of limitations for assessment

### Timing
- Do not leave California mid-year and expect to be treated as a nonresident for the entire year. The departure year will result in part-year resident status at best.
- The earlier in the year the departure occurs, the less income is subject to California's worldwide taxation as a resident.

## NING Trusts: Do NOT Work for California Residents

**FTB Legal Ruling 2019-02** addresses Nevada Incomplete Gift Non-Grantor (NING) trusts and similar structures (also called DINGs for Delaware, and WINGs for Wyoming).

**The FTB's position is that NING trusts do not work to avoid California income tax for California residents.** Under California trust taxation rules, a trust is taxable if it has a CA resident trustee, beneficiary, or CA-source income. Even if structured as a non-grantor trust federally, NING income remains taxable to the CA resident grantor. The FTB actively pursues these structures. Do not rely on NING trusts to avoid CA income tax.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

The OBBBA does not directly address state residency determination. However, the significant differences between California and federal tax treatment of OBBBA provisions (tip exclusion, overtime exclusion, increased SALT cap, bonus depreciation, etc.) increase the tax differential between California residents and nonresidents, potentially increasing the incentive for high-income taxpayers to relocate and the FTB's incentive to audit residency claims.

## Agent Rules

1. **California is the most aggressive state for residency audits.** The FTB has dedicated teams and significant resources devoted to high-income residency cases. Treat any change of residency from California to nonresident status as a high-audit-risk event.

2. **Burden of proof is on the taxpayer — clear and convincing evidence standard.** This is a higher standard than typical tax disputes. Documentation must be thorough and contemporaneous.

3. **Digital evidence is king.** Cell phone location data, credit card transactions, and social media are the primary evidence sources. Advise clients to be aware of their digital footprint.

4. **The safe harbor is narrow.** It applies only to employment-related absences of 546+ consecutive days with specific limitations. Most voluntary relocations do not qualify.

5. **NING trusts do not work.** Do not advise California residents to use incomplete gift non-grantor trusts to avoid California income tax. The FTB has taken a clear position against these structures.

6. **Document the departure year aggressively.** Maintain daily location logs and sever all California connections. The departure year is the most-audited period.

---

## Common Traps

1. **Maintaining a California residence after "leaving."** Keeping a California home available for personal use (even if the taxpayer claims it is for family visits) is strong evidence of continued residency.

2. **Children enrolled in California schools.** This is perhaps the strongest single indicator of California residency.

3. **Excessive return visits.** Spending more than 45 days per year in California undermines both the safe harbor and the general nonresidency claim.

4. **Social media contradictions.** Posts showing the taxpayer at California locations, attending California events, or maintaining California social connections undermine nonresidency claims.

5. **Relying on the safe harbor for non-employment moves.** The safe harbor only applies to employment-related absences. Voluntary relocation (retirement, lifestyle change) must be evaluated under the full facts-and-circumstances test.

6. **Insufficient documentation.** Failing to maintain contemporaneous records of daily location for 4+ years after departure leaves the taxpayer unable to meet the clear and convincing evidence burden.

7. **NING trust reliance.** Using an incomplete gift trust to avoid California tax on investment income will be challenged by the FTB under Legal Ruling 2019-02.
