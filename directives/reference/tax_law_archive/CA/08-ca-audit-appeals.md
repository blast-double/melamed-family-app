## Overview

California has its own statute of limitations (SOL) for tax assessment and collection, its own audit process, and its own appeals system (the Office of Tax Appeals, or OTA, which replaced the former Board of Equalization for most tax appeals). The general California SOL is **four years** — one year longer than the federal three-year general SOL. The most commonly missed compliance obligation in California is the requirement to report federal audit changes to the FTB within six months, a failure that can keep the California statute of limitations open indefinitely.

## California Rule

### Statute of Limitations — Assessment

| Situation | SOL Period | R&TC Section | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **General rule** | 4 years from the later of: return due date or date filed | 19057 | Federal is 3 years. CA is 1 year longer. |
| **25%+ gross income omission** | 6 years | 19058 | Parallel to IRC 6501(e). Overstatement of basis may count. |
| **No return filed** | Unlimited — no expiration | 19060 | FTB can assess at any time. |
| **Fraud** | Unlimited — no expiration | 19060 | Burden of proof on FTB to establish fraud. |
| **Federal change — reported within 6 months** | 2 years from date taxpayer reports to FTB | 18622 | Taxpayer must file amended CA return or Form 540X within 6 months of final federal determination. |
| **Federal change — reported after 6 months** | 4 years from date taxpayer reports to FTB or FTB learns of change | 18622, 19060 | Late reporting = extended SOL. |
| **Federal change — NOT reported** | Unlimited — SOL never starts | 18622, 19060 | **Most commonly missed obligation.** SOL remains open indefinitely. |
| **Federal waiver signed** | Later of: normal CA SOL, or 6 months after federal waiver expires | 19065 | CA SOL tracks federal waiver. |

### Federal Change Reporting — R&TC Section 18622

**This is the single most commonly missed California tax compliance obligation.** When the IRS makes any change to a taxpayer's federal return — whether through an audit, amended return, or other adjustment — the taxpayer must report that change to the California Franchise Tax Board within **6 months** of the date of the final federal determination.

The report is made by filing Form 540X (amended California return) or by sending written notice to the FTB. The 6-month period runs from:

- The date the IRS issues its final determination (e.g., the 30-day letter or 90-day notice becomes final)
- For partnership-level proceedings (post-BBA), the date of the final federal partnership adjustment

**Consequences of failure to report:**
- The California statute of limitations **never starts running**. The FTB can assess additional California tax at any time — years or even decades later.
- Any changes to any tax year resulting from the federal change also remain open. For example, if the IRS adjusted a 2020 credit that carried over to 2023, both years remain open if the change was not reported.
- The FTB actively cross-references IRS audit data and routinely discovers unreported federal changes, often many years after the original federal audit.

**Best practice:** Report every federal change to the FTB immediately, even if the change would not affect California tax liability. The cost of filing an informational amended return is minimal compared to the risk of an indefinitely open statute of limitations.

## Audit Types

The FTB conducts several types of audits:

### Correspondence Audit
- Conducted entirely by mail.
- Typically involves simple issues: math errors, missing schedules, information matching (W-2s, 1099s, K-1s).
- Taxpayer responds in writing with documentation.

### Office Audit
- Taxpayer or representative meets with an FTB auditor at an FTB office.
- Involves more complex issues requiring discussion and document review.

### Field Audit
- FTB auditor visits the taxpayer's home, business, or representative's office.
- Used for the most complex or high-dollar cases.
- Common for business audits, multi-entity issues, and large deductions.

### Residency Audit (Special Program)
- **California is the most aggressive state in the nation for residency audits.**
- The FTB maintains dedicated high-wealth audit teams specifically focused on taxpayers who claim to have left California or who claim nonresident status.
- These audits examine extensive digital and physical evidence (see [10-ca-residency-audit-defense.md]).
- Targeted at high-income individuals, particularly those with income above $200,000 who change filing status from resident to nonresident.

## Protest and Appeal Process

### Step 1: Notice of Proposed Assessment (NPA)

The FTB issues an NPA when it proposes additional tax, penalties, or interest. The NPA is not a final assessment — it is a proposal.

### Step 2: Protest (60 Days)

The taxpayer has **60 days** from the date of the NPA to file a written protest. The protest should include:
- Specific grounds for disagreement
- Supporting facts and documentation
- Legal authorities
- The taxpayer's or representative's signature

If no protest is filed within 60 days, the NPA becomes a final assessment.

### Step 3: Settlement Bureau

During the protest phase, the case may be referred to the FTB Settlement Bureau for potential resolution. Settlement discussions are confidential and without prejudice.

### Step 4: Notice of Action (NOA)

After reviewing the protest, the FTB issues a Notice of Action (NOA) with its final determination. The NOA may sustain, modify, or withdraw the NPA.

### Step 5: Appeal to OTA (30 Days)

The taxpayer has **30 days** from the date the FTB mails the NOA to file a written appeal with the **Office of Tax Appeals (OTA)**.

The OTA is an independent agency (Government Code Sections 15670-15674) that replaced the Board of Equalization for most personal income and corporate tax appeals effective January 1, 2018. Key features:
- **Three-judge panel** hears each case
- **Formal hearing** open to the public (or written briefing if hearing waived)
- **Designated opinions** are precedential and binding on the FTB
- **Non-designated opinions** are persuasive but not binding
- Interest continues to accrue during the OTA appeal process — it is not tolled

The appeal must include:
- The taxpayer's name, address, and identification number
- The amounts and tax years at issue
- A copy of the NOA
- A statement of facts and legal authorities
- The taxpayer's signature or authorized representative's signature

### Step 6: Judicial Review — Superior Court

If the taxpayer is unsatisfied with the OTA's decision, the next step is filing a petition in California Superior Court. **The taxpayer must pay the full assessed amount first** (pay-to-play rule). The Superior Court conducts a de novo review of the factual and legal issues.

## Collection

### Installment Agreements
The FTB offers installment payment plans for taxpayers who cannot pay their full liability. Penalties and interest continue to accrue during the installment period.

### Offer in Compromise (OIC)
Under R&TC Section 19443, the FTB may accept an offer in compromise if there is doubt as to:
- **Liability** — the taxpayer disputes the underlying tax
- **Collectibility** — the taxpayer lacks the ability to pay the full amount

The OIC process is separate from the IRS OIC and requires a separate application.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

The OBBBA does not materially change the federal SOL or audit framework. However, OBBBA changes that generate federal amended returns (e.g., retroactive bonus depreciation elections, Section 179 changes) will trigger the 6-month federal change reporting obligation to the FTB. Taxpayers who amend their federal returns to claim OBBBA benefits must also report those changes to California — even though California does not conform to the underlying OBBBA provisions.

## Agent Rules

1. **California SOL is 4 years, not the federal 3 years.** Always use the longer California period when advising on record retention and amendment deadlines.

2. **Federal audit changes MUST be reported to California within 6 months.** This is the most commonly missed obligation. Failure to report keeps the California SOL open indefinitely. The FTB's litigation roster includes active cases where taxpayers failed to report federal changes from years or decades ago.

3. **Interest does not toll during OTA appeals.** Factor the time value of money into appeal decisions. A multi-year dispute at the OTA level can result in significant interest charges even if the taxpayer ultimately prevails on the substantive issue.

4. **Residency audits are a special program.** The FTB has dedicated teams for high-income residency cases. Standard audit defense strategies may not apply. See the residency audit defense document.

5. **Pay-to-play for judicial review.** If the OTA rules against the taxpayer, proceeding to Superior Court requires full payment of the assessed liability first.

---

## Common Traps

1. **TRAP: Not reporting federal changes to California within 6 months.** This is the most commonly missed California compliance obligation. Every federal audit, amended return, or IRS adjustment must be reported. The SOL remains open indefinitely if this is not done.

2. **Assuming the federal 3-year SOL applies to California.** California's SOL is 4 years. Records should be retained accordingly.

3. **Filing a protest after 60 days.** The NPA becomes final if the protest is not filed within 60 days. There is no extension of this deadline.

4. **Expecting interest to stop during an appeal.** Unlike federal Tax Court proceedings, California interest accrues throughout the OTA process.

5. **Ignoring the 25% omission rule.** If the taxpayer omitted more than 25% of gross income from the return, the SOL extends to 6 years. Overstatement of basis may also trigger this provision.

6. **Not considering the impact on related tax years.** Federal changes to credits, deductions, or income in one year can affect carryover items in subsequent years. All affected California tax years remain open until the federal change is properly reported.
