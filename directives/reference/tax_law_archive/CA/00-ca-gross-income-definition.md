## Overview

California's definition of gross income begins with IRC Section 61 — "all income from whatever source derived" — incorporated by reference through R&TC Section 17071. However, California imposes its own set of modifications. Certain items included in federal gross income are excluded by California (requiring Schedule CA Column B subtractions), and certain items excluded federally are included by California (requiring Schedule CA Column C additions). These differences are the primary drivers of Schedule CA adjustments and the gap between federal AGI and California AGI.

## California Rule

### General Definition

R&TC Section 17071 states: "Section 61 of the Internal Revenue Code, relating to gross income defined, shall apply, except as otherwise provided." This means California starts with the same broad definition of gross income as the IRC but then applies California-specific modifications throughout Part 10 of the R&TC.

The general categories of gross income that apply identically under both systems include:

- Compensation for services (wages, salaries, commissions, tips, bonuses)
- Business income
- Gains from dealings in property
- Interest (with exceptions noted below)
- Rents and royalties
- Dividends (with exceptions noted below)
- Alimony received (instruments executed on or before December 31, 2018; post-2018 instruments now conformed under SB 711)
- Income from discharge of indebtedness
- Distributive share of partnership income
- Income from S corporations
- Income in respect of a decedent

### Registered Domestic Partners (RDPs)

California treats Registered Domestic Partners the same as married couples for California income tax purposes. RDPs must file California returns using married filing jointly or married filing separately status. Community property rules apply to RDPs. This can create significant differences from the federal return where RDPs may file as single for federal purposes (depending on federal recognition status).

## California-Specific Inclusions (Schedule CA Column C Additions)

The following items are **added** to federal AGI on Schedule CA Column C because California includes them in income while the federal return either excludes them or allows deductions California does not recognize:

### 1. Bonus Depreciation Add-Back

California does not conform to IRC Section 168(k) bonus depreciation. Any bonus depreciation claimed on the federal return must be added back. The taxpayer then computes California depreciation using regular MACRS without the bonus component.

- **Schedule CA line:** Part I, Section B, line 3 or line 5, Column C (depending on business type)
- **R&TC authority:** R&TC Sections 17250, 17250.5

### 2. Section 179 Excess Add-Back

California limits the Section 179 deduction to $25,000 with a $200,000 phaseout. Federal law (as amended by OBBBA) allows $2,500,000 with a $4,000,000 phaseout. The excess of the federal deduction over the California limit must be added back.

- **Schedule CA line:** Part I, Section B, line 3 or line 5, Column C
- **R&TC authority:** R&TC Section 17255

### 3. QBI Deduction Add-Back

The federal qualified business income deduction under IRC Section 199A (20% of qualified business income) does not exist for California. The full amount of any QBI deduction claimed on the federal return must be added back.

- **Schedule CA line:** Part II, Section C, line 15, Column C
- **R&TC authority:** No R&TC incorporation of IRC Section 199A

### 4. OBBBA-Specific Deductions Add-Back

Any deductions created by the OBBBA (tip income, overtime pay, auto loan interest, senior citizen bonus standard deduction) that reduce federal AGI must be added back for California because California does not conform to OBBBA provisions.

- **Schedule CA line:** Applicable lines in Part I or Part II, Column C

### 5. Non-California State and Municipal Bond Interest

Interest on bonds issued by states other than California (and their political subdivisions) is taxable by California, even though it is excluded from federal gross income under IRC Section 103. The full amount of out-of-state municipal bond interest must be added to California income.

- **Schedule CA line:** Part I, Section A, line 2a, Column C
- **R&TC authority:** R&TC Section 17143 (excludes only CA bond interest)

### 6. HSA Contributions and Earnings

California does not conform to federal HSA legislation (IRC Section 223) enacted beginning January 1, 2004. The following must be added to California income:

- **Employer HSA contributions:** Excluded from federal income under IRC Section 106(d) but included in California income.
- **Employee HSA deductions:** Deductible for federal purposes under IRC Section 223 but not deductible for California.
- **HSA interest and dividends:** Tax-deferred federally but taxable by California in the year earned.
- **HSA distributions for medical expenses:** Tax-free federally but may be taxable by California (to the extent of previously untaxed California basis).

California requires the full employer contribution and employee above-the-line deduction to be added back on Schedule CA Column C. Interest and dividends earned in the HSA are also added to Column C.

- **Schedule CA line:** Part I, Section A, line 1z (employer contributions); Part II, Section C (employee deductions); Part I, Section A, line 2a (interest), line 3 (dividends), Column C
- **R&TC authority:** California does not incorporate IRC Section 223; FTB Publication 1001

### 7. OBBBA Exclusions Add-Back

Any new exclusions enacted under OBBBA that reduce federal gross income (such as changes to EV credits treated as exclusions, Trump Account earnings, etc.) must be added back for California.

## California-Specific Exclusions (Schedule CA Column B Subtractions)

The following items are **subtracted** from federal AGI on Schedule CA Column B because California excludes them from income while the federal return includes them:

### 1. Social Security Benefits (100% Excluded)

California fully excludes Social Security benefits from income. This is one of the most significant differences for retirees. Federally, up to 85% of Social Security benefits may be taxable depending on provisional income.

- **Schedule CA line:** Part I, Section A, line 5a, Column B
- **R&TC authority:** R&TC Section 17087 (incorporating IRC Section 86 with modification to exclude entirely)

### 2. U.S. Government Bond Interest

Interest on obligations of the United States government (Treasury bonds, notes, bills, savings bonds, TIPS) is excluded from California income under the doctrine of federal instrumentality immunity. This interest is taxable federally but must be subtracted for California.

- **Schedule CA line:** Part I, Section A, line 2a, Column B
- **R&TC authority:** Constitutional (federal instrumentality doctrine); R&TC Section 17131.4

### 3. Railroad Retirement Benefits

Railroad retirement benefits (Tier 1 and Tier 2) are excluded from California income, similar to Social Security. The federally taxable portion must be subtracted.

- **Schedule CA line:** Part I, Section A, line 5a, Column B
- **R&TC authority:** R&TC Section 17087

### 4. California State Lottery Winnings

California State Lottery winnings are exempt from California personal income tax. While lottery winnings are fully taxable federally, the California portion must be subtracted on Schedule CA.

- **Schedule CA line:** Part I, Section B, line 8b or 8z, Column B
- **R&TC authority:** California Constitution, Article IV, Section 19(d); R&TC Section 17131.4

### 5. Unemployment Compensation

California excludes unemployment compensation from income. Federally, unemployment benefits are taxable under IRC Section 85.

- **Schedule CA line:** Part I, Section A, line 7, Column B
- **R&TC authority:** R&TC Section 17083

### 6. State Tax Refund (to extent included federally)

If a state income tax refund is included in federal gross income (because the taxpayer itemized in the prior year and took a state tax deduction), California may require a subtraction because the state tax deduction is not allowed for California purposes.

- **Schedule CA line:** Part I, Section B, line 1, Column B
- **R&TC authority:** R&TC Section 17073.5

### 7. Section 174 R&E Expenditure Difference

Federal law requires capitalization and amortization of R&E expenditures over 5 or 15 years. California allows full deduction. The difference (federal amortized amount minus full expense) creates a subtraction.

- **Schedule CA line:** Part I, Section B, line 3 or 5, Column B
- **R&TC authority:** California does not conform to TCJA amendment of IRC Section 174

### 8. Business Interest (Section 163(j) Difference)

Federal law limits business interest to 30% of adjusted taxable income. California does not apply Section 163(j). Any business interest disallowed federally but deductible for California creates a subtraction.

- **Schedule CA line:** Part I, Section B, applicable line, Column B
- **R&TC authority:** R&TC Section 24344(e) (CTL); similar PIT treatment

## Conformity Analysis

**IRC RULE:** IRC Section 61 defines gross income broadly. Numerous subsequent IRC sections provide specific exclusions (Section 86 — Social Security, Section 103 — municipal bond interest, Section 223 — HSAs, etc.).

**CALIFORNIA RULE:** R&TC Section 17071 adopts IRC Section 61 by reference. Subsequent R&TC sections then modify by excluding items California does not tax and including items California does tax differently.

**Controlling Authority:** R&TC Sections 17071, 17087, 17131, 17131.4, 17143, 17155; SB 711

**Schedule CA Impact:** Column B subtractions for CA-excluded items; Column C additions for CA-included items.

## OBBBA Non-Conformity

**OBBBA PROVISION:** OBBBA created new below-the-line deductions for tip income, overtime pay, and auto loan interest. OBBBA also created a $4,000 senior standard deduction bonus and Trump Accounts (tax-advantaged savings).

**CALIFORNIA STATUS:** Does not conform. Any of these deductions or exclusions claimed on a federal return must be added back as Schedule CA Column C additions. California does not recognize tip income deductions, overtime deductions, auto loan interest deductions, the senior bonus, or Trump Account tax benefits.

## Agent Rules

> **AGENT RULE:** When processing a return with Social Security income, subtract 100% of the federally taxable Social Security amount on Schedule CA Column B. California taxes zero Social Security.

> **AGENT RULE:** When a taxpayer has HSA contributions, deductions, or earnings on their federal return, add the ENTIRE federal tax benefit back on Schedule CA Column C. This includes: (a) employer contributions excluded from W-2 Box 12 Code W, (b) the above-the-line deduction on Form 1040, and (c) any interest or dividends earned in the HSA. Track the California basis in the HSA separately because distributions may have different tax treatment.

> **AGENT RULE:** When a taxpayer holds municipal bonds, check whether they are California-issued or out-of-state. California bonds: no adjustment needed. Out-of-state bonds: add the interest to Column C. U.S. Treasury bonds: subtract the interest in Column B.

> **AGENT RULE:** When a taxpayer has unemployment income on their federal return, subtract the entire amount on Schedule CA Column B. California does not tax unemployment compensation.

> **AGENT RULE:** If a federal return includes any OBBBA-created deduction (tips, overtime, auto loan, senior bonus), add the full amount back on Schedule CA Column C.

## Common Traps

> **TRAP:** Forgetting to add back HSA benefits for California. HSA contributions, deductions, and earnings are the single most common missed adjustment. California has never conformed to HSAs. Every dollar of HSA tax benefit on the federal return must be reversed for California purposes. Additionally, the taxpayer builds a separate California basis in the HSA, which affects the taxability of future distributions.

> **TRAP:** Assuming all municipal bond interest is exempt from California tax. Only bonds issued by the State of California and its political subdivisions are exempt. Bonds from other states (New York, Texas, etc.) and U.S. territories (except certain specified possessions) are taxable by California and must be added on Column C.

> **TRAP:** Forgetting that California lottery winnings are exempt. Large lottery payouts that appear on the federal return as taxable should be subtracted on Schedule CA. However, winnings from other states' lotteries are NOT exempt from California tax.

> **TRAP:** For RDP couples, failing to reconcile community property income allocations. If a federal return is filed as single (or as RDP under prior rules), but the California return must be filed as married, the income allocations may differ significantly due to community property rules.
